Violence Prevention Academy helps promote safer schools

Five-person teams of administrators and teachers from a Valley elementary school, middle school and high school have begun a five-month training process through the Violence Prevention Academy (VPA), a new outreach program of the Partnership for Community Development at ASU West.

The teams from Dysart High School, Desert Sands Middle School and Cimarron Springs Elementary School are attending periodic training sessions throughout the current semester to devise violence prevention plans specific to their schools. Team members, including principals, vice principals, counselors, resource officers and influential teachers, will implement their programs in the spring while receiving continuing assistance from VPA staff.

"The academy takes a data-driven approach to violence prevention," says Todd Armstrong, ASU West assistant professor of criminal justice and criminology.

"We are working with academy participants to devise ways to measure the impact of the anti-violence programs and policies they implement in their schools - for example, by tracking changes in the number of office referrals over time," Armstrong says. "This quantitative information will help us identify which strategies are effective and which ones need to be strengthened or discontinued."

Armstrong and Vince Webb, professor of criminal justice and criminology, developed the VPA curriculum in collaboration with colleagues from the ASU West Social Work Department and College of Education.

"There is a great deal of research-based information available that focuses on violence prevention planning, programs and policies. Unfortunately, this knowledge doesn't always reach those who need it most," Webb says. "We want to help school personnel translate the latest research findings into practical, effective programs."

'Establishment of the VPA resulted from the work of the Violence Prevention Initiative, a diverse coalition of community leaders brought together in the late 1990s by the city of Phoenix, Arizona Supreme Court, Maricopa County Attorney's Office and Greater Phoenix Leadership. The group investigated the root causes, trends and strategies necessary to combat problems associated with violence.

"Even though most school violence is not life-threatening, some form of violence impacts a significant number of students and teachers," says Rick Miller, executive director of the Violence Prevention Initiative.

"The three schools participating in this first year of the VPA program do not necessarily have a violence problem that is significantly different from the typical K-12 school in the Valley," Armstrong says. "Some schools that go through the program may find that they do not have a serious problem, but the experience will help them to understand why that is the case and to keep doing the things that are working for them."

Armstrong says VPA staff members intend to move quickly to a "train the trainer" process, in which school participants who have completed the academy's program are able to train other personnel in their school districts.

For information about the Violence Prevention Academy, call (602) 543-6600 or visit (www.west.asu.edu/vpa).
