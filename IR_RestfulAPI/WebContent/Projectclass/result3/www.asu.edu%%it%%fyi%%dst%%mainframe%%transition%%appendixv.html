APPENDIX V
Transition Documentation Resources

 

Mainframe Documentation

Information Technology (IT) mainframe documentation takes two major forms: online and printed. The online documentation for a given mainframe is almost always stored on that particular computer and viewed through menus or "help" commands. Much of this online documentation consists of detailed information about that computer's operating system, application programs, and peripherals. On some of the IT mainframes, online "news" or "info" facilities provide users with additional, more general information.

The following table, broken down by operating system, provides the name of the command used to reference online documentation.


    Operating System     Help Command(s)
      IBM VM/CMS           HELP, NEWS
      IBM MVS/TSO          HELP, TUTORIAL(ISPF)
      VAX VMS              HELP, NEWS
      UNIX                 HELP

IT's printed documentation consists of locally-written documents (write-ups) which describe the specifics of using Information Technology's computers, networks and software. These are available at the Customer Assistance Center in Computing Commons room 202 and can be accessed online, too.

 

An Information "Transition"

As you might expect, Information Technology continues to use both online and printed documentation to inform the ASU community about its facilities and services and to provide detailed "how-to" information in many different areas. Recent years, however, have brought changes in the way that IT provides its online information. These changes include:

  1. an increased use of electronic methods for distributing information
  2. the distribution of "electronic information" to a larger audience from more accessible locations

There's been an explosive growth in the use of electronic information resources such as Usenet (electronic news), FTP archives, Archie, Gopher, World Wide Web (WWW), etc. The popularity of WWW, for example, is evident when you see electronic addresses (URLs) presented in magazines, radio, and, increasingly, in television commercials. This growing interest in electronically providing and consuming information is as evident within the ASU community as it is outside, and it influences how Information Technology presents information.

Recently, IT developed an information system called "FYI" which makes extensive use of WWW pages. FYI includes descriptions of IT computing services and facilities, as well as detailed technical information. If you have a WWW browser, reference this URL http://www.asu.edu/it/fyi/ to view the FYI pages.

In addition to "consumer demand", IT has other good reasons for using the newer electronic methods. Gopher and WWW pages can link to related documents on computers all over the world, not just information residing on the same machine. Hypertext documents allow the author to create, link, and share information from different media, such as text, graphics, video and sound. In addition, hypertext's non-sequential presentation of information allows the reader to explore and assimilate it in different ways. Besides these specific advantages to hypertext, IT can "distribute" changes in most of its electronically stored information faster than changes in printed counterparts.

This document's "General Overview" section stated that client/server computing was "the direction of the future." This computing trend applies to information systems in addition to statistical, compute and database servers. Information servers, including WWW, Gopher, news, electronic mail, and FTP servers communicate with clients on many different hardware platforms and operating systems. This allows a wider audience to access the information.

As an example of this wider audience, consider the On-Line Unix Help System, which displays a variety of information when you type "help" on "general", "stats", "research" and other Cluster computers. You may also view this same information with a WWW browser - you don't need access to the Cluster or a special userid/password to get it. The URL for the On-Line Unix Help System is http://www.asu.edu/it/fyi/dst/helpdocs/.

A new ASU student at home using Netscape through an Internet provider could view which statistical packages were available on the "Stats" servers and even download instructions for using those packages, without coming to ASU and without access to that server. (The student will need access to that server to use one of the statistical packages.) A veteran IBM MVS SAS user considering a transition to Unix might look at Help material from an office PC to see what this environment has to offer. In general, "transitioners" will be able to view Help information to explore their options before they move in a particular direction.

 

Printed Documentation

IT recognizes that, in addition to the growing popularity and use of electronically stored information, many computer users continue to rely on printed materials. Many of the write-ups are stored electronically in formats that can be downloaded and printed locally at http://www.asu.edu/quicklook/. Refer to this URL for further information: http://www.asu.edu/it/fyi/cacenter.

IT publishes an on-line magazine, InsideIT, on the first Wednesday of every month. InsideIT contains information on topics including internet, client/server computing and general Information Technology resources. To subscribe to InsideIT, go the InsideIT webpage, and click on the subscribe button.

Instructors and others may find the Computing Site personal computers to be an attractive alternative to the mainframe. To get more information on IT computing sites go to their FYI webpages at http://www.asu.edu/it/fyi/sites/
