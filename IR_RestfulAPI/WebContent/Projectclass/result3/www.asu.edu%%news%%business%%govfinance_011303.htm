January 13, 2003

Report analyzing government finance released

The report "Public Finance in Arizona" has been released by the Center for Business Research at Arizona State University. An objective review of government finance in Arizona, the report looks specifically at state government as well as at the combined finances of all state and local governments.

According to Tom R. Rex, the center's research manager, major findings of the analysis include the following:

  • Arizona's projected deficit for the next fiscal year is about one-seventh of the general fund. However, more than 60 percent of general fund spending is "mandatory:" statutorily required. The projected deficit thus is about 40 percent of "discretionary" spending, which largely funds higher education and corrections.
  • Arizona's state government budget deficit projected for the current and succeeding fiscal years is among the largest in the country as a percentage of the general fund. While the deficit results in part from the weak economy, tax cuts much greater than spending reductions - implemented primarily between fiscal years 1995 and 2001 - largely are responsible.
  • The state's tax cuts were far more aggressive than in the average state. Those states that passed the largest tax cuts currently are facing the largest deficits.
  • The state's large tax reductions have caused the state's tax burden relative to the size of its economy to be the lowest during the period of available data (fiscal year 1971 through fiscal year 2003).
  • The combined state and local government tax burden in Arizona currently is well below average - by 11 to 17 percent on most measures of tax burden. Historically (into the early 1990s), the tax burden was near the national average.
  • Total inflation-adjusted per person government spending in Arizona also is considerably below the national average and the historical level. Fast population growth causes capital outlays to be above the national average; spending on current operations is among the lowest in the nation.
  • If the limit on the size of the state's rainy day fund had not been lowered in 1995 from 15 to 5 percent of the general fund, much of the funds used for tax reductions would not have been available, given the requirement for a balanced budget. Thus, not only would the current deficit be much lower, the balance in the rainy day fund would have been greater. The state would be faced with little deficit beyond that covered by transfers from the rainy day fund.

ASU

The "Public Finance in Arizona" report is available on the center's web site (www.cob.asu.edu/seid/cbr). A summary of the report will appear in the January issue of AZB/Arizona Business, a publication of the Center for Business Research.

The center is a part of the L. William Seidman Research Institute in the College of Business at Arizona State University. The Seidman Institute is an affiliation of seven research centers that serves as a link between the local, state, national and international business communities and the creative and intellectual resources of the nationally ranked College of Business.
