Seven ASU Students, Recent Grads Awarded Reach for the Stars Fellowships

Dallas de Lowe Jahaziel Linare Megan Monaghan Demetra Presley
Brent Roam Stephanie Robb Monica Rodriguez

Seven ASU students and recent graduates have been awarded the 2005-06 Reach for the Stars Fellowship by the Division of Graduate Studies (DGS) to pursue advanced degrees. They comprise the inaugural class of Reach for the Stars fellows, adding further to their honor.

The competitive university-wide fellowship program was launched by the DGS to recruit top ASU students into graduate school and increase diversity. The program is unique in that it provides students who demonstrate strong academic merit and the potential to succeed in graduate school with a pathway to graduate school and the preparation, mentoring, and funding for their success. Fellows receive a $12,000 stipend and full tuition remission as part of the award. The program is a joint effort between DGS, the Office of the Vice President for Undergraduate Initiatives, and the Barrett Honors College.

"ASU is committed to supporting these and other talented students who are underrepresented in their disciplines to achieve their educational goals," said Maria T. Allison, Vice Provost and Dean of Graduate Studies. "They are a vital part of the state's leadership and future."

The 2005-06 Reach for the Stars fellows are selected for their strong record of achievement and scholarly promise.

  • Dallas de Lowe graduated from ASU last year with concurrent degrees in religious studies and sociology and will continue to the master's program in religious studies. A member of the Gila River Indian Community, he is an Akimel O'odham/Pee Posh who is fluent in the O'odham language. De Lowe is interested in advancing the scholarly understanding of O'odham religious life, and his intention is to correct and add to the scholarship on O'odham oral traditions.

  • Jahaziel Linare received a bachelor's in justice studies from ASU in 2001. He is a research specialist for Southwest Interdisciplinary Research Center at ASU, conducting research for the "Latino Acculturation and Health Project." In addition, he works part-time helping to facilitate a prevention intervention program aimed to reduce the risk of poor mental health and school dropouts. Linare is fluent in four languages and will begin the master's in social work program in the fall.

  • Megan Monaghan graduated from ASU in May with a bachelor's in psychology and a minor in justice studies. A student of the Barrett Honors College, she was recipient of the National Hispanic Scholarship and Provost Scholarship. Her volunteer work with patients suffering from serious mental illness has motivated her to pursue a master's in social work beginning in the fall. Megan looks forward to improving the quality of life for the mentally ill in a variety of settings.

  • Demetra Presley graduated in May with a degree in justice studies. Domestic violence and prison reform are her two primary areas of interest. Presley's ambitions include becoming an advocate for the development of programs designed to assist former prisoners to transition back into society as well as establishing a domestic violence shelter for abused women and children. She starts the master's program in justice and social inquiry in the fall.

  • Brent Roam, a 1995 ASU graduate in religious studies, is returning to pursue a law degree. He is a Rhodes Scholar and Woodrow Wilson Fellow, as well as an actor and playwright. Currently, he is the communications director and youth ministry team leader at a local church his family founded. With a law degree, and his background in religion, anthropology, and public policy, Roam aspires to have a career in criminal justice and public service.

  • Stephanie Robb received a degree in biomedical engineering from ASU in May. She made the Dean's List every semester during her academic career. Her educational objectives include earning a master's in biomedical engineering. She would like to pursue a career in biomedical research or new product development.

  • Monica Rodriguez received a degree in psychology from ASU in December. Her experience as the primary caregiver of her autistic brother who suffers from cerebral palsy inspired her to seek a master's in counseling. She is currently a volunteer for the Mental Health Association of Arizona. As a counselor, she would like to serve people from diverse backgrounds. In addition, her goal is to incorporate meditation into counseling.



For more information about the Reach for the Stars Fellowship Program, contact the Division of Graduate Studies at (480) 965-7279 or visit www.asu.edu/graduate/financial/DGSopportunities.htm.
