Pulitzer Prize and Tony Award winning Playwright Wendy Wasserstein plays herself in ASU's 2003 Marshall Lecture

Arizona State University's College of Liberal Arts and Sciences is bringing renowned playwright and author Wendy Wasserstein out from behind the curtain.

Wasserstein will be on hand to tell her own story 7:30 p.m. Monday, September 15 in Arizona State University's Carson Ballroom, for ASU's 11th annual Jonathan and Maxine Marshall Distinguished Lecture. Wasserstein's lecture, "A Life in the Theater," is free to the public and presented by the ASU College of Liberal Arts and Sciences. (For reservation information, see the end of this release.)

Wasserstein is, by the estimation of many, "the voice of a generation." Her works have told the story of the social changes that have given us the world of today, examining contemporary life with a critical but humorous eye. Often she recounts the lives of women caught up in the first waves of the women's liberation movement. Throughout her career, Wasserstein's work has been heavily influenced by the events and issues of her own life.

In her lecture, Wasserstein will share her take on making a career in the arts - she has a wealth of experience on which to draw for this topic. Wasserstein's plays include the wildly successful The Heidi Chronicles, Isn't it Romantic, Uncommon Women and Others, The Sisters Rosenswieg, and An American Daughter. The Heidi Chronicles won Wasserstein a Tony Award for best play and the coveted Pulitzer Prize. Wasserstein was the first woman to win the Pulitzer for an original play.

Her talent not limited to plays, Wasserstein is also the author of the children's book Pamela's First Musical; a collection of essays written for New York Woman entitled Bachelor Girls; and Shiksa Goddess (Or, How I Spent My Forties), a collection of 35 humorous essays. Wasserstein is also a screenwriter - her screenplay, The Object of My Affection, became a major motion picture starring Jennifer Aniston. Wasserstein has taught at New York University, Columbia and Princeton.

Washington University writes of Wasserstein's lectures that they are "pure Wasserstein - warm and witty and wise and intelligent, honest and evocative."

The lecture is free, but reservations are required and space is limited. To reserve seats, call 480-965-0051 or email clasevents@asu.edu, specifying the Marshall Lecture, your name, and how many seats are desired.

The Marshall Lecture series is funded by grants from Jonathan and Maxine Marshall, retired publishers of The Scottsdale Progress, and by the Marshall Fund of Arizona. Free parking for the event is available in all ASU parking structures.
