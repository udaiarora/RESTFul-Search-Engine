December 8, 2004

Professors’ ‘Quest for Distinction’ lead to little-known artist

ASU art history professor Gray Sweeney is on a quest to see that landscape artist Gilbert Munger gets the attention he deserves.

In his new book, “Gilbert Munger: Quest for Distinction,” Sweeney sheds new light on an artist that hasn’t had much recognition. It is Sweeney’s second book on the artist, and he hopes to prove why Munger is worth researching.

“He was the perfect representative of the new type of artist,” Sweeney says. “He was original.”

According to Sweeney, “Quest for Distinction” goes into much greater detail and depth than his first book.

“The book tries to show a newer way of thinking about these landscape painters,” Sweeney says. “Not just that he was great, but why he was great.”

Munger began his career painting Western landscapes in the United States. He later moved to Europe, where he found success painting landscapes.

Many scientists and geologists commissioned his paintings, and he had the capability to please them, Sweeney says, adding: “He adapted his style to meet the demands of the market.”

The opportunity to research Munger appeared when Sweeney was invited to put together a catalog of realist paintings in Minnesota . He came across Munger’s work and realized the importance of the artist’s landscapes.

“This was an artist that was really exceptional and not really known at the time, but it was time to make him known,” Sweeney says. “It was a fascinating discovery that fell into my lap.”

Computer scientist Michael Schroeder, who became interested in Munger after reading Sweeney’s first book on the artist, later contacted Sweeney. Together, they co-wrote “Quest for Distinction.”

Sweeney says that writing about Munger has given him a better insight into the way art history is understood.

“There hadn’t been a historical study about Munger until I wrote it,” Sweeney says. “That’s been personally exciting to make the contribution.”
