September 22, 2003

Courtroom vaults College of Law Into 21st Century

The ASU College of Law can now offer its students and area lawyers a place to polish their skills and learn how to better incorporate technology into their court trials with its new 21st Century Courtroom.

The facility, established with a gift from Chip Harris in memory of his late son Ryan, includes a PC-based integrated control system, a state-of-the-art presentation lectern, customized counsel tables, and a touch-screen annotation system, much like the one used by sports commentators to draw on television screens.

The college plans to showcase the new courtroom with an open house from 3:30 to 5 p.m., Sept. 29.

Catherine O'Grady, law professor and the college's executive director of clinical programs, coordinated the project.

"The courtroom will prepare students to practice in the technological environment that exists today," she says. "That's the challenge for all of us, as lawyers. As technology develops, we have to incorporate it as part of the 'lawyering' process. We're extremely grateful to all those who helped bring this great resource into being for our students."

The automated systems were designed and integrated by Integrated Courtroom Technology, Inc., a leading provider of courtroom technology design and installation services, also known as Courttech. Additional funding for the project came from University resources, a donation from Courttech and a long-term loan of S-video cameras from Wolfvision.

O'Grady credits the courtroom's design team members, including the ASU Facilities Department, Dick & Fritsche Design Group and Jerry Davis of Jeremiah Associates, for the courtroom's enhanced teaching capabilities.

"Jerry and our architects were critical players in the completion of this project," she says. "They creatively addressed the college's desire to enhance the courtroom's capabilities."

While the courtroom will provide pedagogical advantages, O'Grady believes it also will be a great tool for use by the Valley legal community.

"When not in use by the College of Law, attorneys may train on this equipment. Also, the courtroom is trial-ready. This is yet another way that the knowledge and resource exchange between the college and the community works to the benefit of everyone," O'Grady says.

Phillip "Duke" Argetsinger, of Courttech, agrees. For him, the future is now, and the college's new courtroom offers a prime example of the new technologies rapidly transforming the nation's courts.

"The 21st Century Courtroom is a valuable resource both for the educational mission of the College of Law and for the legal community at large. The past five years have seen an enormous growth of automation requirements in the litigation environment and a major commitment by courts of law," he says. "It's a revolutionary development."

The courtroom's bench is named in memory of Judge James Ackerman, a 1982 College of Law graduate and Division I Court of Appeals Judge.

Marlowe, with the College of Law, can be reached at (480) 965-1689 or (stephen.marlowe@asu.edu).
