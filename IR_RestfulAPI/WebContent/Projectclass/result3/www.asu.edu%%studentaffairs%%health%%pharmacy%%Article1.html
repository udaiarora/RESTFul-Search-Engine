"Do I Get a Smile With That?"
by Carl Labbe RPh


I didn't notice her until the yelling started. The pharmacy clerk was trying
to tell her that the state would not pay for her prescription so soon. I went over
to assert my authority and bring some reason to the scene. Upon evaluating the
facts I told the young lady that the state welfare system expects a package of
birth control pills to last for four weeks and she had gotten hers filled just over
a week ago. She looked at me with question marks in her eyes and said, "How
do they know?" I told her that there were twenty-eight tablets, one for each
day, so, a 4 week supply. Now her eyes were filled with surprise and she
gasped, "You mean you don't take one every time?"

There are always surprises in health care and there always will be. The
politicians can't seem to decide what is the best way to provide the best health
care for the most Americans. It's a big issue, an important issue. My
experience has shown that the advent of managed or supervised health care is
hastening the departure of compassion and humanity from the delivery of
health care. The focus has shifted to managed care, and with it a sense that the
primary purpose of Medicine is to achieve the biggest bang for the buck.
Managed care is so "managed" that the human components of caring sometime
get overrun. Front line providers of care are increasingly frustrated by
decisions of bureaucratic managers who are not compassionate care givers.

It is easy to lose sight of one's humanity when trying to service a seemingly
endless stream of "state assisted" patients. I've been there. I was having one
of those harried winter days, when the flu bug was raging and prescriptions
were flooding the pharmacy. Although I made it a point to hand each patient
their prescription and tell them how to take it, I made no real human contact.
Then I presented a filled Rx to Mrs. Browning telling her to take one tablet
every morning to control her blood pressure. Thinking my duty complete, I
started to turn away when I heard her ask me, "Do I get a smile with that?" It
was like a splash of cold water in the face of a feverish man. I stopped in my
tracks, took one brief look at the work piling up, then turned towards her,
smiling. I said, "You not only get a smile, Mrs. B, you can have a hug too if
you like." She answered by holding open her arms. After we hugged I took the
time to chat with her, just like in "the good old days." With my spirit
brightened I returned to work with renewed vigor.

It's amazing what a little human kindness and sympathy can do for one's
outlook! It's so important, not only to our routine dealings with our fellow
humans, but is also crucial to our mental and physical health. Time after time
we hear of healing with the aid of psychological or spiritual treatments.

As a child, my family was fortunate to have a doctor who sincerely cared
for his patients. Dr. Pomerleau had an easy- going manner and the ability to
make you feel better with a few well chosen words or a small squeeze on the
shoulder. My Mom said that he could coax the germs right out of your body if
you gave him the chance. We believed he could, too! I had a wart once and
Dr. Pomerleau bought it. He pressed a penny on it, spoke some Latin, then
gave the penny to me and told me to throw it in a river or a stream. I did as he
commanded and the wart soon disappeared.

Most of today's medical care is not augmented by such metaphysical
phenomena. More and more, our medical care is being thought of as another
commodity. I believe that good medicine does not answer to the laws of
supply and demand. Yet, I have seen our state's welfare medical system turn
doctors' offices into "fast health" joints where the patients are sent through like
cattle. I have seen clinics where a doctor is rarely visible.

One patient came to my pharmacy very distressed. Maria was terrified
because she knew she must be deathly ill. She had been to her clinic and this
time they made her see THE DOCTOR! Maria had been going to this clinic
for over a year and had never seen the doctor. Now she had seen him and he
scared her. She feared that her chart that he was holding contained some dark
deadly secret. She was apprehensive, intimidated, and flustered. She left the
clinic and headed straight to the pharmacy. Maria knew that she could talk to
me. She hadn't even dared to ask the doctor any questions, so she didn't ask
him about her bothersome cough. I told her she should consider herself lucky
to have seen the doctor and that I was sure he would have told her if anything
was seriously wrong. As for the cough, I told her that it was a common side-
effect of her blood pressure medicine and suggested a sugar free lozenge.

Many patients, like Maria, leave the doctor's office with little idea of what
their medical problems are, let alone their possible solutions and ultimate
resolution. The delivery system of care is losing its sense of caring. Patients
that do not assert themselves because of fear, ignorance, or inexperience are
often not getting quality care.

The problem does not affect every provider. A great many medical offices
still take the time to interact with their patients even as they examine,
interrogate, and treat. These clinicians, by necessity, spend more time with
their patients. Of course, this means they must be willing to disregard the
financial incentives that drive them to see more patients in less time.
Additionally, many HMOs are now examining patient satisfaction levels, with
some even establishing a maximum number of patients per hour for their
physicians.

But what can be done to ensure that our medical needs are attended to by
concerned, compassionate professionals? We have plenty of boards and
certifying bodies to ensure that the practitioner meets minimum educational
requirements, but what kind of committee can monitor and encourage
compassion? It really can't be measured by outside review, but a discerning
patient can tell when a care giver is acting without empathy.

So maybe a well-informed patient population is a key factor in the
delivery of quality medical care. But it's probably a pipe dream to hope that a
provider group or even an insurance company would pay for a training
program that results in more aggressive patients. I would think that some of
these bean-counting bureaucrats would pale at the thought of knowledgable
patients demanding that their doctors spend enough time with them to explain
their condition and treatment. The patients are the ones that should really be
scared, though. What do you do if you're stuck with an unsympathetic doctor
in a health plan that allows you no other choice? With universal coverage
without freedom of choice, a lot of us could end up in that situation.

We do have a chance to create a health care delivery system that will
retain the human qualities that make some medical recoveries so incredible.
Concern and compassion must be encouraged and allowed to flourish. This
will not happen in an atmosphere of overpowering economic pressures. The
system must be sure to allow patients to search for and find doctors that they
can respect, trust, and believe in. Allow healthy competition to drive the
system towards higher levels of quality.

Pharmacists have always been a very accessible link in the health care
system and should insist that they remain so. It would be to our benefit to
insist on that same accessability for all health care providers.

No matter what kind of health care system we end up with, it will
probably be subsidized by the taxpayer. We can only hope that the taxes are
kept to a minumum. One of my young patients had an interesting comment
about taxes. The young man had been browsing near the pharmacy counter for
quite a while. When I got the chance I asked if I could help him. "I need to
buy some condoms, but I don't have much money," he said shyly. I pointed to
a box of three and said they were ninety-nine cents.
"Great, I'll take those," he said, sounding relieved.
I rang up his purchase, saying, "That will be one dollar and six cents."
He seemed suprised and said, "But you said 99 cents!
"Yes," I replied, "and seven cents for the tax."
With eyes wide, he blurted, "Tacks, if you have to hold them on with tacks
then forget it!" Then he ran for the door.

Carl Labbe R.Ph. 10-94


"Do I Get A Smile With That?" was published in the March
1995 issue of Pharmacy Times, a national
professional magazine published by Romaine-Pierson.
