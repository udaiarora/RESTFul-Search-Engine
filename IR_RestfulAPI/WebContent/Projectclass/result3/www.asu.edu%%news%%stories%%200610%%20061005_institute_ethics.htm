October 5, 2006

Institute helps integrate ethics into curricula for teachers

With the new school year in full swing, many teachers from across the Valley are busy implementing ethics into their everyday curriculum, thanks to a summer institute sponsored by the Joan and David Lincoln Center for Applied Ethics.

Through large-group and small-group activities, nearly 50 teachers gathered to develop a basic understanding of ethics and how to build an ethical community at the third annual ASU Summer Institute for Teachers: ?Building the Ethical Self.? The institute took place at the Foothills Academy College Preparatory in north Scottsdale in late July.

The institute ? a collaborative effort of the Lincoln Center and Foothills Academy ? focused on the ethical issues embedded in school curricula, and how to involve students in ethics as they relate to issues such as immigration, race relations, health care and stem-cell research.

?I took away meaningful information that can be applied in my classes, as well as in my personal life,? says Melissa Francis, a teacher of American government at Thunderbird High School . ?I also learned about what role ethics play in my own life. I really appreciated this, because I feel that I can better teach ethics as I now have a more thorough understanding of what it means ? and, more specifically, what it means to me.?

Donald Blumenfeld-Jones, the project's leader and Lincoln Associate Professor of Ethics and Education in the Mary Lou Fulton College of Education, says the program is important because there is a social consensus on the need for ethics in curricula.

?To live in an ethical society, people need to know what it means to be ethical and what possibilities exist for being so,? he says. ?Teachers can play a crucial role in providing their students with the means of choosing their own ethics and living them consistently and with integrity.?

In addition, Blumenfeld-Jones says the program was particularly useful for teachers because of its structure.

?The institute had many components which are action-oriented and, therefore, immediately useful in the classroom,? he says.

Francis says she has introduced the five ethical theories from the institute by connecting them with the government content in her courses.

?I focus a lot on citizenship and good character in my classes, especially since I have seniors who are about to enter adulthood,? Francis says. ?I feel that introducing concepts in ethics can open my students' eyes about how they treat each other.?

She says her fellow teachers also have benefited from her attendance because the topics are ?cross-curricular.?

The institute, which was geared for middle school and high school teachers, also was open to a small number of school administrators.

Blumenfeld-Jones says program feedback has been very positive.

?The participants left the institute with much to consider and have reported that they've used what we've offered to good effect,? he says.
