February 4, 2005

Law professor joins nuclear nonproliferation effort in Russia

ASU Law Professor Orde F. Kittrie is in Moscow this week, having been named to a blue-ribbon National Academies of Science (NAS) committee on “Strengthening Cooperative Nuclear Nonproliferation Programs of the United States and Russia.”

On the weeklong mission, Kittrie and the other six NAS committee members are meeting with senior Russian officials and experts, including the members of a counterpart Russian Academy of Sciences committee.

A report on “Strengthening Cooperative Nuclear Nonproliferation Programs of the United States and Russia ” is to be issued this spring by the NAS and RAS committees.

“Terrorist and rogue state acquisition of nuclear weapons is the single greatest threat facing the United States ,” Kittrie says. “I am glad to do whatever I can to help prevent it. Our committee’s mission is to examine why U.S.-Russia cooperation on nuclear nonproliferation has slowed in recent years and suggest ways to jump-start it.

“I am honored to be included in such a high-caliber team, and I’m hopeful that we can make a difference on this issue, which is so important to the future of our country and the planet.”

Joining Kittrie on the committee are former Assistant Secretary of Energy Rose Gottemoeller; Maj. Gen. William F. Burns, former director of the U.S. Arms Control and Disarmament Agency; Daniel Poneman, former special assistant to the president and senior director for nonproliferation at the National Security Council; M. Teresa Olascoaga, former deputy director of the International Security Center; and William H. Press, former deputy director of Los Alamos National Laboratory.

Before joining the ASU law faculty in 2004, Kittrie served for 11 years at the U.S. Department of State. He most recently served as the State Department’s director of International Anti-Crime Programs, overseeing United States policy and technical assistance programs for worldwide efforts to promote the rule of law and combat transnational crimes, including corruption, money laundering, intellectual property piracy, cybercrime and alien smuggling.

Kittrie previously served as the State Department’s senior attorney for nuclear affairs. In that capacity, he negotiated five different U.S.-Russia agreements designed to prevent terrorist/rogue state acquisition of former Soviet weapons material and expertise, including agreements on acquisition and disposition of former Soviet weapons-grade uranium and plutonium, material protection and accounting, border security and developing centers to employ former Soviet weapons scientists on peaceful projects.

Kittrie also served as counsel for the U.S. government’s sanctions and other responses to the 1998 Indian and Pakistani nuclear tests.
