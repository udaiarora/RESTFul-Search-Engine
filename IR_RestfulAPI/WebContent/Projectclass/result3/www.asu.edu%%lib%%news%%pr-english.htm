¡BASURA BOOM! Publishing Latin American Literature with Cardboard


(posted 11/22/04  |  En Español)

 

Rare Book Exhibit in Hayden Library, Upper Concourse
Special Collections, Arizona State University Libraries

November 23, 2004 - January 31, 2005

 

The books in this exhibit were all published in Argentina through the Eloísa Cartonera project—a collaboration between cartoneros (cardboard-pickers), artists, and writers. Working out of the gallery No hay cuchlllo sin rosas in Buenos Aires, founders Javier Barilaro, Fernanda Laguna and Washington Cucurto first brought this idea to life in the wake of Argentina’s grave economic collapse of 2001.


Inside the hand-painted cardboard binding and on the inelegant photocopied pages within, one can read previously unpublished material by known (and unknown), alive (and deceased) vanguard and experimental writers from not only Argentina but also Chile, Mexico, Costa Rica, Uruguay, Brazil, and Peru. The multi-faceted vision of the project is to provide a self-sustainable means of publication for little-known authors and little-known works from all over Latin America as well as to help promote broader readership of marginal literature while at the same time making use of the emerging labor force of cartoneros themselves. Cucurto explains: “My dream is to invent a reader: to remove Martin Fierro and García Márquez from them and to show them that anyone can read Aira, Fogwill, Casas.”


The accompanying essay "The Argentine Cartoneros" by ASU Regents' professor David William Foster and photographs by José Enrique Sternberg provide an understanding of the economic and cultural context in which these rare books have come forth. The Arizona State University Libraries are pleased to add these precious literary gems to their rich and diverse collection of literature from Latin America.
