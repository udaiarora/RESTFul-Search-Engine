December 23, 2004

ASU’s Herrera earns invitation to leadership institute

One of the most effective ways ASU continues enhancing student learning and student achievement is with its faculty and staff. Their high standards of leadership are shaping ASU into the New American University.

Marisel Herrera, coordinator of Student Leadership Programs at ASU, was among 22 Latinas in the country recently selected to participate in the National Hispana Leadership Institute (NHLI), which is dedicated to developing Latinas into ethical world leaders.

Herrera’s selection was based on her extensive community service record, her commitment to the Hispanic community and her leadership.

As a participant with NHLI, Herrera will travel to a variety of institutions – including the Center for Creative Leadership in California and Harvard – from February to September. She will meet with some of the foremost scholars and servant-leaders in the country on public policy, community building, management, organizational and leadership skills.

“I am excited by the opportunity to learn from the 21 other women, to gain a national perspective on Latino challenges and solutions, and most of all to bring that back to Arizona , to colleagues and students in order to find meaningful, enduring, culturally relevant solutions to local Latino issues,” she says. “I expect NHLI to be an intense, rigorous, transformative experience that will further intensify my passion for service and for empowering the next generation of Latino leaders.”

Herrera earned a bachelor’s in social work from Marist College in Poughkeepsie , N.Y. , and a master’s degree in higher education from ASU. She has been at ASU for nine years, serving as a student success coordinator with the Multicultural Student Center and as an academic adviser working with students in the Barrett Honors College . She also has assisted with the retention and recruitment of minority students.

For Herrera, one of the most appealing components of NHLI is that it requires its graduates to pass on what they gain through the institute to Latino youths.

“The Latino explosion in Arizona gives us a demographic imperative to develop students who are not only successful and college educated, but equipped with the skills and knowledge to assume leadership roles in their community and state,” she says. “I hope to collaborate with ASU programs and departments, as well as community constituents, committed to Latino student success, outreach and leadership development to create additional initiatives that support the retention, graduation and civic engagement of ASU students. Whether that is ASU Latina/o Institute or a series of workshops is still to be determined, but what is certain is that the opportunities are endless.”

For more information about the National Hispana Leadership Institute, visit the Web at (http://www.nhli.org/).
