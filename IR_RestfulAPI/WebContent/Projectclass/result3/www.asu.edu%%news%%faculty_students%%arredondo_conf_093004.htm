September 30, 2004

Professor brings national Latino association conference to Arizona

Patricia Arredondo, professor in counseling and counseling psychology at ASU, is helping expand the field of psychology in education through her efforts with the National Latino/a Psychological Association.

Arredondo, who has been with ASU for the last five years, serves as the association president. She says the group plays a vital role in promoting Latino perspectives in psychology.

“There are ethnic-specific issues that we can focus on,” Arredondo says. “We want to make sure issues like language, culture and immigration are addressed in professional psychology. For example, bilingual counseling … that’s really important.”

To address some of these key issues, the group will host its first conference Nov. 19 – 20 at the Scottsdale Plaza resort.

ASU students and faculty will be very involved in the event. ASU professors also will be featured during a pre-conference lecture series Nov. 18.

Eugene Garcia, dean of the College of Education , will be the keynote speaker Nov. 20.

ASU students will volunteer at the conference as well.

“Their time is immensely valuable to what we’re trying to accomplish,” Arredondo says.

Arredondo says the conference will bring great benefits to Arizona .

“There will be a lot of research-based presentations. It will provide a great deal of positive applications for professionals here in the state,” she says.

In addition to lectures and research presentations, the conference will offer cultural presentations each night.

Tickets are $195 for professionals and $95 for students for the conference Nov. 19-20. The pre-conference lecture series Nov. 18 costs $150 for professionals and $75 for students.

Arredondo says she was drawn to Arizona for its advocacy opportunities. She spent 28 years in Boston before coming to the College of Education at ASU.

“It was a big change, but I’m very passionate about Latino issues,” Arredondo says. “ Arizona seemed like a good place to go to promote those issues.”

Arredondo has been hard at work during the five years she has lived here.

Under her leadership, the NLPA has increased its membership by 300 in just two years. The association also has important mentorship programs for young professionals and collaborations with other psychological associations.

Arredondo says ASU has been the perfect place for her advocacy work.

“Being located at ASU has given me a great platform for Latino issues,” Arredondo says. “It’s important to be at a university when you do this sort of work with a professional organization. It gives it visibility and credibility.”

For more information, visit the NLPA’s Web site at (http://w3.arizona.edu/~nlpa).
