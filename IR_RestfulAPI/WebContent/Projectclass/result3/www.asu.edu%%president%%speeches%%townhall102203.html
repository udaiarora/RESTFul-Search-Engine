Arizona State University Town Hall

October 22, 2003
Remarks by President Michael M. Crow

Good afternoon. I want to welcome everyone to this town hall session. The purpose of this meeting is a simple one: to get folks together and have a chance to talk about where we are, where we are going, what we have achieved and what we hope to achieve.

First, let me start off by saying the report being printed through the ASU Alumni Association in the ASU Vision Magazine is a summary of our collective efforts this year. I want to thank all of you for your contributions to what I think has been a very successful year at Arizona State University. If you look through the document, you will see significant achievement across a number of areas with regard to students, faculty, initiatives, community outreach and a range of things. All of these achievements are possible only as a function of the hard work of not only all of you who are here, but all of your colleagues and partners who are not here.

Before talking about the report specifically, I want to talk about where we are. I don’t know how many of you are familiar with the University’s history and how we have evolved, but we are in what I would characterize as the sixth evolutionary step of a territorial normal school into a national university. Some of you may have heard me talk about this before: most universities are designed at the time of their origin for what they ultimately want to be. Some universities are not and they go through one or two steps.

We are embarking on the sixth step in a very short period of time. We were only established in 1885, we became a state college in the 1920s, a state university around 1960, and most of our PhD programs had evolved by the early 1970s. We didn’t offer any graduate degrees until the 1940s. We had been rapidly evolving through a range of evolutionary steps, from a territorial teachers college to a normal college, to a state college, to a comprehensive state college, to a state university, and now the step from a state university to a national university.

National universities are places like the University of Minnesota, Ohio State, UCLA, and the University of Texas. Those are national universities, and we are stepping up to that challenge. What do I mean by national university? I mean it is an institution that is competing at the highest possible level in all aspects of its identity: its academic programs, its specialty programs, its athletic programs. Here is an example: the University of California at San Diego was founded around 1960 and was designed from the day that it opened to be a national university. In forty years, they haven’t achieved that yet but they almost have. Within the next ten years or so they will achieve it.

When this institution opened, what was our mission? To train a few teachers for a very small state with a highly disbursed population which had no other colleges. This was it. In 1925, when we became a state college, it was pre air-conditioning. The population of metropolitan Phoenix in 1925 was under 150,000; it was small. So, what they needed in metropolitan Phoenix then, and what Arizona needed then, was a small state college for what was then a small state. By 1960, the population of metro Phoenix was about 600,000 and this small state college had a hard time servicing a major metropolitan area that was growing and evolving. By then, high tech companies had moved in, air-conditioning came along, and more and more people wanted to be here. The economy was diversifying, the population was diversifying, and metro Phoenix clearly saw itself as ultimately becoming a major city. Major cities do not have small state colleges as their only public university. They have universities. So, against the wishes of the regents, against the wishes of the Legislature, the people of Arizona voted to establish Arizona State College as Arizona State University. Just because it was voted in as a state university, however, did not make it so. You then have to build it, and so the aspiration at that particular point was to build a good, solid, high-performing state university. That has been done. That has been accomplished. That has been accomplished in a very outstanding way.

Now, metropolitan Phoenix has three and one-half million people, growing to 7 million people or 8 million people, and the state’s 6 million people is growing to 10 or 12 million. In big cities like Los Angeles, Chicago, Boston, San Francisco and Seattle there are major national-class universities competing on a national basis for everything. That is our next step. We have not achieved that step. If you calculate each of the steps that we have taken, it is the sixth step. I would not necessarily call it the final step because I do not know what the world will look like 200 years from now or what might be required. At that point, we may be building global universities connected into multicultural communities in ways that we can not even imagine at the moment.

Six steps of evolution in a little more than a hundred years is huge. Almost no institution has had to go through that; certainly almost no university has had to go through that. Step six is essential for the success of metropolitan Phoenix and the success of Arizona, and this report is an early summary of the initial things that we have undertaken collectively to prepare ourselves to take step six. Overall, step six is probably something on the order of a twenty-year process, and maybe we would spend five years preparing to take that step, ten years taking the step and five years sort of evening out once the step has been taken. This is the end of the first year of the first five years of preparing to take the step.

What do we need to do to take the step in this particular setting? We need to be able to meet our three great challenges: grow and enhance our quality, expand our research enterprise and imbed the university in the community more deeply. This report, which represents the work of many people, is an effort to communicate both to the community within the university and to the community external of the university how we are preparing to take the step. Let me talk about the elements of the step by going through the Table of Contents page.

The first section that we focus on in the report is the culture of academic enterprise. How many of you have heard me talk about the enterprise model, or being more entrepreneurial? It means, to boil it down very simply, being responsible for ourselves, being responsible for our own destiny, plotting our own course, and moving forward and behaving in ways where we do not act and think like an agency of the government but we act and think like a university, an independent creature.

How many of you were at the naming gift ceremony for the new Piper Memorial Trust Creative Writing Center? That group of nine or ten faculty members from the English department and the Theatre department and the staff that work with them and the students that are a part of that program represent in every possible way everything that I mean when I say academic enterprise, academic entrepreneurial behavior. It is not linked to the Business school, the Engineering school, or the Science departments; it is linked to the faculty members and students, and the groups that have a vision for what they want to be and take extraordinary steps to advance that vision. That faculty group has built writing clinics; they built the national meeting; they have done a range of things to advance their agenda. It was relatively easy when an outside group came along and wanted to invest in the university, settled on the humanities as an area, and then settled on this particular group because they represented academic enterprise in every way: highly achieving, highly productive, high standards, a vision for their own future from themselves. Not from us, not from me, themselves. That is what I mean by a cultural of academic enterprise.

It also means projecting so that people will invest in us, not as a function of our resource deficiencies, not because we have financial needs or budgetary needs, though we certainly do. The three investments that we have received this year are extraordinary in the sense that they are unprecedented in the intuition’s history. The people of Arizona, through their representatives, gave us 185 million extra dollars on top of all the other dollars that we could put together to build some research facilities. They did that because they felt that we could parlay those investments into an expanded and more successful nationally competitive university, and that university would have a positive impact on this community as a function of that investment. Second, we received two $50 million naming gifts. Well, that really was not what they were. They were two private sector $50 million investments in our aspirational goals. The College of Business set an aspirational goal of being among the top 25 of all Colleges of Business in the country, and the Engineering School likewise. Outside folks believed that we could attain that goal if we worked hard and were willing to invest in us to help us to attain that goal. We did not go to any of those three sources of additional investment in the university and ask them to please help us because we need your help, please help us because we are under-funded, please help us because we lack resources. They sensed the energy of an enterprise wanting to move forward and all of those investments were a product of that.

The second section of the report is on academic transformations. Why that term academic transformation? Academic transformation does not mean that what is going on here now is not good or high quality, but as in all institutions striving to achieve greatness, you have to continue to transform yourself to move in that direction. The transformation process for us is one of stepping up and focusing on that continual improvement process. We have identified in the report several items related to institutional achievement: the new School of Life Sciences, which I think will go down over the next several decades as a huge achievement of our faculty who have conceptualized this new approach to the Life Sciences and our staff that work with them; the movement in the creation of a new Computer Sciences Institute and the moving of the Institute to Downtown Tempe on Mill Avenue; the formation of the consortiums for rapidly urbanizing regions. All of these are meant to indicate that this is not a static institution. The new Center for Conflict and Religion is another example of an academic transformation. So, in this part of the report we are talking about groups driven by faculty and staff with high aspirations for what they can achieve academically, driving new conceptualizations of themselves forward, transforming our little version of the academy. We will transform the institution by bottom up initiatives coming from faculty and staff who want to do new things and achieve new levels of greatness, as self-assembled groups. The report highlights about 15 or so of those as examples of how we are evolving.

We also move toward academic transformation by hiring key people, leaders, exemplars, high achievers who come into the midst of the rest of us, and by example and by interaction and by sheer energy, energize the rest of us, not because we are not energized enough, but because we can always be energized more. We can always be energized to move in new directions. New appointments like Dean Reiter in the College of Architecture and Environmental Design are a new energy force. Another new person is Kevin Gover in the Law School. He brings a new perspective, a new dimension, a new focus, new energy, and new experiences. The report also focuses on faculty achievements: Guggenheim Fellows, the high number of high achievers on our faculty. We have tried to identify those folks and what they do.

Academic transformation is also driven by the energy and achievements of our students, and we tried to highlight those achievements, what those students have done, what they mean, what they represent for us. We will be transformed academically as a function of new ideas and new teaming driven by ideas from our existing staff and faculty, new faculty and leaders coming to help energize us more and what are students are able to achieve.

Research innovation is the second challenge of the university that I mentioned just a few minutes ago. The second challenge in becoming a national university, to have the kind of impact on this community that we need to have, to become recognized, to increase our value proposition to the people that invest in us, including to the state of Arizona, is to increase our research activity by at least a level or a factor of three. That factor of three – about $400-500 million a year, depending on how we calculate where we started - is the critical mass necessary to compete among the great national universities. That is the minimum necessary to even be observable on a national map of academic research intensity.

Right now we are proud to say that we are ranked 10th among all universities in the U.S. in athletics. Just suppose that we are ranked 90th in research and 10 years ago we were ranked 90th and, even though we have been growing dramatically in these last 10 years, we have not grown dramatically enough to alter our position. That ranking, by the way, is a proxy for the competitiveness of our creative ideas in those arenas where people are willing to pay for those ideas. This particular section of the report is a projection of the unbelievable achievements that our faculty and staff and our students have attained now, where we are, which will position us for where we need to be. The report segments these achievements. Which of these research achievements are advancing the human condition? Which of these achievements are helping to understand what we are as a species? Where we came from? What we might be becoming? How we are evolving? How does the universe work? These are fundamental questions that, if successful in addressing, we change everything about who we are, what we are, and how we see ourselves.

A critical element of our research agenda is to make sure that our research is not esoteric to the place in which we live. We have highlighted the research that we are doing that has an impact on this region or focuses on this region because we are not here to do research that does not have an impact at all on the place in which we live. And then lastly, and perhaps most importantly, the report includes examples of the creative energy that is out there, coming from anywhere, in any subject, any discipline, any perspective.

We are not here, by the way, to be an institution that only teaches and transforms and tries to be great and makes discoveries. We are also here to try to add additional value to the public, and so the last section focuses on public service. When you hear me talk about social embeddedness it means enhanced levels of public service and direct connections. We talk about the socioeconomic impact of the university, the impact we have as a social economic force, as a change agent, as a teaching mechanism out in the community. Our impact on the Pre K-12 educational arena is an area that we have highlighted. We talk about the specific impact we have on our communities. Not the kinds of things we are hoping to do, but this is a report on things that we are doing. For instance, ASU West received the highest score among 400 U.S. colleges on their public engagement level, setting an example for other universities. We talk about the impact of our public television station, KAET. It is the third or fourth most watched public television station in the U.S. It is a powerful tool, and we walk you through what has been achieved. Three hundred thousand K-12 students watch programs distributed to their classrooms by that station.

This university has achieved much in this year. I believe that we have successfully taken the first baby steps towards this transformation of step six, transformation to a national research university. It is a long road. There will be many reports on our progress. This is a huge institution. We picked examples, exemplars, things that make the point, things that get across the progress that is being made. The hope is that over time we will have an opportunity to recognize everyone and all of their achievements. We will not be coming back to the same people when we advance these types of documents in the future.

As far as I know, we have never produced a document like this before. So, why would we do this? We have a huge communications challenge both inside and outside the university. This is a diverse institution. It is a complicated institution with many, many different programs and many, many different academic subcultures. If you go out beyond the institution into the community of investors, who will invest in an institution they do no know anything about? Who will invest in an institution that would say we do not have an annual report of our progress? Not very many people. This is an annual report of our progress toward attaining a new objective. The hope is that it will help us within the community to understand what is going on, what we are doing, and that it will help us outside of the community to have people get a sense of where we are going. Not as a school or a college or a department or a program or a center, but as a university. We are working to get the message out about what we are doing, what we are achieving, and it is significant.

One of the things we have to overcome both within the university and outside the university is our own view of ourselves. This is a great university, and it is going to be even greater. This is a tremendously successful institution achieving much. Here is some evidence of some of those achievements, not all of them, some of them. Not everyone in our community views it that way. Not everyone in the university views us that way. This report is a part of that process. This meeting is a part of that process, so we can talk about this report and where we are. It is as a part of the process of taking this step towards building a national university that we are here today.

One last point before we open up the floor for comments and questions. The building of a university, the setting of an agenda, the advancement of our message, the transformation of the institution along the lines we are talking about – we are right in the middle of it. We are a very successful state university trying to become a unique national university in a unique place. We have a long way to go. The institution is probably funded at 50% of the level that is needed to carry out the mission we have, 50%. Tremendous things have been done with the limited funding. The people of Arizona, by the way, are the most significant contributor to this institution and person for person they are also the most generous contributor. The alumni contribute very little to this university, almost nothing financial. That is not true at other successful national universities. Private donors contribute very little to this university, almost nothing by comparison. The students themselves and the families through tuition contribute very little. Our tuition revenue, if I get the number right, is $150 million. The University of Michigan’s tuition revenue is $450 million and they have fewer students. For us, that extra $300 million could mean substantial increases in financial aid, substantial improvements in access, substantially enhanced salaries for all staff, substantial expansion of faculty, larger number of advisors, better repair of our buildings, all the things that we need. So those of you who think that the solution rests with the ability of the administration of the university to sell the Arizona Legislature are incorrect. Our future rests in our ability to dream what we want to be and sell it to everyone: to students, to families, to donors, to alumni, to everyone. That is what we are trying to do. This report is but one tiny little piece of that.

Let me switch gears for one moment, not withstanding anything I have said. We are as we speak mounting the most significant campaign yet to secure additional investment from the people of Arizona through their representatives in the Legislature. We will be asking them for a roughly $60 million increase in the base budget of this university, a huge increase at exactly the wrong moment in the state’s fiscal history. We need those resources to help with the cost of additional students and to help maintain the quality of our academic programs and access to the universities. This will be a major undertaking and, if we are successful, $60 million is a lot of new resources to invest in the university to make up our funding deficiencies. We will need everyone’s help to advance this idea. You will see us out there fighting, and I hope as we advance forward, as I glance around from side to side, I will see some of you there fighting with us.

We have to make the case that ASU must have resources to maintain quality and to maintain access or the university will have to begin moving in the direction of doing something that we do not want to do. There are certainly some on this faculty and some on this staff who think we should have shut off the university to qualified students a long time ago. I do not intend to do that but we must have the resources to move forward, the resources we need to educate our students, to maintain access to the university.

At this moment, by the way, the university is becoming more diversified then ever, more accessible then ever, more open to qualified students then ever, more engaged in the community then ever. This is not the time to say we are all full, you can go somewhere else because at least in this state there is nowhere else to go to achieve a full scale university education. So we have to advance this university by seeking additional resources from our prime investor: the people of Arizona. However, I do not want you to take that to mean that is the pathway to our success. It’s not. Go back to my example of the University of Michigan. The University of Michigan’s appropriation from the people of Michigan is larger then ours but not dramatically larger then ours. And even if they gave us the exact same appropriation as the University of Michigan gets from the people of Michigan, it would not be enough to save the day. It would not be enough. We are going to go in to test it, bump it up by $60 million in a single year, and that will help us.

We have a lot of other work to do, a lot of people to sell on our idea about what a great university is so they can invest in it. We have been trying to demonstrate in the past year through a number of different examples what it takes to do that.

I will stop talking and see if any of you have anything you want to add to the conversation. We have a microphone in the front. It will be most efficient if you line up behind the microphone rather then wait for each person to walk over. If you don’t want to use the microphone and shout out your question, you can, and I’ll repeat it for the people who can’t hear you.

Q: Can we expand distance education to expand our role without accruing as much cost in doing so?
A: So far no one has proven you can reduce costs through distance education electronically. It actually turns out, at least for the moment, that it costs more in some ways because you need to prepare the material, invest in the material, so there is a start up cost that we have not figured out completely yet. Second, it is a part of our strategy to expand distance education dramatically as a way of helping us manage the student population that we have where that technology might be appropriately used which is far from everywhere. It is also a way for us to reach out into the community more broadly for students that are place bound for particular reasons. We are hoping to be able to do that. We have a good foundation to build from. I think we offered 60,000 credit hours from some form of digital or distance originated education, although my numbers might be wrong. We already have a good foundation to build from, we just need to expand it dramatically. However, it is not free. It requires a huge upfront investment to get something like this going. But that is a good point.

Q: With a twenty year process ahead of us, what do I see in the next year, next four or five years. Basically, where are we going?
A: I want to say that I literally believe the single most important thing we have to make progress on is mind set. The mind set that we are not an agency of the state of Arizona. We are a creation of the state of Arizona with the free will to build the enterprise that we want to build. I actually see that as critically important. You will see me working with others to try to make as many examples of how that works. More funding, more gifts, more focused investments around that. You will see a lot of that within the next year and more of that in the next four years, if we go up to that five-year benchmark.

One of the things we have got to change is the way we accrue resources, so one of the things we are hoping to do in the next year is to begin the process of rethinking our financial planning and budgetary processes so that deans can be empowered to be the front run leaders. It turns out that it should be the deans and the staff on the front line, and not the president and provosts. We are only a few people, just a handful of us. We have to take a certain set of directions and positions but it is really at the school level, at the college level, at the unit level and at the centers that people have to be advancing the agenda. So, you are going to see us putting in mechanisms that help facilitate that, and we are trying to learn together right now. We are in that learning mode. We are learning what that means and how to do that because that is not how we worked in the past. So that’s the second thing we have to do.

With the Arizona BioDesign Institute, we basically said that ASU has the capacity to build a world-class research institute. We have never built one and we are saying we are ready to. We went out and found a leader, George Poste, we hired a bunch of faculty, and we took some of the best research groups. We gave these people a couple hundred million dollars to get started, which we know is half of what they actually need but it is better then nothing. Within this five-year window, they need to be up, running and on the track to being successful. If that can not happen in that window, then we have a really big problem. It means we are not ready to do that. And if we are not ready to do that, then we need to step back and regroup and think about what it takes to get ready, which is a whole other process that takes a much longer time.

We need to do some serious fundraising. We need more investors and we need more partners. All the talk about staff salaries, faculty salaries, about what we need to make things happens is empty talk without resources. There will be some talk about resources and it can not just be the people I am working with. It has to be every unit, every group, every center, every department, every school thinking about how to generate those resources. Some are advantaged in that over others. It is easier for the biomedical engineering department to generate new revenue than a department I met with yesterday, the theatre department. They are not in the same arena, same type of funding activity, so we need to figure that out.

I think more than anything, beyond this enterprise model, in the next five years we have to find ways to step up in every unit from the departments and centers up and start competing nationally. We are doing it in a lot of places but we need to do it more consistently in terms of faculty recruiting, staff salaries, graduate student salaries, graduate student recruitment, and undergraduate student financial aid. There are certain national standards and we are behind on many of those. We have to start looking at those and focusing on those. These are the kinds of things we will be focusing on.

Q: The question is if the annual report is going to go online.
A: I can only hope so but I do not know. It will go to every investor. In fact, if there is anyone that you think should get this type of document, we need to hear about them. We are trying to put together a list of who it needs to go to. Right now it is an issue of the Alumni Magazine, which is a vehicle to do this and not overburden the taxpayers of Arizona, which is something we are trying not to do. We are trying to get it in the hands of every potential advisor, every potential investor and every existing stake holder, partner, and investor.

Q: Have we invested $200 million in the Arizona BioDesign Institute?
A: The answer is yes.

Q: She thinks George Poste should be talking about what he is doing.
A: Most of that investment, by the way, is in two buildings. If I added in the third building, it is actually more than $200 million. Most of it is being used in the physical investment plan but there are substantial program investment dollars, as well. George will be communicating to the community so we can be aware of where he is headed. Right now he is still getting organized and up to speed. I know that you and I came from the same schools and in our disciplines $200 million would bring you lots of progress. You have to trust me when I tell you $200 million or $250 million in the area where George has been asked to move us is one half of what he needs to even get started. The best way we have found to get George is to wheel out a light and shine it into the sky. It looks a lot like a bat when you put it up there. Then George comes in. He is spread out very thinly right now. We ought to work on getting a session so George can talk about his plans and what they mean.

Q: Where do teaching and learning fit into the priority hierarchy at the university we have, at step five basically. Where does the gift of teaching and learning fit in the things that you have talked about?
A: That is a complicated commentary and question so I will boil it down to the last part which is where does teaching and learning fit in the priority hierarchy of expanding and taking this next step of the university while at the same time we have a step five university to run. You are right on the nub of our most complicated and hairy problem. That is, we have this very large and still growing step five, great state university right here and yet we are trying at this exact same instant, even before we are stabilized because growth is still occurring, to begin towards taking step six. In the context of that, teaching and the creation of a successful learning environment remains the first mission, the mission that we must fulfill first and above all others, to be successful. None of our plans or initiatives are intended to move us away from that at all, but to enhance it. So you will see us trying to do the following things:

  • There will be a dramatic expansion of our on-campus residence living environment to enhance student living and learning activities.
  • Second, we have announced some minor expansion of the faculty through the tuition increase and a merit driven salary adjustment, which will take effect in January. This is activated by the tuition increase, not a function of it, but the tuition increase enables it. This is a positive step to maintain the core focus on teaching and learning.


But here is the scale of our problem, we are 1,700 faculty members short from a built-out university, and on this campus we are hundreds of faculty members short. We will never fund those faculty members, or get staffed at the level that we need to be at, and staff comes along with faculty, until we solve this resource problem. So the reason we are going for the $60 million, the reason that we will recommend a tuition increase again, the reason that several of the deans think of me in a not very positive light at the moment is because of a focus on acquiring the resources to allow the faculty not to be overrun by the numbers of students that we have. We are attacking this from a resource perspective. At some point, we will say this is as far as we can go without additional resources. We are very near that point right now.

Q: I am from facilities management and we are being told we have to possibly do more with less. The university is not getting smaller and newer, it is getting larger and older. And with all these new buildings coming onboard, is there any consideration of
bringing the biotech buildings online for utilities and workforce and things of this nature?
A: The question is as we are getting bigger and older, which is the correct premise, some of our facilities are in the aged category and new facilities are coming online, how can we be expected to maintain older buildings and take on new, more complicated buildings unless we expand our capacity to be able to do that? In our request for the funding from the state is funding for additional commitments for facilities operations and maintenance. So, that is a piece of it. Second, the successful research funding will be what provides the money to maintain the research-intensive buildings that are being built, as is the case at every major public or private research university in the United States. And that is the risk we have taken on, that if we are successful in the research arena we will generate revenues to maintain those buildings above the means that we have now, and we will also generate more investment dollars for the library and for other critical services around the university. Right now we have roughly $10 million a year from indirect cost recovery to invest in the rest of the university. At Stanford, they have $125 million a year from that same pot, and at UCLA and Berkeley, they have approximately the same levels. We have to be successful and then we will have the resources to get the staff to maintain the buildings at the levels that we need.

We do have a big problem with deferred maintenance, with things that have been let go over long periods of time. When Yale University identified that problem they were only $2 billion down in deferred maintenance. We are not quite $2 billion down in deferred maintenance. Every university goes through this problem. They would rather spend the money on the students and on the faculty then worry about the buildings later, usually when they are about to fall down. So, it is a problem and we know it is a problem and it needs attention.

I think that is about all the time that we have. I appreciate everybody being here.
I hope that you find the report useful and that we will have a better one next year.
