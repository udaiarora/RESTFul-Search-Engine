3.C  University Provost?s Report (Milton Glick).

 

I will be brief as I told you at the beginning of the year, and at each meeting I would like to take at least one issue of major importance and discuss it with you.  Today, Tom Trotter will do that on my behalf?he will present on student success, something we have made enormous progress on with persistence to graduation. 

 

I do want thank many of you and your colleagues for dealing with the fallout of the tragic event on September 11.  Things have gone smoothly here as we have hoped, and the thanks goes to our faculty, our students and our staff.  Last Friday, in fact, the Counsel General from India was on campus, having been invited by the students from India to come and discuss things with them.   I was very satisfied that he made remarks in some public meetings that he had gotten very positive feedback from the Indian students on campus, and that he felt very good about the response he has received from them. 

 

We have had a number of infrastructure breaks in recent weeks?As you know, last spring we found out that the Social Sciences building is falling in.  Two weeks ago, a water main on the sixth floor of Goldwater building broke and we had as much as a foot of water on some floors.  Last week a sewer backed up in the College of Business and we had to vacate that building temporarily.  You know, I sometimes feel like you do when you get to the age when things are falling apart and I would like to think that is not happening at ASU.  I keep asking myself, is it something we are doing?  They do assure me they are all random events. 

 

We have received a request from our Governor to reduce our appropriation by 4%.  That went to all parts of the state government with the exception of the public schools and other programs mandated by law.  We have now allocated out 11 million dollars of cuts on this campus.  We managed to limit the cuts to colleges at a level of 1.5 - 2 percent.  That is not easy for your colleges to do.  So, when you are hearing more noes than yeses, you need to know that your dean is trying to deal with these difficult reductions.  While we have said that we have covered centrally and administratively some 2/3rds of the cuts, this will still have fallout on the academic side of the house.  We are very concerned that the governor is now projecting an 800 million-dollar shortfall for this year and another 800 million for next year.  So far, she has only accounted for about 250 million in reductions, so between now and the November 10 session of the legislature, there is going to be a lot of arm twisting going on.  The Governor has said both publicly and privately that she does not wish to increase the hit on the universities beyond 4%.  This is a positive sign on her behalf and we trying to work with the folks downtown to find alternatives for example, the rainy day fund, there is the ability to bond Students First, at some point somebody is going to have to look at the billions of dollars of tax credits and tax reductions which have happened in this state over the last decade. Right now no one is prepared to fight for that, but maybe between now and November 3 they will be. 

 

There is also talk starting now about not exempting the public schools from reductions.  There are different interpretations as to whether 301 gives them total protection.  We have said again and again, we want to do our share but we should not do more than our share.  This is the year that taxpayers by voting 301 in have stated that education is a priority in the state.  It could be tough.  Something you should know is that in many other states they are seeing these types of reductions.  I think it is very important that we find ways to do important things even in times like this.  We have come a long way since 1960, since 1980, since 1990?we are still a very fragile institution.  We are still overachieving and we have to continue the progress we?ve made and build on it.  I would certainly hope, and it is our intent, that we will not have to say no to everything.  We are going to have to say lots of noes and we will have to do some cutting, but we will not put a general hiring freeze on, even though there will be a defacto hiring freeze in many units just because of funding.  We intend to let the responsible parties make the decisions as much as possible on how we take cuts.  We are going to look at vertical cuts, for example--can we reduce things like travel for a year or two.  Travel is usually paid for by the units out of their salary savings and other ways. We do not budget for travel.  All we can do is essentially lobby people to reduce their travel.  Which some of them would be happy to right now.  We are going to look at all the construction we have intended and the renovation--and then say, is this one that can wait a year?  Is this one that has to be done now?  At the top of our list will be renovations.  If we promised a new faculty member something that we would renovate, we have an obligation to renovate it.  Our first concern is to keep our word to people we make promises to.  Nonetheless, we will seriously look throughout the university at buildings and projects and whether they can be put on hold.  For example, the new addition to Wells Fargo Arena has to be put on hold and we are not moving forward on that.  We will be looking at Main Campus and asking are there building projects that we can put off?  We also know that it is very hard to reallocate space.  It involves choices about the maximum usage of space too, and ultimately awarding space to the most productive individuals and activities that will maximally affect programs. 

 

I think we have to continually push on with our research agenda, because although during this time there will be more external money available for research there will also be more and more competition for those dollars.  There is a major summit called by the Greater Phoenix Economic Council in November on what this state has to do to be economically competitive.  Rob Melnick, Director of the Morrison Institute, has been asking a number of faculty to come and serve as resource persons, to bring their expertise (not their needs) and help identify what is needed to help this state be economically competitive. 

 

I believe with what we have done with the first 4% cut while very painful, if there is not another cut to follow, that will slow us down and prohibit progress.  It means that we will have to work that much harder.   I worry that if we have to dig any deeper than that it will begin to tear at the fabric.  You all have legislators, and anything you can do to get across to them the very fragileness of this institution and the centrally of it to this state, I wish you would do so.

 

Senator Boyes: Governor Hull says we can?t raise taxes, and we can?t raise tuition because of tax. How much consideration has been given to actually privatizing a bit, moving away this dependence on the state and forming a coalition base for the universities?

 

Provost Glick: We have had long discussions with the Regents on why we are a low tuition state.  That is partly because of constitutional provision that says education shall be as free as possible.    The Regents are on the whole in favor of  low tuition because they are very concerned about cutting some people out.  It has been a strong, aggressive agenda by the President of the University of Arizona, Pete Likens, to say that you would better serve the people if you are high tuition/high financial aid because low tuition is a general subsidy of the middle and upper classes, whereas high 3.F  Report on Freshmen Persistence (Tom Trotter).         

 

The file with the PowerPoint presentation containing the slides used by Professor Trotter can be provided to senators who want them by e-mail (contact Darby Shaw 965-9245).

 

My job as Vice Provost brings a wide range of responsibilities.  Most recently, I have been asked to coordinate a campus-wide effort to address issues affecting student success ? with the goal of improving ASU?s retention and graduation rates.  This goal has been the topic of continuing discussions among senior administrators, and components of the university, mostly in student affairs, have been hard at work on this goal for several years.  What is new is the effort to bring together a true campus-wide effort, with particular emphasis on the role played by academic affairs.

 

It is fair to ask, ?Why should we care about retention and graduation efforts??  After all, isn?t it the case that year after year, we see more and more students?  And haven?t we been wrestling with resource issues in dealing with the students we have?  So why should we make a concerted effort to hold on to a larger fraction of the students we have?

 

You might also ask whether this is the right time for a long-term effort requiring sustained efforts all across the campus.  We are in the midst of a presidential search, and the near future will certainly be a time of change for ASU.

 

In my remarks to follow, I will try to answer these questions, beginning with some data, which helps to put the challenge in perspective.

 

Slide 3 graphs new undergraduate students beginning in 1993 and continuing up to the current year.  The blue curve (with squares on it) represents transfer students; the red curve (with diamonds on it) is our first-time/full-time freshmen.  As you can see, a conscious effort has been made to transform the undergraduate student body at ASU.  This was a deliberate decision. In particular, the recruitment of larger numbers of new, first-time, full-time students has been a clear goal for President Coor and Provost Glick and they have led a decade long effort to do just that.

 

During this time, we have made no effort to discourage transfer students.  Instead, we have worked hard to maintain cooperative collegial relationships with community colleges.  But we have seen some fall-off in the number of transfer students.  The significant rise on the line with the diamonds/red is intentional; the slight downward trend of the line with the squares/blue is not, and we do not really understand this phenomenon.

 

Here's a thumbnail sketch of the student profile for the cohort entering in the fall of 2000.  The 2001 class did not look much different.  Among the first-time/full-time students, the average SAT score is over 1100.  A lot of people are not aware of this fact.  They don't understand that our entering student body population is considerably stronger than public perception might suggest.  ACT scores average 25, while the average GPA for entering students is a 3.5. A full 30% of our entering class ranked in the upper 10% of their graduating class in high school, and more than half rank in the upper quartile.  The 132 National Merit Scholars place us among the top handful of universities in the country.

 

Slide 5 graphs our retention rates.  It is important to remember that retention is measured only for this cohort of first-time/full-time students entering in the fall semester.  Our percentages have hovered in the high 60's, increased up to the middle 70's, and right now we are at about 74%.

 

Slide 6 graphs our graduation rates.  Graduation rates are computed on a six-year basis. So from the time the cohort enters the university, we go forward six full years and measure the percentage of students that have graduated within that six-year period.  This chart ends in 1994. That means that the last measurement for this chart was a graduation date by 2000.  You can see that we have never hit 50%.  In general the trend is up, but never more than 50%.

 

A Senator: That is terrible.  If they graduated in seven years, they would not appear on this chart?

 

Tom Trotter: That is correct. The six-year figure is used by all universities to measure graduation rates.

 

A Senator: What if they transfer later?

 

Tom Trotter: This is the rule that universities have agreed to use as a common measure.  Each year, we get a new cohort.  We then measure the fraction of that cohort we can guide through to graduation in six years.  You ask if a student transfers later, are they included in that figure or not?  The answer is yes. 

 

Senator Grossman: What is the graduation rate for 2000?

 

Tom Trotter: We do not know yet. This chart begins with the entering class of 1982.  The last figure represents the entering class of 1994.  So this year, we will calculate the percentage of students from the 1995 cohort who have graduated by the end of 2001.

 

Slide 7 is bar graph of the GPAs of students that we retain after one year. This data is for Fall 2000.  Out of a little over 6,000 students, we retained about 4,500.  As you would expect, these GPA?s correlate very well with retention.  So it is a conditional probability statement: if a student stays at ASU--a first time/full-time student--then you may comfortably predict academic success. 

 

Now let's look at the graph showing GPA?s for students who leave ASU.  If slide 7 shows a correlation with retention, then you would expect the one for students leaving to be inversely correlated.  But this is not at all the case.  Looking at the last two bars shows that we lost 150 students with GPA?s better than 3.5.  We lost another 200 with GPAs of 3.0 or better.  There are more than 350 students in those top 2 categories that left ASU.  Why?  It is disturbing to me to see this kind of distribution.

 

Senator Alford: On this chart, are you accounting for withdrawals of a first time student?

 

Tom Trotter: We culled from this list those who did not earn enough hours to get a decent GPA.  So, for example, if someone went halfway through the first semester and didn't earn enough hours, they were not included. So we calculate the GPAs in the standard way. There's no cheating here and we did not fudge the data.

 

Here is a short chart on ASU?s retention rates compared with some peer institutions.  We just used four comparisons, but a more exhaustive comparison is readily available.  All this data is public.  It is that we are consistently being ?whooped? by our sister institution to the south.  Their retention rate is consistently over 75%. 

 

Here is a similar chart for graduation rates.  You would expect them to track the same way as the graduation rates, since it is an integral over retention, and that is what you see.

 

Senator Alford: What is the purpose of showing us these data?

 

Tom Trotter: The purpose for showing you these data is to get you to think about what the challenges are and what possible approaches and solutions we might find together.   It is not to throw rocks at anyone.  The data helps us understand the unique characteristics of ASU and the challenges we face in serving our students.

 

Slide 14 Persistence vs. Class Rank. Remember that the red curve (the line with the diamonds on it) is the first quartile.  That quartile is doing fairly well. Look at the second and third quartiles.  They do about the same.  This means that there is not of substance on which to base these borderline admission decisions. There is not much difference between the second and third quartiles, and there is a lot of room for improvement in the academic success for that combined group.

 

Slide 15 Persistence vs. Course Load. This is an interesting chart because you will notice that it has these little spikes. Is it the case that students who take more courses do better because they are more motivated?  Or is it because it helps them connect more with the university?  We have programs consistently advising students to take 12, 13, or 14 hours in the first semester. The data seems to suggest that this is not the right thing to do.

 

Slide 16 Gateway Courses and Retention. We took a look at some of the gateway courses, courses that have huge enrollments of first-time/full-time students.  There are 3,201 students that took Freshmen English 101.  About half received an A, and 81% of those were retained.  An almost equal number got a B, and of those, 74% were retained.  If a C is received in the first year English course, that is ?bad? because only 58% of those students are retained.  In Math 117, a General Studies Math course, there is a smaller group of 1,543 students.  You notice for A-B-C-D it is almost the same retention rate, and then you get to E with the rate of 39%).  It is reasonable to ask what conclusions can be drawn from this data.  If you had resources, where would you invest them?

 

Slide 17 Persistence and Gender.  It is no surprise that our female students are doing better than our males.

 

Slide 18 Graduation and Gender.   It is the same.

 

Slide 19 Persistence and Ethnicity.  You can see that the pale green curve (the line with triangles on it), represents our Asian students, and they are doing the best.  You can see that under-represented groups are really spread out.  We have some real challenges here, because if we could do a better job of retaining these students to retain and moving on to graduation in a timely fashion, we would be doing those students, as well as the state at large, a great service.

 

Slide 21 Graduating Senior Survey.  This chart has to do with advising in the major. We see that on the graduating senior survey, the majority of students express complete satisfaction with the overall program that they receive here at ASU, but you see considerable falloff in their evaluations of advising.  What is the conclusion? Are students complaining about advisors because advisors have to enforce rules and program standards?   Or is there something we can and should do to improve how advising is done, and how our students feel about the advice they receive?

 

I started off our discussion by asking two questions 1) Is this the right time for ASU to tackle retention and graduation rates? and 2) Why should we try to retain more students when we already have so many?   I want to give you three answers. 

 

Yes to the first question.  We compete on a national basis, and we must recognize the fact that institutional success in retention and graduation efforts are important factors in the public perception of the stature and quality of a university's success.  Public perceptions and rankings published in magazines may not be the most substantive of reasons, but we cannot escape rankings and comparisons, whether they be academic or athletic in nature.

 

As a second reason, I must tell you it is a lot cheaper to retain the students we have than to recruit new students.  The churn of students entering and leaving the university comes at considerable cost to the university.

 

But the third and by far the most important reason is that it is the right thing for us to do.  We should do everything in our power to retain our students ? by making sure that they find special value in their studies and campus experiences at ASU.  We should do everything in our power to assist and guide our students toward graduation in a timely manner.  In making these statements, I must emphasize that I am not talking in any way about the diminution of quality or standards.  Instead, I am talking about is providing special value and service.  I am willing to give my full energies to that cause and I hope you will join me.

 

Senator Mayer: Do we have any idea on why this situation exists?

3.G  Report on NCA Continuing Accreditation (Daniel Jankowski).

(These comments accompanied the showing of a set of transparencies.  The numbers below refer to their topics in order.  These transparencies will be available soon on a public web site.)

1.        It is important to say that this is a continuing accreditation because, after all, ASU is currently accredited.  The name of our regional accrediting agency has changed somewhat since our last accreditation visit.  It is now the Higher Learning Commission of the North Central Association.

2.        There are several reasons for regional accreditation.  The President has indicated that it provides us with a ?license to operate? and others have said that it is a ?seal of approval? because it provides an independent evaluation of what we do and whether we do what we say we do.  On a practical level, our students cannot get various financial aid funds unless we are accredited, and, of course, certain funding for our institution depends on being accredited.  It also impacts the transfer of credits.  We hope that the process of preparing for accreditation will be a rallying point for what ASU is and does and what it should be.  A very broad group of individuals is taking a very broad look at our institution.  Lastly, we are going to have a visit from a group of academics from throughout the country.  They will come and spend a few days on campus and ultimately provide a report that will be an independent evaluation of our strengths and weaknesses.  The report for the last visit in 1993 was about 80 or 90 pages long and obviously contained a lot of information.

3.        Here is a complimentary remark from the last report indicating that while we do have some concerns, they are certainly not serious and there are many areas of strength.  This is a version for certain audiences. For this audience, a slightly altered version, which also contains the remark, ?Arizona State University has effective and visionary leadership,? is more appropriate.

4.        Here are the principal areas of concern that bear on the accreditation criteria, which I will share with you in a moment:  ?1. Whether the university's mission statement is so broad that it does not guide or focus the University's planning; 2. Whether the university's planning and budget allocation processes are sufficiently articulated to allow the University to continue to achieve and make progress toward its declared purposes; 3. Whether the university's evaluation of its education programs constitutes or is developing into a comprehensive program of assessment.?  Recent information says that roughly 80% of the schools that are going through the accreditation process are being ?dinged? because of problems with assessment.  When I say ?dinged,? I mean the institution is not given the full 10-year accreditation period without any conditions, but rather is required to undergo an interim visit or an interim report.  For example, the University of Arizona was given an interim report after five years to describe and explain their assessment activities.  One more concern, which is a long-term problem, is:  ?4. Whether the University?s resources are sufficient to allow it to pursue the aspirations declared in its Mission Statement and its Statement of Purpose.? It will be interesting to see how we talk about these things in our self-study.

5.        Here are the exact criteria for accreditation.  They are actually quite simple.  There is another set of technical things, like do we grant degrees, which are almost boiler plate, that I will not share with you.

The institution has clear and publicly stated purposes consistent with its mission and appropriate to an institution of higher education.

The institution has effectively organized human, financial and physical resources necessary to accomplish its purposes.

The institution is accomplished its educational and other purposes.

Educational purposes obviously apply to the education of students, interpreted broadly, and other purposes include research and creative activities, and service to the community.

The institution can continue to accomplish its purposes and strengthen its educational effectiveness.

The university demonstrates integrity in its practices and relationships.

Doug Johnson and Andy Jackson are the individuals in charge of the self-study chapter that will address the last criterion.

6.   Here are the four goals for the self-study.  First of all, we are going to be accredited.  There is an unstated fifth goal, which says that we should not spend any money to do this!  This may be a problem; as an example, the University of Arizona spent $16,000 just printing their self-study.  We want to make sure that the findings of the self-study are integrated with ongoing university assessment and planning, that is, this effort is not just an event, but it is part of a process of the institution.  There is going to be data generated and it will be analyzed for the self-study.  We hope that this will be information of lasting significance.  There is a virtual data and documents center that is going to be available for the university as a whole once this activity is over.  We hope that we carry out this self-study in a way that is believable and has credibility in the broader community, not only among ourselves.

7.        The charge to the steering committee is to plan and oversee the preparation of a self-study.  The last one in 1993 was about 350 single spaced pages ? we hope that ours is not that long ? and, of course, a successful site visit.

8.        The structure of this activity includes a steering committee.  A few of its members are present; for example, Doug Johnson, Dan Landers, Bianca Bernstein (the chair of the steering committee), Tom Trotter, Ron Barr.  I am the coordinator; that means that I put pressure on everyone to get their work done.  Then there are working teams, divided into two parts:  chapter teams, each responsible for a chapter of the self-study, and resource teams, which are concerned with items of broader interest and will assist in the writing of the chapters.  The chapter teams have core members that are on the teams all the time and consultants that are used to deal with specific matters that come up.  The names of the chapters are (most are connected to the criteria that I mentioned earlier):  Introduction, Overview, Mission and Purpose (criteria 1; Dan Landers and Fran Thurber, co-chairs), Human, Financial and Physical Resources (criteria 2; Nancy Gutierrez and Lee Overmyer, co-chairs), Instruction ? Undergraduate, Graduate, and Academic and Student Support (criteria 3), Research and Creative Activities (criterion 3; Ron Barr, Nancy Russo, and Jeff McWhirter, co-chairs), Community Engagement (criteria 3, Ruth Jones and Loui Olivas, co-chairs), Capacity for Continued Effectiveness (criteria 4, Doug Vinzant and Norma Hubele, co-chairs), Institutional Integrity (criteria 5), and Summary and Conclusions.  The resource teams are concerned with assessment, data and documents, and extended campus.

9.        The timelines for these activities has been established and I am happy to report that we are following them.  The process began in May 2000 at a meeting at which senior administrators were present, Milt Glick, Bianca Bernstein, and others.  The real effort started last fall with planning and the determination of the people to serve on the committees mentioned earlier.  The virtual resource center will house the data and documents that are available virtually, and there is also a physical resource room that will have documents that are available in hard copy.  During the spring and summer of this year, the teams have been working, getting together to talk about this effort in a broad way.  The chapter teams submitted their draft zero at the beginning of this month and for the rest of the fall we are going to continue to work on another draft which is due in the first part of December.  In the spring we will ask for input and comment from Milt Glick and others.  Next summer the final draft will be prepared, hopefully, with the help of an editor.  Next fall we will solicit input from the general community, faculty, staff, and students.  We will do this via a web site that will be established shortly and will contain helpful information.  We will also have focus groups to get people together to help us make sure that the document is, in fact, a true representation of ASU.  Next fall we will begin preparation for the site visit.  The dates are already known, March 24-26, 2003.  This three-day period will be spent presenting, talking and listening, and answering questions for a group of 10-15 visitors. 

My purpose today was to provide you with some information about this important activity.  Perhaps I can meet with you again and provide you with another update, in the spring or next fall.  We are excited about this activity, we think we are on track, and we are trying to do it for the Provost's budget of 17 cents.
