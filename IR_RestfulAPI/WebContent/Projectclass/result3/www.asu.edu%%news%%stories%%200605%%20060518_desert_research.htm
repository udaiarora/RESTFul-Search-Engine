May 18, 2006

Chihuahuan Desert research shapes thinking about evolution

At a series of unique desert freshwater springs, streams and pools (pozas in Spanish) in Cuatro Cienegas, Mexico, 21st century ingenuity is meeting Jurassic Park.

ASU professor Jim Elser and ASU astrobiologist Jack Farmer are partnering with Valeria Souza of Universidad Nacional Autonoma de Mexico (UNAM) to use DNA analyses to tease out the origins of the ponds' smallest inhabitants: bacteria.

Cuatro Cienegas is a unique habitat of aquatic islands in the belly of a vast desert cupped by the Sierra Madre Mountains. It was declared a "Federal Area for the Protection of Flora and Fauna" by the Mexican government in 1994. The area's more than 200 warm springs harbor more than 70 endemic species. The unusual range of water temperatures, mineral compositions and living inhabitants - including the only example of an aquatic box turtle - presents a rich range of ecosystems and complex food webs that can shed light on how microbes and systems can evolve in an array of extreme habitats.

These ecosystems are ideal for the study of evolution, especially in the context of the new field of astrobiology (the study of the conditions for the evolution of life in the universe).

"Insight into the ecological and environmental forces that shaped and eventually stabilized our own systems can do much to inform us about what is possible in other regions of the universe," says Elser, a limnologist (a specialist in the study of freshwater ponds and lakes) with the School of Life Sciences in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences.

Elser and his fellow researchers' first steps to track down the path taken by early microbial life in the Cuatro Cienegas basin have uncovered 38 unique phylotypes (genetically distinct microbes) from 10 major lineages of bacteria. They were intrigued to find that half of the microbial freshwater inhabitants of Cuatro Cienegas were not related to other familiar freshwater microbes from similar systems, but instead were more closely related to marine taxa of bacteria located 500 miles away - in the Gulf of Mexico.

There was an ocean here nearly 65 million years ago, during the Jurassic period.

"That can mean only two things," Elser says. "(Either) these bacteria now at Cuatro Cienegas have their origins via long-range transport from modern oceans - we think we can rule that out - or they have come to us from that ancient time. (This could occur either) by continuous reproduction and survival in the springs of Cuatro Cienegas for 65 million years - or more likely, but still surprising, by recently hatching from Jurassic limestones."

Souza, Farmer and Elser's findings have been highlighted in a paper published by the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences. The paper is titled "An endangered oasis of aquatic microbial biodiversity in the Chihuahuan desert."
