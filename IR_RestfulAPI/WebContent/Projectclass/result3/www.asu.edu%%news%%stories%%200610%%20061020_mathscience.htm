October 20, 2006

Partnership tackles math, science education

As Arizona strives to secure a place in the global economy, the lack of workers who are proficient in math and science poses a giant stumbling block. Current trends show the state will not have enough skilled workers.

Recognizing this as a statewide challenge, Arizona State University and Pinnacle West Capital Corporation have invited 100 key political leaders and educators from K-12 through college to lay the groundwork for reversing the trend.

The introductory meeting of a new coalition, Arizona Initiative in Math & Science Education (AZIMASE), will be held 8:30 - noon Oct. 25 at Pinnacle West, 400 N. 5 th St., Phoenix. The University of Arizona and Northern Arizona University also are participating.

Revealing statistics are that only 26 percent of Arizona 8 th graders performed at the ?proficient? level in mathematics on the 2005 National Assessment of Educational Progress.

Less than half of Arizona high school students are taking the courses they need to enter Arizona 's universities, most notably four years of math. And in the entire state last year, Arizona universities graduated only 58 new high school math teachers.

? Arizona is moving aggressively to forge a high-wage economy driven by knowledge-intensive industries such as bioscience, electronics, aerospace, sustainable mining and advanced manufacturing,? says Eugene Garcia, ASU vice president for education partnerships. ?But our efforts will not succeed unless we dramatically improve the science and mathematics education that both prepares our future workers and attracts the companies that can employ them.

?We want to share current initiatives and programs in the state related to this issue, identify broad goals and plan public-private partnerships in a coordinated effort to meet Arizona 's needs.?

Speakers at the event will include ASU President Michael Crow; Martin L. Shultz, vice president for government affairs at Pinnacle West; the education deans at the UA and NAU; Eugene Garcia from ASU; and ASU math professor Marilyn Carlson.

The goal of the new coalition is to improve math and science education outcomes at all levels, focusing particularly on the critical role the three universities have in teacher recruitment, preparation and professional development.

?Research has consistently pointed to teaching as the crucial element for better math and science outcomes,? says Carlson, director of ASU's Center for Research on Education in Science, Mathematics, Engineering and Technology. ?We need dramatic, research-based changes in how we prepare, support and reward Arizona's math and science teachers.?

For more information, visit http://azimase.asu.edu or call 480-965-1329.
