November 6, 2003

ASU alumnus leads state's effort to strengthen economic, cultural relations

Victor Flores, 52, knows very well the ups and downs of Arizona's economy and cultural growth. As an Arizona native and former small business entrepreneur (owner of Flores Bakery in Guadalupe), Flores has experienced the state's evolution from both the business and cultural perspectives.

As the new president of the Arizona-Mexico Commission (AMC), Flores is dedicated to providing leadership for Arizona that improves the region and delivers opportunities, services and initiatives for business and personal growth - a job that "comes with many challenges and surprises," he says.

"It's hard to predict what any state's future will be," says Flores, who was appointed AMC president by Gov. Janet Napolitano, "but I think the AMC has really made an impact on securing Arizona's growth through partnerships and collaborative efforts with corporations and various groups."

Chaired by Gov. Napolitano, the AMC is a public/private, non-profit organization that formulates programs and action items that impact the Arizona-Mexico relationship.

Set in motion in 1959 by Gov. Paul J. Fannin, who once stated, "God made us neighbors, let us be good neighbors," the AMC has evolved to promote a strong, cooperative relationship with Mexico; facilitate the movement of goods, services, people and information through Mexico and Latin America; and encourage security and sustainable development within our border communities.

Flores, senior government relations representative for Salt River Project (SRP), has collaborated with local, state and federal officials, as well as with Hispanic community leaders, and remains active in the community as a way to represent SRP, one of AMC's sponsors.

"We (Latinos) are so segmented," Flores says. "The growth of the Latino population is so evident, but not everyone is communicating with each other to help bring cultural and economic development to the forefront."

Flores also is involved with other civic and professional groups including the Arizona Housing Authority, the Arizona Center for Public Policy, Valley of the Sun United Way and ASU's Alumni Board, which provides Flores with an opportunity to help guide the direction of his alma mater.

"It's amazing to see how ASU is connecting with the community," says Flores, who received a bachelor of science in engineering/construction management from ASU. "ASU has been involved with the AMC by providing resources and expertise. And now President (Michael) Crow is following his vision for the university through partnerships with institutions in Mexico. As an alumnus I am very glad to see ASU is becoming more involved and at a different level than they have been before."

Visit the Web site to learn more about the Arizona-Mexico Commission at (http://www.azmc.org/).
