April 11, 2003

ASU graduate programs earn place among nation's best

The master of fine arts program at the School of Art in ASU's Katherine K. Herberger College of Fine Arts catapulted to 13th place in the recently released U.S. News & World Report "America's Best Graduate Schools 2004" list. The program, which ranked at 45th when last reviewed by the magazine in 1997, was among several ASU graduate programs listed among the top programs in the nation.

The MFA program ranked eighth among public institutions nationally. The magazine evaluated 213 visual arts programs.

Three of the school's specialty programs also received individual recognition. The School of Art's photography program ranked fifth overall and second among public institutions; printmaking ranked third; and ceramics ranked 14th overall and 11th among public institutions.

"This is a reflection of our accomplished faculty and outstanding programs in these areas and the college's momentum in recent years," said J. Robert Wills, dean of the Herberger College of Fine Arts. "These rankings will further distinguish the Herberger College as one of the nation's leading centers for the study, practice and development of the arts."

Visual arts was the only fine arts area evaluated by the magazine this year. Still in effect are the School of Music's ranking as 19th nationwide and the theater department's ranking as 35th, with playwriting recognized as 20th.

U.S. News & World Report's national rankings of graduate programs in education, once again listed ASU's College of Education among the top ranked programs in the nation.

ASU's College of Education is ranked as the 9th best public graduate college of education in the nation, and the 16th best overall among public and private institutions offering graduate degrees in education.

The college was also recognized this year for having top 20 graduate programs in the following specialty areas: Counseling Psychology, ranked 15th; Educational Psychology, ranked 15th; Education Policy, ranked 15th; Curriculum & Instruction, ranked 16th; and Higher Education Administration, ranked 18th.

"These rankings are a testament to the vision the College of Education has, that our reputation will be based on studying the problems of education as we attempt to solve them - the Pasteur Principle," said Jim Middleton, associate dean for research with the College of Education. "In other words, being part of the solution is a legitimate academic enterprise, and ASU is at the forefront of socially responsible academic research - a model for the nation."

For the third consecutive time, ASU's College of Nursing ranked as one of America's best graduate schools. The 2004 guide ranks the College of Nursing 39th in the nation.

Rankings are based on surveys from deans, faculty and administrators of the nation's accredited schools of nursing with graduate programs.

"I am especially proud of our national ranking," said Barbara Durand, dean and professor in the College of Nursing. "It is a notable achievement, given the diminished resources for higher education in Arizona and the number of highly endowed private schools of nursing among the competition. It is also gratifying that our score increased from the last ranking period in 2000."

The ASU College of Nursing is the largest educator of nurses with Bachelor of Science in nursing degrees, Master of Science degrees and advanced nurse practitioner certifications in the state. The college is also home to the American Museum of Nursing.

The U.S. News & World Report survey of full-time MBA programs ranks the W. P. Carey MBA supply chain management specialization 5th in the nation, and two others are top 25 - accounting at 15th and information systems at 17th. The W. P. Carey MBA - Evening Program was listed at 22nd in the nation.

"This is the first time that 'U. S. News' has ranked the supply chain management field, and we see it as recognition of the critical importance of this field to global businesses," said Joseph Carter, chairman of the supply chain management department.

The W. P. Carey course of study follows Massachusetts Institute of Technology (Sloan), Michigan State University, University of Pennsylvania (Wharton), and Stanford University in the survey.

"The W. P. Carey School has dedicated 24 full-time faculty to teaching and research in the area of supply chain management - an unusual concentration of expertise," Carter said. "Our goal is to be ranked first in this category - we are well on our way to realizing this accomplishment."

Ranked overall, the W. P. Carey MBA is 17th among the master of business administration programs offered at public universities, and 37th among 365 graduate business programs accredited by the AACSB - International.

"Since this survey data was collected last October, the quality of business education at Arizona State University has been validated by a $50 million gift endowing the W. P. Carey School of Business," said Dennis Hoffman, associate dean of graduate programs. "This vote of confidence allows us to further improve our program.

Specialization in fields like supply chain management will continue to be the essential ingredients of the W. P. Carey MBA."

The U.S. News & World Report survey "America's Best Graduate Schools 2004" ranks ASU's College of Engineering and Applied Sciences among the top engineering schools in the nation. This year the college came in at 56th out of 185 colleges surveyed.

"This is a very competitive assessment, and we improved our results in several categories, notably in research expenditures," said Peter Crouch, dean of the college. "We are especially pleased that the ranking of our programs by experts, the deans of other colleges and recruiters, is so strong. We are continuing to progress toward our goal to reach the Top 25."
