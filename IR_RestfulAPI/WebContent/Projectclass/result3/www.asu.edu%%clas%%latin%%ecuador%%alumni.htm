ECUADOR: The Amazon and the Andes Field School: Alumni News

Chronicles of the Ecuador Field school

Jarrett Levine?s time in the Ecuadorian Amazon

?I now often think the past summer in Ecuador. Living in the jungle with fellow classmates, instructors and the Andi ayllu (community) was a remarkable and educational experience. My mind is filled with thoughts of the warm hearted Quichua families who opened their homes to the group and were always willing to help. Touring Ecuador while studying in Dr. Swanson?s Ecuador Field School will have a permanent impact on my life, and my future. I hope that this program continues for years to come so that other students can participate and gain the insight that I did this past summer.?

This is a quote from Jarrett Levine?s field journal. Jarrett is a Political Science senior at Arizona State University. In the summer of 2003, he participated in the Ecuador Field School sponsored by the Center for Latin American Studies at ASU. Jarrett enrolled in this program with the desire to learn Spanish, but never imagined that he will come back to ASU with a newfound appreciation for native Ecuadorian culture and its application to his field of study, International Relations. While attending the Field School, Jarrett was deeply touched not only by the members of the community where the Field School is located, but by all the elements that compose this unique place. Jarrett was quick to recognize the immense socioeconomic pressure that exists in the Amazon. He quickly became aware of the fragile environment he came to visit. He later wrote in his field journal:

?I envision the youth of the Oriente (Ecuador?s Amazon Basin) gaining an education, so that a majority of the young people will be literate in Spanish while maintaining their traditional language as well. Attaining literacy will open doors for individuals to attend universities and receive professional training. This will provide more opportunities for financial success?.

Jarrett?s goodwill and genuine appreciation for the experience he was afforded at the Field School was so great; so much in fact, that after the program ended, he met his parents who came from Michigan to Quito and made the trek across the Ecuadorian Andes and into the jungle. Jarrett and his parents arrived at the Fundacion Cotococha, where they were welcomed by the members of the community and Dr. Tod Swanson, Director of the Field School and the Center for Latin American Studies at Arizona State University. This experience led to very positive results for both parties. Like Jarrett earlier had, the Levines learned a great deal from the people in this place. They were so appreciative of the quality time they had spent there that they made a donation for $5,000 dollars to the children?s school system. One of the main goals of the Field School is to continue to preserve and exchange cultural knowledge, traditions, languages, and forest conservation techniques among others. The Levines? generous donation will have a very positive impact on improving the children?s school system; Jarrett?s vision of education for the youth in the Oriente is now more attainable than before his arrival.

I had the pleasure of meeting Jarrett at the Field School. What struck me about Jarrett is his deep interest in helping others, in always learning about life as it takes place in other places of this planet, his ease to communicate with people, even if he does not speak Quichua, and his charisma towards all those around him.

             

?Hopefully, with proper conservation and protection of the forest, others will be able to enjoy the experience that I did this summer.?---Jarrett Levine

--Cesar Cordova

Tiffany Hardy (Summer 2000) writes that she is getting married in Spain next January to Luis Cuenca, her boyfriend of four years. "It is going to be a unique wedding," she writes. "Sixty people from the United States are coming over. My grandparents who are around 90 years old are thrilled to be going also. We are getting married in Luis's small home town. It will be great to have everyone out here, but I have a lot of planning to do to keep them busy for a week or so. A lot of people who are coming have never left the United States before. I am really looking forward to it." Tiffany has been admitted to the M.A. program in Sociology at Arizona State University for January of 2003.
