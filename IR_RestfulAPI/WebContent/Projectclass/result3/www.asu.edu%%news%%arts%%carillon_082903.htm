August 29, 2003

Beautiful music - Society refurbishing long-lost carillon, plans melodious return

After spending 35 years or so gathering dust in the Matthews Center stacks, ASU's Symphonic Carillon is getting cleaned up and tuned up.

It soon will be ready to make music, bringing back an almost-forgotten ASU tradition.
Maas-Rowe Carillons, manufacturer of the 148-bell Symphonic Carillon, moved the instrument out of ASU storage Aug. 6 and trucked it to Escondido, Calif., to begin modernizing and refurbishing it.

Associated Students of ASU bought the carillon in 1966 for $25,000, using surplus activities funds that had accumulated over a period of years.

There was a grand dedication and concert, at which University President Homer Durham spoke, as well as Robert McConnell, that year's student body president.

The carillon, housed in Matthews Center near the Art Museum, was used for several years, but after a time, no one really cared about it, said Rudy Turk, former director of the museum (who was accused of playing the carillon late at night and spied upon to see if he was doing so).

When Matthews Center was remodeled, the carillon was placed in storage, where it languished, covered with cloths, sitting amidst mop buckets and vacuums.
When the instrument was rediscovered last December, a small group of carillon lovers banded together to bring the carillon back.

Today, the Symphonic Carillon, with a walnut keyboard that is in pristine condition, is worth more than $100,000, and is one of just 100 such instruments manufactured by Maas-Rowe. It's also the only one of the 100 that is not in use.

ASU Main currently has music playing daily, thanks to a small carillon loaned to ASU by Maas-Rowe. It plays music recorded on the company's Grand Symphonic Carillon (including the ASU Alma Mater and fight song, which are played every day at 5 p.m.) The carillon also plays music at noon and 6 p.m., and the Westminster Chimes on the quarter-hour.

Doctoral organ performance student Homer Ferguson says he and other students are anxious to play the instrument, and undergraduate music student Jacob Adler is poised to compose music for it.

The ASU Carillon Society plans to host a series of concerts by professional carillonneurs, such as Lindsay Arthur Lafford, former carillonneur at the University of Miami and Hobart & William Smith Colleges, who now lives in this area, and various ASU students.

The ASU Carillon Society has begun raising the $50,000 needed for restoration, and hopes to place it in Old Main, says Tedde Scharf, director of Disability Resources for Students (DRS) and secretary of the ASU Carillon Society.

The society is optimistic that the ASU community will step forward to help with the restoration. "No donation is too small," says carillon supporter Penny Caslake, DRS receptionist. "Donations so far have ranged from $5 to $500, with some people giving through payroll deduction. We want everyone to participate."

Those wishing to contribute to the fund may send checks, payable to the ASU Foundation, and marked for the ASU carillon restoration, to ASU Public Relations, Mail Code 1002.

Gifts may be made in memory of or in honor of individuals or organizations. For information, call (480) 965-0456.
