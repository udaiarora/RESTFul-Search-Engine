Southeast Asia Collection


The Mimijac Palgen Cambodian Photographic Collection

The collection consists of approximately 500 black and white negatives taken within the Angkor temple complex, 1000 negatives taken in Phnom Penh and the rural areas of Cambodia, and approximately 100 slides taken by Dr. and Mrs. Palgen while on assignment in Vietnam in the early 1960s, Indonesia and Thailand in 1988.


The MimiJac Palgen Collection contains photographs of village and rural scenes, as well as everyday images of the urban lives of the Cambodian people during the period from 1946 to 1962. The earliest negatives from Angkor contain images taken of temples prior to and during restoration work conducted under French auspices. Later negatives contain images of post-restoration work.

Background


In July 1995, Dr. Jack Palgen of Tucson donated to the Program for Southeast Asian Studies and the University Libraries at Arizona State University a collection of photographs and photographic negatives taken by his late wife Mrs. Mimi Palgen-Maisonneuve. The photos reflect Cambodian life during the period 1946 to 1962 when Mimi Palgen-Maisonneuve, a French resident in Cambodia, was a staff member of Radio Phnom Penh.


This is an important period in modern Cambodian history. During World War II, France continued to control the country through its Vichy government. In 1945, the Japanese dissolved the colonial administration, and in March King Norodom Sihanouk declared an independent, anti-colonial government under Prime Minister Son Ngoc Thanh. The new government was deposed by the Allies in October of that same year. Although France recognized Cambodia as an autonomous kingdom within the French Union, the drive for total independence continued, which hastened the French government's recognition of Cambodia's full independence on November 9, 1953. During the 1950s and 1960s, the later period during which Mimi Palgen-Maisonneuve was a resident in Cambodia, the country tried to remain neutral as the Indochinese Conflict developed around it. The conflict caused Cambodian culture to be annihilated by the Khmer Rouge but it is hoped that this culture could be revived with the help of the photo collection.


Accessing the Collection


The MimiJac Palgen Collection is housed in the Special Collections division of Hayden Library, at the Arizona State University Main Campus. Special Collections is located on the second floor, and is open Monday - Friday from 8:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m..


Research Opportunities


Mrs. Mimi Palgen-Maisonneuve was in the process of organizing the collection for donation to ASU when she passed away. She did not have the opportunity to identify any of the images in the collection, which concentrates on three domains:

The people of the village
The people of the water
The people of the cities


Many of the images in the collection are still unidentified as to where and when the photographs might have been taken. This could be a substantial project for a graduate student or other researcher with knowledge of Cambodia. Eventual plans for the collection include digitization of the photographs, to aid with future research.


Anyone interested in working with the MimiJac Palgen Collection is encourage to contact the Program for Southeast Asian Studies for further information.
