January 26, 2004

Non-profit center receives boost

The efforts of the ASU Center for Nonprofit Leadership and Management to support and educate members of the nonprofit sector in Arizona and the region have received a strong boost with a $4.1 million investment from the W.K. Kellogg Foundation.

Since its establishment in 1999, the center in the ASU College of Public Programs has served the nonprofit community as a resource for training, education and research. The four-year grant will greatly expand the capability of the center to provide topical training, assist in management and nonprofit development and enhance the university's already strong research and undergraduate and graduate education efforts in the field.

Specifically, the funds will help add several staff positions, aimed at improving the interaction between the center and professionals working in the various nonprofit organizations in the state. Other positions will help expand and expedite the research on the nonprofit sector done by the center. The funding also provides resources for conferences pertaining to topical issues regarding nonprofit management, philanthropy and civil society.

"This is a great opportunity to build our center," says Center Director Robert Ashcraft." It really positions us as a knowledge and tools leader and enables us to further help build capacity of the nonprofit sector in Arizona and beyond."

Ashcraft says each year more than 1,500 new nonprofits in a variety of forms ranging from schools and churches to youth and social groups to health and welfare organizations start up in the state. In many cases those running the organization are new to the field and need support, whether it's setting up correct articles of incorporation, determining best business practices or in the form of management assistance. Although 35 states have state associations of nonprofits that provide support services, Arizona does not. The ASU Center is relied upon for information and services not typically expected of universities.

"This investment validates ASU's strong efforts in this area and enhances our ability to serve the community," says ASU President Michael Crow. "Nonprofit organizations are a vital part of any community and we are now in an even better position to serve in this capacity.

Beyond its support of local nonprofits, the center performs extensive research on the nonprofit sector. ASU is in the process of developing an extensive look at the scope of nonprofit activity in the state. There is also an ongoing look at salary levels for nonprofit executives and a widely cited study of the scope of giving and volunteering in the state.

Academically, the center offers an extensive undergraduate, graduate and extended education program. Students can participate in the American Humanics Undergraduate Program, the Nonprofit Leadership Management Graduate certificate Program or the noncredit training workshops offered at the ASU Downtown Center through the Nonprofit Management Institute. Ashcraft says the goal for the program is to develop a full graduate degree program.

The Kellogg Foundation has a long history of supporting nonprofits. The W.K. Kellogg Foundation was established in 1930 by W.K. Kellogg, best known as a pioneer in the cereal industry.
