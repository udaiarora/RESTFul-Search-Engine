ASU experts are frequently called upon by the local and national news media to provide insight and opinion on current events and issues of public interest. Following are excerpts of recent news articles featuring ASU representatives.

Relief fund to help college students

An idea was born before the ASU-LSU football game to create a fund to help college students displaced by Hurricane Katrina continue their educations. Sen John McCain and the presidents of the two universities announced the student relief fund, which was the brainchild of President Michael Crow. “We’re going to raise a lot of money; we’re going to make things happen,” Crow says. “We’re going to make as significant a contribution as we can.” Los Angeles Times, Chicago Tribune, San Francisco Chronicle, Houston Chronicle, Newsday, Seattle Post-Intelligencer, Kansas City Star, Sept. 10.

http://www.latimes.com/sports/college/football/wire/sns-ap-fbc-lsu-arizona-st-scene,1,5746783.story?coll=sns-ap-collegefb-headlines

“Starter marriages” helping to reduce stigma of divorce

The stigma of divorce is waning, as not only celebrities change partners but half of all those who marry. “Many people in their 20s have what we now call starter marriages, find they aren’t compatible, then marry someone else,” says gerontologist Kathleen Waldron. “Many times they bring the same mistakes and issues into another marriage.” Delaware News Journal, Aug. 22.

http://www.delawareonline.com/apps/pbcs.dll/article?AID=/20050822/LIFE/508220322/1005

Peremptory challenges survive controversy, calls for reform

The jury selection process still includes peremptory challenges, a controversial practice in which lawyers can reject a jury candidate without giving a reason. Lawyers think it allows them to eliminate biased jurors. “Lawyers are afraid to give up a tool that makes them feel better,” says law professor Michael Saks. “And they have enough influence over the rules that they are going to keep things the way they are.” Dallas Morning News. Aug. 22.

http://www.dallasnews.com/sharedcontent/dws/news/politics/local/stories/082305dnprojurystrikes.d5c6e76.htm\

'Genetic Rescue' helps panthers but puts researchers on the spot

Ten years ago biologists transplanted eight female panthers from Texas to south Florida , in an attempt to reverse the effects of inbreeding in Florida panthers. An analysis concludes the risky experiment paid off and that hybrid cats are surviving better, but the methodology of the analysis is controversial. “I would have much more confidence if the people who had collected the data had made the conclusion,” says population geneticist Phil Hedrick. Science, Aug. 19.

http://www.sciencemag.org/cgi/content/full/309/5738/1162

Illegal coral mining may have intensified Tsunami destruction

Illegal coral mining may have intensified the destruction caused by the December tsunami in Sri Lanka , an ASU engineering professor found. Intact coral reefs in other areas blocked the wall of water and deflected it to man-made holes in the reefs, where it reached greater heights and force. “Mining for coral is a cottage industry,” says Harinda Joseph Fernando. “They put dynamite and blow them up to get coral and fish.” The Guardian (UK), Honolulu Star Bulletin and Taipei Times, Aug. 17.

http://starbulletin.com/2005/08/17/news/story11.html

ASU study: Parents may be passing on the pounds

Parents who skip meals and spend too much time in front of the TV may be increasing the risk that their children will become obese later in life, an ASU study shows. Children who grow up in families with bad eating habits and sedentary lifestyles are 33 percent more likely to become obese. “Our research suggests prevention must begin at home,” says Ashley Fenzl Crossman, graduate teaching assistant in sociology. Toronto Globe and Mail, Aug. 15 and Atlanta Journal Constitution and Forbes, Aug. 17.

http://www.ajc.com/health/content/shared-auto/healthnews/prnt/527430.html

Tensions inflame festering war wounds in China

Japan ’s invasion of China 60 years ago has left an ongoing tension between the countries. China ’s enduring sense of grievance, inflamed by rising nationalism in Japan , remains the biggest obstacle to a stable, long-term relationship between the two countries. “If you had asked me 15 years ago, I would have said they were working through this,” says historian Stephen MacKinnon. “Part of the problem is this muscular nationalism, particularly among the young.” International Herald Tribune, Aug. 15.

http://www.iht.com/articles/2005/08/14/news/vj-china.php

Happel: Growing wealth overcoming lack of savings

The personal savings rate for Americans dropped to zero percent in June, but this doesn’t mean they aren’t increasing net worth. Economists say America remains one of the best places to create wealth. “The savings rate falling to zero isn’t of great concern,” says economist Stephen Happel. “It doesn’t reflect increases in the value of homes or the appreciation of stocks.” Chicago Sun-Times, Aug. 14.

http://www.suntimes.com/output/wiles/cst-fin-savings14.html

Modern crime science isn't enough about science, says law professor

There’s plenty of forensic science on the TV screen, but scant good science actually exists in the courtroom, other than DNA analysis, says law professor Michael Saks. “People probably expect more out of science and technology than it can deliver. At the end of the day we settle for a quality of knowledge in our courts that isn’t as good as the impression we all get, that courts are so careful and scrutinize things.” Wisconsin Journal Times, Aug. 13.

http://www.journaltimes.com/articles/2005/08/13/local/iq_3638031.txt

Sarewitz: Look to baseball for bioengineering’s future

Biological engineering is not just about curing disease anymore. The profit motive is moving science toward drugs and technologies to enhance human performance, as seen by steroids in baseball. But science is offering “the potential to modify humanity itself, to change the essential attributes of humanness,” says Dan Sarewitz, professor of science and society. “The time to consider which directions we want to push the technology, and which we want to avoid, is now.” Los Angeles Times and Houston Chronicle, Aug. 9.

http://www.chron.com/cs/CDA/ssistory.mpl/editorial/outlook/3305448

ASU’s Beaulieu decries 'No Child Left Behind'

Meeting the unique cultural needs of American Indian students in the classroom is getting squeezed out by the artificial standards of the No Child Left Behind mandate, says David Beaulieu, director of the Center for Indian Education. “Issues of tribal culture, language, cognition, community and socialization are central to learning, and they are being dangerously overlooked. NCLB is crowding out the broader purposes of Indian education.” Indian Country Today, Aug. 5.

http://www.indiancountry.com/content.cfm?id=1096411362

Future Mars mission may bring home more than rocks

In the next Mars exploration, most scientists consider bringing Mars samples back to Earth a priority, since the rocks will help sort out the planet’s complicated geological history. Bringing samples home will be difficult and expensive, but NASA’s long-term plan to send astronauts to Mars dictates the need for more knowledge. “I’d be surprised if we didn’t have sample return as a precursor to humans for safety reasons,” says planetary scientist Ron Greeley. Nature, Aug. 3.

http://www.nature.com/news/2005/050801/full/436613a.html
