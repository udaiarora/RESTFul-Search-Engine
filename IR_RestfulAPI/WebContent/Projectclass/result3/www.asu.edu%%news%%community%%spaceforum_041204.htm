Arizonans Asked to Inspire Space Exploration

Arizona, the country needs your input on space exploration. More specifically, it needs your inspiration.

Exploration requires imagination. Consider Christopher Columbus and the other Spanish and Portuguese sailors who began the 'Age of Exploration.' If they had not envisioned something beyond Europe, explorers would not have set forth in the 15 th and 16 th Centuries and discovered a bigger world out there.

On January 14, President Bush announced a new mission for NASA, aimed eventually at mounting manned missions to Mars and the universe beyond. To help chart specific plans for this new mission, he appointed a special, nine-member Presidential Commission on the Implementation of U.S. Space Exploration Policy, a group of business leaders, policy makers and scientists that includes one Arizonan - Arizona State University planetary geologist and Director of the Center for Meteorite Studies Laurie Leshin.

The President also called for public comment,  so Leshin and Arizona State University have organized a special event to allow the people of Arizona to discuss the possibilities for the future exploration of space and a forum for giving input into the decision.

On Friday, April 30, ASU is hosting  "Exploring Our Place In Space: A Community Forum" at 7:00 pm  in Gammage Auditorium.  Sponsored by the ASU Center for Meteorite Studies, the event will feature a panel of space pioneers and policy makers discussing the challenges of the nation's new Space Exploration vision and responding to audience questions and comments. The event is free and open to the public.

Participating on the forum panel are newsman, historian and NASA advisor Hugh Downs, Astronaut and NASA Chief Scientist John Grunsfeld, Cosmos co-producer and NASA engineer Gentry Lee , Laurie Leshin, and ASU student scientist Melissa Wallace. The panel will be moderated by ASU President and national science policy authority Michael Crow, who will help direct a discussion about rationale, costs, and implementation of the vision.

"The goal of the event is to engage Arizona kids, business leaders, scientists, and the public in the emerging national discussion on space exploration," said Leshin. "We have brought together a unique group of space pioneers and advocates to explore the challenges of the nation's new Space Exploration vision as announced by President Bush in January.  Such a discussion is key to creating a sustainable space exploration program in our nation, and both NASA and the President are very interested in hearing what the public has to say."

The public is encouraged to participate and may also submit questions or comments in advance at over the internet at < http://www.asu.edu/explorespace/ > . 

The forum is free but tickets are required. Tickets are available at ASU Bookstores, Borders Books, Challenger Space Center, the Arizona Science Center, and at the door.  For group reservations and more information, call 480-965-0051.
