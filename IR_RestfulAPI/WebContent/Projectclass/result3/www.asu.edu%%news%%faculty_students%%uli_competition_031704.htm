March 17, 2004

Graduate team's architectural design creates a destination

An interdisciplinary team of ASU masters students has been named one of four finalists in the Urban Land Institute (ULI) Gerald D. Hines Student Urban Design Competition.

Harvard University, Massachusetts Institute of Technology and a combined team from Stanford University and the University of California, Berkeley, are the other three finalists.

The teams submitted designs for a 57-acre parcel of "underutilized" land adjacent to the Allegheny River, near Pittsburgh's Strip District, that would revitalize the area and reconnect it to the greater community. They had 10 days to submit their plan.

The ASU team was flown to Pittsburgh to see the site and meet with city leaders, and are working over spring break to refine their design schemes for the March 26 final judging in Pittsburgh.

The winning team receives $50,000, of which 10 percent will be paid to the team sponsors. The remaining teams will split a $30,000 prize.

ASU's team, sponsored by ULI-Arizona Chapter, and the College of Architecture and Environmental Design, includes Prasoon Kumar, Parul Mittal and Timothy Parke, environmental planning; Matthew Muller, architecture; and Mohan Sankrit, business.

The competition site occupies the former rail beds of the now defunct Pennsylvania Railroad. Currently, it is used for parking for downtown office workers and for functions at the nearby convention center. On the site are two buildings: a historic railyard shed and an office building.

ASU's design team, assembled by Kumar, named its concept "Destination Allegheny." It includes a plaza that offers a connection from downtown Pittsburgh to the waterfront district plus a place to have festivals or open-air theatrical productions.

Around the plaza, as the team envisions it, would be cafes, night spots and offices. The old railroad shed would be converted to retail, and the students included row houses so there would be a mixture of uses.

How did a team of five graduate students, most of whom did not know each other before the contest, pull off a winning design in less than two weeks?

"We worked pretty well together," says Kumar. "Since we are from different backgrounds, there was no fighting."

Muller added, "A couple of ideas were thrown out. We adopted one, and we tried to work as much in collaboration as possible."

The jury selected ASU's entry for having "a realistic development proposal, with an anchor centered on a waterfront plaza and retail edge along Smallman Street that ties together the commercial and office spaces to the west with residential uses to the east."

Catherine Spellman, associate professor of architecture, is the team's faculty adviser. Professional adviser is Greg Vogel, Land Advisors Organization.

For more information about the competition, visit (www.udcompetition.uli.org).
