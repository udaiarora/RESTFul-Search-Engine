September 15, 2003

Researchers, students explore domestic violence in Arizona, find prosecution inconsistent

Making courses relevant to real world situations is one of ASU's primary goals. Students in the spring 2003 sections of BIS 302 were presented with a research topic that is definitely real-world - domestic violence.

The students, under the tutelage of Bachelor of Independent Studies Lecturers Kevin Ellsworth, Layne Gneiting and David Wells, conducted the first ever review of domestic violence prosecutions in Arizona.

Their findings, best described as disturbing, include:

. Women victims are often arrested improperly;
. Cases are not designated as domestic violence in a consistent manner;
. Lower conviction rates and lighter sentences are associated with cases designated as domestic violence, particularly at the felony level;
. In Maricopa and Pima Counties, African-American men are three times more likely to be arrested for crimes with a domestic violence designation than white men. If convicted, their sentences are more severe;
. Crimes not designated as domestic violence at the time of initial arrest have little chance of receiving the designation at a latter date, reducing the consequences for perpetrators; and,
. Even when there are convictions, sentences vary widely among the counties in Arizona.

"We found that domestic violence statutes are enforced on an uneven basis," says Wells. "Some counties appear to practice an 'arrest them all and let the courts sort it out' policy rather than applying the law. It may be something as simple as a lack of information among officers and prosecutors or it could be a form of bias. Either way, the situation needs to be dealt with."

Wells says students looked at the consistency and differences in charges for domestic violence filed from Jan. 1, 2000, through Dec. 31, 2002. The database was based on information about a set of crimes often associated with domestic violence. Data includes information on charge, conviction, sentencing and whether domestic violence was designated either at the time of the arrest or time of disposition. Overall, the students looked at a database that included approximately 260,000 charges over two years.

"We were able to ascertain differences in how domestic violence prosecution is dealt with in Arizona as well as key indicators that suggest substantial shortcomings that merit further investigation," Wells says. "It's our hope this report will help policy makers focus efforts on improving Arizona's response to domestic violence.

"The laws on domestic violence appear to be misapplied in many cases," he says. "Arizona may need to do a better job of educating officers about domestic violence so that the laws we have are effectively enforced."

The course, BIS 302, is a research methods class that works in conjunction with local non-profit organizations to conduct field research as a teaching method.

The full report is available online.
