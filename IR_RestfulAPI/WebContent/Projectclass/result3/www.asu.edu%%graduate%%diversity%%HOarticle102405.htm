Hispanic Students and the Pathway to Graduate Education

By Dr. Maria T. Allison, and Dr. Marjorie Zatz

Dr. Maria T. Allison is Vice Provost and Dean of Graduate Studies at Arizona State University and Professor of Community Resources and Development. Dr. Marjorie S. Zatz, former Associate Dean of Graduate Studies, is Vice Provost for Academic Personnel and Professor of Justice and Social Inquiry at Arizona State University.
One of the most serious issues facing educators today, from kindergarten through doctoral levels, is how to insure that the next generation of leaders represents the quality and diversity of our country. The critical nature of this responsibility is driven home most dramatically as we think about how best to enhance the educational successes of our Hispanic students, who will represent an increasingly significant proportion of the United States population in the decades ahead.

Recent studies such as the Pew Hispanic Center Reports, Latino Youth and the Pathway to College (Swail, Cabrera, & Lee, 2004) and Latino Youth Finishing College: The Role of Selective Pathways (Fry, 2004), and the Rand report entitled, Goal: To Double the Rate of Hispanics Earning a Bachelor's Degree (Vernez & Mizell, 2001), provide excellent analyses of trends and critical issues that are essential to preparing and educating the next generation of Hispanic leaders.

Yet as thoughtful and comprehensive as most of these reports are, they often focus their analyses at the high school and bachelor's level. This, we suggest, is not sufficient. We need the thoughtful vision, knowledge and perspectives of our most talented students, including our Hispanic students and others underrepresented in sciences, arts, education, business and a host of other disciplines, as we address the most pressing problems facing our communities, our nation and the world. From our perspective as graduate deans, it is imperative that we set our sights higher and continue to think about how best to move these promising students from the bachelor's degree through completion of graduate degrees.

The data indicate that we have much to do. As of 2001, the National Center for Education Statistics reported that Hispanics earned only 5 percent of master's degrees, and only three percent of the Ph.D. degrees awarded in the U.S. The 2003 NSF/NIH/USED/NEH/USDA/NASA Survey of Earned Doctorates indicated the same percentages for doctoral degree completion two years later. In general, the national data are clear — the number of underrepresented students pursuing and completing graduate degrees in this country is improving slowly but is much lower than it should be. Why are many of these students stopping at the bachelor's level, particularly those students from first generation families? The reasons are as many and varied as those that diminish undergraduate completion rates. They include, among other things, financial stresses, family responsibilities and lack of sufficient mentors to convince them that a graduate degree will provide long term value to themselves and their communities.

We in graduate education need to work directly with our undergraduate leadership to build an educational model that promotes and facilitates an understanding of the value of graduate education and create the support networks and resources to make it happen. In doing so, we can help create an environment in which Hispanic graduate students, and others who are underrepresented in higher education, can thrive.

At Arizona State University (ASU), we continue to build on our strong national bridge programs such as the NSF Bridges to the Doctorate and compete for the very brightest and best students from across the country, but as we explored our range of initiatives, we asked ourselves, why not recruit the best at your own institution? With the support of the 2004 Council of Graduate Schools/Peterson's Award for Promoting an Inclusive Graduate Community, ASU established the Pathways to Success program.

Pathways is specifically designed to enhance the recruitment, retention and graduation of our own talented pool of students underrepresented in a host of disciplines if they choose to remain and pursue advanced degrees at ASU. This program links the efforts and energy of the Division of Graduate Studies with the Barrett Honors College, the Office of Undergraduate Initiatives and faculty and students throughout the university to build faculty and peer mentoring programs, research partnerships and educational opportunities designed to encourage and support the most promising ASU undergraduate students to pursue graduate education. Some argue that "recruiting your own" is not healthy for a student, but we believe that if such a program helps students continue through at least the master's degree, then it is our responsibility to do so.

As educators it is important for us to think about ourselves as tied together symbolically, from kindergarten through the Ph.D., creating an incredibly diverse and high quality intellectual talent pool for this country and our global community. By building educational pathway programs we can work together to demonstrate to our communities and to our students that graduate education has relevance and meaning to their lifelong aspirations.
