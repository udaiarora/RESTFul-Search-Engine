ASU carillon to make music once again

ASU carillon to make music once again

After sitting in storage for several decades, out of sight and out of mind, ASU’s Symphonic Carillon will be back in the spotlight Oct. 28.

The carillon, which was purchased by Associated Students of ASU in 1966, will be re-dedicated with a 3:30 p.m. reception and 4 p.m. concert by carillonneur Linda Vogel Yarbrough.

The reception, to which the community is invited, will take place on the Starlight Terrace of the Memorial Union (MU) on the Tempe campus.

The carillon will be permanently housed in the MU’s Zuni room. Guests will be invited to view the carillon and bells following the reception.

Speakers will include Les Pospisil, who was an advocate for the carillon as a student senator; Steve Yarbrough, now a state legislator, who also was a student senator; and ASU professor of organ Kimberly Marshall.

A representative of a campus ROTC group will offer greetings, since the carillon was dedicated in 1967 as a memorial to ASU veterans.

In addition, metalsmith Barbara Atkinson will unveil her limited-edition holiday bells, which will benefit the carillon restoration fund.

The concert will include two works written for the dedication by Scottsdale composer Robert La Pat.

Vogel Yarbrough, who was the first ASU student to play the carillon, earned her bachelor’s degree from ASU in 1970. She was a member of the student senate from 1965 – 1969 and first vice president of ASASU from 1968 – 1969.

She was accompanist for the University Choir from 1965 – 1969. She completed her master conductor studies at Azusa Pacific University and has taught at Balsz Elementary School, Scottsdale Christian Academy and Grace Community Christian School.

She currently serves as chair of the Fine Art Department and as the choral music director at Valley Christian High School.

The carillon was used for several years, then put in storage, where it remained until November 2002, when it was discovered by carillon lovers.

A carillon is a set of stationary bells usually played from a keyboard. The word comes from Late Latin quatemion, via Old French quarregnon (by fours), with reference to the fact that the original carillon consisted of four bells hung in the tower of a church.

For more information about the carillon, send an e-mail to (carillon@asu.edu).
