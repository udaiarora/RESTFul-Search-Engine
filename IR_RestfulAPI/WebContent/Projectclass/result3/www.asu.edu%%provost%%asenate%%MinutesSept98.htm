3.C University Provost's Report (Milton Glick). 

I want to thank Dr. Davey and Paul Frost and Nancy Higgins for continuing the wonderful history we have had of organizing these legislative coffees. Not just talking to the legislators but understanding the issues that are important. I can not overstate the impact that we as a community have had on the legislature. It is important that we not just act as advocates but we act as informants because a problem that we face as a public university is not just whether they like us but whether they know us. Legislators typically hear from a small number of people (three is a landslide) and they are not the three happiest students or parents we have. That is their vision of the university. The effort that you folks made really makes a big difference. 

I also want to compliment Dr. Burstein on the leadership he is taking in bringing together people to talk about what our needs are in the enterprise of computing systems. It is the right thing for us to be doing now. We have dedicated all of our administrative computing resources for the last two years to the FY2000 Project, and it is working. When I read the horror stories elsewhere, we are already into Beta testing now of the systems and we will be there when we need to be. This is not to say we will not find a few glitches on January 1, 2000 but they will be few in numbers. It is now time to ask how do we bring the systems together. My own view is that we want to have computing systems that do what needs to be done in an efficient way. We do not want to be known as having the best administrative computing in the country. No university is great because it has the "best" administrative support systems but they have to be good enough and that is what I think that Dr. Burstein is trying to organize. One of the problems in computing, like everything else, is the best drives out the good. This is a good opportunity for us. 

The budget that Dr. Davey has asked me to explain is virtually an unexplainable process. I would rather be the one that explains it. First some distinctive features: It is the first two-year budget that we have dealt with. The legislature has gone to a biennial process and we have to treat it as though it will work in the best possible way, and then move from there. 

Secondly, there is now a statewide task force set up by the Office of the Governor to look at salaries of all state employees. As you know, our top priority for a number of years has been fixing salaries at this university. The two major discretionary investments we have made in the last six years are salaries and undergraduate education. We must continue to keep those priorities because if we experience one dry year, we will be back to where we were six years ago. The three presidents, the leadership of the universities, and the board here agreed that the best way to continue the salary agenda is to join forces with the rest of state government and urge an adequate state employee salary package. We have committed ourselves and the Board has recommitted itself to this high priority but to do it by getting the best state employee package. 

The Board did something unusual at this meeting. It is the first time that they said that there are Board priorities that are so high that we are placing them at the top of the university's agenda. Two collaborative efforst are at the top. Those two areas were teacher education and access. With respect to teacher education, there is great concern over the fact that students graduating from our College of Education, this December, will be subjected to two exams; a content exam and a pedagogy exam, neither of which have yet been written. Many may have read about what happened in Massachusetts. If State policy is that if seventy-five percent of our students do not pass, we will not be eligible to be a teacher education institution. I think seventy-five percent is a low number because we would expect ninety percent of our graduates to pass; but when you are talking about a test that is yet to be written, seventy-five percent may be a very high number. Our College of Education is working with the leadership of our College of Liberal Arts and Sciences to try to ensure a good performance on a test we have not seen yet. That is very tough. Once the test is available we will know how to prepare our students. The more difficult process is that two years out they have to take a performance test, not a knowledge test. That performance test will be on students that have been out for two years that we do not have any control over. However, is they do not do well on it, we will certainly have failed. Some of those people taking those performance tests will be students that attended NAU, some that went to ASU, some that went to UofA and some that came in from out of state, or from Grand Canyon University or whatever. We will still be held responsible if our students do not do well on that although we lack control over those students. In the budget we ask for funding to set up a collaborative center between the three universities; that center would be a coordinating center to help each university reach out to those new teachers in our region. We would have an obligation to help teachers in Maricopa County, whether they graduated from ASU, UofA, or NAU, Grand Canyon or Cal State, Fullerton. How we are going to do that is unknown, but we are trying to use the joint center to consolidate our efforts. It can best be done in collaboration with the public schools. No teacher has to come for help; they are now the employees of the public, charter, and private schools; they are not our employees and they are not our students. Therefore, building this collaboration between K-12, the universities and the new teachers is a difficult and important task. 

The Board of Regents has made it clear that this collaboration is their top priority. They have also made it clear that they want a collaboration providing greater access to students both from high school and from the community colleges. On that we have not yet entered a joint project but rather each of us has put in our budget effort to make it more accessible to those students. 

Finally, there is a joint project for a software institute that would help prepare Arizona's work force. We have decision packages (that Dr. Davey will send out to senators) that are in three areas: research and economic development initiatives aimed at making Arizona's future sustainable; continued improvement in undergraduate education; improving technology.

In addition to aiming at first and second year students as we have done in the past several years, we propose investing in key needs at the upper division. As we have expanded our freshman class we are increasing the pressure on units to make sure that there is good available access to upper division with quality instruction and so on. We will emphasize advising. 

The provocative part of the meeting dealt with the fact that our projected tuition revenue was considerably higher than our realized tuition revenue. Our estimates on how many students and the non-resident/resident mix were too high. Part of the reason for this is that we have under projected in the past and the joint legislative budget committee has criticized us for this. We decided that instead of a conservative projection to do an aggressive projection this year. Wrong year! How did we over project, where did the changes come? The changes came in that we have five hundred fewer seniors this year than last year. That could be very good if that means we have graduated people. While we do not know whether our graduation rate is up, we do know that last year we had only three hundred students graduate with between one hundred and one hundred twenty-five hours. This year we had eleven hundred students graduate within that range. We would like to believe that the loss of seniors is because students are succeeding. We do not know that yet. 

The second reason is that we are down in freshman, about three hundred students. I am not happy about that because our goal has been to build the freshman class. I am not surprised by it because last year we had a nine hundred-freshman student increase. You usually do not have that kind of growth without some fallback. The third piece is that we are down about two hundred fifty graduate students. Dr. Tom Callarman told me yesterday that it is almost all attributed to the fact that the state no longer requires a master's degree for public school teachers. We have to look carefully at all of these things. 

The greater shift however was between our resident and our nonresident mix. This was partly brought on by ourselves. We have been very close to twenty-five percent out-of-state students, Board of Regents policy states we should not exceed that. We have made a special effort to recruit high achieving Arizona students this year. It turns out that we stayed about even in Arizona freshman but we lost two hundred out of state freshman from where we were last year. That has a big budget impact. When you take all that into consideration it means that our projected tuition revenue and our achieved tuition revenue is off about six point seven million dollars, a significant budget hit, part of which we took into consideration when we did our budgeting. That does not mean that we lost $6.7M from last year, it means we lost $6.7M from what we projected. We will be able to work through that without having to reduce unit budgets because of contingencies that we have built in but it will mean in some cases slowing down on important things like start up money, computer infusion, computer upgrades, major equipment needs, and slowing down some hiring. There is a real hurt to us but it will not require dipping into unit budgets and withdrawing money. However, if we do not change those numbers next year, the story will be different. We are taking the recruitment very serious for next year. 

With regard to recruitment, I did an analysis of some data on student persistence here. The best indicator we have of freshmen success is whether they return in the second year. Students were divided by high school class rank in terms of five percentile groupings. It turns out that the first two five percent groups do similarly (0-5 and 5-10 percent) and they do substantially better than the goals that we have set for ourselves on persistence. The next three groupings (10 -25) are comparable and they perform above our goal. What that tells us is that we want to place special emphasis on recruiting the top twenty-five percent of the high school class because they are students who are succeeding here. As we drop below that, persistence is substantially worse. That does not mean that we should not admit those students but it does mean that we need to target our efforts on students who will be successful at ASU, for our good, for their good and for the state's good. We also plotted SAT scores and found out there is no predictive value to student success here whatsoever other than the sixty-seven students that have SAT scores above 1400. All the rest of the students have similar persistence. The only differentiating occurs if they take SAT tests. As we develop our recruiting strategy for freshmen, this reinforces the students who are likely to do very well and will benefit from ASU. For several years we focussed on recruiting the top two percent and the top five percent and that is good, those are very important students to attract. That is why we have two hundred or more national merit scholars here today and we want to keep building that. That is not enough high achievers to change the culture of the enterprise. We can get enough top twenty-five precenters to continue to make success a feature of this campus for our students. This is the type of agenda we are on for the next couple of years.
