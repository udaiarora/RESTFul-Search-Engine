August 21, 2003

Plus/minus grading set for implementation in 2004

Following a recommendation by the Academic Senate, and with input from the Associated Students of ASU, President Michael Crow has approved a plus/minus grading system to take effect with the Fall 2004 semester grades.

The new plus/minus grading, which gives faculty more flexibility in assessing student performance, includes a top grade of A+ (4.33), in addition to A (4.0), A- (3.67), B+ (3.33), B (3.0), B- (2.67), and C+ (2.33), C (2.0), D (1.0) and E (0.0).

"The implementation of this new system provides greater flexibility, but it is not mandatory for faculty to use plus/minus in their grading," says Crow.

The Academic Senate, which is advisory to the president, provided its recommendations in writing in a memo dated May 31. In the memo, then Senate President George Watson, outlined in detail the rationale behind the Senate recommendation, as well as the exhaustive process, which had included, he felt, ample opportunity for student and faculty input. The Senate recommended a plus/minus grading system with the top grade of A and bottom grade of C.

Students, through their elected student government officers, expressed concern that their voice had not been adequately heard, prompting a petition campaign that resulted in nearly 4,000 student signatures opposing the Senate recommendation. With that unprecedented level of response from students, Crow felt it was only fair to take their concerns into careful consideration as he studied the plus/minus issue.

"The students made a very strong argument that they would be disadvantaged, compared to students attending other state schools, without the addition of the A+ option," says Crow.

The main concern expressed by students was that, without the A+, students would have no way to offset an A- grade on their GPA, effectively punishing top students and putting some in peril of losing scholarships requiring a 3.5 GPA.

"I'm pleased the president has approved the plus/minus system recommended by the Academic Senate," Watson says. "The addition of the A+ grade, while not approved by the Senate, is a reasonable recognition of the counterproposal by the students. I would have done the same thing in his shoes."

The students had also expressed a desire to include the plus/minus option in the entire grading system, which would have meant allowing C-, D+, and D-. Crow, with considerable input from Executive Vice President and Provost Milton Glick, said it would not be in the university's best interest to implement plus/minus options below the C grade level.

"A 'C' average is necessary for graduation and to gain acceptance into certain programs and courses, and, in my opinion, it is not acceptable for a student with all Cs to lose eligibility due to one grade of C-," says Crow.

Crow says his final decision takes into account all of the major concerns and issues that had been raised.

"The new grading system addresses the desire by faculty to have greater flexibility in evaluating and motivating student performance. With the addition of the A+ option, it also provides students with additional opportunity to excel and gain a reward for their extra effort.

"I believe this is the best decision for all concerned, and I appreciate the input from all of the groups engaged in this process," Crow says.

Watson agrees that the end result is a grading system that will improve the accuracy of grading and serve as a substantial incentive for students to achieve a higher performance level.

He adds, "At the same time, it places ASU more in line with other universities across the nation and gives greater credibility to our grading system."

Antonio Garcia, associate professor of bioengineering and incoming president for the Academic senate, says he anticipates future discussion over the newly implemented grading system.

"In 2007, a university-wide committee that will include the Academic Senate will review the implementation of the new grading system," he says.
