October 21, 2003

'Stitches' help express feelings, experiences

Women across the globe express their feelings about violence and war through their domestic arts. A new exhibit at ASU's Museum of Anthropology, "Stitches of War," highlights their work depicting bombings, invasions and assaults using various textiles.

"For millennia, women have spun, woven, beaded and sewn - all activities compatible with child-rearing and food preparation - while men devoted their efforts to hunting and warfare," says Deborah Boyer. "But women, too, are impacted by violence and war, and they often express their feelings through their handiwork."

Boyer, a doctoral student in art history, curated the exhibit, with art graduate student Boyue Yau Tipp, based on a paper that she wrote in 2001 for a seminar titled "Art as Evidence."

"I've read a lot about war and art," Boyer says, "but usually what was written was about men and war, such as in the paintings of Picasso and Goya."
In her own home, of all places, she discovered a linkage between women's art and war.

"We own some Afghan war rugs and Hmong textiles," Boyer says, "and I began to think. The majority of women throughout the world use textiles, so I started looking for textiles that express violence, and found more examples. It became a quest."

Boyer searched for new and old examples - and found some in such unlikely places as a quilt show at ASU West, where she found quilts by Nelly Ricard-Lopez and Evelyn Link. Many pieces came from ASU faculty, and one MFA student loaned a skirt she had printed with green toy soldiers on a camouflage background.

The 57 pieces in the exhibit include embroidered Hmong storycloths, American quilts, a sweater and vest, Afghan war rugs, Navajo beadwork, contemporary textiles, Latin American dolls, Wounaan/Emberra baskets and South American aprilleras.

Hmong women created the storycloths to document their way of life in Southeast Asia, the impact of the Vietnam War on them and their search for recognition as refugees.

The Afghan rugs that feature the weapons of war - AK-347 assault rifles, Hind helicopters, tanks and grenades - were woven during the Soviet occupation of Afghanistan.

The sweater and vest, which were knitted during World War I and World War II, are displayed with pattern books that urged women to "knit for victory."

Women in Chiapas, Mexico, made the soldier dolls and dressed them in black to look like the Zapista Army of National Liberation and sold them to tourists to raise awareness of their cause and support themselves.

And appliquéd wall hangings by women in Chile and Peru - called arpilleras - depict the arrest and disappearance of loved ones during the unrest of the 1980s.

Textiles by ASU fine arts graduate Susan Davis George make a statement about the Vietnam war. In one piece, George combined remnants of her husband's fatigues from Vietnam with pieces of a flag-themed fabric, which she aged by burying it in her yard for several years.

Navajo weavers responded to Sept. 11, 2001, by making beaded necklaces and bracelets with red, white and blue themes and tiny American flags.

"Women are impacted by war," Boyer said. "These textiles have enabled women to organize chaotic events into manageable segments, and to survive, both economically and emotionally, the ordeals they have endured as the result of warfare.

"Many of these textiles have become public symbols of resilience, resistance and survival, providing economic assistance to women striving to have their stories told."

The exhibit runs through May 28. The Anthropology Museum, in the Anthropology Building, is open from 11 a.m. to 3 p.m. Monday through Friday.
