HST-200: The Atlantic World: The Age of Piracy
Dr. R. Burg
INTERNET

This class is designed for students who are self-motivated and able to work on their own. There are numerous required readings, and in addition to these materials, students will be expected delve into the unparalleled richness of the World-Wide Web to examine articles, documents, and controversies relevant to the period under study. In addition to websites, you may also use any books, articles, library resources, and whatever other materials you select to complete the modular assignments. Over the semester, you will come to understand more than simply what occurred in the Atlantic world and the Caribbean during the seventy-five years from the time the first buccaneers established themselves on Hispaniola to final suppression of piracy in the western Atlantic and the Caribbean in the 1720s. When you have completed HST-200, you will have a deeper and more complete understanding of the forces and processes that created and directed, and ultimately destroyed piracy of the sort practiced by men such as Henry Morgan, Blackbeard, Long Ben Avery, and Bartholomew Roberts.
 

HST 300: Creating Communities. The 19th and 20th Century Immigrant Experience
Dr. Christiane Harzig
MWF 9:40-10:30, EDB 212
SLN 48640

(Im)Migrants have always shaped and altered societal trajectories and demanded recognition. The course will look at immigrants' encounters with receiving societies and their social, political and cultural negotiations. Ethnic communities provided the framework for institution building and identity construction, they also function as a stepping stone to move beyond. Because community formation is a complex process and a historically contested issue, it is also a useful concept to historical "tools." It will introduce a broad range of sources as well as the various historical theories and methods (cultural vs. social historical analysis, quantitative methods, oral history, "classical" hermeneutics, construction and deconstruction). The course will focus on U.S. , Canadian and European experiences, questioning the old vs. new immigration country paradigm and U.S. exceptionalism.

Harzig, Christiane and Danielle Juteau, eds. The Social Construction of Diversity. Recasting the Master Narrative of Industrial Nations. New York , Oxford , UK : Berghahn Books, 2003

Hoerder, Dirk, ed. The Historical Practice of Diversity. Transcultural interactions from the Early Modern Mediterranean to the Postcolonial World. New York , Oxford , UK : Berghahn Books, 2003

Iacovetta, Franca , ed. A Nation of Immigrants. Women, Workers, and Communities in Canadian History, 1840-1960. Toronto : University of Toronto Press, 1998.

Daniels, Roger. Coming to America . A History of Immigration and Ethnicity in American Life. New York : Harper, 1991.

Gabaccia, Donna. Immigration and American Diversity. A Social and Cultural History. Malden , Mass. : Blackwell, 2002.
 

HST 300: Japan and the World
Dr. Ian Miller
TTh 10:40-11:55
SLN 16801

On July 8, 1853, Commodore Matthew C. Perry steamed into Japan 's Edo Bay with a squadron of four heavily armed United States Navy warships. Two were the so-called ?black ships,? ominously painted, coal-burning steamships of the latest design. There, anchored close to the poorly defended capital's port and within view of a stunned populace, Perry issued an ultimatum: open the country to trade or face unstoppable bombardment. Thus began Japan 's modern engagement with the outside world, a new chapter in the broader encounter between ?East? and ?West.? For the US , the visit signified a victory in an international contest for diplomatic, political, and economic superiority in the Pacific. For Japan , Perry's black ships ended an age of feudal isolationism, heralding what some have called the ?dawn to the West.?

This version of HST 300 introduces students to the discipline through modern Japan 's history of cultural and international encounter. You will learn how to evaluate source materials, using them to craft your own approach to the analysis of the past. This is a writing-intensive course and you will be asked to hone your writing and research skills through a series of exercises culminating in a final paper.

HST 300: THE RUSSIAN REVOLUTION
Dr. Laurie Manchester
MW 1:40-2:55, EDB 212
SLN 39119

T he Russian Revolution ushered into power the world's first totalitarian dictatorship. In terms of both its breadth and its extremity, it is considered by many to be the greatest revolution in history. The Marxist revolutionaries who came to power in October 1917 ruled Soviet Russia until 1991, initiating a Cold War that divided the world into communist and capitalist spheres of influence for most of the 20th century. Domestically the Russian revolution was responsible for the most profound social upheavals to have ever occurred on a single geographic territory within a short period of time. Because of the scope of its influence, the Russian revolution has received a great deal of attention and has been the subject of great controversy. In this course we will read excerpts from the major debates that have erupted between historians over the Revolution, examine how Russian filmmakers, poets and artists have depicted it over the decades, and survey a wide variety of primary sources, including memoirs, diaries, letters, folkart, political treatises and translations of newly released archival documents such as peasant petitions and secret police reports. By examining different interpretations of the Russian Revolution, as well as primary sources, this course will introduce you to historical methodology by encouraging you to challenge the assumptions that other historians make about the event's origins, outcome, and significance, while also asking you to develop your own interpretations and analysis through the writing of numerous papers.
 

HST 302: The Western Medical Tradition
Dr. Monica Green
TTh, 1:40-2:55, ARCH 321
SLN 38728

Recent scares over anthrax, smallpox, ?emerging diseases? like avian flu, and drug-resistant strains of common microbes have shaken the confidence in modern medicine that reigned throughout most of the 20th century. We are now perhaps better able to appreciate the fact that disease and attempts to relieve suffering from disease have been and will always be part of the human condition. The manner in which individuals have suffered, however, and the ways in which societies have organized medical knowledge and care have always been in flux. This course will trace the many manifestations of the western medical enterprise?that nexus of theory, therapeutics, and social organization?from the time of the ancient Greeks up through the beginning of the 21st century when ?western medicine? is no longer the property of just the West but of the whole world.

Dr. Hoyt Tillman
TTh 1:40-2:55, Coor 186
SLN 99237

HST 306: The Civil Rights Movement
Dr. Matthew Whitaker
TTh 12:15-1:30, LSE B04
SLN 72934

?The Civil Rights Movement? examines the African American struggle for civil and human rights in American from the end of World War II (1946), to the present. Although this course will focus primarily of the black freedom struggle in the United States , it will also connect this struggle for justice and equality to similar movements, both domestic and foreign, that were influenced by America 's black freedom struggle (i.e. the Chicano Movement, the Native American Movement, the Feminist Movement, and Anti-colonial movements in Africa , etc.). In doing so, this course will closely examine the transformation and transitions of African Americans and emphasizes their creation of a unique culture of struggle and resistance as they sought to give meaning to freedom. Students will analyze the Civil Rights Movement by exploring challenges to the nation's ?separate-but-equal' doctrine, ?Jim Crow,? disfranchisement, political marginalization, and economic exploitation.

HST 306: Origins of Civil Rights: From Slavery to Segregation
Dr. Thomas J. Davis
M, 6:40-9:30 p.m.
SLN 34087

Origins of Civil Rights: From Slavery to Segregation treats the historical development of civil rights in U.S. law as it emerged to consider what, if any, personal characteristics control the legal relation of an individual to others and to the community at large. Many commentators and historians trace the law as emerging in blacks' general emancipation from slavery at the time of the Civil War (1861-1865). They especially see civil rights concepts developing from the 13th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution that in December 1865 outlawed ?slavery? and ?involuntary servitude?. Such attention provokes questions about personal status within the United States , about the role of personal characteristics in determining status, about the character of the nation as a social community and as a community constituted by the rule of law, and about the character of law and character of government.

The course focuses on the years between the U.S. Supreme Court's 1857 decision in Scott v. Sanford, 60 U.S. (19 How.) 393, in which Chief Justice Roger B. Taney declared that blacks in the United States had ?no rights which the white man was bound to respect,? and its 1896 decision in Plessy v. Ferguson, 163 U.S. 537, which sanctioned racial segregation. Such a focus demands attention to the contours of legal theories and constitutional doctrines relating to race and rights. It requires attention to changing and diverse issues and questions within the historical development of public policy related to "legal restrictions which curtail the civil rights of a single racial group," as U.S. Supreme Court Justice Hugo L. Black put it in Korematsu v. United States, 323 U.S. 214, 216 (1944). The basic text is Thomas J. Davis's Origins of Civil Rights ? From Slavery to Segregation: Documents and Commentary on U.S. Law, available in the ASU College of Law copy center on the first day of the semester.

HST 498: British Empire
Dr. Kathryn Lynass
TTh 9:15-10:30, ARCH 147
SLN 22900

This course explores the formation, development, and decolonization of the British Empire from the sixteenth to the twentieth centuries.  It examines the impact of empire on Britain as well as in the Americas , the Pacific, the Middle East, Africa, and Asia .  Themes include formal and informal imperialism, imperial identity, decolonization, and the roles of gender, race, and class.  Course requirements include weekly reading responses and a senior research seminar paper. Students will be guided in historical methods of research and writing as they prepare and present their papers.
 

HST 498: Western Women
Dr. Susan E. Gray
TTh 3:15-4:30, ED 228
SLN 27936

This course fulfills the requirement of a senior seminar for the major in history. Over the semester, students produce a twenty-five page paper, based on research in primary sources, on a topic related to the multicultural and multiracial history of women in the U.S. Southwest between roughly 1850 and the present. The course emphasizes the use of ASU archival collections in researching these papers. Class time will be primarily devoted to discussion of the process of producing a research paper, from formulation of a topic to completion of a final, polished draft. We will also discuss a number of writings by and about women in the Southwest.

HST 498: The Norman Conquest of England ?
Dr. Rachel Koopmans
TTh 3:15-4:30, Coor 4581
SLN 42061

The Norman Conquest of England, culminating in the battle of Hastings between William the Conqueror and King Harold of England in 1066, is one of the most memorable events of medieval history. It is also one of the most accessible topics for undergraduate research, allowing students a wide range of choices for a major research paper, including king-making, queenship, warfare, castles, ship-building, political propaganda, law, religion, economics, and even clothing, feasting, and embroidery. In the course of the seminar, students will read and discuss all the available primary sources for the Norman Conquest and produce an original research paper, from 15-20 pages in length, based on these sources. All students will be expected to participate in class discussion, to present their work to the class, and to do at least one major rewriting of their research paper.

HST 498:Playing for Keeps?Sports, Inequality and the ?American Dream?
Dr. Matthew Whitaker
TTh, 3:15-4:30, NUR 306
SLN 60337

?Playing for Keep?Sports, Inequality and the American Dream,? will examine the social, cultural, political, and legal influence of professional and amateur sports in modern North American history.  The seminar will discuss American sports as positive outlets for athleticism, creativity and competition, and constructive channels that sometimes lead to upward mobility, fame, and power.  The course will also examine the full scope of issues pertaining to discrimination and inequality in sports, specifically the history and life of bias, racism, sexism, and homophobia in American athletics.  This seminar will analyze the emergence of intercollegiate sports, and the unprecedented growth, power and omnipresence of professional sports.  It will also examine major of sports figures such as Jack Johnson, Babe Ruth, Ellen ?The Babe? Didrickson Zaharious, Author Ash, Muhammad Ali, Larry Bird, Magic Johnson, Billie Jean King, Michael Jordan, and Tiger Woods.  Course topics will also include racial inequalities on the playing fields and in the front offices of amateur and professional sports; the impact of NCAA eligibility criteria; the history and life of Title IX; gender segregation and exclusion in professional sports; the impact and persistence of private clubs; the relationship between sports and sexual violence; covert and overt homophobia and sexual orientation discrimination in sports; and sports opportunities for people with disabilities.  Like other HST 498 courses, this seminar will ultimately instruct students in the exigencies of historical research.  The seminar will culminate in production of a 25-30 page research paper that displays substantive knowledge of the professional historian's craft.

HST 498: English Travelers, 1600-1800
Dr. Retha Warnicke
MW 1:40-2:55, ED 232
SLN 06818

This senior seminar is about early modern  English people traveling to another country or culture.  The major assignment is a research paper of about 25 pages based on a journal, letters, or other primary sources written by an English traveler or travelers, describing their journeys.  The travelers may be tourists, diplomats, merchants, explorers, colonists,  military figures, or  missionaries. Students will learn how to create bibliographies,  take notes,   draw up outlines,  document their sources,  and  organize and structure their required papers. Through their reading, their research, and the oral reports of their classmates, they will also learn  about English society and the different cultures  the  travelers  encountered around the world.

From progressivism through the ?cultural wars? of more recent decades, the course examines how religious values and movements interact with political and social developments.

HST 498: Nature and American History
Dr. Andrew Honker
TTh, 12:15-1:30, ARCH 147
SLN 15065

This senior seminar examines the role of nature in American history. From their arrival on this continent, humans have interacted with the environment around them, and in this course we will consider the environment as an active participant in this country's history, not simply a passive backdrop to American progress. We will read a few books in common, but the major assignment will be a research paper of around 25 pages on any topic within the scope of the course from the intellectual role of wilderness in the American mind to the impact of air conditioning on life in Phoenix . As you work on your research projects we will discuss in common the steps of the research process.
