January 13, 2006

Charity recognizes 3 ASU educators

Three ASU educators have been honored with 2005 “Excellence in Education” Awards from the Ronald McDonald House Charities for their exemplary commitment to education, innovation and leadership in the community. The awards were presented Dec. 7 at the organization’s national scholarship breakfast at the Arizona Biltmore.

The ASU honorees are:

• Janice Kelly, who has overseen ASU’s community-based service learning program, ACES, for eight years.

• Delia Saenz, director of the ASU Intergroup Relations Center.

• Peterson Zah, counselor to the president on American Indian affairs.

Awards also went to Wilma Patterson of South Mountain Community College and Roman Reyes of Phoenix College.

“These five college educators have demonstrated exceptional performance toward improving the quality of teaching and learning for students,” says Wayne Stingley, president of the RMHC board of directors. “They are all truly outstanding educators whose distinguished leadership is affecting our Arizona youths.”

Kelly helped coordinate the ACES program that brings more than 300 ASU students each semester into local schools to tutor and mentor children throughout the Valley. She received two consecutive ASU President’s Medals for Social Embeddedness for her involvement in university outreach projects that focus on helping middle school students aspire to attend college. Kelly has taught composition and leadership courses at ASU for 22 years and recently returned to the classroom to teach first-year composition.

Saenz has been recognized at institutional and national levels for outstanding teaching and mentoring of undergraduate and graduate students of color. In addition to her role as director of the Intergroup Relations Center, she is a research professor in the Hispanic Research Center and an associate professor of psychology. Her research focuses on social identity and family dynamics.

Zah has been instrumental in doubling ASU’s Native American student population in the past 10 years, reaching one of the highest levels of any major college or university in the country. In addition to advising ASU President Michael Crow, he helped bring about the ASU Native American Achievement Program, a partnership with tribes that gives scholarships and academic support to promising students from tribal communities. Zah was elected the first president of the Navajo Nation, leading a movement to restructure and modernize its governmental system from a council to a nation.

This year’s RMHC scholarship breakfast raised more than $250,000 to help Arizona high school students attend college. The program is supported through the efforts of neighborhood McDonald’s owner-operators, local and national Ronald McDonald House Charities and the McDonald’s Corp. Since 1995, it has awarded scholarships to more than 700 Arizona students.

This year’s application deadline is Jan 17. For more information, visit the Web site ( www.rmhc.org).
