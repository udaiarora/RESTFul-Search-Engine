July 1, 2003

Book offers design tips for ranch homes

An ASU associate professor and her students are providing Scottsdale residents who own a 1950s or '60s-vintage ranch house, with some valuable options for updating the home's lighting, space and storage.

The suggestions, come in the form of "Every House Changes," a guide prepared by Catherine Spellman, associate professor of architecture, and almost two dozen of her students over the last year. The project was initiated by the City of Scottsdale to help homeowners update, enlarge and remodel their homes. The ranch style home was built by the thousands in Scottsdale - and throughout the valley - from the 1950s through 1970s.

"All of the houses were built by a few developers, and they have similar floor plans," says Connie Padian, a director in the city's planning and development department.
The ranch house, a staple of the Southwest after World War II, is low in scale, generally single story, and the front "addresses," or faces the street, Spellman says.
"Its functions are divided into separate areas, and the focus is efficiency," she says. "The biggest shift, in moving from the bungalow to the ranch house, was to bring the outside in through bigger windows and sliding glass doors."

Ranch houses also were the first to be built on concrete slabs and to have attached carports or garages.

"There was an excitement about them, an acceptance of modernity and technology," Spellman says.

To begin the project, Spellman and the students visited 25 ranch houses in Scottsdale and surveyed their neighborhoods. They created a package of 12 case studies with design suggestions, ranging from furniture choices to major additions.
One of the challenges of renovating a ranch house is to let more light in, Spellman says. The design team recommended cutting slots in all the walls that touch the exterior wall to make "interior windows," replacing solid hallway doors with frosted-glass-panel doors and adding skylights.

Lack of storage is also an issue in the ranch house. One suggestion, Spellman says, is to cut a huge hole in the ceiling, say in the living room and build storage in the attic that's accessible from inside the house. Another idea, is to cut niches out of the walls between rooms, putting bookcases or shelves between the studs.
The larger-scale renovation ideas include adding second-story apartments, remodeling the house into two townhouses, or building a mirror image of the house behind it, depending on lot size and city codes.

"Our design directives were to define good architecture for the neighborhoods, consider lifestyles of today, think about the desert in terms of landscaping, water and light, try to extend the view through the house and maintain a relationship between inside and outside," Spellman said.

The design guidelines are available in book form from the City of Scottsdale and Spellman's "Every House Changes" lecture is being televised at 2 and 9 p.m., June 28, and 10 a.m., June 29, on Scottsdale's Citycable 11.
