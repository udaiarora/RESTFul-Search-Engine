December 20, 2004

Wilson challenges students to see world differently

There are no unchallenged answers in Angela Wilson’s indigenous history classes. She asks students to go beyond the boundaries of traditional thinking, nudging them out of their comfort zones.

Because of her thought-provoking – yet positive – teaching style, students have named Wilson the ASU Featured Faculty for December.

Both her graduate and undergraduate classes are driven by discussion and inquiry, students say. She supports differing views and always encourages students to speak up.

“Dr. Wilson actively works to challenge her students and help them think critically about the world in which they live, and to help them see the world from the perspectives of others,” says Brian Collier, one of three graduate students who nominated her.

The award program was started last year by Co-Curricular Programs to recognize the contributions of faculty in enhancing student learning outside of class. More than 75 faculty were nominated by their students.

Wilson, an assistant professor teaching American Indian history and indigenous history, is in her fifth year at ASU. She considers herself an activist scholar, gearing her undergraduate courses toward creating socially conscious students who understand their impact on the world. Her graduate courses include a focus on the ethical implications of research and scholarship.

Last month, Wilson and three undergraduate students participated in a commemorative walk she organized in Minnesota to honor the Dakota people who were force-marched from their homelands in 1862.

“Students are often found in her office after class, discussing issues brought up in class, as well as more general issues about current events or things going on in their own lives,” says Kara Carroll, a graduate student.
