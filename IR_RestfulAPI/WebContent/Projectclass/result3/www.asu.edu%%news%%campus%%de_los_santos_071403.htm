July 14, 2003

De los Santos to lead Pan American Initiative

ASU's global engagement agenda is moving forward with the appointment of Jorge de los Santos as the director of the Pan American Initiative. In his role, de los Santos will help ASU build and strengthen ties with international neighbors to the south and north, as well as increase the capacity of existing faculty and programs to act on a global basis.

He sees the Pan-American Initiative as an opportunity to advance ASU institutionally and to expand its capacity to benefit metropolitan Phoenix and the state of Arizona.

"We can enhance mutual understanding and cooperation in the Americas by fostering collaboration between institutions of higher education. ASU already has links to Mexico and Latin America, but the Office of Pan American Initiatives gives us the opportunity to build on these links, to create institutional linkages," he says.

De los Santos plans to increase international institutions' awareness of ASU's research program. A part of this will be improving ASU's name recognition in Latin America and helping to coordinate and build existing programs of research and education in ways that will make collaboration advantageous to all parties.

"The Pan-American Initiative is not a means of building competition with our neighbors, but a platform for building partnerships with international and other national institutions with the objective of enhancing our programs in engineering, bioscience and other technology-based learning," he says.

The ASU Pan-American Initiative follows President Michael Crow's vision for the New American University as an institution that is globally engaged, that recognizes the region as a transnational region.

Crow has also emphasized the need to increase diversity in ASU's student and faculty population. The Pan American Initiative will move the university further toward that goal, as it addresses and assists with institutional partnerships with institutions in Mexico, the rest of Latin America and Canada.

Although all facets of programming will be considered, the Office of Pan-American Initiative will put emphasis on four strategic priorities, de los Santos says.
"We plan to focus on Mexico engagement, because of its strong institutions and proximity; industry and governmental involvement and funding; information transparency and coordination; and public awareness of ASU," he says.

De los Santos has a BA in economics from the Institute of Technology and Advanced Studies of Monterrey in Mexico and holds a Masters Degree in public policy and administration from Columbia University in New York City. While at Columbia, he was awarded a Japanese Government and Inter-American Development Bank Scholarship and was named a MacArthur, Ford and Hewlett Fellow in Social Sciences.
