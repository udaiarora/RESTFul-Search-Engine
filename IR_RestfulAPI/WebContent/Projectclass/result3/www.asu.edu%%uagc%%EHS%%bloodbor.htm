Bloodborne Pathogens Compliance Guidelines

INTRODUCTION AND SCOPE

The intent of this guideline is to offer employees assistance in understanding the Occupational Safety and Health Administration's (OSHA) requirements of the Occupational Exposure to Bloodborne Pathogens Standard (29 CFR § 1910.1030). The standard mandates engineering controls, work practices and personal protective equipment that, coupled with employee training, are intended to reduce on-the-job risks for all employees exposed to blood or any other potentially infectious material. The standard covers employees who may be reasonably anticipated to come into contact with human blood or other potentially infectious materials during the performance of their duties. "Good Samaritan" acts such as assisting a co-worker who has a nosebleed are not covered by the standard.

The standard requires the employer, i.e. the university, to establish a written exposure control plan, identifying workers with occupational exposure to bloodborne pathogens (human) and specifying means to protect and train them. Blood and other potentially infectious materials include: blood, blood products, semen, vaginal secretions, cerebrospinal fluid, synovial fluid, pleural fluid, peritoneal fluid, pericardial fluid, amniotic fluid, concentrated HIV (AIDS) and HBV (Hepatitis B) viruses, and saliva in dental settings.

Workers who may be at risk include, but are not limited to:

  • Physicians

  • Nurses

  • Pathologists

  • Research scientists

  • Phlebotomists

  • Maintenance personnel

  • House keepers

  • Lifeguards

  • Police

  • Hazardous waste personnel

  • Dentists and dental workers

REQUIREMENTS

Exposure Control Plan

Employees may incur risk of infection and subsequent illness each time they are exposed to blood or other potentially infectious materials. An exposure control plan (ECP) is the core element to minimize or eliminate risks associated with blood and other potentially infectious materials. The ECP contains procedures relating to the control of employee exposure. Considering that it is possible to become infected through a single exposure, opportunities for exposure must be prevented to the greatest degree possible. The permissible exposure limit for bloodborne pathogens is zero.

Job/Task Identification

The primary step in developing an effective exposure control plan is to identify where exposures can occur. Although the specific tasks employees perform vary, they may by divided into three categories. Category I tasks are those where the employee is routinely exposed to bloodborne pathogens. Typical jobs include nurses, special research personnel, and phlebotomists. Category II tasks are those performed by workers that are not usually exposed, but may be exposed under certain conditions. For example, a health center maintenance worker who is usually not at risk may be exposed when he services a piece of contaminated equipment. Category III tasks are those activities in which employees are never exposed.

Once the tasks have been identified the control strategy can be developed. Finally, the exposure control plan must be written and available for employee review. It must also be updated to reflect changes in methods, procedures and control techniques.

Exposure Control Methods

Control methods include universal precautions, engineering controls, work practice controls, and personal protective equipment. The term universal precautions refers to a method of infection control in which blood and other potentially infectious materials are treated as if known to be infectious for HIV or HBV.

Engineering controls include puncture resistant sharps containers, splash guards, mechanical pipetting, self-sheathing needles, and biological cabinets. All engineering controls must be maintained in good condition or replaced as needed. Engineering controls should be used in preference to other methods to eliminate employee exposure.

Work practice controls are methods and procedures where a task is performed with an effort to reduce a worker's exposure. These methods include hand washing every time after removing gloves, procedures to eliminate needle-sticks such as never bending, re-sheathing or breaking needles, and minimizing aerosols and splashing by using automated mechanical devices.

Personal protective equipment must be provided by the employer for all identified tasks that have a potential for exposure. The potential for exposure shall be determined without regard to the use of personal protective equipment. Employees must use the appropriate personal protective equipment, e.g., gowns, masks, mouthpieces, and resuscitation bags, as deemed appropriate to reduce exposure. The employer must provide for maintenance, cleaning, and disposal of all protective equipment.

Vaccinations

Vaccinations for HBV shall be made available to all employees who have occupational exposure to blood at no cost to employees, at a reasonable time and place, under the supervision of a licensed physician and according to the latest recommendations of the U.S. Public Health Service. Employees must sign a form if they decline to be vaccinated.

In the event that an employee is exposed, the employer shall offer a confidential medical evaluation at no cost to the employee. The employer shall document the circumstances of exposure, identifying and testing the source individual if feasible, and test the exposed person's blood with consent. The employer will also provide post-exposure prophylaxis, counseling, and three and six months follow up testing and evaluation for any reported illnesses.

Waste Disposal

All infectious wastes shall be disposed of in closable, leak proof containers that are labeled or tagged. If wastes are bagged, the bag must be color-coded and labeled. Sharps shall be contained in puncture proof and leak proof containers. Disposal of infectious waste shall be in accordance with all applicable rules and regulations (consult ASU Environmental Health & Safety).

Warning Signs and Labels

Labels and tags must include the universal biohazard symbol and include the word "biohazard". The standard does allow for the use of red bags or red containers in lieu of physical labeling.

Housekeeping

Housekeeping practices shall be maintained to assure that the work place is in a clean and sanitary condition. Written procedures and schedules must be established for cleaning bench tops, equipment, and other surfaces contaminated with potentially infectious material.

Laundry shall be handled appropriately and laundry workers should wear protective gloves to prevent exposures. Laundry bags should be red and labeled and be leak proof.

Training

The standard contains provisions for informing employees of the hazards posed by the materials they will be working with and equipping them with skills necessary to deal with those hazards. The training program must contain the following elements:

  • A general explanation of the epidemiology and symptoms of bloodborne diseases
  • The modes by which bloodborne diseases are transmitted
  • An explanation of the employer's exposure control plan
  • Recognition of exposure situations
  • Practices to prevent exposure
  • Basis for selection of personal protective equipment
  • Information about the HBV vaccination
  • Emergency procedures
  • An explanation of signs and labels and color coding

The employer must also keep records of any reported worker's exposure to blood or any potential infectious materials. The OSHA 200 occupational injury and illness log shall include any medical treatment for needle-stick injuries.

Research Facilities

There are specific requirements for research facilities doing HIV or HBV research in addition to those established by the standard and CDC/NIH guidelines, these include, in part:

  • Restricted access
  • Decontamination of waste before disposal
  • Vacuum lines must be protected with HEPA filters
  • Procedure and safety manual prepared and adopted
  • Biosafety cabinets used and certified
  • Needle use procedures established and followed

It is through these requirements that OSHA expects to protect employees from exposures to blood and other potentially infectious materials in the workplace. Please contact Environmental Health & Safety at 965-1823 for further information and assistance.

UNIVERSITY ELEMENTS WITH BLOODBORNE PATHOGENS EXPOSURE CONTROL RESPONSIBILITY

Environmental Health & Safety has the overall responsibility for developing and implementing an effective occupational exposure to bloodborne pathogen program at ASU. Environmental Health & Safety reports to the Assistant Vice President for Administrative Services.

Individual research operations are responsible for conducting research in a manner that ensures compliance with established regulatory mandates and university policy and procedures. The Vice Provost for Research is responsible for all research conducted at ASU.
