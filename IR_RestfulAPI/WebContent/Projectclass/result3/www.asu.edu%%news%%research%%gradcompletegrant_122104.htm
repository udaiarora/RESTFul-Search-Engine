December 21, 2004

Grant opens door for doctoral degree study

ASU’s Division of Graduate Studies (DGS) has been awarded a grant to study how to increase doctoral degree completion rates, including those of minorities and women in fields where they have been traditionally underrepresented or experience disproportionately high attrition.

The university is one of 21 institutions selected from a highly competitive pool to participate as a research partner in the Ph.D. Completion Project led by the Council of Graduate Schools, the only national association dedicated to representing and advancing graduate education. The three-year project, supported by funding from Pfizer Inc. and the Ford Foundation, is designed to address attrition issues in the areas of the sciences, engineering, and mathematics, as well as the humanities and social science disciplines.

“Like other major universities, ASU needs to work to improve its doctoral completion rates,” says Maria T. Allison, vice provost and dean of graduate studies. “One of the most important expected outcomes of work on this project is that departments and programs will carefully analyze and track doctoral student progress and develop intervention strategies to ensure their students’ timely completion.”

ASU will join the University of California - Los Angeles , University of Michigan , Cornell University , Yale University , and other public and private institutions from all regions of the United States and Canada , in the project.

Each participating university will receive resources to create, pilot and evaluate intervention strategies, with the ultimate goal of having doctoral departments and programs adopt proven strategies to reduce the number of students dropping out. Partners will have full access to information generated in the project as it develops and eventually will share that information and best practices with the broader graduate education community.

At ASU, several academic units will be involved in the project: anthropology, history, the school of life sciences, and computer science and engineering. As part of the grant, participating programs will develop research/learning communities of faculty and doctoral students that will focus primarily on enriching mentoring opportunities, clarifying program expectations, and exploring the implicit and explicit disciplinary and interdisciplinary cultures within the academic unit.

“The goal is to develop institutional models to ensure and enhance doctoral student success for the university as a whole,” Allison says.

Involvement in the Ph.D. Completion Project builds on ASU’s ongoing efforts to enhance doctoral education. In partnership with DGS, the university is involved with the Carnegie Initiative on the Doctorate, which analyzes the character of top graduate programs to create models of success that can be emulated, and the Woodrow Wilson Foundation’s Responsive Ph.D. Initiative, which is developing and testing models for innovation to improve doctoral education.

Coronado, with the Division of Graduate Studies, can be reached at (480) 965-5995 or (nora.coronado@asu.edu).
