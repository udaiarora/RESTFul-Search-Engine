“¡BASURA [trash] BOOM!” – Latin American literature in cardboard

A rare collection of books published in Argentina by artists, writers and cartoneros (people who gather and sell discarded cardboard) is on display at Arizona State University’s Hayden Library through Jan. 31.

The exhibition, titled “¡BASURA BOOM! Publishing Latin American Literature With Cardboard,” is on the Upper Concourse level of the library.

The primitive books, created through the Eloísa Cartonera project, are made of photocopied pages with handpainted cardboard covers and include fiction and poetry, written in Spanish, by little-known authors from Argentina as well as Chile, Mexico, Costa Rica, Uruguay, Brazil and Peru.

Cartoneros  publishing grew out of Argentina’s grave economic collapse of 2001.

According to David William Foster, Regents Professor of Languages and Literatures, the seeds were planted with the Argentine government’s decision in early 1991 to establish parity between the Argentine peso and the U.S. dollar.

“Parity with the dollar, which was accompanied by the slogan ‘Argentina is First World,’ allowed for the enormous influx of American and European products, and for cheap travel abroad to buy these products….”

The imported goods arrived in Argentina packed in cardboard, and thus began the phenomenon of the cartoneros – the cardboard pickers -- and the industry of hunting through the trash for material to recycle, to build with – and to publish books with.

Since the economic collapse, the cartoneros have become a fixture in Buenos Aires. Intellectuals and artists have recognized them as a valid social movement and supported them.

“They have grown to be seen not as a passing manifestation of an unstable economy, but as an integral part of an unexpected impoverishment of Argentina that is not likely to go away anytime soon; nor are the cartoneros likely to go away anytime soon,” Foster said.

So far, Editorial Eloísa Cartonera has published about 50 books. The ASU Library has 34 of the titles in its Special Collections, which places it in a unique position among American university libraries.

The founders of Eloísa Cartonera would like, some day, to turn the publishing over to the cartoneros, who sell them the cardboard for the book covers.

Daniel Ramos, one of the founders, said, “Sometimes, the cartoneros come to sell [books] with us. In fact, the ideal is that they take over the publishing house. They understand it; we are not the bosses.”

Another founder, Washington Cucurto, added, “With the proper infrastructure, with a bigger system, we could employ a lot of people. We convert trash into books here [in the publishing house]. You could make lots of things with cardboard.”

The exhibit may be viewed during library hours. Call (480) 965-3415 for a recorded message. For more information about the books, call Claude Potts, Latin American & Iberian Studies Librarian, (480) 965-4905.
