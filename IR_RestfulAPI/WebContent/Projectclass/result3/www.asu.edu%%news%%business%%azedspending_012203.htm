January 22, 2003

Education spending per student in Arizona among lowest in nation

Public education expenditures per pupil in Arizona were among the lowest in the nation during school year 1999-2000, for elementary/secondary education and for higher education, according to an analysis by the Center for Business Research at Arizona State University. Arizona fell further below the national average on each of these measures during the 1990s.

"Arizona spends little on public education - compared to other states and to what it spent in the past," said Tom R. Rex, the center's research manager.

Per student spending at public community colleges and universities (excluding capital outlays, such as construction) was the second lowest in the nation in school year 1999-2000 in Arizona, 27 percent less than the national average. Nevada had the lowest figure; otherwise every other western state spent at least $1,900 more per student than Arizona. The inflation-adjusted change between 1990-91 and 1999-2000 was 18 percent in Arizona (a little less than the economic growth rate) and 33 percent nationally. Only eight states, including Idaho, had a lesser percent change.
Expenditures per pupil in public elementary and secondary schools in Arizona were 25 percent less than the national average in the 1998-99 school year, the latest with complete data by state. Only Arkansas, Mississippi and Utah spent less. Inflation-adjusted per pupil spending rose only 3 percent in Arizona between 1990-91 and 1998-99, the least in the West. Seven of the other nine western states posted double-digit increases; the national average rise was 13 percent.

Preliminary data for 1999-2000 indicate that expenditures per pupil in Arizona were 28 percent less than the U.S. average. Utah was the only state with a lower figure.
"These data precede the passage of Proposition 301, a voter-approved initiative in November 2000 that raised the sales tax rate, with the additional tax revenues directed to public education," Rex said. "Proposition 301 funding began in 2001-02, when it provided an inflation-adjusted boost in total government education spending per pupil of 8 percent over the prior year."

Including Proposition 301, total inflation-adjusted per pupil education funding rose only 9 percent between 1990-91 and the current school year, less than half of the economic growth rate. "Thus, while the additional spending from Proposition 301 likely has boosted Arizona a little in the rankings of the states, the state probably remains far below the national average on per student expenditures," Rex said.
Elementary/secondary teacher salaries at public schools also were quite low in Arizona in 1999-2000, with the average teacher salary of less than $35,000 about $7,000 (16 percent) less than the national average - 11th lowest in the nation. In the West, the average salary was lower only in New Mexico. Arizona's beginning (minimum) salary of about $25,600 was not as far (8 percent) below the national average. Twenty-two states, including four western states, had a lower starting salary.

The average public teacher salary in Arizona fell 11 percent between 1990-91 and 1999-2000 in inflation-adjusted dollars, the second greatest decline in the nation. Nationally, the average rose marginally. Arizona's minimum salary decreased 5 percent, compared to a national increase of 3 percent. Only five states, including Nevada, had a greater decline.

At 19.9, the student-to-teacher ratio in public elementary and secondary schools in Arizona was third highest in the nation in 1999-2000, with only California and Utah having higher figures. Nationwide, the average number of students per teacher was 17.4. The 0.5 rise in the student-teacher ratio in Arizona between 1990-91 and 1999-2000 exceeded the national average of 0.2.
