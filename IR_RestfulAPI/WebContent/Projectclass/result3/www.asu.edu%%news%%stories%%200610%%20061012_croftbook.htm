October 12, 2006

ASU professor's book details life, death of first modern terrorist

Everyone knows the name Osama bin Laden. But few people know the name Nikolai Ivanovich Kibalchich ? save scholars of Russian history, perhaps.

But according to Lee B. Croft, professor of Russian and coordinator of Slavic Languages and Literatures, Kibalchich, born in 1853, holds the dubious honor of being the first person to use explosive devices for the purpose of terrorism.

Kibalchich, who loaded the concussion-detonated hand grenade used to kill Tsar Aleksandr II in 1881, in essence was the first modern terrorist. Croft tells of Kibalchich's foray into explosives, conspiracy and murder in a new book titled ?Nikolai Ivanovich Kibalchich: Terrorist Rocket Pioneer,? published this year by the Institute for Issues in the History of Science, Tempe .

Kibalchich, a seemingly ordinary young man, was born to an Orthodox priest in Ukraine and his wife, and was the youngest of six children who survived infancy. The Kibalchich family was descended from Serbs, and those two factors ? his Serbian heritage and his place in the family birth order ? played a part in who Kibalchich would turn out to be, Croft believes.

For the past seven centuries, Croft says, Serbs have seen themselves as ?perennial martyrs, resisting oppression to the death? after their leaders were killed at the hands of Islamic Ottoman Turks at the Battle of Kosovo Polje in 1389.

Kibalchich, being the youngest son, had no hopes of gaining resources from his family, and he left home to settle far away.

So how did a young man born into a respected family end up with the Russian People's Will party, whose goal was to free the Russian people from ?political and economic slavery?? And what finally drove him to give up his life ? and future ? by designing explosives to kill the tsar?

Kibalchich was spared from corporal punishment when he showed early tendencies toward violence, an unusual circumstance in the Russia of that time, when sons were beaten, Croft says. He also was ?disillusioned with religion and state, embittered by a bad experience with the inefficient judicial system, deprived of his dream of marriage, deprived by a prison record of positive employment or further study, and encouraged by others like him in isolation to further act on his professed beliefs.?

Ironically, Croft says, Aleksandr II had already emancipated the serfs and ended slavery in Russia ? and, just before the assassination, the tsar was on the verge of granting the country what the People's Will party wanted: a constitution that provided for an elected constituent assembly with legislative powers, in essence turning Russia into a constitutional monarchy.

So why is it important to read about the life of a Russian man who was hanged at the age of 27 for his part in the assassination of the tsar ? and who was brilliant enough to design a rocket-powered flying device that has been cited as seminal to the birth of the modern space age?

After the appalling act of killing the Aleksandr II, ?terrorists no longer considered killing people by poisoning them, by strangling them, by stabbing them or by shooting them,? Croft says. ?Only using explosives to blow their victims' body parts and blood all over the murder scene before numbers of horrified witnesses would thereafter suffice to create the terror of their conception.?
