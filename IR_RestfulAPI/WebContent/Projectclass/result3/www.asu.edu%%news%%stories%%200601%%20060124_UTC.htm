January 26, 2006

Teacher hopefuls get boost from ASU program

When Nora Ulloa moved to Phoenix with her husband and daughter in 1990, she was unsure of what the future might hold for herself and her young family. But there was one thing of which she was certain: She would finish her college degree.

Michael Cooper photo
Nora Ulloa, right, the principal at Western Valley Elementary School in southwestern Phoenix, earned her teaching degree through ASU's Urban Teacher Corps.

"As a high school student, I never thought I could go to college," says Ulloa, who in 2004 opened the Fowler Elementary School District's Western Valley Elementary School in southwestern Phoenix as its founding principal.

At age 18, the Los Angeles native became the first member of her family to go to college when she enrolled at the University of Southern California. Although she earned good grades, Ulloa changed her major several times before eventually placing her studies on hold to work full time.

Shortly after settling in Phoenix, she took a job as an instructional aide at William T. Machan School within the Creighton Elementary School District in central Phoenix. It was there that she learned of an emerging teacher education program offered by ASU's College of Education.

The Urban Teacher Corps (UTC) partners with nine local school districts, including Creighton, to increase the number of qualified elementary teachers by recruiting nontraditional students. The program, established in 1989, is geared for adults already working in schools within the participating districts as instructional assistants, or in other support roles.

Ulloa applied and was accepted to the program.

"I discovered a strong community of learners who were excited about teaching and were never finished learning," she says. "This was something I wanted to be a part of."

UTC students begin taking general studies courses at participating Maricopa Community Colleges, then transfer to ASU. Upon completion, the participants are ready to apply for full-time teaching positions with their home school districts, having earned their Arizona Teacher Certificate and a full endorsement in bilingual education, English as a Second Language (ESL) or special education.

Ulloa finished her bachelor's degree in elementary education with an endorsement in bilingual education in 1994, then joined the faculty at Machan as a second-grade teacher. In 2000, she was promoted to assistant principal. She also continued her education, earning a master's degree in curriculum and instruction from ASU and entering the College of Education's Dynamic Educational Leadership for Teachers and Administrators (DELTA) program, where she is a doctoral candidate.

"I loved being in the classroom, and that's where I thought I'd stay," Ulloa says. "But when I continued to see so few diverse educators serving in leadership positions at heavily minority schools, I was convinced that was what I needed to do."

Jeanne Fain, UTC program coordinator, says Ulloa's professional experiences exemplify the overarching goals of the program.

"Through my leadership role with UTC and the multilingual/multicultural education program, it is my goal to strategically work toward building successful teaching for our urban and bilingual students," Fain says. "These students need teachers who are prepared, caring, and knowledgeable about high-quality, research-driven teaching practices. They deserve the opportunity to participate in an education that is meaningful - and that builds upon their cultural identities as learners."
