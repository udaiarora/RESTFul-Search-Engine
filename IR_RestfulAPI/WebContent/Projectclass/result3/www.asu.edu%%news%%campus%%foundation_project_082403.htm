August 25, 2003

Regents approve ASU Foundation project

The ASU Foundation received a boost Aug. 15 by the Arizona Board of Regents (ABOR), in their efforts to consolidate its operations and advance the interests of ASU.

The ASU Foundation, the university's nonprofit, tax-exempt organization established in 1955, will undertake a major fundraising campaign for the benefit of ASU and other activities such as the formation of Arizona Science and Technology Enterprises, LLC.

The foundation has assembled sizable assets and endowments that will need to be increased significantly to support the expanding programs of ASU. The $50 million gifts for the W.P. Carey School of Business and the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering, are gifts representative of this growth potential.

To carry out the foundation's mission to bring donors to campus and to involve donors and potential donors in campus activities, the foundation proposed the construction of an office building with ancillary retail and parking structure on ASU property.

"The foundation has been doing a tremendous job," said ASU President Michael Crow at the Aug. 15 ABOR meeting in Flagstaff. "This will become the center of the university as it continues to grow."

The development project will be part of significant redevelopment plan by the City of Tempe for the College Avenue corridor from University Drive to Stadium Drive. Other developments for the corridor now in the planning stages include mixed-used commercial and residential projects that will provide appropriate commercial services as well as "for sale" and "for rent" housing to students, graduate students and local professionals.

The project includes a six-story office building of approximately 156,447 square feet, a multi-story parking structure (approximately 1,200 spaces) and a retail component (approximately 15,400 square feet). The total estimated cost of the office/retail component is $28,230,000 and the estimated cost of the parking structure is $10,630,000. The ASU Foundation will occupy approximately 45,800 square feet in the office building. ASU will occupy approximately 90,000 square feet.

"We are really excited about this project," said Grady Gammage Jr., chairman of the real estate committee for the project. "This is a new entrepreneurial endeavor for us. It's a complex transaction, but it is one that is positive to both us and the university."

Regent Gary Stuart said President Crow's vision is clear and the foundation has a bright future.

"Michael Crow said entrepreneurial does not necessarily mean operating like a business," Stuart said at the ABOR meeting. "He has shown that a not for profit can indeed be entrepreneurial by engaging with the community. I'm very proud of you."

"I would like to personally thank the foundation for their work," said Regent Donald Ulrich, who echoed Stuart. "I am pleased that you got it done and it's a great project.

The ASU Foundation project, which begins in January 2004 and should be completed by January 2005, follows ASU's five-year strategic plan, which was also reviewed and approved at the ABOR meeting. President Crow said ASU is using three factors for guidance in the university's strategic plan - to enhance the quality of ASU today; to build an entrepreneurial culture; and design and launch a program and construction effort.

"All of our critical numbers are of record numbers," President Crow said to the ABOR. "Diversification of students, freshmen retention rates and the number of minority faculty members continue to guide us in our efforts to enhance the quality and access of ASU programs."

In addition, President Crow said recent university hires Mark Jacobs, dean of the Barrett Honors College; Duke Rider, dean of the College of Architecture & Environmental Sciences; Juan Gonzales, dean of Student Affairs; and George Poste, director of AzBio Design, all contribute to the university's effort of enhancing quality and access.

The Arizona Board of Regents also approved the following items:

. The establishment of the Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict and the Stardust Center for Affordable Homes and the Family at ASU.

Provost Milton Glick, who presented the item to the ABOR said, "These centers bring people from different disciplines together to share ideas and come up with solutions."
The Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict addresses the problem of global conflict and religious conflict. I think this is one of the most important contributions the university could make to the state and the nation.

The Stardust Center, which was funded through a $2.5 million grant from the Stardust Foundation, is focusing on community outreach as it relates to the Arizona community and the problems with affordable housing.

. The renovation of Building 785 (Agribusiness Center) at ASU East. Approval of this project included a budget increase of $827,092 for the renovation of the Agribusiness Center's design, materials and industry-specific requirements to make the center a highly-technical, first class instructional facility.

. the creation of a Tri-university ARU electronic library. The board approved a one-time grant of $1,301,456 to the Tri-University ARU Electronic Library Project to add significant resources to the electronic collections of the three Arizona university libraries. The grant is divided into two phases: Phase I involves the purchase of the Elsevier backfiles, a new digital product that will give the distance learner access to the same major serials that on-campus student can now use. Phase II involves the purchase of a collection of current books and scholarly electronic monographs (nearly 3,000 books) in focus areas that include business, economics, management, computers, engineering, K-12 teacher preparation and nursing.

For information regarding the Arizona Board of Regents, visit the Web at (http://www.abor.asu.edu/).
