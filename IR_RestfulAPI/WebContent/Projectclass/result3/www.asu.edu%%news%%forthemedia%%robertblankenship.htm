Arizona State University researcher sheds light on photosynthesis

Robert Blankenship, professor and chair of the Chemistry and Biochemistry Department at Arizona State University, is engaged in studying one of Earth's most fundamental and powerful processes — photosynthesis.

"We are primarily concerned with learning about the exact mechanisms of the energy-storing reactions in photosynthetic organisms, as well as understanding the origin and early evolution of photosynthesis," Blankenship says. See: The Power of Green.

Converting sunlight into energy is the cornerstone to life on Earth, and Blankenship wants to know in unprecedented detail how that process works. His work is wide ranging and it has significantly aided our understanding of photosynthesis. It also has led Blankenship to develop new tools that can be used in the study of plant photosynthesis — tools that could lead to more efficient sunlight to energy conversion devices like solar cells. See: Research chips away at photosynthesis mystery.

Blankenship's research has also taken him to the inky black depths of the oceans in search of photosynthesis. A recent project he was involved in discovered green sulfur bacteria that live by photosynthesis nearly 8,000 feet below the surface of the Pacific Ocean. These bacteria use the dim light of hydrothermal vents to live via photosynthesis. Their discovery could have major implications for where photosynthesis happens and where life may reside — on Earth and on other planets.
