Report: Public investment in science, technology pays off

Morrison Institute highlights public return on voter-approved Proposition 301 funding

A new report from the Morrison Institute for Public Policy shows the positive results from the recent public investment in science and technology.

The 36-page report, titled “Enriching Arizona’s Knowledge Economy: Creating the Research Connections, Attention, and Talent Arizona Needs to Compete; Proposition 301 at Arizona State University, FY 2002 – FY 2005,” analyzes results from ASU’s first four years of science and technology research under voter-approved Proposition 301 funding.

The primary goal of the research is to train the next generation of scientists and engineers, discover new scientific processes and develop applied technologies that will help Arizona become more competitive in the global knowledge economy.

“Enriching Arizona’s Knowledge Economy” is the first study to employ CAT Measures, a 21st century assessment tool developed by the Morrison Institute. The CAT Measures track research results that support three pillars of the knowledge economy considered critical to its growth:

• Connections – the networks developed among researchers, entrepreneurs and venture capitalists that help transfer knowledge and generate economic opportunities.

• Attention – the “buzz” generated by research and research networks that attracts businesses, private investors and highly skilled workers to a region.

• Talent – the top scientists, students and technically skilled workers that help make a region conducive to research, innovation, entrepreneurship and economic growth.

The CAT Measures reveal that Proposition 301 research funding from fiscal year 2002 – 2005 produced numerous results that enhance Arizona’s knowledge economy. For example, ASU researchers:

• Established more than 300 contractual research connections with businesses and universities around the world.

• Collaborated with more than 3,400 research colleagues at other institutions and companies.

• Won more than $86 million in competitive, non-state, public and private research grants.

• Published more than 800 scientific papers and received nearly 5,300 citations by other researchers.

• Improved the university’s ranking among top research universities in the United State by 10 percentile points.

• Increased the skills of 245 graduate student and postdoctoral researchers in fiscal year 2005.

• Trained science and engineering students whose starting salaries after graduation increased 8 percentage points over their peers nationally.

“The CAT Measures tool will help state policymakers determine whether public investments in science and technology research are likely to pay off,” says Rick Heffernon, senior policy analyst and the report’s primary author. “They often have to make decisions in an environment of uncertainty, particularly when it comes to long-term economic strategies. The CAT Measures are designed to inform policymakers along the way by providing ongoing feedback on the impacts of research activities.”

ASU’s flagship for Proposition 301-supported research investments is the Biodesign Institute, which oversees interdisciplinary biomedical research centers that investigate new ideas for improving human health and quality of life. It also links ASU to other biomedical research facilities in the Phoenix area, including the Mayo Clinic, Translational Genomics Research Institute (TGen), Barrow Neurological Institute, and the Arizona Biomedical Collaborative – an initiative to operate a medical school and research campus in downtown Phoenix.

In addition, Proposition 301 funding continues to support ASU research in information science, materials science and wireless technologies. Projects include research in three-dimensional modeling, embedded technologies, information security, and the development of nanotech devices for sensing, monitoring, and communications.

“Enriching Arizona’s Knowledge Economy” is the latest report in the Morrison Institute’s continuing effort to keep Arizonans informed of the status of Proposition 301 investments at ASU. It updates portions of the Morrison Institute’s three earlier groundbreaking studies: “Seeds of Prosperity”; “New Returns on Investment FY 2003”; and “New Returns FY 2002 – FY 2004.”
