January 3, 2005

Urban history report pays off for Escobar

Edward Escobar, an associate professor in ASU’s Chicana/o Studies Department and Department of History, recently earned the Urban History Association Award for best article in Urban History published in 2003.

His article, “Bloody Christmas and the Irony of Police Professionalism: The Los Angeles Police Department, Mexican-Americans and Police Reform in the 1950s,” examines a case that occurred in the early hours of Dec. 25, 1951, when seven young men – five of whom were Mexican-American – were brutally beaten while in custody by about 50 Los Angeles police officers. Disclosure of the beatings three months later created a firestorm of controversy between Mexican-Americans and other police critics, and the recently appointed chief of the police department, William Parker.

Escobar’s efforts were an attempt to understand “the often hostile and chronically tense relationship between law enforcement and Chicana/o communities across the United States.”

“Even more broadly, I see relations between Chicana/os and police as a prism through which to examine broad racial dynamics within American society,” Escobar says. “In this particular case, I see police defining Chicana/os’ racial identity by labeling them as criminally inclined and dangerous – but, at the same time, spurring political activism as reactions to police brutality and other forms of police misconduct.”

For his work, Escobar earned a cash prize from the Urban History Association.

This marks the 15th annual bestowing of this national prize to be conferred at the American Historical Association on Jan. 8.

In addition to teaching students and exchanging ideas with other faculty members, ASU faculty members are also dedicated to their research efforts, an area that Escobar says “is inextricably linked with good teaching.”

For more information about the Urban History Association, visit the Web at (http://www.unl.edu/uha/UHA.html).
