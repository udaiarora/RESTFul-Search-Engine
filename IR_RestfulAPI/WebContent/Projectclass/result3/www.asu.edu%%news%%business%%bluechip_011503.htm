January 14, 2003

Many are using their homes as credit cards

Today's low mortgage rates have made housing a much cheaper commodity allowing many more people to get into the game, lowering the cost of a $100,000 mortgage in Maricopa County from more than 34 percent of the median monthly income in 1991 to about 15 percent today. The result has been not only an unprecedented level of affordability, but also the strongest housing market this state has ever seen, according to the latest issue of the Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast.

"Indeed, at least for this economic cycle, the world has clearly changed," writes Elliott Pollack, Arizona Blue Chip contributing editor. "A combination of unprecedented credit availability, 40-year lows in interest rates, continuing population flows, demographics favorable to single-family housing and rising housing prices have created this most improbable situation in which one of the cyclical areas of the economy has remained incredibly stable."

Low interest rates have affected the economy in another significant way: Many homeowners have refinanced to take advantage of the low rates, and others have taken a great deal of equity out of their homes. According to the Mortgage Bankers Association, each refinance lowers mortgage payments by $200 per month on average.

"This could be positive for the economy, according to Pollack. "If consumers are paying down more expensive credit, it will lower the consumer debt burden - which is currently high by historic standards. The downside is that they could be financing their running shoes over 30 years."

Elsewhere in the Arizona Blue Chip, Editor Tracy Clark polled the Blue Chip panel members on their population estimates through 2004.

"If the panel is correct and population growth remains at 2.6 percent for 2003 and 2004, it would be consistent with what most economists believe about the severity of the national recession and would closely match the last bottom in population growth, which occurred in 1989 and 1990 at about the same level," Clark reported.
Maricopa County is likely to continue to attract the lion's share of population inflows, but the growth has begun to shift from the East Valley to the West, a trend that is likely to continue.

The Bank One Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast includes the consensus forecast of economists from 19 groups and institutions. It is published by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute in the College of Business at Arizona State University. For information, call (480) 965-5543.
