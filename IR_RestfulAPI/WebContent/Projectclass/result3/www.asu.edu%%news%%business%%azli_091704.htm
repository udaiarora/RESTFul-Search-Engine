September 17, 2004

Arizona Leading Index remains stable in July

The Bank One Arizona Index of Leading Economic Indicators rose in July to 124.9, an incremental 0.08 percent higher than the previous month’s 124.8 and 2.9 percent above the July 2003 level of 121.4 (1987 = 100).

Positive influences in the July index included the inflation-adjusted value of Maricopa County residential building permits, production, new orders, and employment from the Business Conditions Survey. Hours worked in manufacturing, the inflation-adjusted value of the M2 money supply, materials inventories and delivery times were negative. Sensitive materials prices were neutral.

“The growth in residential building permits continues to surprise, with some contractors reporting that labor shortages are significant enough to limit the number of homes going up,” remarked Tracy Clark, associate director of the Bank One Economic Outlook Center. “Evidence is mounting that suggests the sector has to slow down soon. Building materials, which are already in shorter than usual supply because of demand from China , will be further squeezed by the need to rebuild in Florida and other places hit by hurricanes this season.”

Materials shortages can restrain building not only because of construction delays, but also because of rising costs, Clark said. Higher interest rates also will also have an impact, first on those buying for investment purposes, and to a lesser extent, marginal buyers who are barely able to qualify for financing at current rates. “It is unlikely that well-qualified buyers moving into the area will be prevented from making a purchase; the moderate pace of rising interest rates that are expected in the next two years suggests that the building will only slow down, rather than coming to a screeching halt like it has in some cycles.”

Hours worked in manufacturing was a weak component at the beginning of the year, Clark noted. “Unfortunately, the earnings forecasts by major semiconductor manufacturers like Intel, Motorola and ON suggest that the manufacturing sector in Arizona still has a way to go before recovery really sets in.”

Employment nationally was weak in July, and appeared to be close to the modest expectations for August. “The national economy remains in danger of stalling unless the employment picture improves soon,” Clark said. “Retail sales in August appear to be weaker than expected, which suggests consumers are becoming more concerned about the economy. American consumers continued spending throughout the recession and so far in the recovery. However, the weak job growth picture combined with higher fuel prices is taking some of the starch out of the consumers’ stiff upper lip.”

Arizona remains stuck in limbo, with an economy that will remain sluggish unless and until the national economy improves.
