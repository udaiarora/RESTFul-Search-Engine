Planning Assumptions


Our study of the Libraries' environment led to the development of tentative assumptions that guided us through the rest of the planning process:


Our User Community

  • The Libraries will be serving a growing and increasingly diverse community during the next several years. Because of higher admission standards and the emergence of satellite campuses, entering students are likely to be better prepared, especially in their ability to use electronic resources.
  • The University will develop more innovative programs and there will be a continued focus on initiatives such as faculty development and improvement of the undergraduate experience. The pace of change occurring in University programs will quicken.
  • Ties to the off-campus community will grow as the University seeks ways to establish mutually beneficial partnerships.

Technology/Electronic Information

  • Demand for library services, especially electronic resources and services, will continue to grow and change.
  • In the short term, electronic services available to all users (including the library as intermediary) will show rapid growth in both numbers and diversity; in the longer term, growth will slow down to a manageable rate, especially as widespread standards promote compatibility among systems.
  • Increasing demand and availability of electronic resources will call for significant changes in the responsibilities and job content of library staff. Though there will probably be a short term need for more instruction, especially as related to electronic resources, in the long term, library users will become more self-reliant and less reliant on instruction by library staff.
  • Library users will have access to the services provided by non-library sources.
  • Libraries will search for new ways to add value to the services they provide.
  • Staff will need to develop new skills and adjust continually to change.

Providing Services and Collections

  • Though the focus on technology will grow, traditional collections (e.g., print) will continue to be built to meet user needs.
  • We will continue to implement alternatives to ownership (e.g., commercial services, Internet, resource sharing).
  • We will continue to have a responsibility to acquire, organize and preserve primary source materials.
  • We will move more and more toward quality service practices as the focus on customer service intensifies. This shift will call for more experimentation and risk-taking, more versatility, more just-in-time services. Resources
  • Budget dollars will continue to lag behind needs for the foreseeable future. No large budget increases are anticipated. In fact, given the high rate of inflation in library materials, the gap is likely to grow significantly each year.
  • There will be no significant increases in library staffing as a result of state funding.
  • There will be a greater emphasis on accountability, as evidenced through use of tools such as performance outcome measures.
  • To accomplish its objectives, the Libraries will need to make difficult choices and/or reallocate existing resources and/or find new funding sources.
