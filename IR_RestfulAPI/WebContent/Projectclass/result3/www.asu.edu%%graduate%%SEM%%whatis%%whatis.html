The SEM Program: An Interdisciplinary Approach

The Science and Engineering of Materials Interdisciplinary Doctoral Program at Arizona State University was established in January 1987 by the Arizona Board of Regents and is administered by the Graduate College, with the concurrence of the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering and the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences. The Program is operated by its faculty whose members are drawn from the Center for Solid State Science and the departments of Chemical, Bio and Materials Engineering, Electrical Engineering, Mechanical & Aerospace Engineering, Chemistry & Biochemistry and Physics & Astronomy.
 

The Program provides excellent graduate education in materials through both instruction and research participation, with emphasis on the solid state science underlying the broad spectrum of materials science and engineering. Most research done by students in the program emphasizes synthesis of new materials, microstructural characterization, including methods development, down to the atomic resolution level, and selected properties measurements as functions of microstructure. Between 1990 and the end of 1995 nineteen students completed work for their doctorates in the program. The response of major industry and academic employers to our program graduates has been excellent. All of our graduates readily achieved challenging positions in industry and universities in the US and in other countries.
 
 

The interdisciplinary faculty strives to deliver the best educational experience to students while simultaneously contributing to the development of new knowledge through research accomplishments. Program graduates are well prepared for careers in research, development and teaching.
 
 

The SEM program emphasizes synthesis of new materials, with emphasis on atomistics and bonding approaches, and quantitative structure-property relations education and research. Six graduate courses treating characterization and analysis of microstructure, physical materials science, chemical thermodynamics and reaction kinetics, quantum theory of solids and solid state chemistry and physics provide the academic core for the program. Other courses for students enrolled in the program are determined by the student's faculty supervisory committee in consultation with the student. Study program planning for individual students who enter the SEM Program from diverse engineering and scientific disciplines takes into account the breadth of the students materials knowledge and experience as well as the student's primary materials interests and career goals. Dissertation research topics pursued by students in the program span a wide range of new materials topics. Two recent examples are Interfaces in Silicon Carbide Whisker Reinforced Silicon Nitride Based Composites and Extrinsic Gettering of Impurity Elements by Near-Surface Dislocations in Copper-Diffused Czochralski Silicon.
