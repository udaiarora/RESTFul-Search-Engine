THE   PHAR                  SIDE

A.S.U. PHARMACY NEWSLETTER                            ISSUE TWENTY-TWO

I Know!  Let’s Burn it and Inhale the Smoke!

                        By Carl Labbe R. Ph

As a smoking cessation specialist, I’ve often wondered what prompted that first guy (I really doubt that a woman would do it) to purposefully inhale the smoke of a burning plant.  I suppose that back in the day, way back in the day, some thrill-seeking pre-historic hairy guy, named Gor or some such, noticed a bit of lightheadedness that time he was stuck in a cave trying to burn the half-dried weeds he had gathered.  He even figured that the visit from the great god of the good hunt was part of the deal.  Gor liked talking to gods.  Gor liked feeling a bit dizzy.  Gor often added half-dried weeds to his fires.  It didn’t take long to figure out which weeds had the most profound effects.  Gor and his progeny became the pioneering pharmacists to the tribe, but only in the most rudimentary way, more likely as shamans or some other type of spiritualist.

History tells us that smoke has played a part in many well-known cultures and milestones.  Frankincense and other herbs were burned in ancient Egypt as they made their bloody sacrifices to their stern gods.  The Oracle at Delphi was said to be under the influence of smoky vapors that allowed her to foretell the future.  We can thank Pliny the Elder for such natural remedies as inhalation of the smoke from burning the dried dung of a grass-fed ox to treat “consumption” and inhaling the smoke of burning rabbit fur as an expectorant. 

But my area of interest is tobacco and its pharmacologically active ingredient, nicotine.  Some African and even European plants contain small amounts of nicotine, but none come anywhere close to good old tobacco (Nicotiana tabacum).

Most estimates place the beginnings of the tobacco plant around 6,000 BC in the Americas, particularly from Mexico to Peru.  By year 1 AD it was evident throughout the Americas and early Native American peoples used tobacco in a variety of ways, including smoking, chewing and even enemas.  A clay pot found in Guatemala depicts a Mayan smoking a string-tied roll of tobacco.  The pot, made before 1,000 AD, is considered the first depiction of tobacco smoking.  This may be not only the first depiction of smoking it may have been the first cigarette advertisement.

Tobacco use spread rapidly northward, hastened through the Mississippi Valley and into the colder climates of New England and Canada.  These northern tribes were fans of the pipe rather than rolling their own.  While their Mexican counterparts were rolling tobacco inside cornhusks, tobacco leaves and palm fronds or stuffing it into reeds, the northern people puffed their pipes.  There is one report of a Y-shaped device that allowed the user to inhale through both nostrils, paradoxically predicting the nasal canula used to deliver oxygen today.

Much has been written of the Huron legend that tells of the Great Spirit Manitou sending help to the tribes that were in the midst of a terrible famine.  A beautiful naked maiden came down from the heavens and sat before the people saying that she had brought food.  After she returned to the sky, the people noticed that corn grew where she had rested her right hand and potatoes where her left hand had been.  From where she was seated grew tobacco.  It is not known if this is how the term “butt” became associated with tobacco.

Certainly a gift this good must be shared.  And so it was that in October of 1492 sailor Christopher Columbus was given dried tobacco leaves along with other gifts of fruit, weapons and other cultural artifacts of the Arawaks of San Salvador (or possibly Cuba, Samana Cay or Gran Turk, history is hazy on this).  These leaves were thrown away.  But when he sent two of his men off to explore the island (purportedly looking for the Khan of China), they saw both men and women puffing on rolled tobacco as big around as a man’s arm.  One of them, Rodrigo de Jerez, became the first European to smoke tobacco, taking his first puff when a friendly islander offered a cornhusk wrapped tobacco “musket”.  Rodrigo was an instant fan, taking to smoking a “segar” every day.  This habit got him into a bit of trouble when he returned home and lit up in public.  The smoke coming from his mouth and nose alarmed his neighbors.  He was quickly arrested and thrown into prison for smoking cessation at the hands of the Spanish Inquisition.  His tortures are not well documented but you can be sure they were more severe than the ministrations of today’s anti-smoking campaigners.  Ironically, by the time he was released, tobacco smoking was quite popular throughout much of Western Europe.

In the following decades, tobacco continues to show up in new places, touching the lives of many historically noteworthy individuals.  Jean Nicot, France’s ambassador to Portugal described tobacco as a panacea and talked of its many medicinal properties.  Now you know where the word nicotine comes from.  Nicot also is credited with sending snuff to Catherine de Medici, Queen of France to be used to treat migraines.  Apparently she was impressed, decreeing that tobacco be called “Herba Regina”.  Although Sir Walter Raleigh popularized smoking at the court of Elizabeth I, it is Sir John Hopkins (an early slave trader) and his sailors who introduced tobacco smoking to England.  Raleigh was a hearty pipe smoker and it has been said William Shakespeare visited him in London.  There is no evidence that the bard inhaled.  Legend has it that Raleigh had a pipe in his mouth when he was beheaded for plotting against James I.  It was the same James I who levied the first tax on imported tobacco in 1603.  It wasn’t until 1629 that France’s Cardinal Richelieu initiated a tax on smoking.

During the seventeenth century various countries embraced or denounced smoking, usually related directly to the ruler’s personal like or dislike of tobacco.  Pope Urban VIII wished to excommunicate snuff users since sneezing was thought to be a lot like sexual ecstasy.  Londoners were encouraged to smoke to help avoid the Black Death (bubonic plague).  The king of France made tobacco a prescription item.  In Russia, Czar Alexis ordered whipping, a slit nose and a trip to Siberia for a first offense and death for the slow learners that had a second offense.

Tobacco has a profound role in the birth of our nation.  In the colonies, tobacco served as early currency.  Early settlers paid 120 pounds of tobacco for “travel expenses” of women sent to become wives.  Two years later the price for a future wife had risen to 150 pounds of tobacco.  The growing popularity of American tobacco contributed to black slavery.  The first Africans were brought into Virginia to help with tobacco production around 1619.  These early arrivals were baptized and indentured for five to seven years.  This practice did not continue for very long and Africans were brought over to serve many landowners as slaves, not servants.  Tobacco exports from Maryland and Virginia sextupled from 1663 to 1699 thanks in part to the labor of black slaves.  The evil weed, indeed. 

These are the early days of tobacco.  Perhaps I’ll take the story a little further next time.  Do you know about George Washington, the unsuccessful tobacco farmer?
