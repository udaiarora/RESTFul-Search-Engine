September 17, 2003

Desert cities photo exhibit displays sustainability

Yazed, Tunis, Fez, Sana'a, Antofagasta, Coober Pedy, Jerusalem, Cairo and Phoenix. Though spread throughout the world, these desert cities all share a common thread as a link to the future.

John Meunier, former dean of the College of Architecture and Environmental Design, says the cities represent harbingers of the world's future - if desert cities can be built and run to be sustainable, then there is hope for civilization.

Meunier has spent the last year traveling to desert cities in the Middle East, Africa, China and India to learn how ancient desert cities have managed to survive - and what they have done wrong - for a proposed four-part PBS series titled "Desert Cities: Surviving Into the 21st Century?"

Meunier will exhibit more than 400 photographs from his journeys, titled "Deserts, Oases and Cities," Sept. 15 - 19 in the Gallery of Design in the College of Architecture & Environmental Design. The exhibit is a preview of the PBS series, which he anticipates being on the air in 2005 - 06.

"City populations are exploding," Meunier says. "By the third decade of the 21st century, more than two-thirds of the world's population will live in cities. A significant number of these cities are in the desert, an unforgiving environment where mistakes have immediate consequences.

"We will look at what must be done to meet the challenge of making such unprecedented growth in desert cities sustainable."

The four programs, which Meunier will host, are subtitled "earth, air, fire and water" - four themes that are "critical for the existence of urban environments across the planet," he says.

Among his stops, Meunier visited Yemen, Australia, Chile and Peru, all countries with arid lands.

Meunier took video and still photographs, identifying places and people that will be incorporated into the PBS series.

He is working with Leo Eaton, who has produced numerous programs for PBS, including "In Search of Ancient Ireland," "Conquistadors" and "Classic American Cars of Cuba."

They will retrace Meunier's steps for in-depth filming, and Meunier said they also hope to visit Iraq and several other areas such as Western China and the communities on the southern edge of the Sahara in Mali.

Meunier and Eaton now are securing funding for the series, and planning the production schedule. KAET-TV/Channel 8 will be the primary liaison between the project and PBS.

While Meunier found many reasons to be optimistic about the future of desert cities on his research trip, he also found reasons to be alarmed.

The ancient desert cities were compact, with an average of 35,000 people per square mile, Meunier said. "The average density in Phoenix is 2,700 people per square mile. Phoenix has 1,000 square miles, with a population of 3 million. The population forecast is for 9 to 28 million people in 50 years.

"We are the model for the rest of the world, and we are currently modeling an irresponsible way of life where we consume huge quantities of non-renewable resources and attempt to dominate nature instead of treating it as a beautiful partner in our lives."
