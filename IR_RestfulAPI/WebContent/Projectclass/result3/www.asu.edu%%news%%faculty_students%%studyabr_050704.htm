May 7, 2004

Record number of ASU students win awards to study abroad

More ASU students than ever before will be studying abroad next year on prestigious national scholarships. A record 12 undergraduates have won National Security Education Program (NSEP) awards to study overseas, more than any other university in the country and double the number of ASU winners in any previous year.

In addition, two more students have received Fulbright awards for a year of graduate study and research abroad, bringing the total number of Fulbrights to seven so far this spring.

The NSEP awards provide up to $20,000 for a year's study in countries outside Western Europe, Australia and New Zealand. The aim is to build a critical base of future leaders who understand less familiar languages and cultures, have cultivated international relationships and can become integrally involved in global issues.

Five students will study in Asia, four in Russia and Eastern Europe and three in the Middle East. All will be studying at a foreign university and working on a research or service project or internship.

Going to China will be Natalie Grant, junior in political science and religious studies; Diana Chu, sophomore in political science and history; and Nan Wu, biology senior. George Weihing, junior in accounting and Japanese, will study in Japan, while Rekha Nair, junior in biochemistry and political science, will go to India.

Studying in Egypt will be David Finkelstein, senior in economics and political science; Sofia Khera, biology sophomore at the West campus; and Rebecca Webb, senior in religious studies and English. Other awardees are economics junior Maria Santiago and industrial engineering junior Gina Dumkrieger, both going to Serbia; history junior Ian Kushner, going to Uzbekistan, and Russian language senior Brad Griffin, studying in Kazakhstan.

Fulbright winners are Wing-Yan Choi, finance senior who will study international finance at the Hong Kong University of Science and Technology, and Jessica Thompson, anthropology doctoral student who will analyze fossil bones at Blombos Cave in South Africa.

Choi's award is especially notable because the Hong Kong Fulbright is typically given only to a graduate student. She also was the only American undergraduate chosen to attend a recent Forum for American-Chinese Exchange at Stanford and Beijing. Choi will research the Hong Kong stock market, in an extension of her honors thesis at Barrett Honors College.

Thompson will use new research techniques pioneered by ASU Professor Curtis Marean to analyze butchery marks on fossil animal bones, to provide crucial information on the emergence of modern humans during the Middle Stone Age.
