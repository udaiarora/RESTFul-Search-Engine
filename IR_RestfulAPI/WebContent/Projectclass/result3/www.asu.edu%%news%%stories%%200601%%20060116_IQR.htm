January 16, 2006

Qualitative research institute builds international reputation

Andrew Bennett, who teaches courses on the American foreign policy process at Georgetown University and has advised presidential candidates on foreign policy, made a startling discovery a few years ago about qualitative research methodologies in political science. His survey of academic journal articles showed that more than half of the authors used qualitative research methods, or a combination that incorporated qualitative research methods, yet almost no university political science department required students to take such a methodology course.

"With that information, we quickly realized that there was a gap that needed to be filled," says ASU's Colin Elman, who with Bennett helped organize the Institute for Qualitative Research Methods, now in its fifth year at ASU.

Bennett, an associate professor and the director of graduate studies in the department of government at Georgetown University, said the institute allows top scholars in the field to teach qualitative research methods while having the opportunity to talk face-to-face with each other.

At this year's institute, held Jan. 3 - 13, there were 28 instructors from across the country, including several of the leading qualitative methodologists in political science, says Elman, an assistant professor in ASU's department of political science.

This year's institute featured 105 student attendees from universities across America, including Berkeley, Chicago, Columbia, Harvard, Princeton, and Yale, as well representatives from the Netherlands, Norway, Germany, the United Kingdom and China. They engaged in research design discussions on topics ranging from international relations to environmental policy, from trade to terrorism, and from ethnicity and minorities to comparative political economy.

There are three main research approaches in political science, Bennett says, including statistical analysis, formal modeling (also known as game theory) and qualitative research methods that study a phenomenon in depth.

Bennett gave an example to illustrate the three approaches.

"If you want to understand terrorism or how to fight insurgency in Iraq, for example, you could use logic and game theory to assess what strategies would best deter terrorists," he says. "You could use statistics to analyze the numbers and kinds of attacks on American soldiers. Or you could look in detail at historical cases of terrorism or counter-insurgency, and interview soldiers - and even terrorists - to find out what is happening on the ground. Qualitative researchers are the people who talk to soldiers and terrorists."

Two institute students are working on this exact topic, Bennett says. Victor Asal, an assistant professor at Rockefeller College, is researching why organizations adopt terrorist tactics, and Brendan Green, a doctoral student at Massachusetts Institute of Technology, is studying military innovation in counterinsurgency doctrine, which is directly relevant to U.S. policy in Iraq.

Henry Brady, one of the institute's core faculty and a professor of political science at the University of California , Berkeley , says qualitative researchers study why people engage in war, why terrorists do what they do, and other complicated phenomena. He notes that, because there is a growing realization that there are a lot of different ways to understand reality, it is important to train students in the qualitative research approach.

"And this is the institute where people are being trained in a scientific manner," Brady says.

Bennett says that ASU, with Elman's initiative in establishing the institute at the Tempe campus, is building a substantial reputation as a leader in qualitative methods.

The institute is sponsored by the Consortium for Qualitative Research Methods and ASU's Institute for Social Science Research in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, with additional support from the National Science Foundation.
