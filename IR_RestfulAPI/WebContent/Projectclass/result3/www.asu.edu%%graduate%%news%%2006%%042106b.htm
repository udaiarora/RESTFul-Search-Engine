Graduate Students Recognized for Their Teaching Excellence



The Graduate and Professional Student Association (GPSA) honored 23 graduate students for excellence in teaching at ASU, and two students from the School of Life Sciences received the Graduate Mentorship Award for their commitment to community involvement.

On March 22, GPSA gave out its largest number of Teaching Excellence Awards since the event's inception four years ago. GPSA acknowledges these outstanding graduate students for their academic contributions at the annual awards ceremony held during Graduate and Professional Student Appreciation Week.

"This is a chance for us to recognize the dedication and creativity of our student teachers and to highlight the important contributions that graduate and professional students provide to the ASU community," says Destiny Crider, GPSA's vice president of internal affairs, who chairs the award program along with Mary Aubin, GPSA's director of graduate research.

"This is just a small sample of the outstanding student teaching that is taking place on this campus. It's my wish to see the program continue to expand in the years to come," adds Aubin.

The recipients of the 2005-06 GPSA Teaching Excellence Award are: Ashish Amresh and Pushpak Karnick, Computer Science and Engineering; Christian Blackman, Business; Ginger Burrell, Family & Human Development; Aimee Burke, Social Work; Brandon Cleworth, Religious Studies; Elizabeth Driver, Theater; Nicole Engelke, Math; Amy Greer, School of Life Sciences; Debi Johnson, Fine Arts; Shumin Lin, Music; Kurt Lindemann, Communication; Nicole Manus, Dance; Molly Meneely, English; Justin Merry, School of Life Science; Lesley Poteet, Language & Literature (French); Charlene Roach, School of Public Affairs; Sara Sanabia, Theater; Jill Schiefelbein, Communication; Jennessa Shapiro, Psychology; Lynette Thompson, Education; Robert Trowbridge, Bioengineering; Olena Tsurska, English.

Nate Morehouse and Jon Davis from the School of Life Sciences were presented with the Graduate Mentorship Award for the development of their Graduate Partners in Science Education (GPSE) initiative, a K-12 community outreach program that provides mentoring to 45 local middle school students from underrepresented groups and economically challenged backgrounds to encourage their enthusiasm in the sciences.

Graduate teaching assistants and faculty associates were nominated by students in their classrooms and the selection process included classroom observations by a committee of seven graduate student peer reviewers. This year, GPSA received a total number of 75 nominated teaching assistants and graduate teaching instructors.

Doctoral student Charlene Roach, who received a Teaching Excellence Award for an organizational behavior course she taught over the summer, says that she is truly honored for this distinction. She also credits her experience in the Preparing Future Faculty program offered by the Division of Graduate Studies for preparing her to teach in the classroom.

"The PFF program provided me with the practical classroom training that I needed to start teaching," Roach says. "When your students nominate you, you feel like you're doing something right. It shows me that my students appreciate my quality of teaching and that I am growing as an educator. Receiving this award has been very encouraging."
