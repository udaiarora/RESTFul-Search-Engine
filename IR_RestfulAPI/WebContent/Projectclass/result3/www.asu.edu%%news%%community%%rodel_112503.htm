November 25, 2003

W. P. Carey School goes back to high school to prevent dropouts

When the W. P. Carey School of Business launched the Rodel Community Scholars initiative in 2001, the mission was ambitious: to develop civic leadership in some of ASU's top business students, and to address the conditions that drive Arizona's high school dropout problem.

Now in its third year, Rodel is already paying dividends. The lives of some 310 high school students and their families have been impacted, as well as lives of the 56 Rodel Scholars. The program has expanded from the original three high schools to involve a total of six, including one on the Salt River Pima Maricopa Indian Community, and the business plans developed by the teams promise to affect many students outside the target populations. This year Rodel also moved its focus to campus, as four W. P. Carey MBA students apply their business acumen to the issues that contribute to academic success in the university setting.

It is the business approach that sets Rodel apart, and the program's mission fulfills the school's new motto: "doing good while doing well."

"We asked ourselves how we could develop in undergraduate students the skills and values that would make them want to be civic leaders as well as successful business people," said Rodel Director Raul Cardenas. "We grounded the students in what they know - business. They look at schools as business operations and students as customers."

The approach has yielded business plans for academic retention in Camelback, Tolleson, Chandler and Bourgade High Schools. Teams of Rodel Scholars continue to build upon the plans developed for Camelback and Tolleson, and implementation is under way in Chandler and Bourgade. The plan in each school is different, reflecting the varying needs the Rodel Scholars found in those communities. This year, teams are also working at La Joya Community High School in the Tolleson Union High School District and on the Salt River Pima Maricopa Community at Desert Eagle Secondary School.

At Tolleson High School, for example, the Rodel Scholars determined that awareness, family support and financial concerns were some of the biggest challenges facing students - even those with the talent and work ethic to achieve. The Tolleson plan includes College 101, a course designed to outline the steps needed to be ready to go to college, Tolleson Outreach to Parents (TOP), which builds family support for higher education, and eventually a scholarship program. In response, Tolleson teachers built a course called Rodel math, which compacts two years of college-required math into one, allowing students whose plans have expanded to catch up in time to apply to a university.

As Cardenas puts it, "at a time when these kids are struggling to stay in school, the Rodel Scholars supply the answer to the question why."
