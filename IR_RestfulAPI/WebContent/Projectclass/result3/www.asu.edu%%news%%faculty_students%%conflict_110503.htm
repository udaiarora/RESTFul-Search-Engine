November 5, 2003

ASU student introduces issues of religion, conflict to bright high school scholars

As anyone who reads the newspapers knows, the world has become a very complicated place, with thorny issues of religious and cultural conflict grabbing center stage in the world theater.

Many adult Americans are confused by the complex social issues involved in this conflict - and if responsible adults are mystified, what about the children? The events occurring today will surely play a major role in shaping the world they will inherit.

With this in mind, ASU Philosophy and Religious Studies major Eric Lind has undertaken a project to put sophisticated learning about contemporary religious attitudes and conflict into the hands of leading high school students, in the hope of beginning a chain reaction of learning and understanding.

With the sponsorship of ASU's Scholar-Citizen program in partnership with the Tempe Union High School District's Gifted Education Program, Lind organized "Thinking About Religion in America: Encountering Islam," a day-long conference for honors students from the district. The conference and workshop, held Oct. 25, featured talks by three prominent ASU scholars in the area, a film presentation and small group workshop discussion sessions.

"I have a number of reasons for doing this," says Lind. "I want to introduce the discipline of religious studies to sophisticated high school students, because it's outside their normal curriculum, but it really has a lot to offer in understanding the world around us. Too many people aren't aware how religious attitudes and beliefs shape behavior.

"I also want students to grapple with the general relationship of religion and conflict in the modern world, and in particular to get them to think about understanding religious diversity in America.

"On a broader scale, I also foresee this project as helping to build a partnership between the discipline of religious studies and secondary education, hopefully integrating the academic study of religion into the high school curriculum."

Founded by the college in 1997 following an ambitious suggestion from students, the Scholar-Citizen Grant Program supports projects by ASU undergraduates that apply academic learning to community service work. Proposals are solicited annually and are judged on the basis of their academic validity, community need and outside community participation. Three proposals were selected for funding in 2003.

Hathaway, with the College of Liberal Arts & Sciences, can be reached at (480) 965-6375 or (hathaway@asu.edu).
