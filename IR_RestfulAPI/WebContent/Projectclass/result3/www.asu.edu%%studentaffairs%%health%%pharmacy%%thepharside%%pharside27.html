Get on the Balance!

As the fourth week of the 2004/05 school year winds to a close, students are adapting to their new lives on and around the ASU campus.  With a fresh start also comes the fast and sometimes frantic lifestyle of a college student.  With little or no time between classes, work and extracurricular activities students become stressed and are required to budget their time.  This stressful lifestyle often leads to poor eating habits, usually in the form of fast or junk food.  With the increased intake of these high calorie and high fat foods, students begin to put on excess pounds, sometimes referred to as the freshmen fifteen.  Don’t panic, life has its ups and downs and this one can be rectified.

            First you need to start with some good habits.  Start off your day with a good breakfast, this can help lower your appetite throughout your day.  Drink plenty of water during your day and before meals, this too will help to control your appetite.  Eat what you like, just cut down on the portions.  For instance, one serving of meat is the same size as a deck of playing cards.  Eating foods that you dislike will discourage you from keeping up your good habits.  Sit down to eat and train yourself to eat slowly.  Eating slowly gives your body time to realize that it has all it needs and feel full.  Exercise. Exercise, Exercise!  I can’t stress this enough, it is so important to get out and get mobile.  After sitting in class or in your office all day you should feel like getting outside and enjoying the fresh air.  Exercising aerobically for at least twenty minutes a day can get you on your way to feeling less tired, alert, and trimming up.  Some good aerobic exercises are:  Riding a bike, swimming, running, brisk walk, working a punching bag, basketball.  Whatever it is that you enjoy is the right exercise for you.

            There are a lot of diet programs, special diets, and quick fix diet pills.  You might find yourself asking which is right for you and will it really work.  Here’s what to look for in a proper diet program:   

  • Diets that cut out high fats and sugars, while substituting with complex carbohydrates (vegetables, breads and pastas – especially whole grain).
  • Diets that for proper nutrition by supplying essential vitamins and minerals.
  • Diets based on at least a 1,100-calorie diet.
  • Diets that allow for a variety of foods.
  • Diets that encourage new exercises and foods.

There are some very important, but often overlooked, details about dieting.  Diets that stress only one food do not really work.  You usually end up depleting yourself of important vitamins and minerals and usually end up binging and gaining more weight than you lost.  There is no quick fix for a weight problem.  You simply cannot take a pill and watch the weight just melt away.  If this were true, one of the major pharmaceutical companies would own the rights and you would be paying a lot of money to have it.  Beware the quick fix products, such as TrimSpaâ, Hydroxycutâ, Xenadrineâ, and others like them.  If you look closely at their labels there will usually be a statement similar to this.

“For best results, combine this product with a low-fat, calorie-reduced diet and exercise regularly.”

Well, in my opinion, if I were already doing that, then I would be eating right, in good shape, healthy, and I wouldn’t need their product.  Another potential problem is that these supplements do not have to undergo the same rigorous safety and efficacy trials that today’s prescription drugs do to meet Food and Drug Administration standards.  What that means is you really don’t know what you are getting in that $35.99 bottle of “Get thin and Ripped”, it could just be vitamin C or cornstarch packed into those capsules.  Let’s look at a couple of examples so you don’t think I am crazy.  The Federal trade Commission has recently filed a suit against the manufacturers of TrimSpaâ.  The suit claims that TrimSpa, an appetite suppressant, does not actually contain the ingredient required for the appetite suppression.  While the product does contain the parent ingredient, Hoodia gordonii, it lacks the substrate that actually works as the suppressant.  Many of today’s supplements are now labeled “ephedra free”; because of the health problems associated it.  Ephedra acted as a stimulant to boost a person’s metabolic rate and help increase the “burning” of fat.  Soon it became evident that ephedra was causing cardiac and other health related problems, which prompted it to be pulled from shelves.  Supplement companies are now using a chemical cousin of ephedra called synephrine to act as a metabolic stimulant.  It is derived from a plant called Citrus aurantium otherwise known as bitter orange.  A recent study conducted in dogs using synephrine suggests that it might act to increase metabolism in a tissue known as brown fat.  The only problem is that adult humans have very little if any brown fat tissue in their body.

In conclusion I would like to reiterate that there is no quick fix to losing weight.  The best way to control weight gain is to catch it before it starts to get out of control.  Eat a well balanced diet of fruits, vegetable, meats and poultry, breads, pastas and grains.  Eat proper portions, we’re not children anymore and don’t have to belong to the “clean plate club”.  Don’t starve yourself, it doesn’t work, it just comes back greater and faster.  Exercise, Exercise, Exercise!  Nothing beats physical activity to burn fat, relax the body, and oxygenate those hard working brain cells.  Keeping a healthy balance between eating, exercising, and rest will keep your body physically fit in the long run.  Remember, it’s a matter of the calories that you take in balanced against the calories that you expend.  

Jordan Cottam – Pharm. D. candidate 9/04

What is Chlamydia and What’s the Big Deal?

702,093 cases of chlamydia were reported to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). However, it is estimated that 3 million cases actually occurred and as many as 1 in 10 adolescent females test positive for chlamydia.

Chlamydia is a common and curable infection caused by the bacteria Chlamydia trachomatis. The bacteria target the cells of the mucous membranes including: the surfaces of the urethra, vagina, cervix and endometrium, the fallopian tubes, the anus and rectum, the lining of the eyelid, and less commonly, the throat.  Chlamydia is spread though sexual contact between people. And an already infected person can spread the disease from one area of their body to another (e.g. genital area to throat). 

Most women are asymptomatic, but if symptoms are present they may be minor. The typical symptoms for women include: nausea or fever, vaginal discharge, burning sensation during urination, lower abdominal and lower back pain, pain during intercourse and bleeding between menstrual periods.

Men may be asymptomatic or symptoms may be minor. When symptomatic, men may experience one or more of the following: Pus (thick yellow-white fluid) or watery or milky discharge from the penis, pain or burning during urination, pain or swelling of the testicles.  Abstinence (not having sexual intercourse) and barrier methods (i.e. condoms) are the best ways to minimize the risk of becoming infected with chlamydia.

You may be thinking, “It will go away on it’s own, no one has to know”. WRONG.  What are some of the complications of untreated Chlamydia?  In women, Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) is a serious complication. PID is a serious infection of a woman's reproductive organs. Left untreated, PID can cause infertility.  Cystitis (inflammation of the urinary bladder) can also occur in women.  In men, prostatitis (inflammation of the prostate gland), urethral scarring and infertility.

It is usually easy to treat with simple antibiotics including doxycycline or azithromycin.  But don’t use what you may have “leftover” in your medicine cabinet; the dosing is specific to chlamydia.  See your doctor if you think there may be a problem.  You may be thinking, “When can I have sexual intercourse again?”  Usually when there are no signs of infection, confirmed by a culture, so best to get an OK from the clinician. 

James Waara, Pharm. D. candidate 9/04

 

Exercise Induced Asthma

The idea of early morning exercise is horrific to some and fundamental to others.  Joggers and walkers can be seen huffing and puffing down the empty morning streets. To the romantic part of our souls, this may seem exhilarating and raw.  But the asthmatic inside some of us may be coughing and hacking up phlegm.  So what are the typical signs and symptoms of exercise induced asthma (EIA)?  Coughing during and after exercise, wheezing after exercise, difficulty breathing and chest tightness after exercise are the most common symptoms. 

Exercise induced asthma develops when vigorous physical activity triggers airway narrowing in people who have heightened bronchial reactivity.  Exercise causes the tiny muscles surrounding bronchioles to twitch, thus restricting air flow. The problem is made worse by cold, dry air. A warm, humid environment, such as a heated swimming pool may be less aggravating to asthma symptoms. The moist air surrounding swimming pools that make this the sport of choice for many people with EIA.

The good news is that exercise induced asthma is a reversible airway problem. Many people can manage it by simply avoiding cold-air exercising, warming up thoroughly (15 minutes or more), and performing high intensity portions of workouts only after being warmed up.  Medications, such as albuterol, can also be used before a workout to help open the airways that become constricted. Two puffs 15 to 30 minutes before you workout, and it can make all the difference in the world.  So fear not, your good intentions of starting to exercise do not need to be sidelined by that nagging cough.  Your doctor or pharmacist are good sources of information about exercise induced asthma and the medications used to treat the athlete in you.
