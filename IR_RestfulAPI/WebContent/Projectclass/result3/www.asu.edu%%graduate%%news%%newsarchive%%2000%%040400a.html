AN ASU FIRST: DOCTORAL STUDENTS OFFERED PROGRAM
FOR NON-ACADEMIC CAREER PREPARATION

Non-academic employers and doctoral recipients seeking non-academic careers will benefit from a new Arizona State University program that prepares participating students specifically for non-academic careers. The Preparing Future Professionals (PFP) program will formally begin next fall as the first of its kind in the nation.

“The PFP program recognizes that most graduates take leadership, research and professional positions outside of the academy,” said Bianca L. Bernstein, Dean of the ASU Graduate College. “Launching the nation's first comprehensive program to prepare doctoral students more purposefully for a range of settings reflects ASU's commitment to excellence and innovation in graduate education.”

The PFP program will provide training, information, skills and hands-on experience to graduate students through their interaction with faculty and staff, administrators, business and industry representatives, according to Graduate College Program Coordinator Robin Lattin. ASU expects the program to serve as a model for other institutions seeking to bring about similar change.

Preparing Future Professionals parallels ASU's successful Preparing Future Faculty (PFF) program. The PFF program has since 1994 provided participating students with innovative preparation for careers as college professors.

Gregory Neidert, professor in the Department of Psychology at ASU and CEO of World of Work, Inc., said, “Graduate education needs to acknowledge and respond to the ever changing demands of the students and the marketplace. Not all graduate students will seek or find academic appointments after graduation. Therefore, those progressive institutions that provide venues for exploring options outside of academia truly serve their students. Our Graduate College is such an institution. It acknowledges this reality and offers our students a fantastic opportunity in the PFP seminars. Those individuals who take advantage of this program increase their career opportunities and potential for success, both inside and outside of academia.”

In order to help graduate students prepare for non-academic careers, PFP will provide:

  • Seminars, workshops and presentations designed to provide information, instruction and training related to a range of issues and realities regarding non-academic careers;
  • Non-academic career opportunities related to students' fields of study;
  • Examples of how past Ph.D. recipients successfully pursued non-academic career paths;
  • Information on cultural norms of various non-academic work arenas, how to construct resumes for non-academic positions, and preparing effective strategies for non-academic career pursuits;
  • Projects to promote professional development, through field placements;
  • Opportunities for students to develop networking skills and build networks of professional relationships;
  • Opportunities to learn about current placement realities for students in their program of study; and
  • Development in research capacities.

ASU doctoral students Kristin Hibler and Kelly Maxwell view the arrival of PFP as a relevant program.

“Clearly there is a need for universities to recognize and validate those of us who are interested in applying our skills outside of the academy,” said Hibler, a PFP pilot program participant who will finish work on a Ph.D. in intercultural communication in May. “PFP does just that. It also provides a forum where like-minded doctoral candidates can get together and share concerns. I am open to exploring careers such as diversity training, marketing to culturally diverse audiences, intercultural training or consulting for educational institutions.”

Maxwell, a doctoral candidate in Educational Leadership and Policy Studies and the coordinator of the Voices of Discovery Program at ASU, said, “The Preparing Future Professionals pilot program has been a great opportunity for me to consider all the possibilities that exist beyond graduate school. The goal-setting workshop offered me the opportunity to reflect on my past work and school experiences, to identify what is important for my professional life, what I value personally and how I can incorporate those values into a meaningful career.”

Skill building is a central focus of the program as well, Maxwell said, specifically in recent resumü and interviewing workshops. “The PFP program allowed me the opportunity to interact with others who were considering alternative careers outside of academe,” she said. “We have been able to share our ideas and concerns and provide support for one another as we begin the job search process.”

Lattin said, “A critical goal of PFP is to provide non-academic employers with an understanding about the wealth of skills that doctoral graduates are capable of contributing to their places of employment. Employers will play a critical role in helping to develop a pool of highly-qualified and skilled potential employees, capable of meeting the needs of various non-academic career milieus.”

Employers from public and private, large and small businesses are participating in PFP's educational forums, career development panel presentations, graduate career development initiatives, and offering internships, apprenticeships, job shadowing opportunities and other on-site learning activities.
