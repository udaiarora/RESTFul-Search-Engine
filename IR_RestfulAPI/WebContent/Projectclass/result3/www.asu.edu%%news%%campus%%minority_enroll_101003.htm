October 10, 2003

Minority student enrollment continues to climb at ASU

Minority students enrolled in record numbers this fall, surpassing the overall enrollment increase. Minority enrollment at all three campuses has hit an all-time high of 22 percent of the total student body, up from 10 percent in 1985 and 15.5 percent just 10 years ago.

Hispanics make up the largest group at 6,649, an increase of 10.4 percent over last year. The highest growth rate was in the number of African Americans, at 2,005, a 13.4 percent jump. A record 1,276 American Indians and 2,757 Asian Americans enrolled, both up about 9 percent.

Several years ago ASU stepped up recruitment in target high schools and launched intensive early outreach efforts, bringing students to campus as early as eighth grade. This year the university offered more financial aid, translated additional materials into Spanish and made numerous community presentations, trying to increase access for first-generation and minority students.

ASU also implemented a system to keep track of these students as they progress through high school, maintaining continuous contact with them and their parents. As a result, a record 30 percent of first-time ASU freshmen from Arizona are minority students.

"We have been working on all of these programs for a number of years, and I think the fruits of these efforts are starting to pay off," says Ken Hollin,
starting to pay off," says Ken Hollin, assistant admissions director for student recruitment.

"Increases like these are the result of a collaborative effort of many departments around campus. Many long hours, hard work and solid commitment go into making our efforts successful."

In the midst of a 3.7 percent overall increase in ASU enrollment, the College of Nursing registered an eye-opening 19 percent upsurge. While the college has not yet increased the number of spots in the upper-division professional nursing program from 60 per semester at Main campus and 20 at ASU West, this January they will launch a program at ASU East with 20 - 40 additional students. The state universities have a goal of doubling the number of nursing graduates in five years.

"The number of pre-nursing students admitted was the highest we've ever seen," says Jean Stengel, director of student services. "People hear about the nursing shortage and they respond, as the economy turns south. We also do lots of outreach into the community colleges - we really work on that."

The Colleges of Nursing, Public Programs and Law enrolled the highest proportion of minority students of all the schools on campus.

The Barrett Honors College enrolled 2,696 students, including 812 freshmen. Most of this year's record class of 173 freshmen National Merit Scholars are in the college. High school grade-point average for this year's freshmen was 3.36, the same as last year, but slightly more were in the top 15 percent of their class.

Psychology is the most popular major among all students, with biology, political science and English majors also making up a large portion of the enrollment in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences. In the College of Engineering, more than one quarter are computer science and engineering majors, and in the W. P. Carey School of Business, the most popular majors are finance and marketing. Journalism is the favorite major in the College of Public Programs.
