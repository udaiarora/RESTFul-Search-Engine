May 4, 2004

Research targets cyber attack safeguards for computers

Sifting through your e-mail, you see a note from a trusted friend 'saying hi' with an attachment. You open up the attachment, only to see that the note was not from your friend, but the latest computer virus.

Most savvy computer users have the know-how to protect their computers from would-be hackers, but not all techniques can totally block all attacks.

Industrial Engineering Professor Nong Ye wants to design a system that is proactive and can detect an intrusion before the cyber attack takes place.

"You need to have a second layer of defense which is intrusion detection," says Ye. "This project works on a new approach, which I call a cyber signal detection approach."

Ye has received an 18 month, $800,000 award from the Advanced Research and Development Activity (ARDA) of the Intelligence Community (IC)/Air Force Research Laboratory, for conducting advanced research and development. ASU will subcontract with antiviral software giant Symantec and communications leader AT&T to leverage the research toward commercial applications.

Most commercially available software attempts to safeguard against a cyber attack by one of two methods - signature recognition or anomaly detection.

The first method is reactive, in that it can only detect a known attack, so you have to know the signature.

"If there is a new attack coming out every time, you don't know their signature until it happens," says Ye.

In anomaly detection, the software builds a profile of normal computer use. When a large deviation from normal activity occurs, the software signals that an attack may be occurring. Trying to detect the lurker becomes more difficult the more traffic a network has.

The first phase of Ye's research will utilize a signal processing approach to understand the attack signals.

"First, we have to discover the characteristics of the cyber attack signal and cyber noise," says Ye. "Second, I want to have a signal detection model that combines the characteristics of both cyber signal and noise into signal detection to achieve a level of accuracy you see in systems like radar detection."
