October 17, 2006

Award-winning ?HORIZON' celebrates 25 years

On Oct. 19, the public affairs program that has chronicled Arizona 's political history for a quarter-century is making history of its own, as one of the longest-running local TV programs in the state.

?HORIZON? began in 1981, with a promise to present in-depth analysis and insightful discussion on the issues that would affect the everyday lives of Arizonans. After reporting on six governors, an impeachment, 25 legislative sessions, a multitude of primary and general elections and more than 10,000 stories, the mission has remained unchanged. Each night, ?HORIZON? goes beyond headlines and sound bites to present the information Arizonans need to better understand politics, consumer affairs, the environment, business, health concerns, and social and legal issues.

?HORIZON? airs Monday through Friday at 7 p.m. on Eight/KAET-TV. A one-hour special commemorating the show's anniversary will air at 7 p.m., Oct. 19.

?The milestone is significant,? says Michael Philipsen, news director for ?HORIZON.? ?But even more important is the access we provide to government. Each night, the state's political officials and decision makers discuss, and explain, the issues. If you watch the show regularly, we can help you keep your lawmakers accountable.?

A citizen's access to government was at the show's beginnings. In 1981, the nation was about to nominate its first woman to the U.S. Supreme Court: Arizona 's own Sandra Day O'Connor. Years before C-Span and cable TV's continuous coverage, ?HORIZON? stepped up to offer gavel-to-gavel coverage of the confirmation hearings.

One month later, ?HORIZON? broadcast its first program from a small building on ASU's Tempe campus. The Friday edition of the show was patterned after PBS' ?Washington Week in Review,? with local journalists offering context and perspective on the week's top stories. The remaining shows followed a basic format: long-form interviewing focusing on specific subjects.

Sitting behind the desk as host since the very first broadcast is Phoenix attorney Michael Grant. Grant's accomplishments reach far beyond the television studio. He received his undergraduate ? summa cum laude ? and law degrees from ASU. He was an assistant Arizona attorney general for three years before going into private practice and is with Gallagher and Kennedy in Phoenix .

Before ?HORIZON,? Grant worked as an investigative reporter for a local radio station while attending law school.

Viewers will note a few changes after the historic Oct. 19 broadcast. A newly renovated set will be unveiled, along with a new logo and program graphics. The companion Web site also will be relaunched with the addition of daily online video and podcasts.

?Encouraging civic participation is one of our core missions,? says Greg Giczi, Eight's general manager. ? ?HORIZON' has always provided a forum where lawmakers and citizens work together for a better future for our state. Everyone who has been a part of the production over the past 25 years has much to be proud of.?
