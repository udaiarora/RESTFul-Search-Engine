December 12, 2003

Southwest Borderlands Initiative brings new talent to Valley

So far eight faculty members have been recruited to share their innovative and global insights with ASU's academic community as part of the Southwest Borderlands Initiative. ASU announced the initiative in 2001 as a long-term faculty appointment plan with two primary objectives: strengthening existing ASU scholarly and instructional resources on the Southwest with emphasis on the region along the United States-Mexico border, and enhancing institutional recruitment and retention efforts toward building a faculty fully reflective of the Southwest Borderlands' diversity.

Designed with broad-based campus input as a major ASU faculty-hiring plan, the continuing initiative seeks to integrate current ASU strengths in borderlands research with bold innovation in faculty recruitment.

Ramon Rivera-Servera, assistant professor in the Theater Department of the Katherine K. Herberger College of Fine Arts, hopes to increase awareness and knowledge of the wealth of Latino arts in the United States through his projects."One of the main projects I am currently developing for the ASU community is the 'Performance in the Borderlands' lecture series," he says. "It will bring nationally recognized scholars, critics and artists to the ASU campus for lectures, performances and workshops about the arts along the U.S.-Mexico border."
For Rivera-Servera, expression through the arts is one of the best forms of communication.

"I see the arts as an important site of futurity; a hopeful realm where identities, desires, and hopes are articulated, shared, and contested," says Rivera-Servera, who was an adjunct professor in the dance program at the University of Rochester 2002 - 03. "I think this quality artistic practice has been especially key to groups who often find themselves at the fringes of U.S. official culture."

ASU currently has a variety of programs that focus on different aspects of the Hispanic culture, which include Hispanic Research Center, Bilingual Review Press, Chicana/o Studies Department, Hispanic Mother-Daughter Program, Minority Engineering Program, Latin American Studies and ASU's Spanish department.
Cordelia Candelaria, chairwoman of ASU's Chicano/a Studies Department, says faculty participants in the SWB Initiative will help existing programs "flourish" and continue to serve the community.

"I think these people are going to enhance the wonderful foundation that we have in place and they will help us take it even farther," Candelaria says. "Latino faculty members as scholars are prized commodities. It's important to continue strong recruitment of Latino faculty and students in order to maintain and enhance this global studies perspective."

Another new hire, John Jota Leaños, serves as an assistant professor in the Chicano/a Studies Department in the College of Liberal Arts & Sciences. While obtaining his masters in fine arts (photography), Leaños concentrated on developing public art through computer-generated Digital Murals and through performance and art installation.

He plans to share some of this knowledge with the ASU community.

"I work with people to critically engage technology rather than passively accept its inevitability in our lives," says Leaños, whose work has been displayed in The Whitney Museum in New York and the San Francisco Arts Commission Gallery. "I see no better way to do this than through the production of art and culture. I hope to bring to students and the intellectual community a sense of urgency to be active in one's educational formation, pedagogical goals and political consciousness. This sense of urgency is desperately needed here."

In addition Rivera-Servera and Leaños, faculty members hired through the Southwest Borderlands Initiative include:

. Yolanda Lopez, served as a visiting artist/lecturer in the College of Liberal Arts & Sciences Department of Chicano/a Studies in the spring 2003.
. Sarah Amira de la Garza, hired 2003-04, serves as an associate professor of communication/theater in the College of Public Programs.
. Miguel Astor Aguilera, hired 2003-04, serves as an assistant professor in the College of Liberal Arts & Sciences Department of Religious Studies.
. Seline Szkupinski Quiroga, hired 2003-04, serves as an assistant professor in the College of Liberal Arts & Sciences Department of Chicano/a Studies.
. Daniel Ramirez, hired 2003-04, serves as assistant professor in the College of Liberal Arts & Sciences Department of Religious Studies.
. Alfredo Artiles will serve as a professor of curriculum and instruction in the College of Education for the 2004-05 academic year.

To learn more about ASU's Southwest Borderlands Initiative, visit the Web at (http://www.asu.edu/provost/swb/).
