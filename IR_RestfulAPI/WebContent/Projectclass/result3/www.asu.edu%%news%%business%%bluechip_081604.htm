August 17, 2004

Arizona's reputation as a retirees' state is mostly a myth

An analysis of 2000 Census data puts to rest the myth that Arizona, particularly the Greater Phoenix area, is populated by a mostly older age demographic, according to an article in the current issue of the Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast.

The most recent census figures show that the dominant characteristic of the metropolitan Phoenix population is youth," according to Lee McPheters, contributing editor of the Arizona Blue Chip. "For all age categories under 45, the Greater Phoenix region has a higher proportion than the nation as a whole. For all age categories over 45, the region has a lower proportion than the national average. Moreover, the fastest growing communities are those that attract young families. It is likely that the proportion of older residents in the metropolitan area will remain below the national average for the foreseeable future."

As economic development has become increasingly competitive, the view that Phoenix has a population older than average "has gone from a quaint confusion to a liability," McPheters said. "The source of the misconception likely lies in the very correct observation that Arizona is often listed as one of the 'best places' for retirement due to affordable housing and lifestyle amenities, including climate.

"However, a 'retirement' label may be a red flag to technology firms and others seeking to relocate where affordable costs are accompanied by an able and abundant labor force."

Based on 2000 Census data and compared to the rest of the nation, the Phoenix metropolitan area has:

. A higher proportion of children

. A higher proportion of persons under age 65

. A lower proportion of persons over age 65

. A median age below the national average

The national median age from the 2000 census was 35.3 years. Arizona 's median age was lower, at 34.2 years, and the metropolitan Phoenix area median age (represented by Maricopa County ) was even lower, at 33.0.

The city of Phoenix accounts for more than 40 percent of the population of the metropolitan area. The median age for Phoenix is nearly five years under the national average, at 30.7 years.

Elsewhere in the Arizona Blue Chip, Editor Tracy Clark surveyed the panel members on their assessment of the factors most likely to influence the Arizona economy in the next 12 months.

Factors most likely to influence the economy in a positive way were ranked as follows (in descending order): strong national economic growth, consumer spending, tourism, construction, rising interest rates, and more retirees. Potential negative influences cited were: rising interest rates and the recent drought.

"Interest rates are a positive factor because they are currently low - and a potential negative factor depending on how high they eventually go," noted Tracy Clark, editor of the Arizona Blue Chip .

The Bank One Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast includes the consensus forecast of economists from 19 groups and institutions. It is published by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center , an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute in the W. P. Carey School of Business at Arizona State University .
