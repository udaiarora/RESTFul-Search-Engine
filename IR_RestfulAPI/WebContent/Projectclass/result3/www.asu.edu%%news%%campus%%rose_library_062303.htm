June 23, 2003

Rose builds English law history library for ASU

As an English legal history scholar, ASU Law Professor Jonathan Rose faced a simple decision in using his stipend as the Willard H. Pedrick Distinguished Research Scholar. He took the opportunity and began assembling an English Legal History Library at the College of Law, filled with rare legal volumes.

In 2002, Rose, along with assistance from Associate Dean Victoria Trotta and her staff, acquired six folio volumes called the "Vulgate Edition of the Yearbooks," published in 1679 - 80 and including cases from the late 13th century to the early 16th century. The yearbooks compile cases from the royal courts, initially and primarily the Court of Common Pleas and could be thought of as the law reports of their day. The books focus on lawyers' arguments and the judge's questions, responses and other statements and inform the reader of legal procedure and the art of pleading and may have been compiled as a teaching tool for new lawyers. Thus, the yearbooks preserve the intellectual aspects of litigation rather than the specific facts of any one case.

Rose has sought these books for many years but was hampered by a lack of funding and the inability to find a complete set. Last fall six folio volumes were found at a San Francisco book dealer.

The second purchase for the collection was made a few months ago through another fortuitous circumstance. Rose's legal history endeavors had brought him a friendship with Victor Tunkel of the Faculty of Laws at Queen Mary and Westerfield College in London, England and the Secretary of the Selden Society, a leading association of English legal scholars. Tunkel is also an avid collector of legal history books but was moving to a new home with less library space and needed to reduce his holdings. Rose acquired about 75 volumes from Tunkel.

The selections include many books that could not be found elsewhere and range from a 1637 edition of "Cowell's Interpreter," an early law dictionary, and an 1807 century edition of "Bacon's Abridgement," a seven volume set covering cases from the 17th century through 19th Century, to "Legal Records and the Historian," another published in 1975, but no longer available from almost any source.

The latter volume was written by John H. Baker, Downing Professor of the Laws of England at St. Catharine's College at Cambridge University. Baker is considered the "Dean" of English legal scholars and presented a paper at the Second ASU College of Law Conference on Current Issues in Legal History held in conjunction with Professor Rose's Investiture as a Pedrick Scholar, last September.

"Professor Rose's generosity has brought a number of very significant titles to the library," she says. "We couldn't have purchased such books with our budget limitations. Moreover, some of the titles acquired with Rose's stipend are unique to the ASU Libraries' collections, so this collection has enhanced the university collection as a whole."

Both Rose and Trotta hope to continue building the collection and eventually establish an English Legal History Room in the Ross-Blakely Law Library.
