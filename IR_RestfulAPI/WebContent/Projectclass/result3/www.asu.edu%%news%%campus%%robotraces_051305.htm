May 13, 2005

Robot races test students’ knowledge

It’s off to the races for the robots built in ASU assistant professor Tom Sugar’s industrial engineering lab.

Students in Sugar’s lab build robots from scratch to compete in an end-of-the-semester tournament that tests the builders’ skills. This year, the competition called for the eight robots to dash around a racetrack-style course May 10 outside the Psychology Building.

Each group consists of five or six students, typically a mix of industrial and mechanical engineering majors. The students design and program the robots during the first half of the semester and work on building them during the second half, before the semester-ending tournament.

For this competition, the fastest robot earns its engineers a trophy – and respect. The catch: The robots have to guide themselves around the track.

To complete this task, students were provided with a kit with fundamental supplies needed to get their robots rolling. The kit includes a microprocessor, a power supply, a motor controller with two motors, a 12-volt rechargeable battery, a steering motor and four infrared sensors.

The sensors are the key to the race. Students must program the sensors to recognize and follow a stripe of black tape that runs around the center of the 20-by-30-foot track. For the students designing the robots, it’s a sensitive game of adjusting lines of code and experimenting with different sensor placements.

Precise attention to detail and real-world design experience is what Larry Duff, a machinist who works in the lab and manufactures the necessary parts, believes will give these students a leg up in the workplace.

“We treat the class just like the real world,” Duff says. “It gives the students a little bit of experience with how manufacturing is done.”

Many mechanical and industrial engineering students just out of college will have trouble succeeding right away because they have not had any practical experience, he says.

“Students in this lab will be able to get a job and take a design to a machinist, and they won’t be laughed at because they’ve made these designs before,” Duff says.

The students are not alone in their endeavors. Along with Sugar and Duff, students also work closely with lab manager Sean Dengler to ensure that their robots function properly.

Dengler has been running the Integrated Manufacturing Engineering Lab for seven years, and his experience and expertise is available to all of the students.

Still, Dengler says, the project is a challenge. The students have to work with a single gear ratio, as the robots have no transmission. The groups were responsible for finding vendors, and designs had to be submitted and resubmitted to machinists before they were approved and manufactured.

“These robots should probably be built in two semesters, but we’re doing it in one,” Dengler says. “It’s ‘boot camp’ automation.”

The student lab is just one aspect of Sugar’s research and development of new robotics at ASU. He is involved in a variety of projects, such as wearable robots to help rehabilitate stroke victims and developing autonomous mobile robots with advanced navigational capabilities.

In years past, the lab has consisted of a variety of robotic challenges like kicking a soccer ball, sumo wrestling and a “castle war,” where the robots had to detect and topple a target within another team’s fortress.
