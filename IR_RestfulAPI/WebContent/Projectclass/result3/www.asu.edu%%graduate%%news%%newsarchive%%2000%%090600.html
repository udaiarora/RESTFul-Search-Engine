NAVAJO STUDENT AT ASU RECEIVES FORD FOUNDATION PREDOCTORAL FELLOWSHIP FOR MINORITIES

Vicky Lomay, an Arizona State University graduate student from Page, has won the prestigious Ford Foundation Predoctoral Fellowship for Minorities. She is one of just 50 Ford Foundation Predoctoral Fellowship for Minorities winners, according to the National Research Council of the National Academies, the administrator of the award.

Lomay won an annual stipend of $14,000, an institutional award for tuition and fees, and three years of support and paid expenses to attend three conferences of Ford Fellows. The awards are made to graduate students who, in the judgment of the review panels, have demonstrated superior scholarship and show the greatest promise for future achievement as scholars, researchers and teachers at institutions of higher education.

A member of the Navajo tribe, Lomay’s maternal clan is Honaaghaani (One Who Walks Around One), and her paternal clan is Tsinaajinii (Black Streaked Wood People). She is the daughter of Jimmie R. and Mary Ann Tsinnijinnie.

Lomay is concurrently enrolled in the master of counseling and Ph.D. in counseling psychology programs, in the Division of Psychology in Education. She plans to complete the master’s degree program in May 2001, then begin doctoral studies. Lomay received her bachelor of arts degree in special education at ASU in 1997.

“As I progress further in my studies, I see a dearth of research on Native Americans,” Lomay said. “This includes all aspects of assessment, practice, theory and ethical considerations. My professional objective is to continue my research on Native American people through knowledge gained about counseling psychology. It is not only my goal to better understand aspects of Native American psychology but to also educate others about this area so they may be more effective counselors and psychologists.

“I have chosen the field of counseling psychology for the sole purpose of helping other Native Americans. I am interested in learning how and why my people do the things they do -- cognitively, developmentally and emotionally. Most importantly, I want to learn how to help when it is needed. Being Native American, more specifically Navajo, I am beginning to see a great need for counseling professionals who have the sensitivity and the proper education to work effectively with Native American populations.”

Until recently, many issues related to Native American mental health have been relatively ignored. Lomay’s research interests focus primarily on issues related to cultural/racial identity, more effective methods of prevention and intervention, theory development and unique considerations when working with Native Americans.

With more than 20 federally recognized tribal reservations in Arizona, Lomay is in close proximity to the people she plans to assist. ASU’s doctoral program has a nationally acclaimed reputation with an emphasis on multicultural issues. Educational opportunities at ASU include a diverse faculty, support services for Native Americans and excellent research facilities.

Vivian Ota Wang, an assistant professor of psychology in education at ASU, is Lomay’s advisor. “Vicky Lomay is a person whose sensitivity, coupled by her need to understand things in their proper context, gives her insight for understanding issues within a real world perspective,” Ota Wang said. “Her strength of character, integrity and commitment have shined through her own work as well as those with whom she has touched. Her natural talent of combining inquisitiveness and intellect with real world issues was recently highlighted when she presented some of her original research at the national Relevance of Assessment and Culture in Evaluation, R.A.C.E. 2000 meeting, this past January. There she discussed culturally responsive assessment when working with people who are Navajo. Vicky is truly committed to learning, helping and understanding and she is a student any professor would be privileged to be able to work and collaborate with.”
