MLA Style Guide Quick Reference

This quick reference should not be used as a style guide; it is only a reference tool to be used along with the most recent version of the MLA style guide as indicated below.

The Modern Language Association Handbook for Writers of Research Papers (MLA) must be followed for headings and subheadings, tables and figures, block quotations, in-text citations and references. However, regarding overall document considerations (i.e., margins, pagination, etc.) the Format Manual requirements supersede those of MLA. For more information, see Using Style Guides at www.asu.edu/graduate/format/UsingStlGds.html.

Headings/Subheadings (pp.  127-128)
No guidelines specified; consult committee. Make sure, however, to be consistent with the style and levels of headings throughout the document. While there are no specifications in the MLA Handbook, the MLA Style Manual (Gibaldi, J. The MLA Style Manual and Guide to Scholarly Publishing. 2nd ed. New York: Modern Language Association of America, 1998) suggests labeling unified sections of thought (i.e., chapters and subdivisions of chapters) with Arabic numerals, headings or both. Numbers alone are centered; headings, with or without numbers, appear flush left.

Block Quotations (pp. 81-83)
Use for quotations of more than 4 lines of text; double-space; indent an additional 1" (10 spaces) from left margin; no quotation marks. A parenthetical reference follows the quotation after end punctuation.

In-text Citation: Author Mentioned in Sentence (p. 241)
Cite the page number in parentheses immediately following the quotation, followed by end punctuation.

In-text Citation: Author Not Mentioned in Sentence (p. 241)
Cite the author's last name, followed by page number(s), in parentheses, followed by end punctuation.

Figures (pp. 136-137)
Label all types of illustrations (i.e., photographs, maps, graphs, charts, etc.) as figures (e.g., Figure or Fig.) underneath the corresponding figure, double-spaced and followed by an Arabic numeral and a title/caption.

Tables (pp. 134-135)
All tables should appear as close to corresponding text as possible. Use the label "Table" above the corresponding table, followed by an Arabic numeral (e.g., "Table 1") flush left. A title/caption should appear below the label, double-spaced, flush left, and in title case* (with hanging indentation, if necessary). Source of the table and/or notes appear below the actual table, double-spaced, and flush left (with hanging indentation, if necessary).

References

            General/Spacing (pp. 145-147)
Bibliography/works cited should be arranged alphabetically with hanging indentation (one-half inch) and double-spaced throughout.

          Books and Nonperiodical Publications (pp. 147-179)
List the following: (1) the full author name(s), with only the first author name in reverse order, followed by a period; (2) book title, which is italisized or underlined and in title case*, followed by a period; and (3) publication information, which includes city, followed by a colon, the publisher, followed by a comma, and the publication date, followed by a period.

Example:

Smith, Joseph and Jane Johnson. Title of the Book: When Titles Don't Make Sense. New York: Norton, 2004.

          Journal Articles (pp. 180-193)
List the following: (1) full author name(s) in reverse order, with only the first author name in reverse order, followed by a period; (2) article title followed by a period and in title case*, enclosed within quotes; (3) journal title in italics or underlined, followed by volume, year of publication (in parentheses), a colon, page numbers, and a period.

Example:

Shoemaker, Elizabeth. "The Title of the Journal Article: How to Name Articles for Maximum
     Readership." Journal of Useful Facts for Publishing 35 (2004): 45-57.




*The first letter of certain words within titles (e.g., initial words, important words, words with 4 or more letters, etc.) are always capitalized. See pp. 102-104 in the MLA Handbook for more information.
