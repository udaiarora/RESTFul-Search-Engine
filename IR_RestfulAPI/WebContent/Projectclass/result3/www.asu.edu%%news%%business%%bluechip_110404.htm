November 5, 2004

West maintains lead in sluggish U.S. job growth picture

The Western states have taken the lead in economic growth, but the overall pattern of slow growth in the rest of the states is creating a drag on the nation as a whole, according to an analysis in the current issue of the Western Blue Chip Economic Outlook.

“In the 12 months preceding August 2004, more than half of the states reported job growth of less than 1 percent,” reports Dawn McLaren, editor of the Western Blue Chip, which is a product of the Bank One Economic Outlook Center at ASU’s W.P. Carey School of Business. “Over the same period, the civilian labor force as a whole increased by 873,000 people, according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics’ Household Survey. The labor force participation rate, however, fell from 66.1 percent to 65.9 percent. This compares to 67.3 percent at its most recent peak in early 2000.

“While the Western states, for the most part, have returned to job growth, the question is how much the jobs are worth,” McLaren said. “While this information won’t be available for some time, we have personal income figures which were most recently benchmarked in September. From this we can gauge that growth in personal income fell considerably in 2001 and has not yet regained a strong footing.”

The Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast publishes the consensus forecasts of panels of economists in 10 Western states. Panelists are drawn from leading firms, universities and state agencies. The publication also features historical data for each state.

The Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast is published 10 times a year by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute at Arizona State University’s W. P. Carey School of Business. The cost is $110 for 10 print issues ($99 for e-mail subscription). For subscription information, call (800) 448-0432.
