September 15, 2005

Law students to teach for Constitution Day

ASU College of Law students and volunteers will teach the U.S. Constitution in 27 classes at three different schools Sept. 16 and the following week as part of Constitution Day.

Last year, Congress designated Sept. 17 — the day the Constitution was ratified in 1787 — for public schools receiving federal funds to deliver educational programming highlighting the Constitution. Because the Sept. 17 falls on a Saturday, many schools will offer programs on Friday, Sept. 16. Others will provide it the following week.

For two years now, second and third year law students at the ASU College of Law have been teaching constitutional rights at South Mountain High School as part of the Marshall-Brennan Fellowship Program. Named in honor of the late United States Supreme Court Justices Thurgood Marshall and William J. Brennan Jr., the program is designed for law school students to teach constitutional rights and responsibilities, including a special curriculum on the history and future of democracy and the right to vote.

The ASU College of Law was the fourth law school to adopt the program, which originated at the Washington College of Law at American University in Washington D.C. in 1999. The goal is to promote constitutional literacy in the hope that students will benefit from learning the many rights and responsibilities provided citizens under the U.S. Constitution. Marshall-Brennan Fellows teach in geographic areas where citizens don’t always participate or may feel disengaged from politics.

A total of 21 College of Law students and two volunteers will teach in 27 classrooms at three schools, South Mountain High School , the Sequoia School for the Deaf and Hard of Hearing and Madison Park School.
