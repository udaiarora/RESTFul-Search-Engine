September 26, 2003

Two-time U.S. poet laureate continues ASU Distinguished Visiting Writer Series

Poetry may be a quiet art, but some poets are undeniably stars, and command a lot of public attention. Billy Collins, in his own subtle, funny way, is one of those poets.

In an age when only a very small percentage of the reading public have ever bought a book of poetry, publishers fight publicly over the right to print Collins' strong-selling books. A recent story in the New York Times said matter-of-factly: "It can be argued that with his books selling briskly and his readings packing them in, Mr. Collins is the most popular poet in America."

The two-time US Poet Laureate will be in the Valley on October 3, reading from his work at the Scottsdale Center for the Arts. (For more event information, see the end of this release.)

Collin's reading is part of the Distinguished Visiting Writers Series, a major new reading series created by ASU's new Virginia G. Piper Center for Creative Writing. Presented in collaboration with the Scottsdale Center for the Arts and aimed at broadening the public reach of ASU's exceptional creative writing program, the series will bring visiting writers of the highest caliber to the Phoenix area.

Billy Collins recently served two terms as U.S. Poet Laureate from spring 2001 to spring 2003. As poet laureate, Collins' job was to raise the national consciousness to a greater appreciation of the reading and writing of poetry. He created the Poetry 180 program to promote the reading of daily poems in high schools across America. His work has appeared in The New Yorker, The Paris Review and The American Scholar. He is a distinguished professor of English at Lehman College, City University of New York, where he has taught for more than 30 years.

Collins is the author of seven books of poetry including, Sailing Around the Room (new and selected poems, 2001) Picnic, Lightning (1997), The Art of Drowning (1995) and Questions About Angels (1991, selected for the National Poetry Series), The Apple That Astonished Paris (1988), Video Poems (1980), and Poker Face(1977). He also has recorded a CD of his poetry, The Best Cigarette (1997).

Though there superlatives abound in reviews of Collins' work, many critics also cite his approachability, his humanity and the humor in his poetry. New York Times Reviewer Cynthia Magriel Wetzler wrote: "Billy Collins writes funny poems, poems in which a dog barks out a solo with the oboes in a Beethoven symphony or in which the writer spends the morning shoveling snow with Buddha. Luring his readers into the poem with humor, Mr. Collins leads them unwittingly into deeper, more serious places, a kind of journey from the familiar or quirky to unexpected territory, sometimes tender, often profound." Renowned poet and John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation President Edward Hirsch said of The Art of Drowning: "Billy Collins is an American original, a metaphysical poet with a funny bone and a sly questioning intelligence. He is an ironist of the void and his poems--witty, playful, and beautifully turned--bump up against the deepest human mysteries."

Collins has received fellowships from the New York Foundation for the Arts, The National Endowment for the Arts and the Guggenheim Foundation. He has also won a variety of awards from America's oldest and most prestigious poetry publication, Poetry magazine: the Bess Hokin Prize, the Frederick Bock Prize, the Oscar Blumenthal Prize, and the Levinson Prize. In 1992 he was chosen by the New York Public Library to serve as "Literary Lion."

Collins' reading will be held on Friday, October 3 at 7:30 p.m. at the Scottsdale Center for the Arts. Admission is $20. For more information on this and other lectures in the series, email clasevents@asu.edu <mailto:clasevents@asu.edu> or call 480-965-0051.

Future visiting writers in the inaugural 2003 series will include the novelist and Arizona native Janet Burroway on Thursday, October 16, C. D. Wright on Thursday, Feb. 12, and novelist Alexs Pate on Friday, April 9. Locations and ticket prices will vary.
