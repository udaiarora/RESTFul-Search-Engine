April 1, 2003

Conference focuses on recruitment of minority STEM students

Obtaining a degree in the field of science, technology, engineering or mathematics (STEM) is a challenging endeavor for many students, especially for those who are in the minority of the U.S. population.

On April 1, ASU experts in these research-related fields will gather at the ASU Memorial Union (MU) for the third annual Arizona Minority Graduate Education at Mountain States Alliance (MGE@MSA) Student Research Conference and Graduate Fair, in an effort to educate and mentor students interested in STEM studies.

ASU Regents Professor and Hispanic Research Center Director Gary Keller and Hispanic Research Center Projects Administrator Michael Sullivan say this event is a result of a study conducted 14 years ago. In the spring and summer of 1989, a group of universities reviewed the conditions of STEM programs at universities in the Southern Rocky Mountain states. During the fall of 1990, with a grant from the National Science Foundation (NSF) a model program addressing the problems of STEM underrepresentation at both the undergraduate and graduate levels was established. Since then, the Western Alliance to Expand Student Opportunities (WAESO) and its dedicated doctoral component, MGE@MSA, were established and continue to operate today.

Sullivan says the conference, which begins at 8 a.m., April 1, in the Arizona Room 207 of the MU, exemplifies the efforts being made by MGE@MSA and WAESO to recruit and retain STEM students in Arizona, New Mexico, Nevada, Utah, Colorado and Texas.

"On that day, almost 400 students will demonstrate their research potential and learn about the opportunities and skills necessary to complete doctoral studies in STEM," Sullivan says. "About six dozen faculty members will be on hand to give presentations and speak with underrepresented doctoral students. In doing so, they will join almost 200 previously trained faculty doctoral mentors who are now actively recruiting, retaining and graduating underrepresented doctoral students."

Included in the MGE@MSA conference is the third annual Arizona Faculty Doctoral Mentoring Institute, which serves as a centerpiece for addressing faculty decision-making and doctoral student development.

Antonio García, ASU associate professor in bioengineering, says this conference emphasizes the importance of learning rapid interdisciplinary communication as a key skill in the world of technology.

"Companies rely on individuals who can communicate their ideas within a team whose members have different scientific and technological backgrounds," Garcia said. "In the research arena, problems worthy of attention and resources know no disciplinary boundaries. In both worlds being able to assemble, work with and bring disciplinary expertise to achieve a goal through highly developed communication skills is highly valued."

Along with García, Sullivan and Keller, other speakers at the MGE@MSA conference will include ASU President Michael Crow; Armando A. Rodríguez, ASU associate professor of electrical engineering; Albert McHenry, dean of the College of Technology & Applied Sciences at ASU East; and Wilson A. Francisco, 2002 MGE@MSA Doctoral Mentor of the Year and assistant professor in the ASU chemistry & biochemistry department.

For information, call Sullivan with the Hispanic Research Center, at (480) 965-5388.
