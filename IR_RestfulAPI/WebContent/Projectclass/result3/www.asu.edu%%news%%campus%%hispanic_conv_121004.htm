December 10, 2004

Hispanic Convocation adds ethnic flair to ASU tradition

Everyone is invited to honor the class of 2004 at ASU’s Hispanic Convocation on Dec. 18.

This tradition celebrates 20 years of bringing a cultural and diverse experience for graduates and their families at ASU.

In 1984, the first ASU Hispanic Convocation was held at El Tiangus Plaza, located in the town of Guadalupe. María Elena Coronado, program coordinator, senior, of the Cesar E. Chavez Programs and Hispanic Convocation, says the convocation has taken on a life of its own.

“The first convocation had 34 students participating,” she says. “We now have an average of 300 students participating in the combined spring and winter ceremonies each year.”

The fall Hispanic Convocation is scheduled from 4 – 5:30 p.m., Dec. 18, at Wells Fargo Arena. Coronado says almost 80 students will participate in the ceremony, emceed by Telemundo television personality and ASU alumnus José Ronstadt.

“It’s a great way for these students to get their younger siblings to come and see these amazing young men and women who are graduating,” says Coronado, who has been coordinating the event for the past five years. “It’s terrific when younger people are exposed to this type of event, because it offers a glimpse into the academic successes at ASU.”

Mariachi ASU will be performing at the ceremony that also will feature special invited guests, including Congressman Ed Pastor and his wife, Verma, who will be presenting awards for the outstanding undergraduate and graduate students. The Pastors, who also are proud ASU alumni, have participated in the event for several years.

These awards are based on the students’ academic successes, community campus involvement and their abilities to overcome tremendous obstacles to obtain their degrees. The awards are sponsored by the Los Diablos and Hispanic Business Alumni chapters.

Not all Hispanic students participate in the convocation, but those who do say it’s nice to have the opportunity to celebrate their academic success in a cultural setting.

“I feel like I know these people that I’m graduating with,” says Luke Dorsett, who is graduating with a bachelor of arts in Chicana/o Studies.“It’s (Hispanic Convocation) more personal.”

Dorsett, the son of Delia Saenz, director of the Intergroup Relations Center, is applying to graduate schools to study either law or filmmaking.

Orietta Verdugo, who is earning a bachelor’s degree in industrial engineering, also is looking forward to achieving her career goals. She says the Hispanic Convocation is a great way to celebrate her academic achievement.

“I like the fact that the ceremony is bilingual, and I like the environment that it provides for the graduates and their families,” says Verdugo, who is the third person in her family to graduate from ASU, following her older sisters, Nitza Verdugo-London and Emily Verdugo-Aldrich.

“I have volunteered at the convocation for the past four years,” says Verdugo, who volunteered as a member Kappa Delta Chi sorority. “It’s nice to have a choice, and I think my family and friends will enjoy it.”

For more information on the Hispanic Convocation, call (480) 965-8889 or visit the Web site (www.asu.edu\ia\convocation).
