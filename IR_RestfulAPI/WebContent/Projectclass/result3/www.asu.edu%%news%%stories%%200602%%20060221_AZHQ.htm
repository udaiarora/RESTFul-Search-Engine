February 21, 2006

Network opens doors to health data

ASU researchers are taking a novel approach to creating access to community health information. The result is the nation's largest community health network, Arizona Health Query (AZHQ).

The AZHQ database, created by ASU's Center for Health Information & Research (CHIR), is working with health care groups throughout the state to consolidate existing health care data into a comprehensive data warehouse that follows patients across health systems over time. The goal is to create a comprehensive data set to give health care professionals and policy makers a more timely and complete look at the health of Arizona communities, and to provide a source of data that can be used by health care researchers, all while maintaining patient confidentiality.

"Say a community wants to know how many people under age 18 were treated for diabetes in the last year," says research professor Mary Rimsza, a physician and co-director of the center. "We provide that information - not just on their patients, but for all the other patients in the community who have received care at other hospitals in the community."

The database holds health information from more than 100 million billing records on more than 5 million individual patients from insurers, hospitals, community health centers, health departments and physician practices throughout Arizona. The advantage of establishing a single community health database is that this routine "encounter" data already is collected by health care groups but generally goes unused beyond billing purposes.

"We wanted to build a health data system that could answer health questions in advance of the questions," says economics professor and CHIR director Bill Johnson, the originator of the system.

In contrast, the traditional approach to such research would be to commission a survey or other study that sometimes would take years to complete. With the rapid changes in health care today, the results would be out of date before the study's completion.

But with Arizona HealthQuery, participants - a.k.a. "data partners" - contribute disks or tapes with updated administrative health information quarterly. Thus, a report on a specific condition - or even multiple conditions, such as asthma patients with heart disease - can be identified in a few days.

The project started as a pilot project in Yuma six years ago, to test the feasibility of using a community health data system to determine the number of uninsured. The original design, created by CHIR, part of the W. P. Carey School of Business at ASU, eventually grew into Arizona HealthQuery in 2002.

Johnson and Rimsza stress that the system meets all HIPPA (federal medical record confidentiality) guidelines and is stored in manner that assures patient confidentiality. Once merged into the database, the identifying data is removed. None of the data is transmitted electronically, and nothing that identifies a single patient is ever released.

On the application side, researchers, health professionals and policy makers are making the most of the information. In Yuma County, the data is being used to assess health care disparities of Latino children in a study funded by NIH.

Another study used the database to identify areas of Maricopa County with increased rates of asthma. CHIR is now collaborating with experts in ASU's new Center for Biomedical Informatics to determine if air pollution or other factors in these communities may be responsible for the increased rates.

The innovative project, sponsored by ASU and St. Luke's Health Initiatives, has received recognition for its positive impact on the community. In 2005, ASU President Michael Crow awarded the project with his Social Embeddedness award, recognizing projects that transfer expertise from academia to the community.
