October 6, 2003

Investments help build interdisciplinary research powerhouse

Selected investments in research areas such as urban ecology have helped elevate ASU as one of the leading institutions in studying the impact of humans on the environment.

The university, through work primarily in the Center for Environmental Studies as well as other small groups throughout campus, has long been recognized for its leading research in urban environments. But with the support of a targeted research investment, the effort was taken to a global scale this summer with the launch of the Consortium for the Study of Rapidly Urbanizing Regions.

The new entity draws together projects such as the Central Arizona-Phoenix Long Term Ecological Research project, ASU's satellite-based remote sensing studies of urban environments, extensive outreach programs and initiatives to help decision makers understand the long-term implication of urban policies. Taken individually, the programs represent university strengths, but as a whole provide new ways to look at important urban ecological issues on a global scale.

The effort serves as a powerful example of the university's plans to target its research investments to long-range visionary interdisciplinary initiatives with a lasting impact for society as a whole. The use-inspired initiatives are being developed with an eye toward creating a competitive niche for the university.

"Investing our resources wisely is one of the most important things we can do as a university," Crow says. "To maximize this investment we must build endeavors that will impact the world around us and help us generate a return on that investment for future endeavors. We are not taking a narrow view, but are trying to create solutions across departmental and disciplinary boundaries."

Since July 2002 more than $18 million in seed funding from several sources - primarily Proposition 301 along with a fraction of the indirect costs from grants awarded to ASU researchers - have been invested through the Office of the Vice President for Research and Economic Affairs. This initial portfolio of investments has engaged a broad spectrum of scholars in the ASU academy including those in the areas of religion and conflict; international digital libraries; health and disability policy; science and technology in the law; the human dynamics of homeland security; arts and engineering; computer and information sciences; and wireless integrated nanotechnologies.

Other university efforts that have been launched using a variety of private funding sources and major gifts include ASU's Pan American Initiative to build international ties, community nursing clinics from the College of Nursing, the Virginia G. Piper Center for Creative Writing funded by the Virginia G. Piper Charitable Trust and the Stardust Center for Affordable Homes and the Family.

Coupled with initiatives in biosciences and life sciences and engineering, all of these efforts are part of an overall aggressive plan by the university to triple the research capacity of the university over the next five years.

Greg Raupp, ASU associate vice president for research, says the widespread effort is based on six key principles. The initiatives must be driven by a long range, visionary and compelling research agenda; differentiate the university from the competition; provide use-inspired solutions with a societal benefit; be well conceived with specific timelines and milestones; be interdisciplinary in nature; and provide a high return on the university's investment.

"Ultimately we want to transcend the disciplines, to create a fusion of disciplines," says Raupp. "We want to come up with new ways of doing things, new ways of research and new solutions to problems that in our current way of thinking we cannot even comprehend. We are seeking ideas that will have a positive impact on our community, on our society and our quality of life broadly. That is a driving force behind all that we are doing."
