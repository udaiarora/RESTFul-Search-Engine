March 28, 2003

White House honors science, engineering mentoring programs

Two ASU mentoring programs have received the Presidential Award for Excellence in Science, Mathematics and Engineering Mentoring. The programs are among 10 outstanding individuals and six organizations being recognized by the White House.

A White House statement said "those honored have achieved remarkable results in increasing the participation of minorities, women and disabled students in science, mathematics and engineering."

The two ASU programs honored are the Arizona Mathematics, Engineering, Science Achievement (MESA) and the Strengthening the Understanding of Mathematics and Sciences Institute (SUMS)

The president annually recognizes the people and institutions that have provided broad opportunities for participation by women, minorities and disabled persons in science, mathematics and engineering. No more than 10 persons and 10 institutions are selected each year for recognition. The annual awards, administered by the National Science Foundation, include a $10,000 grant and a presidential commemorative certificate.

"It's a special point of pride for two ASU programs to be recognized by the National Science Foundation and the White House," said ASU Provost Milton Glick. "It's also important to note that in the few years these awards have been presented, ASU has been honored four times, twice this year for our programs and twice when faculty members have received individual awards for their service to our community."

The SUMS Institute is a seed program in its 22nd year that has helped more than 1,600 inner city and reservation students prepare to study mathematics, engineering and science. A large majority of the program's participants go on to college, about half attending ASU, and many have become scientists, engineers and teachers. A large number of SUMS mathematics majors also return to their home communities as high school teachers.

"Administrators constantly have to make hard choices about where to place scarce dollars," says Joaquin Bustoz, a mathematics professor who created and continues to run the SUMS program. "For more than 20 years they have chosen to support the SUMS mission of bringing low income students into mathematics and science. The Presidential Award is not solely a SUMS honor, the honor is shared by the ASU community and especially by Milt Glick."

The MESA program is a consortium between the University of Arizona, ASU and Estrella Mountain Community College that is in its 20th year as a year-round program to prepare underrepresented, first generation and low-income students from 55 schools for mathematics, engineering and science at the elementary and secondary education levels.

The program helps high school students succeed in college through academic advising, after-school enrichment programs, and tours of university and industry sites. More than 80 percent of the graduating seniors in the program attend college, and 60 percent declare math, engineering or science as their major.

AZ MESA has two centers, one southern regional center at the UA and the central regional center at ASU. ASU MESA works with more than 30 schools in the valley and with several American Indian reservations.

"This award pays tribute to all their efforts and recognizes all the students that the program has touched," said Peter Crouch, dean of the College of Engineering & Applied Sciences at ASU. "It is especially gratifying that this national recognition is given to a partnership between local industry, ASU and the U of A. It demonstrates the power of the two universities working together."
