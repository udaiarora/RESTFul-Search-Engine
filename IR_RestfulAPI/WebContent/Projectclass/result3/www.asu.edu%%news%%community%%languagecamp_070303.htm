Expression through technology: ASU camp helps special needs kids communicate

The Speech and Hearing Drama Camp at ASU is combining fun and fantasy to teach special needs children one of the most important skills of all - communication.

The camp, which is in the Speech & Hearing Clinic of the ASU speech & hearing sciences department, is hosting six children, ages 5 - 10, most of whom have cerebral palsy, a developmental disability that can hinder motor skills and limit speech.

Children attending the camp have special augmentative communication devices and are gaining new skills to help them use the devices more effectively. The main goal of the five-week camp is to create a fun way for the children to practice using their communication devices, and what's more fun than putting on a play? The children learn their lines and rehearse the play, which will be performed for friends and family June 25.

"There are all kinds of summer camps for kids, but it's not very likely that kids with communication disorders or cerebral palsy could participate," says Pamela Mathy, director of clinical services for the speech and hearing clinic. "Our camp offers a fun and motivating experience that is more like playtime, than therapy time."

The augmentative communication devices are computer-based and allow the children to construct messages and communicate using combinations of pre-programmed words and spelling. Once the message is created, the device will "speak" using either a digitally pre-recorded phrase or a synthesized voice.

Depending on their physical ability, the children can use a touch screen display or use a method called "scanning" where the device presents a series of options and the child touches a micro switch to select a message. To become proficient, practice is essential.

Kim Daswick, mother of 10-year-old Sidney, who has participated in the camp since it began three years ago, says special needs children generally go from school to therapy, and are constantly working. The camp provides activities similar to any basic summer camp with music, arts and crafts and drama, presented by ASU clinical faculty and student therapists in both music and speech.

"It's therapy, but in a totally different format," says Daswick, who brings Sidney to individual speech therapy at the clinic year-round. "They get the opportunity to learn so much, but in the format of what typical kids do. This way they get all the good stuff."

For some of the campers, who are just beginning to learn to use the devices, it allows them to see other children communicating.

"Just in the last few weeks he has made great strides," says mother Tammy Otto of her 5-year-old son Aaron. "It is very motivational for him to see how everyone else communicates. He can see the benefits of using it."

Another group benefiting from the camp is the team of five adult volunteers who are recovering from severe strokes. These individuals are in the process of regaining their language abilities and attend therapy sessions at ASU each week. The camp lets them help the children and work on their own communication skills in a fun setting.
Also lending a hand are the families of the campers. Sydney Daswick is a triplet, and her brother and sister, Tyler and Alexis, also have parts in the play "Eagle Boy," which was written by Nelson Suarez, a graduate teaching associate in languages & literature, whose son has participated in past camps.

Daswick says of all the benefits of the camp, one of the biggest may be the sense of pride in the campers.

"The one thing we can't underestimate is for the parents to be able to see them on stage and doing something fun for themselves," says Daswick. "To see the kids in that role. parents of special needs children don't often get to see them like that. It's an amazing, fun experience."

For information on the June 25 performance and the Speech & Hearing Clinic, call (480) 965-2373.
