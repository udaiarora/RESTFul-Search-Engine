July 1, 2003

Figy helps build mind, body through recreation therapy

There are only two rules John Figy has for his clients. 1. You're important. 2. If you don't believe you're important, refer back to rule number one.

Figy, program coordinator in Adaptive Recreation, has worked at the Student Recreation Complex (SRC) at ASU for the last 28 years helping many people. He assists those with temporary or long-term disabilities in their athletic pursuits and eases their pain through relaxation exercises.

"We show you how to use the application of movement and the application of exercise," Figy said.

Figy's interest in adaptive recreation stems from when he enlisted in the U.S. Navy as a medic. When he was given a physical on his birthday in 1969 he was declared "4-F," which meant he was disabled from military service. When Figy saw his friends coming back from the war with different disabilities he felt he was saved so he could help others.

Figy went on to work for Recreational Sports at ASU and graduate with a degree in leisure studies with an emphasis in therapeutic and community recreation. He held a job in the Phoenix county hospital in physical therapy from 1972 to 74, where one of his patients was Diana Polaski, the director of Disability Resources at ASU. It was Polaski and Keith Jacobson, director of Intermurals, Club Sports and Recreation that hired Figy to start an adaptive recreation program at the SRC.

Figy is in charge of many programs in the SRC including Exercise Therapy, Wheelchair Tennis instruction and Renewal Through Movement, where the levers of the body such as hips and shoulders are moved in such a way that endorphins are released. These programs are all offered at no cost to members of the ASU community.

"We're an educational source, and a renewal source for students, faculty and staff," Figy said. "People need the recreational sports department; it gives them a binding to the university."

One of the most important things to Figy is that students excel in their education, and he is always willing to help students reach their goal of graduating.

"I believe we are a real benefit to a person reaching their goal of education," Figy said.

Figy says his experiences with "a lot of wonderful people" are the most rewarding part of the job.

"I wish everyone could have the experiences working with people that I've had," Figy said. "It doesn't matter how you get around in life, whether it's walking, whether it's limping or seated in a wheelchair, everyone has something to give. No matter what position we're in, in life, they have something to give."
