Dennis Durband

November 10, 2003


REGENTS' PROFESSOR IVERSON RECIPIENT OF OUTSTANDING DOCTORAL MENTOR AWARD

Peter Iverson
Advancing a mentoring style described as well beyond the norm, Regents' Professor Peter Iverson has been selected as the ASU Outstanding Doctoral Mentor for 2003. The history professor is the twentieth recipient in the sixteen years of the award presented by the Graduate College and the ASU Foundation. The award and its $5,000 prize recognize ASU faculty members who demonstrate excellence and commitment to doctoral student mentoring.

Since arriving at ASU in 1986, Iverson has earned respect for his ability to recruit, mentor, and place graduate students, and work with them following graduation. He repeats four words over and over again to his students: "You can do it." Thirty-one of his students have obliged him by earning Ph.D. degrees.

Professor Noel Stowe, Chair, Department of History, said, "Iverson's work, taken as a whole, is that of an active scholar and dedicated teacher and mentor. His work with his students is far beyond the norm of others on this faculty or elsewhere. He has an effective mentoring style." After

completing the first draft of his book, "We Are Still Here": American Indians in the Twentieth Century, Iverson invited a dozen of his Ph.D. students to critique it. They provided many suggestions that were incorporated into the final draft, checked page proofs and watched the creation of the index. Iverson included their names in the book acknowledgements and gave each a signed copy.

Colleagues say Iverson's style is to work with his students, keep track of them, and encourage them to finish their graduate programs. His students complete graduate programs in less time than what is considered the national norm for the discipline. "This demonstrates his skill in meeting different needs and his ability to work steadily with his students and to nudge them to completion," Stowe said. "As Iverson's students near completion in their degree programs, he helps his students work the job market and navigate the applicant and interview process."
Dr. Peter Iverson, Regents' Professor of History, was honored as the ASU Outstanding Mentor for 2003 at a November reception. Standing next to him are Dr. Noel Stowe, Chair, History Department, and Dr. Maria T. Allison, Interim Dean of the Graduate College.


In Iverson's view, "Teaching is taking people from where they are, rather than where you imagine they should be. When you enter the academy, you must come to terms with a particular and, especially to the uninitiated, a peculiar culture. Twenty-seven of my thirty-one Ph.D. graduates did not grow up in an academic environment. Many are the first person in their family to graduate from college, let alone finish a Ph.D. Part of my job as a mentor involves demystifying this culture."

This mentoring approach helps Iverson's students stand out in the crowd. Three of his students—Gerald Betty, Wade Davies and Scott Riney—have had their dissertations published by highly regarded publishers, and three more—Eric Clements, Stuart Rosebrook and David Wolff—have books in press. Still others have advance contracts for their forthcoming books. Stowe said, "This is a strong recognition of the worth of the research underpinning their dissertations and confirms the value of the mentor's judgment in guiding the dissertation work, as well as the program he leads."

Former students of Dr. Peter Iverson surround him at the Outstanding Mentor 2003 Award Reception in November.


Several of Iverson's students have won prestigious awards and fellowships, such as the Spencer Fellowship, Smithsonian Fellowship, Woodrow Wilson Postdoctoral Fellowship, and American Philosophical Society award. During 2002 and 2003, Iverson helped place Ph.D. graduates in tenure-track positions at Creighton University, Southern Connecticut State University, University of Idaho, University of Montana, University of Nebraska—Lincoln, and University of Texas at El Paso (UTEP).

A recent Ph.D. graduate from ASU, Jeffrey Shepherd is now a tenure-track professor at UTEP. He sought out a doctoral program that would provide opportunities to do pathbreaking research in American Indian history and a mentor with a proven record of graduating and placing Ph.D.s in tenure-track positions, one with a personal commitment to working closely with graduate students and possessing a moral dedication to the contemporary lives of native people. Shepherd came to ASU realizing that Iverson fit the criteria and now regards him as a "mentor without peer."

Former student Eric Walz, a faculty member at Brigham Young University—Idaho, echoes Shepherd's sentiment: "Dr. Peter Iverson represents everything an outstanding doctoral mentor should be." Scott Zeman, assistant professor of history at New Mexico Tech, said that Iverson's mentorship has continued well beyond his graduation. Ongoing mentoring is typical of many ASU professors who establish and maintain long-lasting, working relationships with their student colleagues. Iverson, who will be honored at a fall reception, said, "I look forward to that special occasion. I have been honored to serve as the mentor for these students. Each day they remind me that I have the best job in the world. This award also honors our department and its commitment to graduate education, and it honors both current and past ASU graduate students in American Indian history and the history of the American West."
