February 2, 2004

Air Force selects ASU student satellite for launch

How can something so small accomplish something so great?

That could be the theme for the ASU satellite program. The student-led program, started ten years ago under the direction of Dr. Helen Reed, professor of aerospace engineering and associate director of the NASA Space Grant Program at ASU, has been remarkably successful delivering results in a field typically led by industry.

Today students learned that their hard work had paid off once again. The Air Force officially announced they will launch the student's "Three Corner Sat" (3CS) satellites this July from Kennedy Space Center.

The students started the 3CS satellite project in 1999. Lauren Egan, the program coordinator, was a freshman when she started work on 3CS. She is now a senior in chemical engineering, graduating this May.

"This program is a great example of what students can accomplish when they work hard," Egan said. "Each satellite is only about thirteen inches tall, but they will accomplish important mission objectives, demonstrating the effectiveness of small-scale satellites in performing crucial procedures such as imaging, communications, following commands and performing experiments."

Each of these steps will be operated and controlled by a team of students from ASU, New Mexico State University , and the University of Colorado at Boulder. The team, led by ASU, worked together to design various aspects of the three satellites.

The ASUSat Lab and NASA Space Grant Program at ASU has allowed for great student success over the years. "It's all about the students," said Reed. "I do it because it is most satisfying when a student gets it. It is quite an accomplishment for students to have designed and launched satellites."

"These programs give students hands-on experience, and it allows us to complement and strengthen our academic education," said Egan. "It is because of this project that I decided to go to graduate school to study-aerospace engineering and spacecraft propulsion."

The original plan was to launch the satellites on a space shuttle in 2002, but after the Columbia disaster, that option quickly faded. The Air Force launch, part of the Air Force's University Nanosat I/II Program sponsored by the Air Force Office of Scientific Research (AFOSR), the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency (DARPA), the Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL), the Space and Missile Command Space Test Program and NASA-Goddard, allows the students to complete their mission and gather useful scientific information as they had originally intended.

For more information on ASU Satellite programs, visit nasa.asu.edu .

For more information on the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering, visit www.fulton.asu.edu .
