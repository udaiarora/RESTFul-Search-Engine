November 14, 2006

Name game may lead to wealth

Of the 100 people included in a recent list of the highest-paid executives of publicly held firms in the metro Phoenix area, four of them are women. The gender gap in pay and advancement has been studied at length, and those familiar with the data nationally are probably not surprised at the disproportionate representation of men on the list. But does it also pay to be named Robert or Steve?

According to the current issue of the Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast, this conclusion could be drawn from a review of the individuals listed in the Business Journal of Phoenix's ?Book of Lists 2006,? under the heading ?High Rollers.? Of all the potential names for male babies, three names make up 20 percent of the ?High Rollers.? Further, only six names account for more than one-third of those on the list.

A standard chi square test shows that the difference between the actual and expected frequency of names among the Phoenix High Rollers is significant at the 0.001 level.

?It must be emphasized that it would be imprudent to attempt to make too much of these results, due to data limitations and an almost complete absence of any applicable economic theory,? says Lee McPheters, contributing editor. ?Still, it appears that having a popular name is not an impediment to career advancement; it actually seems to help.?

Elsewhere in the Arizona Blue Chip, editor Tracy Clark asked the panel whether the Federal Reserve should be more concerned about inflation or recession in the coming year. Last year, 60 percent of the panel thought the Fed should be more worried about inflation; this year, 100 percent think the focus should be on recession.

The Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast includes the consensus forecast of economists from 19 groups and institutions. It is published by the JPMorgan Chase Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute in the W. P. Carey School of Business at ASU.
