Is It Okay If I Have A Few Drinks While Taking This Medication??????

            It’s Friday afternoon and you have just picked up your antibiotic for a respiratory infection that you have been fighting all week long.  Tonight is the kegger of the century and all you have been thinking about all week is relaxing and tipping back a few cold ones.  You say to yourself, “Having a few drinks while taking this medication won’t kill me.”  While it may not kill you, drinking alcohol while taking an antibiotic may not be the best idea.  Many antibiotics require an adequate intake of fluids, namely water, throughout the course of treatment.  Since alcohol has a tendency to cause dehydration, (major cause of a hangover too!!!), it may be a good decision to put that party on hold until the antibiotics have helped your body eradicate whatever bug has invaded it.

           

There are a number of prescription medications in addition to common over the counter medications that can interact directly with alcohol or indirectly as mentioned in the example above.  Prescription cough medications that contain codeine can interact with alcohol by causing overly excessive drowsiness.  This can also be the case when taking NyquilÒ and alcohol together.  Prescription and over the counter non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs used for pain and inflammation, such as naproxen

(NaprosynÒ, AlleveÒ) and ibuprofen (MotrinÒ, AdvilÒ) can also cause excessive drowsiness when combined with alcohol, in addition to greater stomach irritation.  Acetaminophen (TylenolÒ) use at high doses (greater than 4 grams/day) in conjunction with alcohol use may potentially lead to liver damage. 

Combination cough and cold products that contain an antihistamine for allergies such as diphenhydramine (BenadrylÒ) and chlorpheniramine (Chlor-trimetonÒ) can have an additive drowsiness effect with alcohol intake because these antihistamines can be very sedating.  If you are not sure if the medication you are taking interacts with alcohol ask your pharmacist or doctor.  It’s that simple. 

            So it may be your best bet (for your health) to avoid drinking alcohol while you are recovering from this infection, get plenty of rest, and drink plenty of fluids, mainly water and juices.  Don’t worry, that keg won’t go dry while you are taking care of your health!

-Mark Gavelda, Pharm D candidate

To Sleep or Not To Sleep

 

Many college students are faced with long nights of studying, stress over exams and just plain anxiety over life decisions.  All of those things can lead to sleepless nights.  Insomnia is defined as the difficulty falling asleep, maintaining sleep, or of not feeling rested despite a sufficient opportunity to sleep.  If you have experienced any of the symptoms described in the definition of insomnia don’t worry, there are a lot of ways to help treat insomnia and most of them do not involve medication.  Some of the things that can cause insomnia are anxiety, depression, and alcohol usage.  If you are experiencing insomnia, start by keeping a sleep log.  Record what time you go to bed, what time you fall asleep, how many times you wake up at night, what time you wake up in the morning, what time you get out of bed, and how many naps you take during the day and how long each nap is.  This diary can help determine what the cause of insomnia is and if there is a pattern to your insomnia. 

            Try to establish a regular waking time and bedtime.  This includes the weekends.  Try not to sleep extra on the weekends to “catch up” on sleep you missed during the week, this can disrupt any sleeping pattern set by your body.  As a result you might not be able to sleep in the evenings.  Sleep only as much as necessary to feel rested.  If you can’t fall asleep get out of bed and read or watch TV until you are tired again.  Use the bed only for sleep and intimacy.  Try to study at a desk, a study area, or at the library that way your body associates the bed with sleeping.  Avoid daytime naps.  They may feel good at the time but they can disrupt your sleeping patterns at night.  Exercise routinely.  Discontinue or reduce use of alcohol, caffeine, or nicotine.  All of these substances can interrupt the body’s ability to get to sleep and/or stay asleep.  Avoid excessive fullness or hunger at bedtime.  Avoid large quantities of liquids in the evening to prevent to need to wake up and go to the bathroom.  Finally do something relaxing and enjoyable before bedtime. 

            If none of the above mentioned things are working, talk to your physician.   There are medications that can be used to help insomnia, but they should only be used for short term.  In addition, there are certain medications and medical conditions that can cause insomnia.  The best way to stop insomnia is to find out the reason why you it is happening and work on eliminating the cause.

-         Jamie Obenstine, Pharm D candidate

 

Chilled Urine Goes Under the Counter!

 

I am not making this up!  According to the Calcutta Telegraph there has been a wave of demand for “gomutram” at restaurants and “health” stores.  Gomutram turns out to be cow urine and is recommended by some practitioners of yoga, ayurvedic medicine and homeopathy.  At first attracting diabetics and cancer patients the beverage is developing a steady stream of customers.  Local cow herders had been advertising their own claims of superior cow urine based on what the cows were fed and if they were strays or had some sort of pedigree.  But then the tax authorities got PO’ed when they heard of this and claimed that sellers of cow urine should pay a VAT (value added tax).  So now the product is less advertised and sold “under the counter” to regular customers.  There is debate on the issue as to whether urine has added value over water, should this be a prescription product and how best to avoid the “fake” urine being sold in some outlets.  The fake urine is often buffalo urine or perhaps that of an unscrupulous shop owner. 

The usual daily dose, by the way, is 50 to 100 mls., usually chilled.  Salud!

 

Sex Foods (part two)

 

PharSide #28 made it as far as chocolate in exploring the wonderful world of aphrodisiac eats, so I will plunge ahead from that moment of interuptus.

Let’s start with Coffee, as many of us do.  The physiologic effects of coffee (caffeine) include constricting cerebral blood vessels while dilating peripheral blood vessels.  So  coffee will not only help with that pesky vascular headache it can improve blood flow to those body parts that express an interest in sex by filling with blood.

Cilantro (coriander to you gringos) has been reputed to improve fertility and various appetites for thousands of years.  In the Arabian Nights, a man who was unable to father a child through 40 years of attempts became a dad after drinking some sort of cilantro tea.  For the Freudians among you, note that the plant is often described as having an erect stem and is part of the carrot family.

More C’s:  This from botanical online: “With the cilantro, the cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum), the clove (Syzygium aromaticum), the ginger (Zingiber officinale) and the cinnamon (Cinnamomum Zeylanicum) it was carried out a drink, called " Hipocras " that was used in the weddings. This same drink was imported to Europe by the members of the crusades and later on exported to many nations of South America, but it was banned because it stimulated the libido too much.”

It goes without saying that eggs are sexy.  Because of its connections with new life, the egg has been touted as a fertility aid and an aphrodisiac.  Peasants in Central Europe rubbed eggs on their ploughs to improve their crops.  You can read that literally and figuratively.  Brides in France broke an egg on the doorstep to ensure a large family.  Various egg rituals have been reported to predict the sex of an unborn child.  Eggs do contain some cholesterol, which among other things, is necessary for sperm production.

Ripe figs are thought to emulate either male or female genitalia, probably depending on your point of view and proclivities.  A man using his fingers to open a fig and then eat the insides is considered a provocative and seductive invitation in many cultures.  Consider this passage from a cookbook: "And its feel, oh its feel. A knife slices through the fruit like soft butter. The tiny, edible seeds seem unending, weaving layer upon layer of texture and flavor within the succulent fig. All ridges work inward to a core, painting a relief portrait of the soft world of the inner thighs. When you eat a fig, you are tasting history, Cleopatra, Dionysian orgies, the Roman Saturnalia. And when its juice runs over your tongue, you are drinking pure, unadulterated sensuality."  Heh, we aren’t talking about a Fig Newton here!

Garlic has a reputation for curing just about anything, including low libido.  It has been touted for hypertension, high cholesterol, called “nature’s antibiotic,” good for arthritis, the common cold and on and on.  If you’re thinking of eating some to enhance your lovemaking, I suggest that both participants partake.  Fight fire with fire.  The science is that garlic does seem to increase production of nitric oxide synthase, the enzyme responsible for nitric oxide production.  Nitric oxide is an important chemical involved in erectile tissue.

Ginger was a redhead I knew in college.  She could…  whoops!  Ginger root stimulates the circulatory system and you should know by now what that means.

Honey is rich in B vitamins and amino acids.  The lore of honey goes back to the dawn of man.  Its beginning from the interaction of the bee with sexual flower parts and the value in the reproductive process of the matriarchal hive add power to its reputation as an aphrodisiac.  Mead, a drink made from fermented honey, was a medieval favorite of seducers of both sexes.  Newlyweds drank mead on their “honeymoon” thereby “sweetening” their union.  Honey is often combined with one or more of these other fun foods to enhance flavor and potency.  The same might be said of adding honey to your partner.  Time to wrap things up, my sweet tooth is tingling.

The scary truth about HPV


20 million people are currently infected with HPV or human papillomavirus with about 5.5 million new cases diagnosed each year.  It is estimated that 50 percent of men and 80 percent of women who are sexually active will have HPV at some point during their lives.  This virus accounts for up to 1/3 of all newly diagnosed STD’s and yet most people still don’t know that much about it.

So what is it? The human papillomavirus is a collection of about 100 different strains, 30 of which are sexually transmitted.  The sexually transmitted strains are further divided into low-risk and high-risk types.

High-risk HPV types are associated with pre-cancerous cell changes in the genital area, which if left untreated can lead to cancer.  In fact, cervical cancer is now thought to be caused exclusively by HPV.

Low-risk HPV types are associated with genital warts.  The warts can appear pink or flesh colored, small or large, alone or in clusters.  Sometimes they are recognized by their cauliflower shape. They can appear in and around the genital area on both males and females.

The problem? Since HPV is spread by skin-to-skin contact and not bodily fluids, condoms will only help prevent a small number of infections.

When will you know if you are infected? It’s different for each person and sub-type.  Some people with the low-risk type may see warts:

  • Within several weeks after having sex with a person who has HPV
  • Within several months after having sex with a person who has HPV (this is MOST common)
  • Within several years after having sex with a person who has HPV
  • NEVER (they may never know they have HPV)

Due to the variation of time, it may be hard to know exactly when you got the virus and who passed it.

High-risk virus types are detected only through a pap exam.

Can HPV be treated or cured? HPV is a virus, which means it can become latent or non-visible but cannot be cured.  Patients with high-risk subtypes require frequent examination of the area to ensure pre-cancerous changes do not evolve into cancer.  Patient with low-risk subtypes can be treated topically based on the size and location of warts.

The most common treatment choices for the external low-risk subtypes include Aldara cream and Podofilox solution. Both products are applied several times weekly and left on for a set number of hours before being washed off.  It is important to note that wart removal products found in your local drugstore should not be used to treat genital warts from HPV.

The most important thing you can do when it comes to HPV is get frequent sexual health checkups by your doctor. Also, use condoms with every sexual encounter including oral, vaginal, and anal sex.  While condoms aren’t effective in preventing all HPV transmissions they certainly help with some, and when it comes to your health it is better to be safe than sorry.
