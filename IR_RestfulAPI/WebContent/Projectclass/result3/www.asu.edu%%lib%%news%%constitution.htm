Constitution Day Celebration

 

Sponsored by the ASU Libraries and the Ross-Blakley Law Library

 

(posted 9/1/06)

 

 

The ASU University Libraries and the Ross-Blakley Law Library are proud to invite the ASU community and the general public to an afternoon celebrating Constitution Day 2006, on Friday September 15 from 1:00-5:00pm, in the Carson Ballroom of the Old Main building on ASU's Tempe campus. Everyone is welcome to attend individual sessions or the entire program.

 

Most Americans know that July 4th is our nation's birthday. Far fewer Americans know that September 17th is the birthday of our governments, the date in 1787 on which delegates to the Philadelphia Convention completed and signed the U.S. Constitution. The ideas on which America was founded?commitments to the rule of law, limited government and the ideals of liberty, equality and justice?are embodied in the Constitution, the oldest written constitution of any nation on Earth. Constitution Day is intended to celebrate not only the birthday of our government, but the ideas that make us Americans.

 

Senator Robert Byrd of West Virginia accomplished a long-standing goal, passing a law designating September 17th as Constitution Day . Schools and federal agencies are required to hold educational programs on the Constitution on Constitution Day.

 

All programs will be held in the Carson Ballroom in Old Main on the Tempe campus. Refreshments will be served and audience members are invited to browse Constitution-related library materials during breaks between presentations.

 

The ASU Libraries will provide video streaming of this event through the Library Channel for those who are unable to attend. The video stream will be available about one week after the event. This event is free and open to the public.


Program

1:00-1:15: Opening Remarks

 

1:15 ? 2:00: The Constitution in Action: Review and Preview of Upcoming Constitutional Supreme Court Cases

Dr. Catherine O'Grady, Professor of Law and Executive Director, Clinical Program, Sandra Day O'Connor College of Law.


Professor O’Grady is the Executive Director of Clinical Programs for the College of Law, and she oversees the College’s seven clinical offerings. She regularly teaches Civil Procedure, Civil Justice Clinic, the Practice of Law in a Digital Era, and an upper-class seminar on the United States Supreme Court. She regularly appears as a legal commentator on KAET’s Horizon program to speak about the United States Supreme Court. Professor O’Grady’s current research focuses on law and emotions, clinical pedagogy, and the legal profession. Before joining the faculty, Professor O’Grady clerked for William Canby on the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals, and she practiced law with the Phoenix firm of Meyer, Hendricks, Victor, Osborn & Maledon.

 

2:00 - 2:15: Break. Audiences members will be able to enjoy refreshments and displays of Constitution-related library materials.

 

2:15 ? 3:00: Rebirth of a Nation: Origins and Ratification of the Constitution

Dr. Catherine Kaplan, Assistant Professor of History, College of Liberal Arts and Sciences.

Catherine Kaplan focuses on the early national period of U.S. history. Her forthcoming first book, We, the Readers: Culture, Community, and Dissent in the New Nation, 1790–1812, explores the importance of conceptions of masculinity to literary and political communities during the formative years of the republic, and investigates women's contributions to ostensibly all-male cultural projects. Her new research concerns the life and philosophy of Elizabeth Ann Seton, whose efforts to build a Catholic women's community in the United States both challenged and relied on notions of womanly piety and domesticity.

 

3:00 ? 3:15: Break. Audiences members will be able to enjoy refreshments and displays of Constitution-related library materials.

 

3:15 ? 4:00: Watchdogs or Lapdogs?  The Role of a Free Press and the First Amendment

Dr. Joseph Russomanno, Associate Professor of Journalism, Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication.

Joseph Russomanno focuses on the First Amendment, press freedom theory and mass communication law. His research includes the historical development of free expression and its philosophical roots. He has authored several articles and books, including Speaking Our Minds: Conversations with the People Behind Landmark First Amendment Cases, a book that provides first-person accounts from individuals at the heart of noteworthy cases. He also edited Defending the First: Commentary on First Amendment Issues and Cases, a collection of analytical essays by attorneys who argued First Amendment cases before the U.S. Supreme Court. He is also co-author of The Law of Journalism & Mass Communication, a textbook.

 

4:00 ? 4:15: Closing Remarks

 

 

 

The ASU Libraries and the Ross-Blakley Law Library would like to acknowledge the Sandra Day O'Connor College of Law, the Department of History in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences and the Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication for their support.
