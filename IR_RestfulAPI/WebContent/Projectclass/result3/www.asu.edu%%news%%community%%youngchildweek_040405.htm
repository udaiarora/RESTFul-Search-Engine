April 4, 2005

Child Study Lab activities celebrate week of learning opportunities

April 4-7 is the Week of the Young Child.

What does this have to do with a university? Plenty, says Nancy Hoth, director of ASU’s Child Study Lab.

“Brain research and experience show that children’s earliest years are critical learning years,” Hoth says. “Week of the Young Child is a chance to celebrate the learning opportunities for young children – and to recognize the responsibilities we share for helping them make the most of those opportunities.”

ASU’s Child Study Lab, in operation for more than 30 years, will celebrate the Week of the Young Child by inviting parents and children to visit the school next week to participate in activities and learn more about the lab.

There will be a picnic on the CSL playground from 10:30 a.m. – 11:30 a.m. , April 7, and the lab will be selling scholastic books April 4 – 6.

CSL also will be collecting children’s Fiskars scissors, paper towels, markers, crayons, flashlights, spiral notebooks, construction paper, glue sticks and other school supplies, plus clothing and food to donate to the Thomas J. Pappas School for homeless children.

CSL, located in the Psychology Building , with an attached outdoor playground, offers classes for children as young as 15 months up through pre-kindergarten.

In the very first class, Parent/Toddler, parents attend with their children. The class provides a “first-school” socializing and learning experience for the children, while providing education and support for parents. Gradually, the parents leave their children in the classroom alone and go to their own discussion groups led by the teachers. There are observation areas so parents – and students in the psychology program – can unobtrusively watch the children as they play and learn.

Faculty in the Department of Psychology, which houses CSL, sometimes conduct research studies with the children, with the parents’ permission.

One such study under way is early map use.

“Specifically, we are investigating how children become able to deal with the challenge of using a map along a route that turns,” says Jordan Vosmik, a doctoral candidate in developmental psychology, who is conducting the study. “Turning poses a special obstacle to map use because the map’s relation to the space is changing.”

While the Week of the Young Child honors the more than 35 million children ages birth through 8 in the United States, and the families, teachers and other adults who care for and teach them, the celebration also emphasizes the problems facing those who work with young children.
