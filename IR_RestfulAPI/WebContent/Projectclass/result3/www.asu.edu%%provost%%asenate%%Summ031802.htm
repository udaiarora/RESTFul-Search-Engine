3.A  Senate President?s Report (Morton Munk).

           

As you know, the Presidential Search Committee has concluded its work.  It forwarded the names of three acceptable candidates to the Board of Regents: John Schumacher, President of the University of Louisville, however, withdrew almost immediately after his name was sent forward.  Karen Holbrook, Senior Vice President and Provost at the University of Georgia, Athens.  She visited our campus and withdrew the weekend right after her visit.  The remaining candidate is Mike Crow, Executive Vice Provost at Columbia University.  I know as much as you do about the status of the search at this stage, and some of it I have read in the newspapers.  As you may know, Mike Crow is involved in negotiations with the Regents, and my sense is that we will hear something about the outcome within a matter of days.  I think that is the wish of the members of the Board of Regents.

 

I want to acknowledge the work of the faculty advisory committee that was advisory to the presidential search committee.  That committee was chaired by Doug Johnson and includes six past presidents of the Academic Senate, Andy Jackson, a representative from ASU East and Vince Waldron, a representative from ASU West.  That committee submitted a final assessment of actually only one candidate, because by the time that report was written there only was one candidate.  The committee did support the appointment of Mike Crow and that is the current status of the work of the search committee. 

 

Coming to another issue, as I think you are all aware, the issue of the grading system here at Arizona State University is very much in the spotlight.  I did indicate to you earlier that it is my belief that it is appropriate, in fact wise, for the Senate to revisit that issue since the initial policy decision was made ten years ago but never implemented for technical reasons.  I think it is especially important that we do this given the student concern, which has been expressed (It is true that it is a relatively small number of students, but nonetheless there is some student concern.).  As you know, the student government (ASASU) has weighed in and has been very critical of the change to the plus minus system.  Given all of this, I do think it is appropriate for us to revisit the issue, and I have asked President Elect George Watson to chair a representative ad hoc committee to revisit the grading policy.  The committee ought to include members of the faculty, some student members and representatives from Information Technology to make sure that if we do come up with a proposal this time, it will be one that can be implemented. The charge to the committee will be to examine the entire range of grading options, pros and cons, and to look at the expected consequences to the best of their knowledge, of the changes or the recommendations that they will make.  The committee report will be comprehensive and make recommendations, which will be the starting point for the Senate?s discussion and debate. 

 

I have invited Regent Jack Jewett, who is president-elect of the Board of Regents, to join us at the next Senate meeting on April 15.  I am pleased to say that he has agreed to do so and he will speak on a topic of his own choosing. 

 

3.B  University Provost?s Report (Milton Glick).  

 

On the budget?You have got me!  (Much laughter here)  Seriously, we are in the dark as to what the likelihood of the outcome on the budget negotiations will be.  I think that you read in the newspaper as much as we hear.  In special session, the Senate passed a budget which revoked the earlier salary commitment and put in its place 5% for people making up to $30,000 (4% for the next year and 3% for the next year) up to $82,000, at which there would be ?0? percent.  I hope you would find this an unacceptable way to deal with salaries.  I find both the across the board nature of it, but also the unwillingness to deal with many of our senior faculty and staff in a meaningful way, a very problematic statement about the value system.  I do think bottom weighting of salaries is appropriate because of the enormous burden that the health benefits increase has placed on all of us, but especially on people in the lower income brackets.  We are supportive of bottom weighting, but not of eliminating people in higher income brackets, nor of completely eliminating merit from consideration, and we are taking that stance very strongly.  The Senate passed this mainly to get something on the table with an understanding, if I understand it, that the House would then come to conference committee?instead the House when they could not work anything out simply passed it, to send to the Governor for her to line item veto.  It has not as of this morning been transmitted to the Governor yet, which could mean there is a modification underway, but we do not know that.

 

Past President Johnson: Do we have the attorney general?s opinion?  

 

Provost Glick: No.  I do not believe there is one yet.  Legislative Counsel, that is the legal adviser to the Legislature, has argued that this is not a valid business, one where she (the Governor) can throw out the salaries.  It is not an appropriations bill; it is a different kind of bill.  You can only line item appropriations bills.  That is not an official statement.  Legislative Counsel has no official role in this, so I assume that someone will ask for the Attorney General?s opinion, if that is what happens.  In addition, I think they have asked for another quarter percent out of this year?s budget.  They then adjourned the special session, and today they are back into regular session working on next year?s budget and other bills.  I hear it could be weeks or months away from the 2003 budget.  We do not know where we are.  We are having conversations with deans, vice presidents, vice provosts, about what are possible ways we can reduce the budget should we need to again in ways that are not across the board.  That is to say, are there functions that we think are worthy but that we can simply reduce substantially, or eliminate, in order to protect the overall health of the university?  We do not intend to make any decisions.  We are just starting the thinking process should you have to do that and there will be open discussions and debates, whatever processes we must go through.  At this point, we are trying to get a sense of things and start people thinking about what they are doing now that they would not do, if there were further cutbacks in the budget (without doing damage to the unit or the enterprise).  We are trying to do it in a collegial, candid way, recognizing that should we have to make those cuts we want a lot of processes and a lot of discussion.  I wish I could tell you more than that, but I have told you slightly more than what I know. (Much laughter here).

 

On master planning, I think this will be an important discussion that Vice Provost Harrison is going to lead today.  We have not revised our Campus Master Plan for a decade.  This Master Plan, I believe, is more thought stimulating and provocative than the last one.  I felt the last one, which was finished just as I came in to the university, largely cataloged what was already in place.  This revised Master Plan raises some very interesting issues.  The master planners would like to see residence halls seated within the core campus.  The argument for that is a good one?it is that if you want to have a 24-hours campus, it gives you definite centers of people.  The argument against this is that it takes up critical academic space and perhaps devalues the residence halls that are already not on the main campus.  However, those are good debates to have and I think you will find that a number of these issues are raised by the master planners, which we really need a lot of input on from you. 

 

We are also interviewing this weekend (if you have questions, Dr. Harrison can answer them) the candidates for the position of Director/Associate Vice Provost of Human Resources.  Susan Malaga formerly held this very important role. 

 

Finally, let me take about one and one-half minutes to give you a bird?s eye view of what you have been reading about in the paper, the International Genomics Consortium and the Arizona Biomedical Biotechnology Institute (ABBI), and see if I can help with some perspective on them.  There has been so much written and so little conveyed.  Jeff Trent is head of the Human Genome Project and much of his work has been at the National Institute of Health. What he wants to do is create in one or more places, not as part of NIH, but a freestanding, either for profit or not for profit, tissue bank and that would be the International Genomics Consortium (IGC), which would have hundreds of thousands of cancer tissues and complete patient histories with those tissues, to provide a comparative data base such that when you take a cancer tissue and you start looking at its genomes and its composition, you have some basis for comparing it with other genomic studies of cancer.  Then separately, he is hoping to create in one or more places, an organization that will do research on drug discovery and diagnostic tools, using these genomic and other markers but tying them to basic biology research.  One of the places that he might select to come to is greater Phoenix, largely because of his long history in Arizona.  What we are trying to do is find a way to make this possible, in ways that will leverage the University?s commitment to biomedicine and biotechnology, rather than draining the University?s commitment.  This is a very delicate battle.  He understands that we need strong university basic research to make these other things work.  ABBI would not replace the university?s research, but as I perceive it would be an organization with ASU, U of A, and NAU, and some of the hospitals that would all join as consortium members to give us a place to physically or perhaps just virtually, help raise the profile of bio-medicine and bio-technology in greater Phoenix, which will be accomplished in the absence of a medical school in this area.   They are now talking $75 million over five years to bring this to the Valley, which may or may not be an accurate number, and it may or may not be what the size of it would become.  I think there really is an opportunity to increase substantially the presence of bio-medical/bio-technology research in the Valley and that would be good.  I have two concerns.  One is that I fear the media and some of the proponents are blowing out of context the outcome of this project.  That is, this project will not make us one of the leading biotechnology states in the union.  Part of my job within the university has always been to manage expectations?I think it is important to manage expectations in the community as well.  I need to report that the community may think that if they gather this money and they bring ABBI and IGC here, overnight we will become one of the national leaders in bio-technology research.  It just is not going to happen.  You still have California, Atlanta, Baltimore, and Washington.  My other concern is that if this does not happen, they may then say?Well, we cannot do anything in bio-medicine and bio-technology here--we must really do some of that work, for the good of the State of Arizona and the Valley of the Sun.  On the one hand, I worry that they are overstating the benefits but under-realizing that these are things that we must do even if at a different level--no matter what--if we are to be competitive in the next decade.  What we are trying to do is manage expectation of the university from the community, while being full participants and keep reiterating that these are things that we need to have in the Valley, and at ASU, while not promising that if we do this, we will never have another recession!   No, there will be another recession when this one is over, unless they really revoked the laws of economics.  Paul Burgess has assured me that they cannot get two-thirds majority in order to do that.  (Much laughter here)  Therefore, that is a bird?s eye view, which probably leaves you knowing even less than before I began.  However, it is what the entire editorial section of The Arizona Republic was about yesterday, and it seems to me that it is great to have both newspapers?the Tribune and the Republic?say it is important to invest in research in Arizona.  My concern is?the comment that if you do that, then we will not have any economic problems.  Delivering on all those promises is what I worry about.  That concludes my report.

3.C Update on the Campus Master Plan (Vice Provost for Administration Mernoy Harrison).

 

As Dr. Glick indicated, we had not updated our Campus Master Plan since 1992.  Since that time, there have been many changes to the campus, the campus environment.  A lot of things that were assumed in the 1992 Master Plan have not come to pass: ASU had not achieved Research I status at that time; we had not completed our Campus Strategic Plan when the original Master Plan was created, and our Campus Strategic Plan calls for a number of things, some of which have already come to fruition.  For example, they wanted an increase in our freshman class and the advancement of our research student product.  With that as a background, Dr. Glick agreed to put up the funds to allow us to do an update to the last Master Plan and we have been working on this throughout--last fall and during this semester with the Master Planning Committee.  There have been a number of focus groups held and Dr. Munk has provided valuable insight.  What this presentation will describe are the thoughts behind the Master Plan.  We wanted to focus on education, planning, and particularly the interdisciplinary connections between disciplines.  We wanted to blur the boundaries a bit between one discipline and another discipline.  One of the things we asked the consultants to look at is how to organize the campus perimeter within our current footprint.  The campus has more than one edge, and we had gotten to the point where our campus boundary and edges were not well defined.  We also wanted to emphasize the pedestrian environment at the perimeter of the campus core in particular, but also throughout the campus.  We wanted to minimize the vehicular travel, so that you do not have bikes, cars and pedestrians in conflict as much as we do now.  We also wanted to stress the connections between the university and the Tempe community.  The updated Master Plan will illustrate this with the Tempe Center Project, and connect with the Rio Salado Project.  Then, as Dr. Glick also pointed out, our Master Plan consultants, based on their experiences at other institutions, have really been pushing us to think of the university as a 24-hour campus.  Some of the other things that we have talked about in addition have been the density of the campus, and the Master Plan consultant has indicated that from their standpoint we can re-densify the campus core.  Therefore, that began our study as we started to look at the historical campus, around 1900.  Old Main was the only building on campus at that time.  You can see from the slide that there was an orchard around Old Main.  This is an aerial photo taken in 1939--you can see the Rio Salado River was not in its existing channel at that time.  Our campus boundary and our campus geography have changed greatly since then.  Today the campus has very little vegetation.  You can see how the campus has progressed to the west and to the south, and now we have started to move to the East. 

 

The slide presentation provided by Vice Provost Harrison included:

 

1) Existing campus use districts-cultural fine arts facilities ? parking structures- various academic buildings-a small amount of open space-Campus Cafes (suggested for emphasis by the consultants spread around the campus). 

 

2) Existing campus edges-We now have an edge on McAllister, edges at Rural Road, at Apache, at University, and edges all around the North Campus.

 

3) Existing open space and pedestrian malls--What stands out is how little green space there is in our campus core--there are few places to throw a Frisbee or just run around.  This will be replaced by pedestrian malls modeled after old Tempe streets. 

 

Our consultants were concerned about the lack of organization of the existing malls.  A mall is something that the consultants thought should provide good shade, but also provide a way to traverse the campus.  There are alternative treatments planned-shaded vegetation surrounding the campus cafes and lining the malls in a formal fashion.

 

The consultants talked about how to build the campus up.  There will be expansion of current facilities and new facilities in the future.  Tempe Center will be the subject of redevelopment; there will be the new Liberal Arts and Sciences Mediated Classroom Building; there were three recommended new Residential Life Facilities (like Sonora Hall). On McAllister, they are suggesting a new campus mall and a new campus mall by the new research building they are planning to build.  You can see with this layout that the creation of campus malls, campus open space, and courtyards is something that the consultants are trying to encourage.  On one slide, you can see that the existing Administration Building is gone and is replaced with open space.  There will be a potential research corridor identified. 

 

A campus mosaic will be composed of academic facilities that are closely related?a Fine Arts district, an Education district, College of Business district, Science and Liberal Arts district, new research facilities (a research corridor) along McAllister.  They have also suggested that we spread campus cafes throughout the campus in each of our academic areas. 

 

The next diagram focuses on campus gateways.  There will be a signature gateway to the campus.  The consultants feel that instead of focusing on a single gateway, we should emphasize entry points to the campus on several locations.  Three gateways are mentioned.  Again, since these are landscape architects, they focused on open spaces.  If you remember on the previous existing open space diagram that I showed, there has been a significant increase in open space?courtyards, open spaces with green, emphasizing the Alumni Lawn.  They want to create in the Tempe Center some open space and some green space.  This diagram shows the open space in the core of the campus.  Again, you can see the places where the open spaces have been expanded.  This is just a diagram of how they put in new trees and vegetation, showing the development of that compared to what it is now.  This is the Orange Mall entryway?one of the things that the consultants have asked is to open up the existing malls, so that pedestrians and bicycle access are included.  They think that if we open up the malls, we can create a better-used space.  Since most of our facilities will be built on what are now parking lots, we tried to identify in a parking master plan new parking structures, in order to complement the lost ground space for the new buildings as well as parking spaces.  We have a bike plan developed.  We asked the consultants to really give some thought to bicycle circulation, because we know today that there is a significant problem.  We have not finished our discussions on this but the consultants notion is to not try to create an environment where there are no bicycles, or places where only bicycles go, because the experience is that pedestrians and bicyclists do not follow the rules about going where they are supposed to travel!  Oftentimes bike lanes make traffic worse, not better. 

 

How would the new campus core look relating to all the open spaces, the green spaces, the new buildings and the mall treatments?  On Orange Mall, the idea is to open that up all the way from Payne Hall down to McAllister.  Another important mall is being created, McAllister Mall to connect the North and South Campus.  Again on Gateways, there will be one for Gammage Parkway, and the Alumni Lawn will have a gateway to the campus, Old Main and the University Club (and what was Languages and Literature Building) is another.  This is Orange Mall and Hayden Lawn?you can see how much more densely populated with trees this area will be.  For Forest Mall and Orange Mall?shade is the goal.  This is one of the areas suggested for new residential facilities that will frame the Student Recreation Complex, and the SRC itself will be expanded. An academic facility is planned where the old PE West Building is now.  There will be a mixture of academic facilities and residential facilities, and the idea is to create an active campus.  I must tell you, however, that we have not yet agreed to mix residential and academic facilities. 

 

The consultants want to build another large recreation field in the South Campus.  That will be the key component of the South Campus.  There will be a parking structure so that the students would not have to cross Apache to live here or park.  Again, this is all organized around open spaces.  North Campus will be primarily athletic facilities, but you can see here the treatment is to connect to Rio Salado into the core campus. 

 

That is it!  This is where we are so far with the updated Campus Master Plan.  I will take any questions you have.

 

Past President Johnson: I did not notice in any of your drawings the assumption of a light rail. 

 

Vice Provost Harrison: We are actually working on a revision to this plan to accommodate that.  The assumption is that a portion of McAllister will be closed.  As some of you know, there has been some concern expressed about whether or not we can accommodate a light rail on McAllister, because of concerns about vibration and electromagnetic interference.  So, we also are starting to look at how we would reorganize the campus if the light rail came here.  We would need to find another location, which would still allow us to develop these as research facilities outside the area that will be impacted by vibration and electromagnetic influence.  We have studies underway now to see how large that exclusion zone would need to be.

 

Parliamentarian Burstein: In the science area we have to have deliveries to buildings that are in the third square from the right upper of your diagram.  Is there ability to have deliveries into these buildings in the new plan?

 

Vice Provost Harrison: The malls currently serve as both malls and service areas for deliveries.  What we are trying to do, and we will still have that capability, will be to put in a new service drive, without having to come down Orange Mall.  There will be other service drives in addition to that.

 

Senator Garcia: I was going to follow up on the comment on service vehicles.  I felt that it has always been a great distraction to pedestrians to walk around our campus.  There is a gross abuse of service and excessive ASU vehicle use, and even non-service people, to the extent that delivery people will sometimes drive down the mall in excess of thirty miles per hour. However, bikes seem to be periodically singled out.  I think ultimately this issue should be addressed.  There is a security issue as well that large vehicles can currently drive around campus and park.  I am still surprised that is allowed. 

 

Vice Provost Harrison: Service vehicles are allowed only on certain malls, at certain times of the day, unless they are actually working on a building in that area.  If you see anyone going down the malls at an excessive rate of speed?they are only supposed to go as fast as people walk.  Feel free to report them because we definitely have a policy against that.  We periodically have officers out on the mall and they do stop people and write citations and warnings to vehicles that are on the malls improperly.  We would like to think we have improved on that situation.  We put a big emphasis on that last fall by putting officers out on the mall.  We cannot put them there all the time, but we do try to minimize service vehicles.  Sometimes the service vehicles you see parked on the mall are actually in the building that they are parked in front of. However, they are not supposed to park anywhere.  So, as I said before please call DPS and they will go after them. 

 

A Senator: If one of the goals is to emphasize or try to make us more interdisciplinary, why did they decide to group the fine arts in one area, business in another area, science in another area, etc.? 

 

Vice Provost Harrison: That is one of the things that we have been debating with the consultants about, whether or not that is a better way to organize interdisciplinary research.  Their response has been--this doesn?t mean that you necessarily put all the fine arts here or all the business here but this is just to indicate, or provide an identity for the various activities that may take place.  It is more by type of activity and discipline that these areas are identified, and we are still talking to them about that.  It is a challenge to try to figure out the best way to encourage interdisciplinary activities/research since we are organized by discipline.  We try to focus on what kind of activity we can have, so that the disciplines that will work together are located in the same general area.  I do not think we have the expectation that we are going to have one building here, and it will be called an Interdisciplinary Building as such.  One of the things we felt is that since our academic disciplines and colleges are spread out in multiple buildings throughout campus, and in order to achieve what the consultants believe is adding interdisciplinary work, the disciplines need to be located together.  I am not sure that is a very satisfactory answer, but it is a challenge trying to work on this whole notion of interdisciplinary research given that we are organized by discipline. 

 

Senator Guleserian: What is the projected time of completion on these buildings?

 

Vice Provost Harrison: We are now looking at a roughly twenty-year timeline.  In terms of actual buildings, we do not have a time frame.  We are just looking at accommodating what we have perceived as the growth of the campus, the space needs that have been identified by the deans and others, and how could we organize that.  We have not put the updated Campus Master Plan together with a financial plan yet.  We are looking at how we think the campus might look in twenty years.

 

Senator Burns: I know you said the emphasis of this effort is on the core of the campus, but what about the relationship with Tempe and with the rest of the Valley.  It is realistic that we approach the campus by automobile today--and those employment and activity relationships will probably change in twenty years.  How is that being brought forward?

 

Vice Provost Harrison: We have looked at this in terms of how you access the campus and that is why we talk about these gateway concepts.  We also put in a parking and transit master plan because you are right; many people are still going to come in cars to campus and primarily down Rural Road.  That is going to be from the 202 and from the 60.  Those will be the primary places in which people access the campus, so this entry point is going to become much more important, and we are hoping that people do use the light rail.  We hope that will be the access to the campus through its proposed gateways, from the rest of the Valley.

 

President-Elect Watson: Have we made a decision as to what we are going to do with the five acres that the University has along the Rio Salado? 

 

Vice Provost Harrison: We own about 36 acres along the Rio Salado, and we have just started to talk about what we would put on the Rio Salado?the Rio Salado master planning effort was not the focus of this exercise.  We have an agreement in principle with the developer to put apartments and working with some core developments.  The university has retained five acres separately for university activities.  Then we have nine acres that we are talking actively with Sun Core about, and are considering them as a possible location for the Arizona Biomedical Institute.   We have reached no conclusion about the development of Rio Salado, but in general, we are looking at a mixed use: office, residential, retail, and possibly a major university biomedical complex. 

 

Senator Gully: How sensitive do you think the designers are in having the campus green areas established?to the use of native plants?

 

Vice Provost Harrison: Very.  We have two consultants, Hargraves and Associates is the principal consultant but they are in association with Todd and Associates, a local landscape planning firm.  In fact, Todd and Associates has done a lot of the landscape planning for the Rio Salado.  That is why they are emphasizing that this landscape uses local plants, plants that do not require substantial amounts of water.  You have to be considerate of that.  So, the answer is very much so.

 

Thank you all very much, and again if there are further comments you may want to make, just let Dr. Munk know because he is an active member of our committee.  We hope to have the Master Plan completed by the end of the semester, so we can present it to the Board of Regents in June.  We are coming down toward the final version, we hope. 

 

Senate President Munk: I think this is all very exciting for the future, and I think the Campus Master Plan will provide a blueprint as to how we proceed.  I am very pleased with the outcome of this work on this update.

6.A  Executive Committee (Morton Munk).

I would like to mention that in addition to the business that is coming before the Senate today, there was another matter discussed at the last meeting of the Executive Committee (March 4).  That was the matter of a Faculty Staff Appreciation Day that most of you are familiar with.  For those of you that may have been present at the event this past year, you probably noticed that it was not very well attended.  In fact, it was not well advertised and I do not think it turned out to be the most satisfying experience for many of those that attended.  Thanks to the efforts of Dave Burstein and Provost Glick, there will be an effort made to restore the luster and the importance of that event.  A committee has been appointed, chaired by John Davis, who is a manager of Season and Group Sales for Intercollegiate Athletics, and he and a number of other people including faculty members and myself are involved in trying to plan that event to restore some of the importance to it.  It is one of the very few events that we have where faculty, staff and administrators can get together in an informal social setting, and to mingle with student athletes and some of our coaches.  I think it is a very important activity that we should try to support and it is very clear, certainly to the Provost and others that we really need to improve the quality of that event so that it will be meaningful.  The plan is to build this event around a major game next year, and that would be the Stanford and ASU game on September 28.  This is also a time when awards are made to members of the university community in recognition of outstanding service to the university and the community at large, and that will take place during the half?time ceremonies.  I just wanted to alert you to this and to ask you to talk to your colleagues and see whether we might really try to make this the kind of important event that it ought to be.  There will be discounted tickets for the game, some sort of food service will be arranged, and we hope to have some student athletes there along with some of the coaches.  It will be an opportunity to interact that you do not often get to do on campus.  Again, I hope this is something that you will support.
