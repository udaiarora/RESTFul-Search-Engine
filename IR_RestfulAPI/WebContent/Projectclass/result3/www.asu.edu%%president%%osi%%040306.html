SustainableBusiness.com

Companies today understand that corporate social responsibility (CSR) forms an inextricable part of their reputations and brand identities. They spend ever-increasing amounts of corporate resources on improving the social, human, and environmental conditions under which companies operate.

Making Energy Self-Sufficient Buildings A Reality
CarbonFree News
The World Business Council for Sustainable Development says it is forming an alliance of leading global companies to determine how buildings can be designed and constructed so that they use no energy from external power grids, are carbon neutral, and can be built and operated at fair market values.

New Organic Dairy at UNH to Help Regional Farmers Go Organic
Boston Globe

An organic dairy farm currently under development at the University of New Hampshire is intended to help save family farms in the region by helping the farmers transition to organic production. 

Social Footprint Introduces Simple Elegance in Measuring Corporate Sustainability Performance
SocialFunds.com
There is no dearth of metrics to measure corporate social and environmental performance. What is lacking, however, is a means to assess the actual social and environmental impacts of corporate action--in other words, the degree to which corporate social responsibility (CSR) initiatives actually advance toward true sustainability.

Students Leave Green Legacy
Duluth News Tribune
Ask most college students whether they contribute enough to their institute of higher learning, and you're likely to get a nasty look. Between tuition, room and board, books and fees, most students are up to their eyeballs in debt.But at Northland College in Ashland, students voted a few years ago to raise their own student fees and give even more money to their school.

The sustainable seafood movement seems to be gathering momentum, but the question remains: Will it actually help protect the oceans from environmental concerns such as overfishing, or will it just mean more expensive fish sticks for U.S. consumers?
