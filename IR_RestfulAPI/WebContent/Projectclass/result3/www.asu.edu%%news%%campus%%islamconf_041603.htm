April 16, 2003

Contemporary Islam conference provides insight on conflict

Understanding the world of Islam has never been so important to Americans.

With the tragic events of September 11th, 2001, subsequent wars in Afghanistan and Iraq, and now the reconstruction of those countries, the U.S. is ever more engaged with the Islamic world. A major question on everyone's mind lately is: do we have any idea of what we are agreeing to do?

For more than a millennia, the Islamic and Western worlds have co-existed, yet remained largely foreign to each other. American military forces now find themselves occupying lands where ancient tribal differences, conflicts between the different branches of Islam, and complex political histories all serve to complicate an already thorny situation. Islam is also a growing religion within the U.S. In general, the Islamic world is strategically important to us, yet many Americans know very little about it.

With this in mind, Arizona State University's new Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict is presenting "Faces and Voices of Islam in the Modern World", a public conference on April 29 and 30, featuring internationally known authorities in the field. Among the presenters will be scholars in Islamic religion, history, law, politics and contemporary affairs, including journalist, author and CNN terrorism analyst Peter L. Bergen, who will give the keynote address and moderate the conference's concluding panel discussion.

Bergen's opening lecture is at 7:30 p.m. on Tuesday, April 29 in the Carson Ballroom of ASU's Old Main. Conference presentations begin at 8:00 a.m. and end at noon on Wednesday, April 30, also in the Carson Ballroom. Linell E. Cady, ASU Professor of Religious Studies and Director of the Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict will host the conference. ASU President Michael M. Crow will give the welcoming remarks on both days.

"Major research universities, such as ASU, have an overarching responsibility to address some of the most pressing societal issues of our time," says Crow. "Our new Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict will be a model for a new kind of 'use-inspired' scholarship that can have great meaning and effect on the world around us. Its larger goal is to provide our community and our country with the comprehensive study of the interaction of religions, cultures, and societies," he said.

The conference's opening lecture is likely to be of interest to anyone who wants to understand the motives and issues behind Islamic terrorism. For nearly a decade, Bergen has covered Osama bin Laden and the Jihadist terrorist network. He is one of only a few Western journalists to have interviewed bin Laden face to face. He is an internationally respected authority on global terrorism and has traveled extensively to the places where al-Qaeda has flourished - Afghanistan, Yemen, Egypt and Pakistan - to learn the truth about bin Laden and those who work with him. Over the past four years, Bergen has interviewed scores of insiders, from bin Laden associates and family members to Taliban leaders, CIA officials, and former Pakistani Prime Minister Benazir Bhutto. His expert commentary on Islamic terrorism is regularly seen and heard on CNN, ABC News, PBS, National Public Radio, The Daily Telegraph, The New Republic, The Washington Times, Vanity Fair, and The Times of London.

Bergen's recent book, Holy War, Inc.: Inside the Secret World of Osama bin Laden, has been a New York Times bestseller, and is considered by many to be the most authoritative analysis to date on the world of al-Qaeda.

Other conference presentations include three lectures on the morning of April 30 by prominent scholars in the fields of Islamic religion, law and history. The first presentation is a lecture entitled "Conjuring Islam after 9/11" by Bruce B. Lawrence, Nancy and Jeffrey Marcus Humanities Professor of Religion at Duke University. His scholarship is especially concerned with the interplay between religion and ideology. His award winning monograph, Defenders of God: The Fundamentalist Revolt Against the Modern Age (1989/1995) is a seminal study of fundamentalism. His latest monograph, Shattering the Myth: Islam beyond Violence (1998/2000), focuses more specifically on fundamentalism in Islam. He is also the author of New Faiths, Old Fears: Muslims and Other Asian Immigrants in American Religious Life (Columbia University Press) and The Complete Idiot's Guide to Religions Online (Macmillan, 2000).

The second presentation is "Conflicting Visions and Competing Voices of Islam After September 11, 2001" by Khaled Abou El Fadl, the Omar and Azmeralda Alfi Distinguished Fellow in Islamic Law at the UCLA Law School. The author of six books on Islam and Islamic law, including The Place of Tolerance in Islam, Rebellion and Violence in Islamic Law, and The Authoritative and Authoritarian in Islamic Discourses, El Fadl is a controversial scholar of Islam that U.S. News and World Report has called "a thorn in the side of reactionary Muslims around the world." His scholarly work concerns the relation of Islamic law to religion and daily practice. As a legal scholar, he is famous for questioning the accuracy of literalist interpretations of that law that are practiced by Islamic fundamentalists.

The final lecture of the morning will be "Conflict and Controversy in Contemporary U.S. Islamic Trends" by Yvonne Haddad, Professor of Islam and Christian-Muslim Relations at Georgetown University. Haddad is an expert on twentieth-century Islam, including the intellectual, social and political history of the Arab world, and Islam in North America and the West. Her current research is on Islamic revolutionary movements and Muslims in the West. She has published 14 books and is a member of the Council on Foreign Relations, Middle East Policy Council, the New England Region of the American Academy of Religion, the American Council for the Study of Islamic Societies, and the Arab-American Anti-discrimination Committee.

A panel composed of ASU Religious Studies scholars M. Sani Umar, Mark Woodward, and David Damrel will briefly respond to each presentation. The morning will conclude with a panel discussion, hosted by Bergen and featuring the conference presenters.

"Faces and Voices of Islam in the Modern World" is free and open to the public, but reservations are requested, as space is limited. For reservations, call 480-965-1044. The event will also be webcast.

ASU's Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict was established in January 2003. The Center's mission is to stimulate and support research and education on the role of religion in public controversies, from the civil to the violent, in a global context. Committed to the interdisciplinary and comparative study of religion, the Center will sponsor a broad range of programs and activities, engaging many audiences, that create a robust climate of inquiry and discourse within the ASU community, the Phoenix metropolitan area and, more broadly, nationally and internationally.

The Center's location in Arizona, one of the fastest growing population centers nationally, provides a unique opportunity to address the religious heritage and beliefs from numerous communities characterized by recent immigration and historical representation. Faculty and students from across the university will be actively involved in broad-based activities that include: seed grant programs for collaborative research and curriculum development; scholarly and public conferences; visiting lecture series; faculty seminar series; visiting appointments; doctoral and post-doctoral fellowships; and undergraduate research awards.

For more information on the conference, the Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict and on future events, got to http://www.asu.edu/clas/csrc/ or call 480-965-7187.
