May 15, 2003

ASU launches global effort to study cities, urban growth

ASU is taking its leading research on urban areas and environments global with the formation of the Consortium for the Study of Rapidly Urbanizing Regions.

The effort will combine ASU's successful environmental science and engineering research activities in metropolitan Phoenix, the most rapidly growing urban area in the United States, with other related studies and expand the work to global dimensions.

"More than ever before, the world is becoming urbanized," said ASU Center for Environmental Studies Director Charles Redman, who will direct the new consortium. "The greatest single challenge to the world today is the issue of rapidly urbanizing regions - having them develop so that they don't become seeds of poverty and terrorism, but sustainable places. Our work in this area has been strong and an expanded research partnership could have major impact."

The consortium is one of a series of new "use-inspired" research initiatives being developed by ASU President Michael Crow to apply fundamental research to pressing community, national and global problems.

"The policy decisions that we make today will determine the quality of our environment 100 years in the future," said Crow. "To think clearly about sustainability, we must understand the relationships between all the variables involving human interaction with natural systems past, present and future."

Much of ASU's research in urban areas and environments has been conducted under the direction of the Center for Environmental Studies (CES). CES is home to the Central Arizona-Phoenix Long Term Ecological Research (CAPLTER) project, one of only two urban sites in the National Science Foundation's system of ecological research centers. Since the creation of CAPLTER in 1997, the center has leveraged funding to become an extensive socio-environmental research enterprise, receiving NSF support for a new interdisciplinary graduate research program in urban ecology and for a new nationwide study of Agricultural Landscapes in Transition.

Other ASU projects related to urban research include satellite-based remote sensing studies of global urban environments, extensive educational outreach programs, cutting-edge environmental informatics projects and Greater Phoenix 2100, an initiative to help decision-makers understand the very long-term implications of current urban policies and issues.

"Phoenix is the ideal platform for this initiative in that it is among the most rapidly urbanizing regions in North America," said ASU Vice President for Research and Economic Affairs Jonathan Fink.

Building on the work of the many ASU projects, the consortium will bring a new focus on rapid urban development issues.

"The consortium will be involved in developing global partnerships while being based at ASU," Redman said. "It will be a place where urban things at all scales will be thought about and pursued. Beginning next year, it will sponsor conferences and other events to engage the community locally, nationally and internationally. The goal is to be a world leader in this critical field of social and scientific study."
