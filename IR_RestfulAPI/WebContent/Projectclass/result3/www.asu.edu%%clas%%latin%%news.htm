May 23, 2006

The Andes and Amazon Field School Initiates its Seventh Annual Gathering

The seventh annual Andes and Amazon Field School kicks off next month! The summer 2006 group includes approximately 25 students and 8 expert faculty members from ASU and various other universities. Session one will be from June 6-30 and session two will be from July 4-28. During each session, students will be embarking on a truly rich and culturally diverse experience including a stay with the Santo Urcu Amazonian Quichua Community. To accommodate the interests of all the participants, classes are available on the areas of Quichua Language and Culture, Tropical Plant Diversity & Ethnobotany, International Health & Nursing, Medical Anthropology and Indigenous Education. Teachers also have the opportunity to take part in a bilingual education program in Ecuador as Teacher Fellows. This program prepares students for serious research on indigenous cultures. To date, there are over 300 alumni from ASU and other universities that have participated in the Field School; many have proceeded to further their academic careers and have become experts in their areas of study. The Center for Latin American Studies is proud to facilitate the relationships and learning experiences that are evolving between ASU students and Indigenous peoples of Latin America through the Andes and Amazon Field School in Ecuador.

May 24, 2006

Quichua Language Classes Available This Fall

Luz María de la Torre Amaguaña,
Quichua Language Instructor.

Luz María De La Torre was born and raised in the Otavalo community in Ecuador and has an impressive professional record of research, teaching and community involvement. Quichua language is a gateway into Quechua culture, which values community and mutual help (ayni, minga, randi-randi). Many aspects of modern Amerindian cultures and worldviews are encoded in the language, and there are many interesting folk tales and oral histories recorded in Quechua. Moreover, Quichua speaking people are highly organized and visible actors in at least 6 different countries of South America. Quichua language courses at Arizona State University prepare students for serious research on indigenous cultures.
