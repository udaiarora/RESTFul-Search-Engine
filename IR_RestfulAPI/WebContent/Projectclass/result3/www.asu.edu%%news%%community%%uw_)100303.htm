October 3, 2003

Volunteer experience sheds light on United Way funding needs

Giving away loads of money to local charities in need sounds like a fun gig. But, two ASU employees who have served on United Way allocation committees for many years know firsthand the job is full of tough choices.

As volunteers for the Valley of the Sun United Way, Margo Rivera-Wilson and Wendy Hultsman say deciding the financial fortune of community programs is an experience that is both heart-wrenching and worthwhile.

Rivera-Wilson, a program coordinator for Extended Campus Programs, has been volunteering for Valley of the Sun United Way for about 15 years as an allocation committee panelist. Valley of the Sun United Way has several allocation committees, each charged with distributing funds to charities in a particular service area - such as "Investing in Youth" or "Overcoming Disease and Disabilities."

"Allocation committee visits are a chance for the agency to show us what they have done with their United Way dollars and help us to understand what they are asking for in the coming year," she says. "These site visits are very rewarding and really give the volunteers a snapshot of what the agency does for the community."

Hultsman is an associate professor for Recreation and Tourism Management at ASU West. Like Rivera-Wilson, she has served on allocation panels for several years and says volunteering on a committee can seem a bit overwhelming at times, but is always time well spent.

Hultsman says the annual process begins in September when she and her fellow committee members conduct site visits to all agencies who will be submitting proposals to their panel.

At the site visits, panelists listen to presentations from board members, clientele and staff. Agencies explain how United Way funding from the past year was used to improve the community.

"The agencies whom we visit are truly making a positive impact on the lives of thousands of individuals and families in Maricopa County," Hultsman says. "Until you actually make these site visits you do not get the full breadth of how important, and daunting their roles are."

In January, panel members receive program proposals from each agency and evaluate them based on established criteria.

Early each spring, each panel meets to discuss submitted proposals.

"We then determine if there are any questions we would like to ask of the agency and if there is a need for them to come in for a short presentation," Hultsman says. "We get a preliminary idea of the level of funding we, as a panel, recommend - full, partial or none."

Then in May, allocation committees meet so panelists can go over all of the data, and make the final funding recommendations. Hultsman says this is always a heart-wrenching job because there is never enough money to go around.

Rivera-Wilson says she hopes her fellow ASU employees will support the United Way either by making a monetary donation, by becoming a volunteer or by doing both.
"I feel it is very important to give to United Way," Rivera-Wilson says. "If you do not have dollars to donate this year for the campaign, I would look at volunteering for either United Way or one of the agencies that receive United Way dollars. This is such a good way to give back to your community."

United Way pledge cards have been distributed to all ASU employees and are due Oct. 15. This year employees will also be able to conveniently make donations online. For more information, contact your department's United Way unit representative or visit the Valley of the Sun United Way Web site at (www.vsuw.org).
