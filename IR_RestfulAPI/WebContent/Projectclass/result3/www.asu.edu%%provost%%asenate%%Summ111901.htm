3.A  Senate President?s Report (Morton Munk).

 

I would like to discuss three items today.  First, an update on the presidential search.  The search committee has been active in three areas recently.  We just finished our twenty-second open forum with various groups, both internal and external stake holders of the university.  A summary of the forums is being prepared for the search committee?s use and that summary will be used in developing a final set of leadership characteristics that the committee will use in selecting candidates for the next president of ASU.  There are applications at the present which are being reviewed by the committee and there are what we call prospects, potential candidates for the position and the committee has been discussing the merits of these prospects for the position.  We are still accepting nominations and I urge any of you that ideas or know promising candidates to submit names to me or any member of the search committee.  I am pleased that many of you and your colleagues have already made some interesting nominations.  You can find the final revision of the leadership characteristics and also many of you are to check on the membership of the committee, that is available on the Arizona Board of Regents Web Site which can be accessed via the ASU homepage, just click on the box entitled presidential search.  The committee hopes to invite the most promising candidates for the position to campus, if all goes well, in February.  There will be open meetings for each candidates visit and you and your colleagues are strongly urged to attend these open meetings.  The committee wants to hear from you on your view of the strengths and weaknesses of those candidates and I assure you that that information will be factored into recommendations that are made by the search committee.  There are five members of the faculty, from Main, East, and West on that committee and I can assure you that ours will be a strong voice in arriving at some of the final recommendations. 

 

At the last meeting of the Board of Regents in September, there was a study session on intercollegiate athletics.  It is clear that some issues relating to intercollegiate athletics are very much on the minds of some members of the Board of Regents but one thing that may have stimulated this interest and discussion is the latest Knight Commission report: A Call to Action: Reconnecting College Sports and Higher Education.  That report contains some interesting and controversial recommendations.  Then on October 31st, Regent Jack Jewett asked the three Senate presidents (ASU Main, NAU, and UofA) to meet with him to discuss some of these issues.  He asked for our involvement in a review of some aspects of intercollegiate athletics; this is an issue, as you can imagine, that can easily the nerves of some people, but I think that if handled thoughtfully, the faculty can make some constructive contributions to this review process.  In particular, one of the goals is to improve the academic experience for student athletes and this is an area in which we can make a contribution.  Jerry Kingston, our Faculty Athletic Representative will be giving us a report today on the academic status of our student athletes.  This is an ongoing discussion and there will be a meeting in Tucson at the time of the next Board of Regents meeting.  I have been invited to that meeting and I will certainly keep you informed as this discussion unfolds. 

 

The University Master Planning Committee has been meeting throughout the semester; they have also been meeting throughout the summer months.  There has been substantial progress.  A draft report was presented last Friday, the early version of the Master Plan which was prepared by architectural firm of Hargraves and Associates from San Francisco.  Two of their members worked very close with the committee in developing this master plan.  This comprehensive plan will guide the development of the university campus over the next ten years or so.  From my perspective, this draft report is a very creative plan that ought to very substantially enrich our campus environment.  I am very impressed with some of the suggestions.  There are a few elements I can call to your attention.  1) There is a conclusion that there is adequate space within the existing boundaries of the university?s future needs for construction.  There may have to be some discussion about the height of buildings depending on the extent of growth here on our Main Campus.  The plan reflects what appears to be a trend toward interdisciplinary programs here on campus.  This has been taken into account in developing the organization of the campus, trying to put units and colleges that interact with one another as close together as feasible, in order to further encourage this kind of interdisciplinary interaction.  The plan is based on what the architects are calling ?The Campus Mosaic Concept.?  Some of the other important features include developmental aspects of the East Campus which is not very extensively developed, also the South Campus across Apache Blvd and the North Campus at the Rio Salado area, a plan with the University controls.  There is concern about the creation of attractive gateways to the campus and the preservation and enhancement of open spaces, enhancements to the malls.  There is considerable and attention to the movement of people, bicycles, vehicles, skateboards, etc. across our campus.  As this plan moves to the later stages, I will invite one of the campus architects if they can meet with us and give us an overview of the Master Plan.

3.C  University Provost?s Report (Milton Glick).

 

I will comment on a couple of items that Mort and George brought up with respect to George?s comment on the situation in the mid-East.  I know that everyone or almost everyone is really stretched but to the extent there are some of you that might wish to give a small one-hour seminar on issues relating to that, I think it would be a great thing for our students because never before our ignorance of Islam in particular, but of geography and of greater significance now.  If anyone has expertise and they would be willing to set up a one-credit course, I think it would be a wonderful service to the greater community. 

 

On the issue that Mort brought up on the Master Plan, I believe it is a very interesting Master Plan; I think it is more interesting than the one we did ten years ago.  It tries to take us further outside our historic bounds.  I think there are three issues that are important 1) What do we do about parking?  Every time we take out a lot out of service and put up a new building we have to put up a structure--and structures cost $9,000 per parking space.  2) In addition to that problem, the campus edges really suffer from the parking structures because there is no real way to get into this campus.  It is a very unfriendly edge.  I think that is something, which in the long run hurts this community.  3) The one that a lot of us have different opinions on is the planners? recommendation for residence halls within the core campus.  I think we have a whole variety of opinions on that issue but it is one worthy of serious exploration.

 

As an update on Tempe Center, the design of the center was to be that the north five acres would be commercial and the south five acres would be protected for academic use.  We then want to have a parking structure in the north five acres.  The developers, the City and the University have been in constant negotiations and discussion and at the last council meeting, a proposal was made to the council in which we said that we would not allow the commercial or parking structures to intrude into the south five acres.  That is, we would be disserving our legacy to the future if we did not protect the south five acres.  There had been real pressure to expand the structure into the south five acres.  There was real pressure to expand into the south five acres.  Secondly, there was pressure from the City of Tempe to create Ninth Street; Ninth Street is halfway between Tenth Street and University, which is really Eighth Street.  That street has never existed and we are firm that it cannot exist.  That would be a huge disservice, so, the entry to the structure will have to go underground.

 

Both of those are issues that we feel strongly on, the President feels strongly on.  It turns out neither of those issues was discussed at the Council meeting but the main issue was the desire of Tempe to have a grocery store.  There is a place in the commercial five acres where a grocery store could exist but it would require the city to subsidize it substantially because grocery stores cost four or five dollars a square foot and downtown space is more like twenty-five dollars a square foot.  The City of Tempe rejected this plan and there may be a motion to reconsider at the next Council meeting, which is November 29. We do not know about that for sure.

 

I want to give you a little insight on the budget because up until the end of last week, there was no serious conversation at the legislature.  Many of you saw that the Governor had brought forward a proposal for closing what she believes to be a 600-675 million dollar gap in each of the next two years.  Remembering that this year is already 1/3 of the way gone.  She proposed in that the 4% that we have already agreed to take, and we have already taken our cuts and got those cuts to our vice presidents?She also proposes delaying the 5%, taking each of the 5% increases, one was supposed to be in April 2002 and one in April of 2003 and moving that to a single 3% increase on July 1.  We think that protecting those salary increases is an absolute top priority.  For so long we worked so hard to try to narrow the gap for our faculty and staff with the market and we have made some progress and that would just put us way back.  The good thing is the legislature is really taking ownership of these salary increases.  I believe that they will try to protect those increases as long as they can but I am sure that some of you read about the many state employees who were picketing at the legislature when the governor made her statement at the legislature.  We will continue to fight very hard to protect those salary increases.

 

As of the end of last week, Laura Knaperek and Ruth Solomon, who are the chairs of the House and Senate Appropriations Committees and were strong supporters of education in general, and higher education in particular, had put together a potential set of reductions and revenue enhancements which depending on how you read it comes with something between $400-$600 million.  We just got a new version five minutes before I walked over here, so that is why I am being so unclear.  I was clear up until then.  Included in that is the 4% reduction.  They do not at this point put the salaries on the table, which means that as of now they are protecting salaries.  In the second year, Main Campus was to get restoration of the Eminent Scholar money that was removed from our budget five years ago.  That was to have been restored and never was.   They take that away from us, which is about $500,000 that we lose that would have come to us in the second year?it is a modest thing compared to these other things.  Other than that, I believe the universities were left whole.  The only major shift in the Knaperek-Solomon plan is the loss of the eminent scholar money in the second year.  If we could walk away keeping the salaries in tact, given the serious nature of the state?s funding problems, I think that could be a very good resolution.  Remember that no one has agreed to that yet and we do not know how many votes it has.  We don?t know if the calculus works, but at least that is a good sign. 

 

On a good note, I am pleased to inform you that freshmen persistence, that is how many of the freshmen that enrolled first time last September showed up here this September?  We are at 76% or 77%; ten years ago we were at 68% and had been there for fifteen years.  This was a strategic goal of the long range planning committee that Paul Burgess chaired, and of the leadership of the university for the last decade, to move that retention rate, which I consider a critical element of us taking our proper place among universities from 68-76% is really huge and it would not have happened without a lot of hard work from you on these problems.  I again want to express my appreciation for the way people have really worked to make that happen and to tell you it is not high enough yet! 

 

Thank you.

3.E   Faculty Athletic Representative (Jerry Kingston).

 

Thank you for the opportunity to come before you as I have in the past at least once or twice per year, to give you an update on the progress of our student athletes.  I have a couple of other issues that I would like to visit with you about briefly today. 

 

Restructuring of our audit program. In 1989, then President Nelson set up an Athletics Compliance Audit Committee that met three times a year.  There were 14 or 15 people that got together in a formal way until about 1997, when it became clear to us that we needed to revisit the whole method of conducting athletic compliance audits.  Between 1989 and 1996 this formal exterior process was replacing, or inhibiting, the development of internal controls and internal compliance systems.  When Kevin White arrived in 1995 as Athletic Director, I began to work with him and others to try to develop an internal control system in which the Intercollegiate Athletics Department would be in charge of their own compliance program.  Over the last several years we have tried to revise our audit methodology.  We are testing the outcomes of the internal control systems within the Athletics Department.  And that further suggested that we needed to have a month-by-month capability to oversee and work with the people in the Athletics Department.  At my suggestion and with the concurrence with many others who were involved in that effort, last year President Coor disestablished the old Athletics Compliance Audit Committee and appointed a new team to work with the Athletics Department, including Paul Ward, Vice President and ASU General Counsel, Gene Smith is directly involved as Director of Athletics, Tom Callarman, chair of the Intercollegiate Athletics Board, Jim Rund, Associate Vice President for Student Affairs and myself are now working on this activity.  We have recently let a new contract through a competitive bidding process, to obtain an outside firm to assist us in developing what we hope is going to be a state of the art compliance system.  We recently awarded that contract for a three-year period with options to renew, if both parties wish to go beyond that.  We have today the development of a new athletics compliance system, which I believe will be more effective.  The athletics director is interested in using the outcomes of a real time reporting system on violations and perhaps as well incidences of not following good practices even if there are no violations, to be able to use that in personnel evaluations each year.  That system is up and running.  There is now an NCAA requirement that every aspect of the intercollegiate athletics program needs to be subject to audit of Baseball, so they had the 1998, 1999, and 2000 AY and then in 2001 whether or not any of them returned.  In fact we had seven student athletes in that class and none of them were here in 2001, and a significant factor in those cases was the junior year draft.  If I go back and calculate the 2- year persistence rates for just the 1999 and 2000 classes, and then ask who was here in 2001, the persistence rates for men and women?s sports are identical.  If I do a one-year persistence rate, not by design but by accident, it is also identical for men and women.  A good piece of the problem is still the end of year junior draft in the sport of Baseball but if you look through those numbers generally, you have reason to be encouraged that we are going to continue to see some higher graduation rates, as future classes end up going through the six-year cycle. 

 

The final set of comments I would like to make today have to do with the reform movement in intercollegiate athletics, and to follow up on a comment that Dr. Munk made earlier.  The Knight Commission started off a very strong reform movement in 1991 when they published their first report.  They expressed public concerns about academic reform, about a need to restructure the governance of the NCAA to put the university presidents more nearly in control, to develop a certification program that was something like the accreditation programs we use elsewhere.  The NCAA put all of those reform elements in place by 1995.  Now, ten years later, the Knight Commission came back and produced a follow-up report, which was published in June of 2001 this year.  About a month later, Miles Brandt who is the president at Indiana University gave a speech in front of the National Press Club in Washington, in which he used some of the criticisms of the Knight Commission report.  He also began to talk about an Academics First sort of program.  That, in conjunction with some other books that have come out in the last year or so criticizing the role of athletics and its fit in higher education, caused our Academic Senate in the spring of last year to pass a resolution encouraging the Presidents and Chancellors of the Pac10 to seriously begin to engage a number of those issues.  They have been doing this, and now they have been joined by the presidents of the other 5 bowl championship series conferences.  Those are the biggest 1A conferences in the US; the Big East, Big12, Southeastern Conference, Atlantic Coast Conference, and Pac10 are now all meeting together and starting to try to produce consensus among the 60 or 70 largest athletics programs about how to address these issues.  Two representatives of each of those 6 conferences are meeting tomorrow to develop specific legislative recommendations that I believe will be sent forward to the NCAA Board of Directors in the next three to four months.  In addition to what Pac10 is doing, as Mort has mentioned, the Regents are paying close attention to the Knight Commission report, and some of the things that we are doing on our campuses to address these matters.  All three of the campuses within the state are currently involved in that effort and I think that our Academic Senate has a legitimate interest in this also.  If there are any new developments that come up during the year, I will be glad to come back to visit with the Senate and update you on our progress. 

 

Arizona State is close to completing its response to the NCAA Committee on Athletic Certification.  We were the first school to be processed through the second cycle of the certification effort.  We submitted our self-study and we had a peer review on campus last year.  We submitted a report and the Committee on Athletics Certification came back to us and said there some issues to address.  I hope that sometime soon in 2002, they will announce that Arizona State has been fully certified, for a second cycle of certification.

 

Provost Glick: That 72% is really for three years, not one year. 

 

Jerry Kingston: Yes.  The 72% is a combination of the 1998, 1999, and 2000 classes.

 

Provost Glick: That is really impressive progress.  Also, what is the difference between a recruited athlete and a scholarship athlete?

 

Jerry  Kingston: A recruited student athlete according to the NCAA is any athlete that made an official campus visit and each student athlete who receives more than one recruiting phone call to that student?s home.  For the majority there is a big overlap, but there are some very good recruited student athletes who receive academic scholarships and grants and aid, and they end up being taken out of the calculation.  That has caused a problem and the coaches are asking us to put that information in on all of the students that have been recruited to join our program, even if they do not get an athletics grant and aid because they may at least gotten an academic one and that is why we now include those students in our calculations.
