March 31, 2006

2 engineering professors win NSF's Early Career Awards

Cody Friesen and Karamvir S. Chatha, assistant professors in the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering, have earned recognition for outstanding research efforts in the form of National Science Foundation Early Career Awards.

The awards, which together total $900,000, represent the top honor the U.S. government bestows on scientists and engineers beginning their careers. It recognizes those who show exceptional potential for leadership in advancing knowledge in their fields. Each year, about 15 percent of a few thousand applicants are chosen to receive the award.

Friesen's five-year, $500,000 NSF award will advance research and education in nanomechanics at ASU. His research holds promise to have far-reaching impact on a broad range of applications. It also will fund programs to introduce high school teachers and students to some of the latest endeavors in nanomechanics.

Friesen's research will focus on ultra-thin metallic films of the kinds used in everything from computer technology, to window coatings, to single-atom-thick films used in fuel-cell catalysts.

Improvement in the strength and resiliency of ultra-thin metallic films and the effectiveness of such catalysts could lead to significant advances in a number of multibillion-dollar industries, Friesen says.

The Early Career Award grant will enable Friesen to establish three educational outreach projects.

Through Research Experience for Teachers, Friesen will bring a high school teacher from the White Mountain Apache tribe in Arizona to the ASU campus for two months in the summer. The teacher will get a firsthand view of nanotechnology projects at the university.

"The idea is that the teacher will take the excitement about what's happening in nanotechnology back to Native American students and maybe inspire their interest in the field," Friesen says.

Native Americans are the most underrepresented group in science and engineering, he points out.

A six-week Summer Research Experience in Nanomechanics (SREN) program will provide opportunities for top Arizona high school students to participate in some of Friesen's research.

Another program will involve the development of nanomechanics visualization software to produce movies depicting atoms going through the motions of various processes, ranging from melting to deformation. Its aim is to capitalize on the visual orientation of "the video-game generation" as a way to spark youngsters' interest in science and engineering, Friesen says.

Friesen graduated from ASU with a degree in materials science and engineering. He returned to the university in 2004 as an assistant professor after earning a doctorate in the field from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

Chatha's five-year, $400,000 NSF grant will be used for research to develop advanced high-performance and energy-efficient microprocessor chips.

He says his research data will quickly find its way into graduate-level and undergraduate class work and research projects, as well as courses and other resources available to industry.

Chatha's winning proposal outlines a research endeavor aimed at vastly improving the communication infrastructure of microprocessor chips.

The goal is to increase the capabilities of high-performance processor chips used in computing systems, such as those for cell phones, multimedia set-top boxes, high-definition television, medical devices, automotive technology, network processing and gaming terminals.

Today's computer chips typically contain two to four processors on each chip, but chip designers want to be able to produce high-performance System-on-Chip architectures that each contain tens of thousands of processors.

Innovative on-chip communication infrastructure is essential for achieving the high-performance targets for the future System-on-Chip architectures.

Chatha's project will address the development of such an on-chip communication infrastructure, or Network-on-Chip, for the next generation of computer systems.

"This research is about answering questions, such as, 'What does such a communication infrastructure network look like?' " Chatha says. "How many of these processors can I use on a chip, and how do I connect them together? How are the microprocessors going to use the on-chip network for high-performance communication?"

The challenge is to find a way for the Network-on-Chip to support high-performance computing without using a lot more power to operate or requiring a larger area.

Chatha's project includes an education plan, part of which is to involve graduate and undergraduate students in various aspects of the research.

He also plans to develop a catalogue of course projects that delve into Network-on-Chip design, develop industry-focused curriculum on the subject, use the promising research to recruit students for graduate studies and improve instruction about engineering at the K-12 education level.

Chatha has been an ASU faculty member since 2001, after earning a doctorate the same year in computer science and engineering from the University of Cincinnati.
