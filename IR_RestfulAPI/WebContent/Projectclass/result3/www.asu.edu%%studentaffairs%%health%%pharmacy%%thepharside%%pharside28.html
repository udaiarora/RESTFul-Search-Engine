What is All This Talk About IBS?

            Leading the life of a college student can be difficult and stressful at times. Juggling between homework, studying for midterms, and working a part-time job can cause anxiety and stress. Emotional stress can exacerbate a condition known as Irritable Bowel Syndrome or IBS. Irritable Bowel Syndrome is a gastrointestinal disorder that ranks second next to the common cold as a cause of missed work and accounts for approximately 3 million physician visits in the U.S. each year. IBS afflicts 10-20% of the general American population and 11% of university students. This disorder occurs more frequently in women between the ages of 20 and 30. Only a small proportion of people with symptoms of IBS actually seek treatment and many rely on over-the-counter remedies alone.

Irritable Bowel Syndrome is normally characterized by altered bowel function and abdominal pain. Symptoms of IBS can vary from person to person but common symptoms include: diarrhea alternating with constipation, abdominal pain that follows meals and is relieved by a bowel movement, gas, bloating, and mucus in the stool. IBS can be classified as mild, moderate, or severe. Therapy is specific to each classification.

 

Irritable Bowel Syndrome is generally classified as a functional disorder where the primary abnormality is an alteration in a physiological function (how the body works). IBS is not caused by an identifiable structural or biochemical cause. IBS can be tricky to diagnose because x-rays and blood tests usually reveal no abnormalities. IBS appears to be caused by a disturbance in the interaction between the brain, autonomic nervous system, and the intestines. This disturbance can alter bowel motility and/or sensory function which are the cause of the symptoms that an IBS sufferer experiences. Foods, medications, and stress can trigger symptoms of IBS. Stress can cause colon spasms, while chocolate, milk, alcohol, and fatty foods can cause diarrhea or constipation.

 

The objective of treatment for sufferers is to relieve the symptoms since there is no cure for IBS. Patients with mild IBS need to manage stress and make changes to their diet and lifestyle. Diet changes may include increasing fiber and eliminating fatty or greasy foods, alcohol, and gastrointestinal stimulants such as caffeine. Patients may manage stress through regular exercise or other relaxation techniques such as meditation or massage therapy.

 

Patients with moderate IBS need the same therapy as mild IBS sufferers but need additional medications. These patients may need to add a fiber supplement such as psyllium or methylcellulose for constipation and/or an over-the-counter medication such as loperamide (Imodium) to help control diarrhea. Antispasmodic medications such as hyoscyamine (Levsin) or dicyclomine (Bentyl) taken before meals may help with abdominal pain and bowel urgency.

Severe IBS patients need the same therapy as moderate IBS sufferers but may need to add tricyclic or SSRI antidepressants, or 5-HT3 or 5-HT4 medications. Tricyclic antidepressants such as nortriptyline (Pamelor) or amitriptyline (Elavil) are useful to treat abdominal pain. SSRI’s such as fluoxetine (Prozac) or paroxetine (Paxil) and other antidepressants such as bupropion (Wellbutrin) or venlafaxine (Effexor) may help to treat abdominal pain and depression. A 5-HT3 antagonist such as losetron (Lotrenox) may be useful to treat abdominal pain, urgency, and diarrhea. A 5-HT4 antagonist such as Tegaserod (Zelnorm) is useful for abdominal pain and constipation in women.

 

Once a diagnosis is made, patients may control the symptoms of IBS instead of allowing this condition to control them. It has become easier to diagnose this disorder because of the increase in number of IBS sufferers. The biggest challenge is to get patients to make diet and lifestyle changes to control their symptoms. It is vital that IBS sufferers talk to someone about their condition instead of suffering in silence.

-         Alysia Johnson, Pharm D candidate

 

SEEING RED!

Early Mixtecs in pre-Hispanic Mexico wore colored clothing that signaled social status.  Bright blues and reds were worn with pride.  We’re talking pre-1500’s here, so there was not a highly developed chemical industry to produce “fast” dyes.  “Fast” colors do not fade.  The Mixtecs used indigo for blue shades and cochineal for reds.  In the 1400’s, after the Aztecs conquered the Mayans, Mayan cities paid a tribute to Montezuma that included 40 bags of cochineal powder.  Native legumes served as a good source of indigo and small, scaled insects called cochineal (Dactylopius coccus or Coccus cacti) were an excellent source of crimson dye.  The cochineal lives on the pads of the prickly pear cactus (Opuntia platyopuntias), with the female sucking out juices while secreting a waxy substance.  The males of the species are puny little things that only live for about a week, giving them time for a brief orgy of mating activity, visiting as many females as possible.  Nature has seen fit to grant the males wings that no doubt enhance this process.  The adult males are so busy, they do not even have time to eat!

A complex enterprise arose to supply the culture with bright red dye.  Planting an excised pad can easily reproduce prickly pears.  Mixtec farmers planted pads already colonized by female cochineal.  These prickly gardens were fertilized with detritus like wood ash and other organic garbage.  The farmers were very protective of these little critters, pulling weeds, fencing out animals, shielding them from heavy rains and even building fires nearby on particularly cold nights.  There seemed to be some careful selection of insects that produced the best dyes, but there is evidence that these bugs were weaker, easily stressed by cold and rain. 

The actual color comes from carminic acid.  The Mixtecs used females, most likely hand picked and sun-dried.  We know from folk art practices still seen in some parts of Mexico that brighter reds are obtained by

boiling tejuté leaves (Miconia argentea) with the cloth to be dyed, then adding the powdered insects and finally fresh lime juice.  The tejuté leaves tend to make the dye “faster” and more intense.

The bright red colored fabrics impressed the Spaniards and they starting shipping the red cloth home to Spain, where it eventually became popular throughout Europe.  Profits were good, and local farmers grew wealthy enough to threaten the hacienda owners that lorded over the land.  The little insects were threatening the status quo! 

Inevitably, infested pads were smuggled to other locales (as far away as the Canary Islands and India) including the American Southwest.  In the 1800’s Navajos (who previously had not used red in their weavings) traded with Spanish soldiers for bayetas.  These bayetas were flannel blankets, often dyed with cochineal, that the Navajo unraveled and reused the red threads in new weavings.

In 1875 a red dye synthesized from coal tar became a cheaper alternative to cochineal and by the early 1900’s these aniline dyes had pretty much replaced cochineal.

By now, you may be wondering what this is doing in a pharmacy newsletter.  Cochineal has also been used as coloring in food, drugs and cosmetics.  Here in the U.S. it has shown up in pharmaceuticals, sausage, fish and shrimp, candy, pies, lipstick and rouge, and, of course, maraschino cherries.  Red Dyes 2 and 40 currently used in cherries are being evaluated as potential carcinogens and cochineal is being reconsidered as a possibly safe cherry colorant.  A small percentage of people are allergic to carminic acid, but probably no more than that are allergic to Red Dye 2 or 40. If you see “E120” on a food label ingredient list it contains cochineal.  Different salts of carminic acid are still used in the food industry.  The insoluble calcium carmine and ammonium carmine are used in alcoholic beverages, cheeses, various desserts and sauces.  Water-soluble forms appear in alcoholic drinks.

The Hyperactive Children’s Support Group recommends carminic acid, its salts and other cochineal entities for elimination from children’s diets.     CKL

Eat Your Way to Sexual Health


Mankind has long been fascinated by the idea of certain foods as aphrodisiacs.  The very word derives from the love goddess Aphrodite.  Word on the ancient streets was that Aphrodite considered sparrows to be sacred because of their “amorous nature.”  The heathens responded by grinding the poor little birdies into their love potions.  We’ve come a long way since then.

It started by identifying foods that resembled genitalia or were seeds or eggs.  Often foods were identified as treatments for loss of libido, impotence or infertility simply because they were nutritious.  Undernourishment was quite common in the old days and good nutrition is a primary path to sexual health.

So lets take a quick look at foods with a reputation.

Before we get started on the sexy stuff, I should mention that the ancient Greeks warned against dill, lettuce, watercress, rue, lentils and water lilies as anti-aphrodisiacs.  Hmmm, maybe I’ll skip that salad.

Both ancient Greeks and Romans claimed that sucking on Aniseeds was said to increase your desire.  I found them flavorful but that’s about it.

Asparagus is thought of as a sensual food, mostly due to its phallic shape and there may be something to feeding your lover these green stalks.  It makes for potent urine but no discernable pharmacological effect.

Arugula, frequently found in upscale salads is a pungent green whose seeds are sometimes called “rocket” seeds.  You get the image?

The Aztecs called the avocado tree “Ahuacuatl” which translates as “testicle tree.”  They do tend to hang in pairs.  They are delicious with a sensuous texture.

Bananas have a marvelous phallic shape and make excellent training tools when teaching about proper condom use but offer little other than providing potassium, B vitamins and suggestive oral maneuvers.

Mass quantities of sweet basil are reported to boost sex drive and fertility.  Haven’t tried it.

Those fun-loving Aztecs called chocolate “nourishment of the gods”.  We know that good chocolate affects neurotransmitters in a positive manner and that can’t be bad for sex.  A nice dark chocolate with a glass of vasodilating wine when given by your lover in soft candlelight with Barry White singing in the background…  OK, I got as far as the letter C and I have to go now.      Be sure to let me know if you want more:]     CKL
