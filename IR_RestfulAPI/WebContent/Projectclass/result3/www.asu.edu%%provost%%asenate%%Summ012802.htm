3.B  University Provost?s Report (Milton Glick).     

    

I am sure many of you are aware that several key legislators have announced that they believe the stadium should be built on the Karsten Golf Course.  The President has taken a strong stand on that issue, not saying that university property should not be used to help a stadium come about, but if used, that it must be reciprocated in full value.  If it is ASU property that has potential long term use, it has to be replaced by equivalently located space, and clearly there is no way to replace the entire golf course around the stadium.  I believe the President will be making a statement on that today.  He has laid down a set of conditions which he has communicated with the PSA, the Governor, the Legislature and the City of Tempe, that I think lays out well the conditions under which some of our property may be used.  One of the major concerns about this proposal is that they will be talking about parking for 100 events a year.  That will have a great impact on our campus.  The other strange thing is, it states that the windfall that  comes out of this will be split by the three universities.  The windfall that came out the UofA?s Cotton Center was not shared by all three universities, but we will be happy to send them a bill, tomorrow, if it has not gone out already (much laughter here).

 

On the budget, I think that we handled the budget, thanks to many of you, in the least disruptive way possible, that is, we took a 4.5% cut.  We said we would first protect student progress and we did that and we added sections actually.  We said we would, to the best of our ability, protect strategic research programs after that, which we tried to do.  We have the largest enrollment in our history and the suggestion coming out of the legislature that we would have our additional tuition removed, as part of balancing the state?s budget, is really so detrimental to the institution and to the state.  We understand the problems the state has, and that is why we could take on a 4.5% cut without too much whining, and we know that if they have to find more, we are prepared to help with that problem.  However, we can?t protect student progress and then lose the tuition and the enrollment growth money that goes to pay for that.  It really does cost money to educate students.  We have tried to protect not only class access but the quality of classes. If you tell us that we can?t keep the new money, which is a combination of new tuition and enrollment growth money and also results from higher rates which the students agreed to in exchange for certain qualitative improvements, it puts us in an impossible situation.  So, we feel very strongly that a solution is not to take the growth and tuition money and put that into the state?s budget problem, although we understand that the problem is very real.  We have been, and will continue to be, as constructive as possible in taking on our share of the burden, as we know that something has to be done.

 

The other issue that I know is on all your minds is salary.  At this point we are operating on the assumption that there will be an April 1 raise; it was legislated last year and it was protected during the fall session, thanks to some very strong feelings on parts of the legislature that it would be bad for the state to not give you a raise.  The guidelines are on our HR web site.  First, we have to remind ourselves that this is last year?s raise, based on last year?s performance, and I think it is important that we keep reminding ourselves of that.  The intent unless legislature makes a different call on that, we will on April 1 be giving raises that are merit based on performance, market, and equity, with a minimum of $1,500 for all full-time employees that were here last year and got an evaluation.  We continue to believe that will happen.  We are presently developing tools to give to your unit leaders, and we are developing a set of guidelines, which follow the guidelines developed in 1992 by Paul Burgess?s group, that laid out a compensation plan which includes market, performance, and equity.  But we are still on track for providing raises to continuing employees on April 1.  We hope that we will be saying that on April 1.  I believe the state?s problem is genuine, and we can argue how it got there.  How much was tax cut?  How much was growth in prisons and health care?  How much is alt-fuel related?  How much was just a general economic hit?  You can argue what caused it, but you can?t rationally say that it is not a major problem.  As one of the important parts of the university, we need to do our share, but we also need to remember that an investment in the universities is not an expenditure, it is an investment in the long term economic and lifestyle health of the state.  Because without the universities being strong, the state will have a much less positive future.  I will be happy to answer any other questions, or listen to any comments you may have.

 

3.C  ASASU President?s Report (Jeremy Helper).

 

I would like you to hear from Tom Murphy today, who is in charge of our online Teacher Evaluation Program.   

 

Tom Murphy: Our student government has worked on this project for about the last six years, which was when I was a freshman in high school.  I became the director of the ASASU Teacher Evaluation Program last year.  Finally we found a way that students could execute an evaluation of their teachers on campus and then make that evaluation information available to other students on the campus.  There were a lot of problems in finding an effective way to do this so that a lot of students could participate and a lot of faculty could be evaluated and then that information could be shared.  I have worked together with a subcommittee of the Academic Senate and some of our student senators in developing questions that we felt would be accurate to evaluate teachers on.  The questions were agreed upon and from there we started designing a web site last year.  That web site hopefully will be launched in about four weeks.  On that web site, all teachers on ASU?s campus will be able to be evaluated by all students on ASU?s campus.  We really feel there is a rising demand for this because there are a lot of web sites that are commercially motivated, like pickaprof.com is actually the largest one, where there are people with commercial motives there designing web sites for people to evaluate professors on public university campuses.  We thought it was important to design something where this could be done securely, where not just anyone could log on and evaluate your teachers.  So we have been working with ASU Student Technology Services to develop a web site where using your ASU login, students will log in and evaluate only the teachers they have and they can only evaluate them once.  Then that information will be turned back in and other students will be able to view when they are selecting their courses for next year, what other students have rated that teacher on.  We really think that this is important, and that small universities have gotten a big head start on us, especially in the East.   I am excited to report that for a university our size this is something that we are going to be a predecessor for and an example of.  I would like to invite you to a special presentation of this program, which will allow you to view an example of the web site and there will be opportunity for questions.  This presentation will be on Wednesday, January 30, 3:30 p.m. in the Student Services Conference Room, 274.  We are also inviting members of the Student Senate to attend and ask questions.  This will be the last opportunity that we have to discuss our program before the web page goes into the final production stages.  Students will be able to evaluate teachers in the last five weeks of the semester, before grades are released.  So, the last day to evaluate your teachers will be the last day of classes for the semester.  I would encourage all of you to attend the demonstration of our program on Wednesday. 

 

Jeremy Helper:  The students will have a lot calmer semester we hope than it was in the fall.  The big thing students are going to discuss this spring?1) We will rally to keep our tuition low and we know that the state?s financial crisis is real and that they are going to definitely raise tuition.  Hopefully, we can keep it as low as possible.  2) This year the students would like at least some of the programs to come back that were taken away because of the budget cuts last year. 

3) We will be taking applications again for the Centennial Professorship awards. The deadline will be the end of February, and we will take applications around to all the offices on campus that can nominate teachers.  Each person wins a total of $10,000 to use any way they wish, and an additional $10,000 that will go for more teaching assistants and more technology in the classroom.

6.D  Personnel Committee (Douglas Johnson).

 

Senate President Munk: As you know one of the major items before the Personnel Committee this year has been the report of the university Task Force on Promotion and Tenure.  The recommendations from this report as well as the report of the Senate Personnel Committee have been made available to you on the Academic Senate Web page.  I will turn the meeting over at this time to the chair of the Personnel Committee, Doug Johnson.

 

Senator Johnson:  One of the most important things that has been going on at the university for the last year and a half has been the work of the Task Force on Promotion and Tenure.  The Task Force is dealing with issues that affect our lives, that affect the success of the university for the future--and we need to tweak it a bit to make sure we are getting a system that serves us well.  I would like to spend some time with each of these today and talk a little bit about the document.  Hopefully you have been able to review this document beforehand.  It has been posted on the Center for Learning and Teaching Excellence Web site for almost a year now.  It is currently on the Senate Web site along with the report of the Personnel Committee that has been studying the report of the Task Force.  I would ask you to make sure to review both documents before the next meeting.  And I ask Darby Shaw to post my power point slides on the Web after the meeting.  I will talk today about some areas that the Personnel Committee would like to focus your attention on, and perhaps help you a little bit in going through this long and complex document.  I apologize in advance if my slides seem complex, but the document is complex and there are some details that we need to discuss. 

 

The Task Force was appointed by Walter Harris, it is chaired by Anne Schneider and David Burstein was a member.  That group did a great job and worked hard.  They were persistent in their task and provided a document that gives a big impetus for approving it.  The report has four parts, the first being a summary of major policy changes.  It highlights issues in the report and it is like an executive summary.  The second part provides a road map of implementation steps for the approval of the recommendations.  Today we are beginning that process by introducing Senate Resolution #3 that was published in your agenda and is repeated for your consideration:

 

?Whereas, the Senate agrees that the Arizona State University Promotion and Tenure Policy (ACD 501-513) should be updated to improve clarity, coherence, and consistency and to promote high quality decision and review processes.

 

The Academic Senate recommends the acceptance of the Promotion and Tenure Task Force Recommendations subject to the clarifications and changes recommended by the Academic Senate Personnel Committee. The Academic Senate recommends the appointment of a university task force to investigate best practices for evaluating the quality of teaching/learning and recommend models of evaluation to facilitate implementation by academic units.

 

Furthermore, the Senate applauds the formation of the Provost?s Personnel Advisory Committee (PAC20) as a mechanism for insuring campus-wide input into the process of implementing and maintaining university personnel policies (ACD Manual). And the Senate looks forward to reviewing the updated ACD Manual including the updated statement of policy on Promotion and Tenure.

 

The Senate commends the Promotion and Tenure Task Force for thoughtful and thorough deliberations and open hearings which have allowed campus-wide issues and concerns to be incorporated into this important process.?

  

Senator Johnson: This resolution is now introduced and will come for consideration and a vote at the next Senate meeting.  The Provost Advisory Group will be involved in writing the final language for the ACD Manual after that.  The next step is that each academic unit review their policies and make sure that they are consistent and up to date with this policy and then interpret it for their unit?s context.  After that we are into a sort of continuous improvement step, in trying to make this an updated process on track with our mission statement and so that it will serve the university in the future.

           

The third part of the report focuses on the eight charges to the Task Force.  Essentially they are to review and codify a variety of changes, to clean up the language in the ACD Manual, making it a much more useable and understandable document for all of us.  The ACD Manual has been developed over a number of years and it has been amended a number of times, and these inconsistencies have been troublesome from time to time.  At the most general level, we are perhaps trying to deal with improving the language of the document.  But there are also issues, in terms of clarifying those issues, that have been troublesome for the university and for individuals.  Today, we will begin by talking about the eight charges that the Task Force dealt with.   

 

The Task Force did a Herculean job; they met on a regular basis for over a year.  They made presentations at the Deans? Retreat, they provided a draft on the Web site of the C.L.T.E., there have been opportunities for comment university-wide.  The draft was again presented to the Council of Deans and open hearings were held on campus.  There have been discussions in various colleges about this draft.  You have probably seen it in one or two forms already.  Hopefully you have begun to think about what is important and what is useful in this document. 

 

In developing the draft there were a number of issues that influenced the Task Force.  The aspirations of the faculty and the university are particularly at the top of the list: our desire for national leadership; the forming of a metropolitan research university concept; what is it that it takes to become a university for the 21st century?  These are all things that were focused on by the Task Force in developing their recommendations.  The impact that the university has on the state and the impact we have on the Phoenix metropolitan area are certainly important considerations.  A variety of other kinds of things are in the report that many of you would like to have as considerations in the development of a document like this?What does it take to enhance our position as a research university?  What does it take to be fair to our faculty?  What can we do to make sure that all aspects of faculty work are properly rewarded?

 

The Personnel Committee began to consider the Task Force report in August and this group has met weekly through the fall.  It has been a very hardworking group, thoughtful in its discussions, sometimes heated too.  You can see the members of the committee listed here; my contribution was probably choosing a time that Senator Tillery could not be there, but everyone else was closely involved.  Even so, Bill did provide me with his input and comments by email and I thank him for that. 

 

If we take the Task Force charges one at a time and look at areas where the Personnel Committee felt there was some difference or concern, I would like to highlight them for you now.  First of all in section 1.1, the overall goal of rewriting the ACD Manual is just very confusing and it really needs attention.  We encourage the Provost Personnel Advisory Committee to proceed to work on that language, and we look forward to a time when the Senate will be able to review the final language.  On 1.2, the recommendation is for peer review in the annual performance review process.  The Task Force said that it was desirable unless the unit voted otherwise.  The Personnel Committee would rather that we strike the part about voting otherwise, because rather than encouraging units not to participate, we feel that peer review is really an important part of what goes on.  It is not a matter of you not trusting your chair in this process, but rather it is your next chair that you are concerned about.     

 

The Personnel Committee agrees that the pursuit of excellence in both research and teaching is important and ought to be included in faculty evaluations.  This is really based on the ABOR requirement of excellence in all areas.  We can hardly ask less of ourselves.  It does require us to think about our process a little bit differently than perhaps we have in the past.  It makes it imperative that departments formulate and communicate their policies to their faculty. 

 

The Personnel Committee agrees that service should be evaluated as an integral part of faculty responsibilities, and in that regard, greater reliance on negotiated workload agreements seems appropriate.   If workload agreements are included in the performance process, the evaluations should be fair, and reflect activities, guidance and how faculty allocate their time. 

 

The second task was to recommend changes that would improve flexibility and adaptability.  We agree that promotion to associate should be based on excellence in both teaching and research, that promotion and tenure decisions should reflect affirmative action, opportunity goals and the service contribution of the individual.  We would prefer a slight language change instead of ?contributions to a collegial work environment,? we would like to see ?contributions to a productive, professional environment,?

as we are afraid that collegiality smacks of cronyism in situations such as this.  All professional contributions should be considered in the evaluation process, and again, workload agreements seem to be the best way to make sure that there is fair consideration of all contributions. 

 

The Personnel Committee also agrees that it is desirable to streamline the process, to use common definitions, using as many modular components of evaluations as possible, as recommended by the Promotion and Tenure Task Force, but there are differences in reviews and we want to make sure that those distinctions remain clear.  The annual reviews, probationary reviews and promotion and tenure reviews are really different purposed, and we do not want to link them too directly in the process. 

 

The concept of excellence is one that the Personnel Committee struggled with and debated.  We do believe that excellence needs to be evaluated within the context of the unit?s goals and the individual?s workload agreement.  Pursuit of excellence is important and indicators of continuous improvement should be considered. 

 

The Personnel Committee believes that a national reputation can be established through any form of scholarship, but that it is probably unusual to do so in any other than a research and creative activity area.  We also believe that increasing the expectation of excellence in teaching should not be considered in any way a decrease in the importance of excellence in research and creative activities. 

 

The Personnel Committee agrees with the recommendation that service be assessed for promotion to full professor, but disagrees with the language in the Task Force report.  It focuses on service to the public, and we believe that professional contributions, service to the university, department and college should also be given consideration. 

 

If we are going to place more emphasis on evaluating and rewarding teaching, we need a measurement structure to support that which goes beyond student evaluations; multiple measures of instruction are needed; peer review process for instructional materials and courseware is highly desirable.  We need to enhance and support efforts to accomplish that on campus.  A university task force needs to be appointed to determine and communicate best practices in evaluating performance of teaching.  We applaud creation of the Provost Advisory Committee for personnel matters, that will be referred to as the PAC20.  It is a major step forward in systematically implementing our personnel policies across campus. 

 

In terms of continuous improvement, if the University Promotion and Tenure Committee makes recommendations to improve the process, the Senate Personnel Committee would certainly like to hear about it. 

 

Two areas of concern traditionally have been, how do we evaluate people on joint appointments and how do we deal with interdisciplinary activities?  The Personnel Committee believes that the traditional, normal guidelines need to apply here, but that workload agreements need to be clearly negotiated to specify emphasis and split assignments in a way that will make evaluation in these situations fair.  Perhaps taking this to departments where personnel committees really make the decisions that guide the university, we think it is critical that units clarify their policies and communicate them effectively, and what their expectations are, with the faculty.

 

In your agenda, you have a copy of Senate Resolution #3 and that will be voted upon at the February 18th meeting. 

 

Senator Cobas: The language first came to us as collegiality--I am very gratified that you would change that, but I want to know what the exact new language will be?

 

Doug Johnson: The Personnel Committee preferred the language of ?contributions to a productive, professional environment.?

 

Senator Cobas: That still bothers me, but I will have something to say about that later.

 

Doug Johnson: That is a sensitive issue, collegiality, and I think when it is used it could be interpreted that way.  We certainly do believe that faculty members need to make a contribution to the success of their units and that all contributions in that direction ought to be evaluated.

 

Senator Cobas: But some of the great minds of academia have been very difficult people!

 

Doug Johnson: We know that only too well.  (Much laughter here).

 

Senator Cobas: It bothers me that we are bringing this criterion from the world outside into our process. 

 

Senate President Munk: If there are further comments or questions, we have Vice Provost Gail Hackett and Dean Anne Schneider, chair of the Task Force, with us today.  We hope that you will give this the consideration  that it deserves, and that you will give your colleagues in your units the opportunity to review and comment on the document as well.  This will come up for further discussion and a vote at our February Senate meeting.  Thank you, Doug.
