March 2, 2006

ASU wins coveted Campus Ecology Recognition

The National Wildlife Federation has granted ASU its coveted Campus Ecology Recognition for its commitment to addressing the desert environment and restoring wildlife habitat.

The award was based on ASU's Comprehensive Development Plan (CDP) for ASU's four campuses. The plan was completed after two years of extensive research with the help of Ayers Saint Gross Architects & Planners of Baltimore.

The CDP, which addresses the physical needs of the university, is "a framework for growth for all the campuses," says Steven Nielsen, ASU assistant vice president of real estate and university physical planner. "We took a look at the needs of the university, such as parking, buildings, landscaping and access - everything you could imagine - and how the campuses would grow over time.

"The CDP is a road map for growth. If we need a new life sciences building, for example, we know where it will go."

Nielsen says the Wildlife Federation award recognizes ASU's commitment to "the overall environment of campus - how we are addressing sustainability and the environment."

In the application for the award, Nielsen and Richard Stanley, senior vice president and university planner, wrote: "The goal of the CDP was to require the university to be aware of - and sensitive to - the unique desert environment of central Arizona, and to plan and develop its campuses accordingly."

The plan calls for campus development to use appropriate desert landscaping; use and re-use water responsibly; and develop attractive and comfortable outdoor spaces at each of its campuses.

When new buildings are constructed or existing buildings upgraded, the CDP requires that the spaces "include landscaping sensitive to the desert environment."

The award application notes that "the best campuses in the world are memorable not necessarily because of the actual buildings, but because of the space between the buildings. The importance cannot be overstated of climate-responsive design; shade; integration with the natural environment; human scale; memorable-spaced outdoor spaces; active streets and walkways; clear hierarchy of buildings; treatment of the campus as a whole, as opposed to a collection of moments (or monuments); and development of palettes of desert-appropriate materials and details."

ASU's Tempe campus, the application says, has "a special opportunity to provide a living laboratory of using waste water, such as storm water and condensate, in the urban landscape. A variety of sustainable water feature systems are proposed for the campus, in addition to the traditional methods of using potable water for fountains. The special desert outdoor spaces created by the responsible use of water and landscaping are crucial to the character, coherence and comfort of the campus."

Now that the CDP has been completed, and won an award, what changes can the university community expect?

At the Tempe campus, the oldest of ASU's campuses, crews have been installing new water and sewer lines, Nielsen says.

"We're building a new backbone for the campus," he says. "We have to start with the basics. Then we will start to look at things like the landscape around Old Main, the aesthetics of Rural Road, and redevelopment of the malls. We'll also take a hard look at several buildings, to see whether they should go or stay. It's a series of dominoes.

"The most important thing is that we have a vision. We know where this campus should be in the future, and we have a road map to get us there. This award is a recognition by our peers that we're on the right track."
