August 16, 2006

Emeriti faculty earn top mentor honors

Geography is more than memorizing the location of European countries or naming the U.S. state capitals.

Much more, in fact.

And this is what has won the Distinguished Mentor Awards for Malcolm Comeaux and Robert Mings, professors emeriti of geography.

The award, presented by the National Council for Geographic Education, honors university faculty who have mentored graduate students in geography and geography education.

But dearer to the hearts of Comeaux and Mings is the fact that it also recognizes their work in inspiring K-12 teachers in Arizona to teach ?real? geography.

?Geography is focused on places, why and where things are, and the relationship between humans and the environment,? Comeaux says. ?It studies both humans and environments, and tries to integrate the two.

?Geography looks at things spatially, such as climatology, culture, economics and urban studies.?

The effort to train teachers to teach geography began in 1993, when Comeaux and Mings helped establish the Arizona Geographic Alliance (AGA).

The AGA helps ?teacher-consultants? develop lesson plans in geography, which are then shared with other teachers in Arizona and around the United States .

The alliance recruits teachers by contacting organizations such as the Arizona Department of Education and the Arizona Education Association, and by asking current teacher-consultants for recommendations

?We then select K-12 teachers to attend our summer institute, based on background, experience, stage in their career, letters of recommendation, and even from where they teach,? Comeaux says. ?We try very hard to get teachers from areas around the state.?

Lesson plans are developed during Advanced Institute, held every other summer. This summer, the teachers went to California to study the physical geography of the Mammoth Lakes region.

?Eighteen teachers attended, and each will develop at least one lesson plan,? he says

During the summer institute, the alliance trains the teachers to present their lesson plans ? which, Comeaux says, is very difficult for the teachers to do in front of their peers. Once they finish the course, these graduates become teacher consultants.

Further geography education happens during field trips for the teacher-consultants twice a year.

?Last semester, we went to the site of the Oatman Massacre,? Comeaux says.

(While traveling from Missouri to California, pioneer Royce Oatman, his wife and four of their seven children were killed by Indians about 25 miles west of Gila Bend in 1851.)

?The next field trip will be to the Tonto Basin and Camp Reno , where we'll look at the environment and early military presence, Indians and mining in the area,? he adds.

The alliance has just started working with English language learners, adapting lessons in a program called GeoLiteracy, which integrates geography skills with tested language-arts standards. The lessons will be tested throughout Arizona this fall.

?We encourage teachers to make geography useful and interesting to students while they're learning,? Comeaux says.

The AGA's efforts are paying off, Comeaux says.

?I came to ASU 36 years ago,? he says. ?During the first 25 years, not one freshman came in and said, ?I want to be a geography major.' Now, some are coming in as freshman majoring in geography.?

Most of the teachers who participate in the AGA are from middle schools, Comeaux says, with a few kindergarten through third-grade teachers.

?There's a smattering of high school teachers, particularly those who are teaching advanced placement students,? he says. ?We wish we had more high school teachers.?

Before the AGA was established, Comeaux says, ?very little geography was taught in schools. Now we have many school districts offering very solid geography education.?
