May 1, 2006

ASU aids displaced Katrina scholars

Several universities in New Orleans were in the path of destruction wrought last year by Hurricane Katrina, forcing campuses to close and students to scatter to schools across the country to maintain studies and research projects.

Two National Science Foundation (NSF)-supported projects headquartered in ASU's Hispanic Research Center recently aided some of those displaced students in their recovery from the impact of one of the worst natural disasters in U.S. history.

The displaced included about 250 students in the Louis Stokes Louisiana Alliance for Minority Participation (LS-LAMP). Like ASU's Western Alliance to Expand Student Opportunities (WAESO), it is one of 34 alliances that are part of a broad NSF program to motivate students from groups underrepresented in science, technology, engineering and mathematics to pursue careers in those fields.

LS-LAMP's annual conference was canceled last fall in the aftermath of Hurricane Katrina because of the large number of students who had to leave Louisiana to avoid interruption of their education.

So ASU's WAESO and the More Graduate Education at Mountain States Alliance (MGE@MSA) invited LS-LAMP members, as well as members of the Graduate Alliance for Education in Louisiana (GAELA), which also was forced to cancel its annual conference, to join in their conference at ASU April 20 - 21. More than 140 Louisiana students attended.

The event rekindled camaraderie among students who had been separated in the wake of Katrina, says Jeanne Clair Williams, a senior mathematics major at Southern University in New Orleans.

"Some people's spirits were down (after Katrina)," she says. "The students were losing connection. It's been emotionally strenuous, so getting together like this was very important."

It was an especially welcome respite for biology and physics major Shukura Bonner and biology major Nekeithia Wade, seniors at Dillard University in New Orleans.

Dillard's campus is still closed - and, like other universities in New Orleans, the school has had to lodge students and faculty in hotels and hold classes in trailers or use public middle school and high school facilities.

"It's a very tough life right now," Bonner says. "It's hard to study and learn in this situation."

She and Wade say it's been a struggle to keep research projects going with few laboratory resources.

Tulane University science and engineering majors Ashok Manepalli, Laura Linhardt and Harold Hatch describe other travails. New Orleans campuses lost not only their library and lab facilities, but hurricane damage left universities in financial difficulty that threatens many academic and research programs, they say.

But Jenifer Placide, a junior bioengineering and French major at Louisiana State University, echoes Volanda Harris, a junior biology major at Grambling University, in expressing gratitude for being able to keep the LS-LAMP program momentum going by joining the conference at ASU.

The conference gave many of the Louisiana students an opportunity to make research presentations, including Aubrey Turner, a senior mathematics major at Southern University in Baton Rouge, who is working on ways to more precisely predict hurricane behavior.

Hurricane Katrina - and the devastation that storm brought - made the conference not only a time to mark LS-LAMP students' progress, but also a time to acknowledge their "perseverance in trying times," says Antonio Garcia, an interim associate dean for academic affairs in ASU's Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering. Garcia also is project director for WAESO and associate director of ASU's Hispanic Research Center.

Keeping LAMP and other alliance groups going strong is important to more than students and faculty, said Albert McHenry, dean of the College of Technology and Applied Sciences at the Polytechnic campus. He's also project director of MGE@MSA.

"We need more Americans in these fields to compete in the global economy," McHenry says. "It's about sustaining our lifestyle and economic edge, and its means everybody has to contribute."
