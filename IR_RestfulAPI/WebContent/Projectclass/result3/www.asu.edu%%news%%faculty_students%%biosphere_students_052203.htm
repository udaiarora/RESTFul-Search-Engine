May 22, 2003

Biosphere offers Fisher eye-opening experience

Being a college-aged student who volunteers to live in relative isolation in Arizona's Sonoran desert flies in the face of conventional wisdom. There is hardly the kind of excitement that the typical twenty-something craves and "night life" is confined to a few nocturnal birds of prey.

But ask ASU senior Amber Fisher about her Fall 2002 semester at the Earth Institute and she will tell you that it was the single most influential experience of her college career. Fisher, who graduated May 15 with a bachelor degree in political science, says she would not trade her experience for anything.

ASU is one of more than a dozen college and university partners in Columbia University's undergraduate program at Biosphere 2 Center near Oracle.
Known as Earth Semester, the program is team-taught by social, biological and physical scientists and immerses students in studies that focus on the planet as a huge, complex interactive system. The program is innovative in that it provides experiences that are an important adjunct to other programs.

Fisher says the Earth Semester was an eye-opening experience.

"It's amazing to see a whole other side of school," she said. "Being part of an isolated campus taught me so much about myself, allowed me to develop close personal friendships and experience how remarkably dependent we as humans are on our planet."

Earth Semester is offered twice a year at the Oracle facility and offers students 16 hours of undergraduate credit.

The 250-acre campus is nestled in the foothills of the Santa Catalina Mountains, and its centerpiece is a 204,000 cubic-meter enclosed glass and space frame structure that houses seven of the earth's ecosystems in miniature. Within the confines of the manmade dome are a rainforest, desert, savanna and a 900,000-gallon ocean - the largest of its kind in the world.

Scientists and students who work inside the Biosphere 2 facility do numerous research projects that are geared to better understand our planet and environment.

Fisher worked with other students to perform ecological and hydrological impact studies of the campus landscape and then applied that data to design a public policy on land use. What impressed her was how the classroom experience could directly translate to real world experience. Students not only performed earth science, but also went through the permitting and review processes before writing their public policy.

"We had the opportunity to experience both sides of the procedure - the ecology side and the government side, and play the roles of all stakeholders," Fisher said.

Students live in on-campus housing, and classrooms are equipped with the most current technologies. More than 400 students, many from ASU, have enrolled in the program since it was launched in 1996, soon after Columbia took on management of the Biosphere 2 center.

Fisher said some of the closest friends she made during college were made at the Earth Institute. The experience also forged meaningful friendships with professors due to the close-knit community environment and numerous field trips where faculty and students work together to accomplish targeted goals.

"At ASU we've placed a major priority that our undergraduates engage in research and other capstone experiences. The relationship with Biosphere 2 provides them with yet another opportunity to do this," said ASU provost Milton Glick.

After graduating from ASU, Fisher is spending the summer in a conservation leadership program with Northwest Youth Corps before entering graduate school with a focus on environmental public policy. She says in one short semester the experience and knowledge gained at Earth Institute has inspired her for a lifetime and given her direction for the future.
