Division of Graduate Studies Awarded Grant to Promote Responsible Research

Arizona State University's Division of Graduate Studies (DGS) has been awarded a $15,000 grant by the Council of Graduate Schools to provide educational activities to promote the responsible conduct of research.

The two-year grant will help fund the Responsible Conduct of Research Project, a collaborative effort between DGS, the Office for Research and Sponsored Projects Administration, the Consortium for Science, Policy & Outcomes, the Joan and David Lincoln Center for Applied Ethics, and specific academic units.

The initiative will be under the leadership of Joan McGregor, the director of the Bioethics Program. She will work through DGS to better instill ethical training in all levels of graduate education at ASU.

"This is another great example of how ASU's Division of Graduate Studies is working hard to help us differentiate our research programs from those of our peer institutions," said Paul Johnson, Associate Vice President for Research. "The award is especially relevant at this time, as we are rapidly growing our research activity in the biosciences and bioengineering areas, seeking to become better recognized for all of our research programs, and pushing the frontiers of science and engineering."

The award recognizes ASU's leadership in graduate education nationally, building on its current involvement in the Carnegie Initiative on the Doctorate, the Woodrow Wilson Foundation's Responsive Ph.D. Project, and the Ford Foundation's Multicontextuality and Higher Education Project.

"We are very pleased to receive this grant because it provides the opportunity for us to work in partnership with key academic and research leadership across the university to promote and reinforce a proactive model in the responsible conduct of research, not only among faculty but among our graduate students as well," said Maria T. Allison, Vice Provost and Dean of Graduate Studies. "Moreover, given ASU's growing focus on interdisciplinary and transdisciplinary research, it is absolutely essential that there be broad levels of discussions about research ethics that transcend disciplinary borders."

Added Associate Dean Marjorie Zatz: "The possibilities posed by these new research endeavors are enormous and exciting, but we also must create an institutional culture in which consideration of the ethical and social implications of science are at the forefront of researchers' minds as new protocols are developed."

The goal of the Responsible Conduct of Research Project is to establish and maintain an institutional culture in which individual researchers, administrators, and students have certain core values to guide their decision-making on matters of confidentiality, plagiarism, intellectual property, protection of research subjects, and others that arise in research.

To that end, the project will begin ongoing annual training workshops and a web-based training program in the ethics of research that involve human subjects. Also, workshops designed to address ethical issues, ranging from data management and authorship to the ethical and social implications of science, will be developed. In addition, workshops on curriculum development will be designed for faculty and graduate students in biomedical science, and later for those in the behavioral sciences and other fields of study. A speakers' series on the responsible conduct of research also will be part of the project. And finally, the project will create comprehensive and informative web pages on the responsible conduct of research.

McGregor said, "This initiative aims at moving the university from a regulatory compliance oriented institution to one that expects and exemplifies the highest standards of integrity in research, or in other words to doing the right thing because it is the correct way rather than simply because the rules say so.
