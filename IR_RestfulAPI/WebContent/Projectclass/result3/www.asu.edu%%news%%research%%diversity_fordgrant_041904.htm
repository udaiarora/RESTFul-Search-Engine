April 19, 2004

Ford funds diversity study proposal

ASU researchers with the Women of Color Research Collective (WoCRC) based within the Intergroup Relations Center have garnered a $300,000 Ford Foundation grant to study diversity efforts in American universities.

The Ford Foundation, created with bequests by Henry and Edsel Ford, strives to strengthen democratic values, reduce poverty and injustice, encourage international cooperation, and promote human achievement.

Kathleen Wong, program coordinator, senior with Intergroup Relations, also serves as executive director of WoCRC. Wong says the group is composed of many administrators who are women of color from across the nation dedicated to focusing on multi-university research.

The grant will enable research contributing to institutional and public understanding of the challenges and promises of diversity in academia.

"My hopes are that our research will capture the landscape of what is happening in terms of efforts to diversify universities, using reliable measures and analysis," Wong says.

Wong says this award is just the first of many research pursuits for WoCRC.

"We hope to be able to develop our organizational infrastructure in the coming years in partnership with other national and campus research communities," Wong says. "We are pursuing entrepreneurial, nationally-focused research projects that will help in transforming higher education to meet our future needs to educate and lead a diverse society."

The grant proposal was co-written by Wong; Delia Saenz, an associate professor in the School of Psychology; and Caroline Turner, a professor with Educational Leadership and Policy Studies.

Saenz says she is very pleased the Ford Foundation saw merit in their proposal.

Saenz says recent Supreme Court decisions concerning affirmative action have left institutions of higher education searching for ways to interpret and respond to competing mandates.

"On one hand, legal mandates appear to be curtailing policies and practices aimed at diversifying institutions," Saenz says. "On the other hand, the K-12 pipeline demographic compels us to recognize that universities need to be responsive to their student and public constituencies."

Saenz and Wong agree the Ford Foundation is progressive in its commitment to capturing the pulse of higher education on the issue.

"The award is a recognition that scholarship can and should aspire to be both empirically and socially relevant," Saenz says. "Our methods and theoretical framework will build on current knowledge in various social science disciplines; our specific focus addresses an issue of great importance in contemporary society."
