October 25, 2005

CASS program teaches educators a lesson

For Mariana Herrera Escobar, the opportunity to join a yearlong professional development program at ASU that will equip her with new teaching tools and expertise to enhance her work with indigenous children and families in her native Nicaragua was an irresistible proposition.

But the exciting and life-changing learning opportunity has not come without sacrifice for the veteran primary teacher and mother of seven.

"There are moments when I miss my children very much,” says Herrera Escobar, whose children range in age from 3 to 18 years old. “But when I think of the teaching capacity and quality of education that I will be able to offer my students, school and my own family, I feel very happy to be here and to have this opportunity.”

Herrera Escobar is one of 21 educators from Nicaragua, Guatemala, and the Mexican states of Jalisco and Chiapas who are participating in “Strengthening Education for Indigenous Children,” a concentrated and innovative full-immersion program geared for primary teachers and administrators working with indigenous students in schools in Mexico and Central America, and the Mexican states of Jalisco, Chiapas, Guerrero and Oaxaca. The program is being presented for the second straight year by the College of Education’s Center for Indian Education, through funding from Georgetown University’s Cooperative Association of States for Scholarship (CASS) program.

CASS cohort participants arrived in Tempe Aug. 9 and immediately began an intensive four-week orientation program that included instruction devoted to “survival English,” the most basic skills the participants will need to negotiate in a foreign environment.

"A year is a long time to be away from home, so it was important that we begin by providing the participants with the support they needed to adjust to a new country, new culture, new food, new everything,” says Sally Gonzales, program manager with the Center for Indian Education.

Following the orientation program, the CASS participants began the comprehensive academic program that is being delivered through a combination of university-level coursework, seminars, internships within the Tempe, Creighton and Roosevelt elementary school districts, focused workshops, job shadowing, and professional and community linkages.

"The educators in this cohort have extensive teaching experience, anywhere from three to 25 years,” Gonzales says. “They are well prepared to fit in to the classroom settings, where they will observe and learn from their mentor teachers.”

The rigorous program also includes a Saturday computer class to help the educators hone their technology skills. Over the course of the year, the participants will work with their faculty mentors to develop a project that they ultimately will implement in their home schools. A one-year certificate will be awarded to each participant upon completion of the academic program in July.

"I’d never been away from my home a single day before joining the CASS program,” says Bartolomé Vázquez Lopez, a primary teacher from the Escuela Primaria Bilingue in Chiapas, Mexico. “But the need at my school is so great that I’m willing to sacrifice a year away from home to learn what’s necessary to provide a better service to my community.”

The 11 women and 10 men participating in the cohort are forming a small learning community of their own as they share five apartments located near the Tempe campus.

"We’ve become like family to each other,” Vázquez Lopez says.

Gloria Elvira Reyes Xitumul, a teacher with the Centro Oficial de Preprimaria Bilingue in Guatemala, agrees.

"Learning about the pedagogy used in Mexico from the other program participants also has been a very good experience for me,” she says.

For more information regarding the program, call (480) 965-9785.
