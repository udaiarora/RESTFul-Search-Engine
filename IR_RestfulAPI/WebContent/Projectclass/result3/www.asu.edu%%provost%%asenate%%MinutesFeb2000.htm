3.A. Senate President's Report (David Burstein).

We will be going out of our normal sequence today in order to accommodate a bipartisan meeting with the Intellectual Property Committee. They must consider the same issues that this body does regarding the ASU Intellectual Property Management Implementation Policy, which I will refer to as IP-squared. We both must consider the ruling of the Arizona Supreme Court which affects this document.

Morton Munk, chair, Intellectual Property Policy Committee, called the committee to order.

President Burstein: The Senate must first untable the amended IP-squared policy (January 31, 2000). Do I hear motion to untable Senate Motion #4 (1999-2000)? So moved by Senator Mayer and there are multiple seconds. Now, we can begin to discuss the IP-squared document. The IP Committee and the Senate will now constitute a "committee of the whole." We will all be able to ask questions on this document.

First, I will address the issue of what happens if we must enforce a subpoena. It will go to the civil court at that point. Anyone who is going to challenge a subpoena from the IP Committee needs to have a lawyer present, because at that point even the issuing of the subpoena may be challenged in the civil court. If that person decides to not adhere to the subpoena, they also will come under the rules of the civil court. Mary Stevens has said that the General Counsel's Office has not actually had to deal with a case in the fourteen years she knows, in which people were not forthcoming with information requested of them. Senator Jacob, does that answer your question from our previous meeting? (Yes.)

Are there further questions on either representation by counsel or subpoena power of the committee?

Senator Rose: My understanding at our last meeting was that we agreed that Mary Stevens would provide something to us either in writing or by speaking to us. Am I now to understand that she told you this instead? Are we going to get anything in writing?

President Burstein: I did invite Mary to attend. I also know that the University General Counsel Office does a lot of legal consultation with the other two universities as well as with our campus issues--but Mary is here now and can address those issues herself. Mary, I told the Senate you said that there has never been a case where documents were refused to be produced. And I also told them that if we come under the new rule, and a subpeona was issued, that it would then go to a civil court. Can you elaborate from that point?

Senator Rose: Why would the initial challenge not be in front of the IP hearing officer before it goes to the court? That way it might be resolved before it causes the courts to become involved.

Mary Stevens: You are right. We don't expect much activity on this item because in order to obtain a court subpoena, the IP hearing officer is the one that has the authority to issue the subpoena and who must get it enforced if it is being violated, or it is not being complied with. Before you get to that juncture, the university would hire an attorney. We would not expect a hearing officer to do that on his or her own. We would hire counsel to go into court to enforce the subpoena. But again, before that you make a lot of determinations, and as David said, we have never had the need to get a subpoena enforced. That is not to say that a subpoena has not been issued, because sometimes people do not want to testify or are unable to because they are out of the country on sabbatical or travel. In those cases, we have worked reasonably to obtain the testimony. In the case of confidential documents, they have been made directly available for the hearing panel, but not to all the parties in the hearing. You really must, in the case of subpeona, decide if the witness's testimony or the documentation is absolutely necessary for the case. We have not so far had those kinds of issues to confront.

Senator Jacob: Are we already governed by policies that give boards subpoena powers that could invoke a civil court action?

Mary Stevens: No. We did not have that language until last November when the court made its ruling. Anyone who is asked to give testimony at a hearing, or produce a document, is expected to do so. That has always been sufficient. There is an expectation that the university will produce documents necessary for a hearing. Because of the decision by the Arizona Supreme Court in the Kimball case, we must now add in that language to conform.

Senator Jacob: Must we then give all our grievance policies this language, and the subpoena power?

Mary Stevens: What the Supreme Court says is that when grievances comes under our policies, we must provide that person with the right to full representation by counsel, and the right of subpoena power to the committee that hears the grievance.

Senator Jacob: We have had a closed system of governing ourselves up until now, and this now opens grievance hearings up to being decided by state authorities. Do we have any discretion at all?

Mary Stevens: No, we cannot reject a Supreme Court decision.

President Burstein: No one in the university, your faculty leadership, the General Counsel's Office, the administration, no one here or at UofA likes it, but yes, we have to obey the law.

Senator Feller: That is a very strong statement. I would like to know what basis you have to make that statement.

President Burstein: We, as Senator Jacob said, tried to have as collegial a setting as possible with hearing panels. The new policy permits our grievance hearings to become trials by situation. We are not happy about this. How many of you will be willing to serve on grievance committees if they become a lawyer versus lawyer situation?

Senator Feller: I am not debating that matter. I am against your statements such as "we don't like it and very few people like it." I am a member of this Senate, and no one has ever asked me whether I like it or not. Please clarify your statements to speak for yourself and not the Senate as a whole.

President Burstein: I thought I made it clear whom I had spoken with on this issue. Obviously, I had not consulted you.

Senator Rose: As a counter point, I have read this document, and I do not share the philosophy of doom that you have reflected and that you say others share. It basically says that in a proceeding where something important is at stake, you have to give the person who is considered a victim a right to have an attorney, which is also true under the Administrative Procedures Act with any other state agency deciding these kinds of questions. You can debate whether this is a good or bad idea. It may have some very good aspects. I have sat on university procurement grievances before, and it is common to have attorneys in a university setting. I am sure there are other bodies where this happens. I do not think that the sky is falling on this issue. We must live with it. It remains to be seen how may people will demand attorneys and secondly I would say that giving this right to people who might lose their tenure or have something else important to lose is not such a terrible idea in any event. I think there has been a considerable amount of overreaction to this. Nor do I think, as Senator Jacob suggested, that we have always had a closed system and that now it is open. People who are unhappy sue the university, and in some ways this new language may make it more difficult to sue the university. Court trials mean further trials, and this alone could curtail the number of suits. You can still sue the university, and then, we have to hire attorneys to defend the university and this means it has not been a closed system up until now.

Mary Stevens: Just to follow up. When I first came here, I was surprised to hear that attorneys could only advise but not participate in grievance hearings. I had come from litigation, where attorneys have a much greater role than that. In the 14 years that I have been here since, every single grievance at the university where the attorneys have come in, we have had to deal with the arguments of the grievant's counsel. So, it did not surprise me that the Arizona Supreme Court decided the way it did. The bigger problem that some of us are having is the faculty grievance committees at the university function, in my opinion, extremely well, and they are quite effective in conducting their hearings in a collegial manner where parties can speak directly to one another. In the ones that I have observed over the years, I have found that process a good one. Our bigger concern here is that we do not yet know how the change to allow full counsel will affect the hearing process. The hearings of the American Aribitration Association, of which I am a member, allow counsel and their arbitration processes seem to run efficiently and probably last a shorter amount of time. We do, for your information, retain the right of the members of the committee to talk directly to the parties.

Senator Grossman: If our current practices allow for counsel, and the Supreme Court is defining who may do what, why then do we have to have a policy in the first place? The policy seems to state the conditions under which we exist anyway. For that reason, even if it may be rarely used, I would be reluctant to invest one of our policies with subpoena power--it is a rather contentious type of exercise anyway--if a subpoena came from someone in questionable standing and they could still go to court. Why must we have a policy when the Supreme Court ruling dictates what we will do anyway?

President Burstein: Although the revised statute says that there should be right to counsel and subpoena, it is not spelled out that we actually operate on a three-tiered system--if the grievant chooses no counsel, the respondent and the committee cannot have counsel. If the grievant chooses to be advised by counsel, the respondent and the committee can be advised by counsel. If the grievant chooses full representation by counsel, the respondent and the committee may have full representation by counsel, with the latter of the two being supplied either indirectly by the university or directly by the university. That is not spelled out in the ABOR policy itself.

Senator Grossman: How about the issue of subpoena authority?

President Burstein: We have to state that because all it says in the statute is the committee chair shall have subpoena power. We have to spell it out. We have to get that into the record as to how we will implement that right here on campus.

Senator Jacob: I am not at all concerned about counsel being present. My concern is that we would have a force wherein our own grievance procedures could invoke an appeal, and then the sanctions for refusing that appeal could be sanctions outside our own usual sanction system within the university rules. In this case, a board within the university now has the power to send a matter to the civil authorities. Of course, if it gets to a civil action, there are civil court sanctions involved. On that one particular point, we are now outside the university with our sanctions, rather than inside, and cannot control the outcome of those sanctions.

Mary Stevens: You are not wrong on that. However, the possibility of getting there is complicated. First, in the policy, you have to have a party refusing to appear or refusing to produce documents. To the extent the university can control a document, we are not refusing to produce documents. Then, you have to have the party decide they absolutely must have that document or that witness. Then the hearing officer has to decide that it is so important that the hearing officer wants to enforce it. It is discretionary. The hearing officer does not have to issue a subpoena. They just have the authority to do this. If the testimony can be provided in a written document or they can proceed without a document, then the hearing officer will not proceed with the enforcement. They will probably not only consult with the hearing panel, but also our office, because it is so critical of a document or testimony that it must be enforced. Then it actually goes to court and that means there are arguments on both sides for production. One attorney will argue why it should not be produced and the other attorney will argue for the other side that it should be produced. The court may decide in our favor. At that point the penalties may set in, but the courts generally do not assess penalties where there is good faith involved. We would not go into court and attempt to enforce a subpoena unless we felt we had a good argument. Getting to the courts is a long involved process and the judge may not enforce a subpoena in the end. If they do enforce it, there could be a penalty, such as a fine.

Senator Jacob: You are speaking to the likelihood and I do not think that is the issue; the issue is the existence or the nonexistence of a possibility. But then I would ask, what would be the difference if we were just to say vote down this language? It (the policy) would simply be imposed on us anyway by state statute or by Supreme Court decision. Why are we discussing whether or not to vote or not on that?

Mary Stevens: I am discussing the likelihood because if there is a concern of some change that we are required to make, then we should have some idea of the likelihood of the event.

President Burstein: Let me answer with respect to why we are voting on it, to remind people of the history. We are voting on this policy because of its history. We were first given the Intellectual Property Management Implementation Policy for the Senate to approve, as we approve all other policies dealing with faculty. That was held in abeyance due to an Arizona Supreme Court ruling that says we must add certain stipulations to the IP-squared. First of all, we must approve this policy because it is a policy of the Senate. We do not have much discretion in terms of what we can do about the Supreme Court ruling because we do not have discretion there.

Are there any questions from the IP Committee?

Al Poskanzer: I would like to explain why this policy was brought forth in the first place. The Board of Regents policy (ABOR 6-908) specifies there shall be an appeal procedure, but intentionally leaves out the details of how that procedure is to be constituted. Each of the three universities apply their own procedures. This IP-squared policy sets forth the appeals procedure that we have to have in place.

President Burstein: Are there other questions? Hearing none, Dr. Munk, please call your committee to order.

Dr. Munk: The vote of the IP Committee is affirmative.

President Burstein: I will now entertain a vote of the Senate. The Amended Intellectual Property Implementation Policy (January 31, 2000) passed by voice vote, with two abstentions.

We will now return to the normal order of business. Thanks to all of you for your patience during this debate.

As a further announcement, I mention an issue that is before the State Senate that could be damaging to our tenure and promotion policies--Senate Bill SB1512--is a so-called revision to the whistleblower statute. SB 1512 provides, in the event that a faculty member becomes a whistleblower, in connection to their tenure or promotion decision, that person can have that tenure or promotion decision taken up by a committee of five--two of whom the grievant picks, one the grievant and the university pick, and two that the university picks. Again, I cannot act as a lobbyist and tell you what to say to your legislators. However, I feel that with that kind of a provision in a bill, anyone who is up for tenure, and is unsure that their tenure is secure, could take it upon themselves to be declared a whistleblower on some issue, then the whistleblower statute would be invoked and the party could, at that point, forego the internal eight-tiered process within the university and have the tenure or promotion decision made by one outside committee of five. In my opinion, that is a trojan horse with respect to promotion and tenure.

Senator Rose: I assume that this bill supercedes the language in the existing law that says university employees are not subject to the state whistleblowing statute if they develop competent policies of their own. Because this decision that you dislike so strongly, also said that university employees were not subject to the state statute on whistleblowing because the universities have adopted policies.

Mary Stevens: We are more concerned about the whistleblower bill (Senate Bill #1512) than the Kimball case (A.R.S. 41-1062). With the proposed language, the current whistleblower statute has an exemption in it for the universities, and the Board of Regents provided that we must have a policy in place to protect whistleblowers from retaliation. The whistleblower policy we have now, we have spent a great deal of time on; and the Senate voted on it. It was just effective in March of 1999, so we have not had any faculty cases come under it yet; we have had a couple of staff cases come under it and it seems to be working very well. Because the sponsors of the bill in the last couple of years have wanted to take our exemption away from us, we feel it would be unconstitutional, because the Board of Regents governs the universities, including our personnel policies and our hearing processes, not the state legislature. We feel they are trying to get around that by saying, "you still have the exemption, however, your policy must say this, this, and this and be identical to the law," which is even more of an infringement than before. Some of the language says that the hearing officer can make a recommendation that the tenure decision will be made by this body that has five chosen members, rather than the normal process within the university. There are several things we are concerned about in this bill.

President Burstein: At the Statehood Picnic today, the legislators that I spoke to said they are only getting email in favor of this bill. If any of you have concern about this, or if you wish the bill to pass, I would urge you to contact your legislators. I would like you to bring that message back to your departments and schools. In other words, if others are as concerned as we are here, I would like you voices to be heard.

Senator Ferraro: Is there any indication of a veto if the bill should pass.

Mary Stevens: We have discussed that option but I do not know. I do know if it passes, there will be discussion on the need to challenge it. I do not know if any of you are aware of an employee at the University of Arizona, Carol Bernstein, who for three years now has tried to get the whistleblower policy amended. She feels that the university employees do not have enough protection. She regularly writes to her legislators as well as posts letters for others to use as examples in contacting their legislators. She is in favor of S.B. #1512.

President Burstein: My other remarks will be short today because I will put further remarks on this item and others, in the next Academic Senate Newsletter.

3.B University Provost's Report (Milton Glick).

My concern on Senate Bill #1512 is that many legislators are willing to believe the worst truth about the universities. To me that is of greater concern than the specific legislation itself. They seem ready to accept things about the universities that are negative, without challenging the evidence.

I want to let you know, and some of you know already, that our partnership with the German Company, SAP, has ended. With David Burstein's leadership we have been working with the SAP to help them develop a student information system which they were then going to market. At the end of the first year of our work together, it became evident that we would have to either adopt the whole suite of SAP components for an Enterprise System (including Human Resources and Finance) or walk away from this partnership. After two months of traumatic discussions, we have decided to put our student information system on the back burner. We now have a better sense of what it would take in out of pocket and in kind resources. That total came to about 31 million dollars, of which, 15 million would be out of pocket cost and the rest was going to come from reassigning people. That reassignment was an opportunity cost--it meant that we would not be able to get the web page front ends we need, we were not able to deal with plus/minus grades, a whole series of other things. And as we looked at the institutional priorities, we felt that to commit that much resource to a system, part of which has not yet been built, balanced against other institutional priorities, we do not think it was the right thing to do. Therefore, we have terminated our relationship with SAP, not because they failed, but because we both came to understand the magnitude of the undertaking. We will continue to track their progress, and over this past year we have learned much about the needs and priorities in information technology at the university. My colleague, Dr. Burstein, thinks we are absolutely wrong on this decision, that we have to make that adjustment at some time, and now is better than later. He is a Physicist and I am a Chemist; he is sure that I am wrong and I am not sure I am right (much laughter here). We will try to harvest from this experience what we have learned and set a series of institution-wide information technology priorities that will try to implement over the next couple of years. We will continue to watch the issue of the Enterprise Resource System. Senator Philippakis, who probably knows a great deal more about this, could tell you that there is now a lot of tension between priority for an Enterprise Resource System and a Customer Relationship Management (CRM) system and this debate is ongoing. I became convinced that you could only justify an investment of this large magnitude if you thought your current system were going to fail, or if you were going to use the new system to dramatically enhanced either the revenues or the effectiveness of the university. And that means doing so within a culture that we all live in and try to protect. That is where we are with our decision.

At the last Senate meeting, someone asked me whether the Craig and Barbara Barrett gift would be used to mediate the problem on graduate waivers. I said no, but that it is a step in that direction. I also promised to come back with more information. As many of you may have heard, our success in recruiting better and better classes has had an impact on our availability of scholarships--a very slow impact. We are authorized by the Regents to give resident waivers--the $2,000 that a resident student pays for tuition, or alternatively $2,000 of the $9,000 that a non-resident pays. We are allowed to give 10% of our total student body resident waivers without having to repay them. Please understand that those are not free. That means at $2,000 times 4,000, even assuming that some of those students may not have attended anyway, means in effect we have lost 8 million dollars in unrealized revenue. That is a real cost to the institution, and we are not making any headway.

Second, we are allowed to give 10% of our non-resident students, non-resident waivers, which means again a loss of 7,000 dollars in foregone revenue. To the extent we give waivers beyond that point, we have to find a source outside of our general fund to pay for those tuition waivers.

This is the situation for graduate student waivers. If you assume that we give out resident waivers and allocate them proportionally to what fraction of our students are graduate and undergraduate students, then we can calculate the number given to graduate students. Resident waivers are given either to resident graduate students or as a part of a non-resident student packages. In the past, we have only used about 250 of those resident waivers for graduate students. So undergraduates got a disproportionate share of the resident waivers. In the future, we will be giving the graduate students their proportional share of resident waivers.

On non-resident waivers, which some of you are more concerned about, the proportional allocation of graduate non-resident waivers is about 220. In the past, we actually gave about 500 to 400 non-resident waivers; in the future we will be giving them the proportionate share, so in essence, we have increased the number of graduate resident waivers and decreased the number of graduate non-resident waivers to bring them into proportion.

Finally, since all graduate assistants automatically get a non-resident waiver, that is an additional 1,000 non-resident waivers given. The total number of graduate non-resident waivers has gone from about 1500 to about 1200 as a consequence of the above. The total of resident waivers for graduate students has gone from 200 to 550. In fact, we are giving out more waivers than you see here (in the overhead), but those are being paid for by some other fund source. I will be happy to answer questions at this point.

Senator Rice: One indication is that the non-resident waivers will continue to drop next year as well.

Provost Glick: That is correct. The non-resident waivers are only used for students that do not have graduate assistantships. Graduate assistants get a non-resident waiver automatically.

Senator Gooding: Has anyone done an analysis of what impacts it will have on our ability to recruit non-resident top graduate students?

Provost Glick: We recognize that if we do not find additional resources for recruitment, it will have a negative impact on both the quality and the number. On the other hand, what has happened is that we have been over-allocating in the past. One of the major reasons we are pushing so hard to raise our external funding, both through gifts and through grants, is so we can have a financial profile more like other universities that do use complete support strategies.

6. New Business (Senate Committee Reports and New Motion for First Reading).

6. A Executive Committee (David Burstein).

6.A.1 Report of the Bylaws Revision Committee (John Crawford, Chair).

The proposed revisions were introduced for a first reading.

Senator Crawford: We are required by our bylaws to revise the bylaws every four years. The last time they were revised there were extensive changes. This time the changes are more narrow and clear. We set out to increase the enfranchisement of the individuals involved in this body. We decided to try to simplify the parliamentary complexity of what we do and we tried to simplify our committees. You have all received the draft revisions via email. Under 1.A, under membership, we enfranchised "the faculty". Secondly, we allowed all full-time lecturers and senior lecturers (on three-year renewable contracts) to be enfranchised, rather than to specifically limit it to lecturers in the Honors College.

We dealt with the issue of what it takes to have a legal quorum. We returned to traditional quorum that Robert's Rules of Order specifies, a simple majority, instead of 2/3 of our membership.

The last thing we did, under the old committee structure: we had two committees under University Affairs Committees: Financial Affairs and University Services and Facilities. Financial Affairs has been nonfunctional now for several years. So, we have dropped both of those and collapsed them into one University Affairs Committee and took the functions and combined them under the new committee.

These revisions are straightforward and I will answer questions at this time.

Senator (Donna) Landers: Since I am now the elected representative of the Lecturers and Senior Lecturers, and just received this document several hours ago, what is the reason for using the wording of 3-year renewable contracts?

Senator Crawford: This language was inserted because there are also individuals that are hired on a one-year contract and we felt that they may not have the vested interest in the welfare of the institution to such a degree that they should be enfranchised

Rationale: This request is due to the recent reorganization of faculty in the College of Education with a primary interest in Educational Technology and faculty with a primary interest in Learning.

6.C.3 Senate Motion #15 (1999-2000) was introduced: "The Curriculum and Academic Programs Committee recommends Academic Senate approval of a proposal submitted by the College of Education to merge M.Ed. in Learning and Instructional Technology and M.Ed. in Educational Media and Computers to form M.Ed. in Educational Technology." Rationale: Educational Media and Computers (EMC) and Learning and Instructional Technology (LNT) programs overlapped sufficiently to cause comment and discussion over the years as to the wisdom of maintaining separate programs. Both programs are highly respected and productive, albeit with somewhat different but complementary strengths. Both EMC and LNT M.Ed. programs at ASU emphasize several aspects of Educational Technology. The focus of the M.Ed. in LNT is on the design, development, and evaluation of instruction; the M.Ed. in EMC emphasizes the use of media and computers in education. Students in both programs study the design, development, production, utilization, and evaluation of instructional programs.
