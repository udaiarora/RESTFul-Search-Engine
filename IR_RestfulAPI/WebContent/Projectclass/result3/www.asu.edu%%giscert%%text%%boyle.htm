The Influence of Forest Fragmentation on Primate Distribution and
Traveling Patterns of Chiropotes sagulatus in the Brazilian Amazon

ABSTRACT

Forest fragmentation occurs when sections of forest are cleared, leaving a mosaic of patches surrounded by a non-forested matrix. I used GIS to examine how fragmentation affects: 1) the spatial distribution of six primate species, and 2) traveling patterns of the bearded saki monkey (Chiropotes sagulatus) in the Brazilian Amazon. 

I conducted primate censuses in forest fragments and areas of continuous forest. When bearded sakis were present, I recorded their location every five minutes with a Garmin V GPS unit. I plotted the distribution of the six species and the bearded saki traveling routes on a satellite image of the study area. I then determined daily distance traveled and home range size using the Home Range Extension for ArcView 3.x. 

Results indicated that fragment size and matrix condition influenced primate distribution. Furthermore, individuals in fragments had smaller home ranges, traveled shorter distances, and had more circular and repetitive travel paths.
