Computing Commons

Infrastructure

Architectural Overview

The architectural objective of the Computing Commons is to meet operational and functional change over the life of the building. We wanted as much flexibility as we could afford. The building has accessible floors throughout -- 6" or 18" raised floor on floors 1 and 2 where changes occur more frequently; cellular decking on floors 3 and 4. All furnishings are systems-based and movable. While some things are easier to change than others, nothing is fixed in place. Lighting throughout the building is non-glare and flexible as well. Power and air-conditioning systems will easily accommodate increased occupancy and/or workstations over the life of the building.

Data Communications

There are over 1000 computer/print/workstation connections (twisted pair 10/100 mbps) in the building. In order to accomplish this high level of connectivity and to provide for future moves and changes, a number of design elements were included in the building infrastructure. For example, the first two floors (the building public spaces) are constructed on raised floor to provide almost limitless reconfiguration over the life of the building. The third and fourth floor office spaces are constructed with a cellular deck flooring that allows power and communication facilities to be installed on a grid that is about 5 feet by 5 feet on center; not quite the same as raised floor but extremely flexible for the slightly less dynamic office environment

Data, voice, and video capabilities can be provided anywhere; growth can be accommodated due to oversize conduits with pull strings, as well as cable trays which run vertically and horizontally. Each floor of the building has four communication equipment rooms located so that any workstation location is within 150 cable feet. This means that there is complete accessibility for all current and most anticipated forms of communication without needing to rewire the workstation locations.

Video

By taking advantage of the same equipment rooms, cable-way and conduit system infrastructure, the building is able to support video to virtually any room. Satellite uplinks and downlinks are possible in several rooms. There is plenty of room for growth and change with respect to multimedia cability for years to come.

Today the main lobby provides a television running the SCOLA 24-hour foreign language news broadcast. CNN, C-SPAN I and II as well as other cable channels could be programmed.

The Commons Video Conference room, CPCOM 123A, is equipped with PictureTel compressed digital video conferencing equipment. This equipment is connected into the Tri-University system so that video conferences with ASU West, U of A, and/or NAU are easily scheduled. Video programs received via the several satellite antennae (both C-band and Ku-band) operated by Telecommunication Services can also be brought into this facility.

The Commons Auditorium is connected to the building and campus infrastructure so that any of the video programs mentioned above can be viewed in this 62 seat facility. The dominant projection device used in the classrooms and the auditorium are Promixa projectors.

ADA

Throughout design and construction, Information Technology worked closely with ASU's Disability Resources for Students office to ensure that all accessibility issues were addressed with respect to building architecture and furnishings. Every classroom and the first floor computing site has electric, height-adjustable tables. All students, not just those who use a wheelchair, enter the first floor computing site via ramps. Likewise, adaptive technology hardware and software are provided.

Security

Building security is a big challenge, as it's difficult to keep an open, friendly environment and not compromise the security of equipment. In the Computing Commons, several approaches are taken: a proximity card access system, keys, Closed Circuit TV, tattle tale system for documentation, tie-downs, and visual security by IT staff.

The Future

The Computing Commons represents technology for today and an infrastructure for the next 20 years.
