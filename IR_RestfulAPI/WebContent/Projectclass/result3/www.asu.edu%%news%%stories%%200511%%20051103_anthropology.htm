November 3, 2005

Anthropology makeover builds new research strength

ASU launches new School of Human Evolution & Social Change

Arizona State University is launching its new School of Human Evolution & Social Change (SHESC) to re-energize the field of anthropology and focus it on the challenges of modern societies.

The new school, housed in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, was officially launched Nov. 8 and builds on an already highly ranked ASU Department of Anthropology by also including non-anthropology faculty members. The result is a school that will focus not only on the past and present, but also on the future functioning of humans and human societies, said Sander van der Leeuw, director of SHESC.

"Our new School of Human Evolution & Social Change breaks down the traditional disciplines of anthropology and re-directs the energies of the school to problems faced by modern societies,” said ASU President Michael Crow. “Problems like the spread of disease, environmental concerns or unhealthy growth are problems encountered throughout history. We feel we can make significant contributions to the challenges of today by better understanding how they played out in the past.”

The role of the new school is critical because of how little we truly know about the planet we inhabit and how little we understand of the impact we have on it, van der Leeuw explained.

"At the beginning of the 21st Century, for the first time in the history of the planet, every part of the world, however distant, is interconnected,” van der Leeuw said. “People, knowledge, products and energy, as well as viruses, bacteria and other health threatening organisms can migrate anywhere, anytime.”

"The world population is expanding and rapidly becoming more urbanized,” he added. "Many resources are under the threat of exhaustion, extinction or pollution. Globalization thus fuels interest in the health of our planet.”

The new School of Human Evolution & Social Change will study these challenges by bringing together its core competencies in anthropology with a number of non-traditional fields. Seven non-anthropologists — including scholars trained in computer science, environmental economics, geography, mathematics, physiology, prehistory and sociology — are joining the school’s faculty this year. These faculty will build upon the well-established strengths of ASU’s anthropology program, including the study of rich ethnic diversity, indigenous traditions and the desert environment of the American Southwest, to undertake research that spans the globe.

The school has made several significant new hires, including bringing in Jane Buikstra, an eminent bioarchaeologist and National Academy of Sciences member; Charles Perrings, a noted professor of environmental economics and environmental management; and Alexandra Brewis-Slade, a professor of medical anthropology. They will be joined by several faculty from within ASU, including sociologists Bob Bolin, Ed Hackett and Sharon Harlan.

The school is set up thematically around six primary research themes. They are:

•Human origins, evolution and diversity, to cover the evolution of the species and its long-term survival;

•Societies and their natural environments, which will explore the interface between society and its material and natural environments;

•Natural, social and cultural dimensions of human health, to explore the relationship between society, environment and health;

•Urban societies, the institutions, policies and governance of complex urban societies;

•Cultural, heritage and identity, to study the cultural dynamics that govern the interactions between people; and

•Global dynamics and regional interactions, which will study the causes and effects of globalization.

"We are integrating archaeology and anthropology in a very long term transdisciplinary approach that will contribute to our understanding of the future,” van der Leeuw says.

Meeting a complex future

One of the school’s important aims will be to advance new and innovative approaches to policy relevant research. Thus the school will be among the first social science programs to include the complex adaptive systems perspective in its research and teaching.

The complex adaptive systems approach uses theories and tools across many sciences. Coupled with advanced data gathering and computer modeling systems, this novel approach helps researchers from different disciplines to conceptualize and analyze problems involving change and transformation.

There are a number of schools, such as Carnegie Mellon University in Pittsburgh, that apply the principles of complex systems in the hard sciences like physics and engineering.

"The social science side is very undeveloped,” van der Leeuw says. “That is where we see our niche. The application of complex adaptive systems to the social science of primates and humans.”

No matter how complex the challenges that confront humans today there are core issues that drive them. Identity, resiliency and adaptability are among these issues and they were issues for our ancestors as well.

“How is it possible for human beings to survive so many epidemics, so many different kinds of viruses over the millions of years they have been in existence,” van der Leeuw asked. “Where are the robust parts of us as a system. Where are the parts that ensure our survival.”

"Answering those key questions and meeting the needs of today’s society will help us become a more resilient society,” van der Leeuw added. “To do so, the comparative study of the long-term dynamics of many societies is essential.”
