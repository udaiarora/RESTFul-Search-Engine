FAMILY TIES: GOING HOME FOR THE HOLIDAYS

Food for Thought

Along with anticipation of winter break, sleeping late, and no homework, students may be approaching their extended visit home with both feelings of excitement and anxiety. Most students look forward to “going home” to relax, enjoy home cooking, visit with old friends, and share time with family.

However, for some students, the visit home may be stir up mixed feelings. Being away from home has meant living with more freedom and less parental involvement. Some families see their sons and daughters as the same in November as when they left them at school in August. Thus, your family may have difficulty accepting your new pattern of coming and going, established at school, and may expect you to adhere to family routine.

Your new independence as an adult may not be fully understood at home. Conflict may result when you describe how your attitudes or way of thinking has changed in a number of areas—through new experiences and your studies. Your family’s response to how you have changed could lead to feelings of frustration and emotional distance from your family.

Recipe For the Visit Home

1. Phone, write, or email your parents to discuss plans and expectations BEFORE arriving home. Plan to continue the conversation once you are at home.

2. Talk over with roommates, friends, residence hall staff, or others how you will talk to your family about “difficult” subjects, e.g., academic progress, new relationships, finances, negotiating house rules.

3. Plan to share your thoughts, feelings, and new ideas at a pace that “works” for your parents. Give them time to think about each new concept you have described.

4. Be open to listening to your parents’ views. And, if your new outlook clashes with your family’s traditional values, be prepared to listen to their feelings and opinions. The conversation could be interesting and lively!

Bon Appetit!

When you return home for holidays or other visits, you may find that your family is treating you more like a guest than a live-in family member. Your family will want you to relax and to spend time “catching up.” Whatever the reception you receive at home, work to keep the lines of communication open and expect a mixture of feelings about your experience from arrival to departure.

The Staff at Counseling and Consultation wish you a rejuvenating break and invite you to use our services for support, problem-solving, or other issues before you leave for home or upon your return to school. See our website for more information: http://www.asu.edu/studentaffairs/counseling/

Finally, a helpful book for students to consult (or to purchase for your parents…):

Beating the College Blues: A student’s guide to coping with the emotional ups and downs of college life. By Paul A. Grayson and Philip Meilman. New York: Facts on File, Inc. 1982, pp.231.
