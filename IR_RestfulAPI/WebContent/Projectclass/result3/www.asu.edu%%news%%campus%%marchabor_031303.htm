March 18, 2003

Regents approve tuition increase, residence hall rates

The Arizona Board of Regents has approved a tuition increase tied to an enhanced financial aid package for resident undergraduates, saying the move will provide for greater access to the public university system while protecting low-income families.

The board voted 9-0, at its meeting March 6, in favor of raising ASU tuition $1,000 for resident and nonresident students and $1,200 for resident and nonresident graduate students for the 2003 - 04 academic year. In doing so, the board fully adopted the recommendations set forth by ASU President Michael Crow.

Crow commended the board on its action, saying the revenues generated by the increase would not only maintain the academic quality of the university but would allow ASU to move forward in achieving its strategic goals to increase student services.

Crow said the 40 percent set-aside of funds earmarked for financial aid was "an equivalent of a $400 million endowment to protect thousands of Arizona families" against the rising costs of higher education.

In approving the tuition increase, the board moved the Arizona University System away from the lowest tuition rate among the 50 states. The increase should keep the system within the board's goal of maintaining a position ranked in the lower third of senior public institutions, however.

Regent Gary Stewart of Phoenix said by approving the tuition, the board is effectively creating much needed funding that should stay in the university.

"We need to remind the good, well-meaning legislators who are trying to solve a difficult state budget deficit that these are funds that were raised by us and should stay here," he said.

The board also approved a 5 percent increase for ASU residence hall rates for the 2003 - 04 academic year. The residence hall increase averages $158 and will allow Residential Life to make repairs and renovations to address deferred maintenance.

In other action:
. The board approved project implementation for safety improvements to the Gammage Auditorium rigging system and fire curtain to correct significant safety and code issues. The project is estimated to cost $1.6 million and should begin in June.
. Proposed changes to the board's policy regarding definitions of special class fees or requests were discussed. Proposed changes include modifying the allowable uses of class fees to include supervisory instruction for in-context training classes and accompanists for music and dance classes. The issue will be brought back for further discussion in the April meeting.
. The board voted to modify its approval action taken at the November 2002 meeting for the formation of a South Campus student residential project to be built using a public/private partnership. A modification was needed to respond to a recent tightening of the bond rating system by rating agencies on non-recourse student housing debt. The board voted to allow ASU to provide contingent operating assistance in the ground lease for the project to significantly decrease financing costs.
. The board held its first discussion on a Tri-University Voluntary Separation and Retirement Incentive program to be instituted upon approval under the Changing Directions Initiative. Crow said such a policy is needed to move ASU ahead in its transformation by providing the proper incentive mechanisms for retirement to individuals who do not share in the vision of ASU's new direction. Further discussion is expected at the board's April meeting.
