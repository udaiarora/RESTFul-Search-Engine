April 7, 2003

Famous last words - Students choose gifted teachers for 2003 Last Lecture Series

Snooze through one of these lectures? Not a chance. In a celebration of great teaching, ASU students have chosen three of their favorite professors to give a Last Lecture. The teachers are asked to speak as if it were the last appearance they would ever make before a class, on topics close to their hearts.

In the eighth year of what has become an honored tradition sponsored by Co-Curricular Programs, three dynamic teachers have been selected out of 106 who were nominated.

They are Greg Durham, senior lecturer in finance who has taught at ASU eight years; Daniel Brouwer, assistant professor in the Hugh Downs School of Human Communication who has been at ASU three years; and Chris Smith, associate professor of history, who is completing 32 years at ASU.

Durham begins the series at 7:30 p.m., April 9, in the Old Main Carson Ballroom, with "Parting Thoughts: Alternative Perspectives on Education and Capitalism for the Overachieving Undergraduate Student." He will share some "slightly idealistic philosophies" about what a student should seek from the collegiate academic experience.

"I'll talk about viewing classes, coursework and grades not so much from a competitive perspective, but from a collaborative one," he says. "I make great efforts to try to instill in my students a set of values that is completely different from the fundamental principles which govern the professional business environment."

A student who nominated Durham said, "He makes finance come alive before his students' eyes."

Brouwer's lecture on "The Rhetoric of the Body" will be at 7:30 p.m., April 21, in the Memorial Union (MU) Pima Room.

"I plan to combine a lecture with humorous and poignant performance of personal narratives," he says. "This will provide me with the opportunity to dramatize the interconnections between teaching and studying, between lived experience and scholarly abstractions, and between the university and public life."

He also will comment on how people's relations to their bodies are altered after being diagnosed with a serious illness, specifically HIV and AIDS.

Students praised Brouwer: "He garners respect while treating his students with respect, as well."

Smith said he will talk about historical items in his collections of Americana, from political and labor buttons to songs, kitchen artifacts and private journals. He will present "Looking for History Everywhere and Anywhere I Can Find It: Insights from the American Material and Popular Culture," at 7:30 p.m., April 29, in the MU Pima Room.

"I have always been searching for the historical insights that jump from the artifact and cry out for understanding of our past," he said. "We never outgrow, nor should we, our need to show and tell, for it shows our roles in the history of our time and the history of the nation."

A student wrote that "Professor Smith conveys his passion for history in and outside the classroom. He approaches his subjects like no other."

The three professors were chosen by a panel of students and each will receive a $500 honorarium. For information, call Kim Miller at (480) 965-9600.
