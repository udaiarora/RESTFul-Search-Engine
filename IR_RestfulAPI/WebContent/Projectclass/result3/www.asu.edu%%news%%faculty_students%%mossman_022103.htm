February 24, 2003

Mossman earns recognition, continues research in radiation

Since he first set foot on the campus of Wayne State University in 1968, Kenneth Mossman has been studying physics and biology.

For his dedication and countless contributions in the area of health physics and research Mossman, health physics professor and director of the University Office of Radiation Safety at ASU, was presented with the 2002 Health Physics Society Founders Award.

In an article presented at the 47th Annual Meeting of the Health Physics Society, Rick Jones, deputy assistant secretary of the U.S. Department of Energy wrote, "Dr. Mossman is the model of a consummate health physicist, unselfishly giving of himself to provide exceptional service to the Health Physics Society, his many students over the years and to his colleagues, who have depended upon him for sound, technically-defensible knowledge in radiation biology and, more specifically, the risks of radiation exposure and the low-level effects of radiation in humans."

Mossman said he had always been interested in the areas of physics and biology, but it was reading a book by Erwin Schroedinger that fueled his motivation to pursue a career in health physics.

"Reading Erwin Schroedinger's little book 'What is Life?' was particularly enlightening because of its interesting discussion of life processes from a physicist's perspective," he says.

Currently, Mossman is conducting research on nuclear regulatory science and policy, which has focused on strategies to reconfigure the regulatory framework for U.S. radiation protection with the goal of maintaining or improving public and worker health and safety, while reducing regulatory compliance costs. A description of Mossman's work was published in the January 2003 issue of "Environmental Health Perspectives."

In addition, Mossman is advising governments on radiation science and policy and developing a system of radiation protection for the environment in collaboration with the International Commission on Radiological Protection (London) and the Nuclear Energy Agency of the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (Paris).

Mossman says promoting safe practices involving nuclear technologies is his main objective.

"In my capacity as director of the ASU Office of Radiation Safety, I am responsible for making sure that radioactive material, lasers and X-ray machines are used safely in research and instruction," he says. "Nuclear power and medical X-rays are two examples of how society benefits from radiation. However, the uncontrolled uses of radiation may be potentially hazardous. I have a long standing interest in promoting safe uses of radiation technologies for the betterment of society."

Mossman served as assistant vice president for research at ASU, 1990 - 92; as a faculty member at Georgetown University, 1973 - 90; and was professor and founding chairman of the Radiation Science department in Georgetown's Graduate School from 1985 to 1990.

Mossman earned a B.S. in biology from Wayne State University; M.S. and Ph.D. degrees from the University of Tennessee in radiation biology; and a M.Ed. degree in higher education administration from the University of Maryland.

For information regarding Mossman's research projects, visit the Web at (http://lsvl.la.asu.edu/microbiology/ faculty/mossman.htm).
