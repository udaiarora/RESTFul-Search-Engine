August 10, 2006

Barrett Summer Scholars get taste of campus life

Students living in the halls at Barrett Honors College this summer weren't typical college kids. Many of them were less than 5 feet tall, backpacks slung on thin shoulders, T-shirts reaching halfway down to their knees. On weekends, their parents showed up to help them do laundry.

They were just 12 to 14 years old, after all ? the first group of seventh-, eighth- and ninth-graders in the Barrett Summer Scholars Program. Almost 90 of Arizona 's most gifted students lived on campus for three weeks July 10-28, taking rigorous academic classes from Honors College faculty.

They read Thoreau, Sartre and Plato, writing papers in class. They studied advanced math, went on archaeological digs and learned digital animation. They took field trips to the other ASU campuses, the Arizona Science Center and the Phoenix Zoo.

The students also got to relax, swimming at the Student Recreation Complex and watching movies.

Beyond challenging classes and fun times, the students got to meet some of the nation's most notable leaders. They met Sandra Day O'Connor, the recently retired U.S. Supreme Court Justice who became the first female on the Supreme Court; Sally Ride, America's first woman astronaut; and Maj. Gen. David Rataczak, the much-decorated leader of the Arizona National Guard.

The scholars were a diverse group, but all were recommended for the program by the gifted program coordinators at their schools because they scored in the 97th percentile or above on a state-recognized cognitive ability test.

ASU hopes the taste of campus life at a major university will whet their appetite for more, creating a pipeline of talented youngsters who will enroll as freshmen in a few years.

?It's been a good experience for everyone,? says Mark Jacobs, dean of the Barrett Honors College. ?The faculty loved teaching these kids. Our regular students have been resident advisers. And the kids clearly loved all their courses, which were intellectually challenging but not beyond their abilities at all.?

Thomas Martin, Honors College faculty who helped devise a curriculum for their Human Event humanities class, says the students' level of thinking and class discussion was impressive.

?These are very bright kids,? he says. ?The purpose of the Human Event was not to provide them with prepackaged answers, but to help further develop their critical faculties so they can develop answers throughout their lives. Hopefully some of these students will return and continue that journey with us when they are a little older.?

Barbara Barrett, a driving force behind the program, also spoke to the group and attended last week's culminating talent show with her husband, Craig. Barrett Summer Scholars was supported largely by the Office of University Student Initiatives, along with the Honors College.
