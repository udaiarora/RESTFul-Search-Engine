July 28, 2003

Marchant to study legal impact of human genome sequencing

ASU Law Professor Gary Marchant has been awarded a $500,000 grant over two years from the National Institutes of Health (NIH). He will analyze the legal, ethical and policy effects that sequencing the human genome will have on federal environmental policy and regulation.

“Mapping the human genome will cause an explosion in man’s knowledge of genetic factors that affect individual human susceptibility to environmental pollution,” Marchant said. “The law needs to adjust and expand to meet this explosion. With this grant we will start what surely will be a long-term legal effort to catch up with this emerging knowledge.”

Marchant, executive director of the Center for Law, Science and Technology at the ASU College of Law, will lead a team that includes Andrew Askland, director of the Center for Law, Science & Technology; Richard Sharp of the Baylor College of Medicine and Jamie Grodsky, associate professor of law at the University of Minnesota Law School.

The study will focus on:
• The impacts new data on human genetic susceptibility to adverse environmental factors have on existing laws and regulations;
• How the new human genome data can be integrated into existing legal structures;
• The ethical and policy considerations that must be addressed in using the new genomic data in environmental decision-making; and
• The development of criteria for using data about the human genome in environmental regulation based on information developed from the team’s analyses.

“New genetic knowledge has revealed that we are all individually unique in how we respond to foreign materials entering our bodies, whether they be industrial chemicals, pollutants, drugs, foods or viruses,” Marchant said. “The way we regulate the environment will have to be revised to take account of our new ability to identify those who are most likely to be adversely affected by particular environmental exposures and those who are not.”

Marchant received both undergraduate and doctoral degrees in genetics from the University of British Columbia. He also earned a master’s in public policy from the Kennedy School of Government at Harvard and a juris doctor from Harvard Law School where he was editor-in-chief of the “Harvard Journal of Law & Technology” and editor of the “Harvard Environmental Law Review.” Before joining the ASU College of Law, he was a partner in the Washington office of Kirkland & Ellis, where he practiced environmental law. Currently, he teaches environmental law and the “Law & Genetics” course at the college.

The National Institutes of Health is an agency within the federal Department of Health and Human Services. It is composed of a number of institutes, two of which, the National Institute of Environmental Health Sciences and the National Human Genome Research Institute, are funding Marchant’s study. The overall goal of the NIH is to provide knowledge that will improve the public’s health. This fiscal year, Congress appropriated approximately $23.4 billion to conduct research in its laboratories, support research at universities and health related institutions and foster communication of medical information.
