April 10, 2003

Program takes ecology lessons to classroom

Ecology Explorers is a K-12 education program that gives Valley students and teachers the opportunity to work within the Central Arizona-Phoenix Long Term Ecological Research (CAP-LTER) project at ASU.

Through this program, students can collect data on insects, birds, plants and test hypotheses about the impact of urbanization on our local ecosystem. In doing, students are engaged in scientific collaboration that encourages literacy and contributes to the long-term monitoring of our desert city, says Monic Elser, educational liaison for ASU's Center of Environmental Studies.

Since its inception in 1997, Ecology Explorers, CAP-LTER's educational outreach program, has worked with 77 public school teachers at 54 schools in 22 districts in and around the metro Phoenix. Currently the program is reaching an estimated 2,000 students each year, and as more teachers sign on, the result is a groundswell. Ecology Explorer programs are also ongoing in local private and charter schools. National Science Foundation funds the project, one of only two long-term urban ecological studies in the United States.

Recently a dozen K-12 teachers experienced some of the barriers that can impede scientific discovery. The exercise was part of a daylong Ecology Explorers Workshop, one of several such workshops hosted annually at ASU. The workshops are designed to improve teachers' understanding of the data gathering processes commonly used in science and how to relay those skills to students performing at different learning levels. Teaching young researchers how to tighten their observation skills is essential to any scientific endeavor, especially if that project has long-term ramifications.

On this day, ASU Graduate Student Amanda Coleman leads the teachers' workshop. She begins with a game where teachers are paired and sit back to back. One explains an inkblot while the other tries to draw it unseen. The teacher illustrating is not allowed to ask questions and must rely only on the description of their partner.

While some of the drawings were way off the mark, teachers saw first hand how a lack of common terminology and detailed observation skills could lead down the path to no-man's land. The teachers have the opportunity to then apply their newfound understanding to a second exercise, this time describing a common houseplant leaf. In this exercise, knowing basic leaf parts, shapes and terms, as well as having a common vocabulary proved to be key to success.

Once these skills are mastered, comparing research notes and adapting target skills to age-appropriate and practical classroom exercises seem less daunting. Teachers also find plenty of onsite support from ASU faculty, graduate teaching and research associates and plenty of classroom materials available through the program's Web site.

For information on the Ecology Explorers program, visit (http://caplter.asu.edu/explorers).
