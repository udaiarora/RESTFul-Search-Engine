June 21, 2006

Study shows Arizonans recognize benefits of science, technology

A new statewide survey shows that Arizonans – regardless of location, income, education or gender – are convinced that science and technology will deliver on high-paying jobs, personal benefits, and solutions to environmental and health problems.

The results of this first-ever, in-depth study are presented in “Arizonans’ Attitudes toward Science, Technology and Their Effects on the Economy,” a new report from ASU’s Morrison Institute for Public Policy.

A new survey by ASU's Morrison Institute shows that Arizonans see the economic and quality of life benefits of vibrant science and technology efforts in the state.

The survey reveals that Arizona residents agree that science and technology play an important part in the state’s economic development. In addition, Arizonans want their state to be a leader in science and technology research. Responses by Arizonans also acknowledge that leadership would require investment and a commitment to science education. For example:

• More than nine out of 10 respondents said that it is important for Arizona to be a national and international leader in science and technology.

• Nearly 90 percent of Arizonans surveyed felt developments in science and technology help create new high-paying jobs.

• More than 70 percent of those surveyed said they would be willing to pay higher taxes to support science and technology research that would create new jobs and improve health care.

• Eight out of 10 said they believe that science education is at least as important as teaching math, reading and writing.

• Two out of three reported they are more likely to vote for statewide candidates who support science and technology research in the state.

“Arizona leaders now have data that show how strongly positive Arizonans are about science and technology investments, research and education, and their potential for economic benefit,” says Rob Melnick, director of the Morrison Institute and associate vice president for economic affairs and public policy at ASU. “These results will surprise a lot of people – particularly in relation to support for political candidates. There is just no doubt that Arizonans foresee a science and technology-based economy, and they expect investments to be made that will deliver on that future.”

Arizonans also see themselves as fairly informed about science and technology advancements, even as they admit the coverage of advances can sometimes be confusing.

Health was most frequently cited as the area where residents felt they benefit from science and technology research, while health, energy and the environment were picked as the most important problems that science and technology could help solve.

“This survey shows Arizonans realize they have a stake in what happens in science and technology,” says Richard Toon, the survey’s principal investigator. “Residents seem ready to become involved in decisions about the future, and they are looking for great things from science and technology.”

The survey, conducted by telephone in March of a representative sample of Arizonans, included almost 800 randomly selected heads of households across Arizona and has a margin of error of plus or minus 3 percent.

“Arizonans’ Attitudes toward Science, Technology and Their Effects on the Economy” is available at www.morrisoninstitute.org.
