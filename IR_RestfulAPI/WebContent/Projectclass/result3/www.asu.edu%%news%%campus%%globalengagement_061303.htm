June 13, 2003

Crow delivers on global engagement

ASU President Michael Crow is continuing to meet with Mexican and other Latin American leaders, making the case for the university's global engagement. ASU is moving forward at full speed in this endeavor, and soon will officially launch its Pan American initiative, first announced during Crow's inauguration.

"We're looking for partners," said Crow as he featured ASU's newest entrepreneurial efforts at the Partnership for Prosperity 2003 meeting in San Francisco June 9. Crow highlighted ASU's efforts, including the Arizona Biodesign Institute, the Consortium for the Study of Rapidly Urbanizing Regions and the creation of Arizona Technology Enterprises.

"All of the important scientific innovations that drive industry progress are generated by universities," Crow emphasized as he discussed human capital and innovation with Conacyt (Mexico's national science foundation), the U.S. Agency for International Development, the Council for Competitiveness, the Mexican Ministry of the Economy, U.S. Department of Labor and the Mexican Ministry of Labor.

Crow also participated in a roundtable discussion of university presidents, which included the leaders of 18 Mexican institutions and President Richard Atkinson of the University of California, President Diana Natalicio of the University of Texas-El Paso, President John DeGioia of Georgetown University, Vice Provost Stanley Johnson of Iowa State University and President Peter Likins of the University of Arizona.

U.S. President George W. Bush and Mexican President Vincente Fox launched the Partnership for Prosperity in 2001, as a way to stimulate private investment and bolster cross-border economic development, particularly in Mexico.

This summer, ASU will heighten its efforts to engage Mexico and Latin America, finalizing the design and conceptualization of its Pan American initiative.

Crow's participation in the Partnership for Prosperity meeting was a continuation of his efforts to engage ASU in Mexico and the rest of Latin America. In June 2002, Crow challenged current and former Latin American ministers of education and the economy to allow their higher education institutions to become more autonomous.

This high-level group had gathered in Monterrey, Mexico, at the World Bank Policy Forum for Knowledge for Development in Argentina, Brazil, Chile and Mexico.
Earlier this year, Crow led a group of ASU administrators to Nogales, where the challenges of economic and social development at the border were manifested through meetings with alumni, a business roundtable and a first-hand look at border customs and inspections.

This spring, the Office of University Initiatives led a small team to Mexico City and Monterrey, speaking with university and government officials about the possibility of research collaborations, educational exchanges and joint graduate programs.
