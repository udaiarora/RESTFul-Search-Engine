Preliminary Matter Requirements

CONTENTS AND ORDER
The preliminary matter consists of the following pages, in this order:
  1. Title page
  2. Approval page
  3. Abstract
  4. Dedication (optional)
  5. Acknowledgments (optional)
  6. Table of contents
  7. List of tables (if tables appear in document)
  8. List of figures (if figures appear in document)
  9. Other lists (e.g., nomenclature, definitions, glossary of terms, etc.)
  10. Preface (optional; must be less than 10 pages)
For all the preliminary pages listed above, the following format specifications apply:
  • The margins are set to 1.5 inches on the left and 1 inch on the right, top, and bottom.


  • The same font and size are used for all text.


  • The headings for each preliminary page (except the “Dedication,” which does not require a heading) are centered between the margins, in ALL CAPITAL LETTERS, double-spaced, and begin on the first line after the 1-inch top margin.


  • No bold type.


  • Italics or underline are used only for species, genera, book titles, musical compositions, or foreign words.



PAGINATION OF THE PRELIMINARY PAGES
The title page and approval page are counted as pages one and two, but no page numbers appear on them. All subsequent preliminary pages are paginated with lowercase Roman numerals. Starting with ?iii? on the abstract, place all page numbers at the bottom of the page, centered between the left and right margins and on the first line outside the 1-inch bottom margin. Continue numbering consecutively on the subsequent pages up to the first page of the main text. You may use a footer feature if your software provides one, but be sure that your page numbers are the same font and size as the rest of your text and are set to begin at 1 inch from the bottom edge of the page.

The specifications for each of the preliminary pages are presented below.




TITLE PAGE
UMI/ProQuest specifies that students use words in place of formulae, symbols, superscripts, subscripts, Greek letters, chemical names, or other non-alphabetical symbols in their titles, and ASU conforms to these requirements also as certain symbols cannot be replicated when listing your thesis/dissertation title on your official transcript. Also, additional charges for binding may apply for these items (referred to as ?unusual stamping?) as well as titles that exceed the character limits set by the Bookstore. Please see bookstore.asu.edu for more information.

In choosing a title, please ensure that it accurately reflects your document?s content and scope and is punctuated appropriately, using a colon if there is a subtitle. It is suggested that you write out abbreviations and acronyms to facilitate searches related to your topic, especially those conducted by researchers in related and/or similar fields. Please be aware that if your title changes based on committee recommendation after your defense you will need to resubmit revised title and approval pages for an additional review before the dean?s signature can be obtained.

Each sample title page contains a description in the center of the page, which you should reproduce word-for-word, adding your correct degree and corresponding document type. Please note that there are exceptions to the description?s wording for certain degree programs, as shown in Table 1.

General Directions for the Title Page
(See Figures 1 and 2 as models.)

  2. On a new line (double-spaced and centered), type “by” in lowercase letters (if necessary, turn off your software’s automatic capitalization feature—e.g., AutoCorrect in Word® and QuickCorrect in WordPerfect®).

Approval Page
This page, once signed by your committee members after a successful defense, is an official record of your committee?s approval and the department?s acceptance of your thesis or dissertation. Therefore, you must have this page approved for format BEFORE you defend and ensure that the correct page is signed at the defense. Your title on this page should match the one on your title page exactly in terms of wording and punctuation; if you change one title, please make the other title consistent. If your title changes based on committee recommendation after your defense, you will need to resubmit revised title and approval pages for an additional format review, even if you already have final format approval, before the dean?s signature can be obtained.

To create a correctly formatted approval page, reproduce precisely the sample in Figure 3, which indicates the proper spacing and arrangement and refer to the general directions to create this page.

Please note that the sample reads ?Insert Appropriate Title? after the ?APPROVED? line. Most majors are in departments, and the correct title for this space is ?Department Chair.? General Directions for the Approval Page
  1. Using the same font and size as the title page, type the title in ALL CAPITAL LETTERS and double-spaced, centered between left and right margins with the exact wording and punctuation as the title page.

  2. On a new line (double-spaced and centered), type “by” in lowercase letters.

  3. On a new line (double-spaced and centered), type your full name as it appears on your transcript as listed with the Registrar's Office in title-cased letters.

  4. Add two double-spaced lines (four single-spaced lines) after your name and type “has been approved” in all lowercase letters and hit return to add one double space.

  5. Type the month and year of your oral defense (not graduation) formatted as follows:
    1. Month (title-cased).

    2. No comma between month and year.

  6. Change alignment to left (instead of centered), type “APPROVED” in all capital letters, followed by a colon (:) and hit enter.

  7. Add signature lines (to create lines in Word: hold down hyphen key and hit enter) as follows:
    1. Create the number of signature lines equal to the number of your committee members.

    2. Begin lines at the left margin and extend to the right margin.

    3. Make the lines solid and horizontal, with double-spacing between them.

    4. If you have one chair, type “Chair” (not “Chairman” or “Chairperson”) flush right on the top line.

    5. If you have two chairs, type “Co-Chair” (title-cased) on the first and second line, flush right and underlined.

    6. Add a comma (,) and one space before “Chair” or “Co-Chair.”


  8. Change alignment to centered, and directly underneath the last signature line, type “Supervisory Committee,” title-cased and single-spaced.

  9. Type “ACCEPTED” in all capitals, beginning at center (at 4.5 inches) between margins, followed by a colon (:).
    1. Begin lines at center between margins and extend to right margin.

    2. Make the lines solid and horizontal, with four single-space blank lines between them.

    3. Type the appropriate title for your degree under the first signature line in title case (see Table 2).

    4. Type “Dean, Division of Graduate Studies” under the second line in title case.

  10. Ensure the “APPROVED” section is vertically centered between the title and the “ACCEPTED” sections; make adjustments so that the spaces before and after the “APPROVED” section are as close to equal as possible while retaining the margins.

  11. Ensure the font and size for all text on the page are consistent and match the title page.

  12. Double check that your approval page looks like the sample (Figure 3).







Abstract
The abstract is your opportunity to present a succinct summary of your research and results. Many researchers read abstracts to determine the relevance, reliability, and quality of a source; therefore, if you create a clear and concise abstract, others are more likely to read your entire document. Before you write the abstract, read a number of abstracts in your field in order to recognize the qualities of a good abstract—and to learn what to avoid. Sample abstracts are easily accessible in Dissertation Abstracts International (www.il.proquest.com/products/pt-product-Dissertations.shtml), and, if you are a doctoral student, your abstract will be uploaded to the site as well. (Readers may also search or browse digital dissertations.)

For more guidelines on writing a successful abstract, refer to The Purpose of the Abstract at www.asu.edu/graduate/format/AbPurp.html. Please note that the Division of Graduate Studies dean reads your abstract before signing your approval page. If your abstract changes based on committee recommendation after your defense, you will need to resubmit the revised abstract for an additional format review before the dean?s signature can be obtained.

When formatting the abstract, refer to Figure 4 as a model.

General Directions for the Abstract
  1. Set the margins to 1.5 inches on the left and 1 inch on the right, top, and bottom.

  2. Type “ABSTRACT” in ALL CAPITAL LETTERS, centered between left and right margins, and double-spaced, using the same font and size as the title and approval page text.

  3. Summarize the research (or creative activity) that formed the basis for your document. Also, include your findings and indent paragraphs as indicated by your style guide.

  4. Ensure that your text complies with the following guidelines:
    1. All acronyms or abbreviated phrases are written out fully at the first use and followed by the acronym/abbreviation in parentheses. Subsequent uses of the terms are consistent with the indicated acronym/abbreviation.

    2. Avoid future tense when referring to the document contents (e.g., “This thesis will discuss . . .”).

    3. Most abstracts are written in third person. Avoid first person (“I” or “we”) unless commonly used in your discipline.

    4. No bold type.

    5. Italics or underline are used only for species, genera, book titles, musical compositions, or foreign words.

    6. Formulas, symbols, Greek letters, and abbreviations are expressed in words, even if the shorthand forms are conventional in your field.

  5. Add the lowercased Roman numeral three (“iii”) at the bottom of the page, ensuring that it does not fall within the 1-inch bottom margin. Ensure that the page number is the same font and size as the rest of the text. Continue numbering with “iv” if your abstract is two pages.

  6. Ensure that the abstract does not exceed the length requirement as indicated by UMI/ProQuest. If you are a doctoral student (or a master?s student who plans to have your thesis microfilmed), your abstract may be edited by UMI/ProQuest if it exceeds the word count. Therefore, please revise, if necessary, to meet the length restriction of no more than 150 words for master?s theses and 350 words for doctoral dissertations.
Note to Students in the Languages and Literatures Department. Documents produced to fulfill the requirements of a Master of Arts degree in Asian Languages and Civilizations (Chinese or Japanese), French, German, or Spanish or a Doctor of Philosophy degree in Spanish may be written in the language of the discipline. You must, however, include an extra abstract in English for archiving purposes.





Dedication and Acknowledgments (Optional)
Generally, the dedication contains a more personal note to family or loved ones, while the acknowledgements usually consist of professional and academic assistance. Both of these pages are optional, although most documents have a brief paragraph of acknowledgments to recognize the contributions of committee members and others. The dedication and the acknowledgments should be on separate pages. If you include these pages, you must maintain a professional tone and follow the formatting as follows (in addition to the preliminary matter formatting requirements, including pagination):
  • Double-spaced (optional for dedication).

  • The dedication and acknowledgments cannot exceed three pages combined.

  • Headings: The dedication does not require a heading, but “Acknowledgments” should be in all capitals and centered between the margins on the acknowledgments page.

  • Text: The text of the dedication may be centered on the page. The text of the acknowledgements should follow the indentation of paragraphs recommended by your style guide.

  • The end of your acknowledgments is an excellent place to note sponsorship or funding for your research, i.e., military, corporate, etc.




Table of Contents
The table of contents is compiled by listing the headings in the document pages that follow it; therefore, it should not include any pages preceding it (i.e., Abstract, Dedication, Acknowledgments, etc.). Be consistent in the heading levels that you include; for example, if you list the second-level subheadings from the first chapter, you should list the second-level subheadings from all chapters that contain this level. Each level of subheading should be consistently indented a few spaces more than the preceding level. There are a few choices for formatting the table of contents: those with numbered chapters (Figure 5 and Figure 6) and those without numbered chapters (Figure 7). Do not forget to number the pages consecutively, using a lowercase Roman numeral in the same size and font as the rest of your text, one inch from the bottom of the page. (Master of Fine Arts in Creative Writing students, see note following this section.)

General Directions for the Table of Contents
  1. Set the margins to 1.5 inches on the left and 1 inch on the right, top, and bottom.

  2. Type “TABLE OF CONTENTS” in ALL CAPITAL LETTERS, centered between left and right margins and double-spaced, using the same font and size as the previous pages.

  3. Begin listing any preliminary pages that follow the table of contents (e.g., lists) in ALL CAPS.
  4. Double-space between entries; you may single-space all two-line entries.

  5. If you have chapters, create the headings “CHAPTER” and “Page” as shown in Figures 5 and 6. If you do not have chapters, use the word “Page” as shown in Figure 7. Repeat such headings on all subsequent pages of your table of contents.

  6. You must include all headings and subheadings up to five levels of headings; if you have more than five levels of headings you may include as many as you see fit after the fifth. Use the same wording, punctuation, and capitalization (but not the same typeface) for each entry as used in the corresponding text. You may abbreviate lengthy entries but ensure that the wording is identical up to the point of abbreviation. Use indentation to differentiate between heading levels

  7. Add leader dots or solid lines between all listings and their corresponding page numbers. Ensure that all page numbers are vertically aligned. Create leader dots according to your software’s help menu specifications.

  8. Add page number(s): lowercase roman numeral(s), consecutive from previous section, centered between left and right margins, 1 inch from bottom of page.

  9. Double-check after any main text revisions that entries in the table of contents and lists match the ones in your text in terms of exact wording and punctuation and that the actual contents appear on the page numbers listed.

Note to Master of Fine Arts in Creative Writing Students. A creative writing practicum will not require a table of contents if there are no formal divisions within the document.





















List of Tables, List of Figures, and Other Lists
If your document has tables and figures, include these lists to help your readers access relevant data. Both the “LIST OF TABLES” and “LIST OF FIGURES” follow essentially the same format, resembling the table of contents. The samples in Figures 8 and 9 that follow provide models that are easy to read and work well for any document. Other lists, such as terms or nomenclature, should be formatted similarly. Double-check after any main text revisions that entries in the “LIST OF TABLES,” “LIST OF FIGURES,” and other lists match the ones in your text in terms of exact wording, punctuation, and capitalization (but not typeface) and that the actual contents appear on the page numbers listed.
