Krech earns minority fellowship from American Psychological Association

ASU doctoral student Paul Rock Krech has received a prestigious Minority Fellowship from the American Psychological Association (APA). The award will pay for Krech's tuition and fees for the next three years as well as travel costs to the annual APA convention, where he will present a research paper.

Krech, a clinical social worker with ASU's Counseling & Consultation is currently working toward his Ph.D. in measurement, statistics and methodological studies in the Psychology in Education program. For the fellowship, Krech will build and develop a tool to measure attention and mindfulness in elementary school-age children.

"The research is primarily in measurement and instrument construction," Krech says. "But the ultimate goal is to help children pay attention."

Krech says he believes many students suffer from high levels of anxiety in the classroom, which can lead to physical symptoms such as rapid breathing or an elevated heart rate. He says these symptoms can unconsciously distract a student, making it harder to pay attention and ultimately causing more anxiety.

While many of the exercises are simple, the difficulty comes in accurately measuring the students' attention. Krech's goal is to develop a series of classroom exercises for teachers that will measure these unconscious actions or "diffuse attention." Once these diffuse attention levels can be accurately measured, controlled breathing and other exercises, can be taught to reduce the anxiety.

"Test or school anxiety is a very real issue," he says. "But by using a series of breathing exercises or other techniques you can calm yourself down. It's just a matter of learning to listen to yourself."

Krech says the main motivation for the research comes from his two-year old son. He hopes to create something that can be in place by the time his son reaches kindergarten or elementary school.

Krech, holds both a bachelor's and master's degree from ASU, and has worked for more than 12 years as a social worker, community builder and grant writer. With Counseling & Consultation he provides individual counseling sessions and facilitates anger management and alcohol recovery groups. He also supervises master's level students and develops statistical measurement tools for the office.

As a Native American, he says he has spent much of his time helping people within Arizona's Indian communities and believes this current effort will provide a new way to help.

"I think you can give students a culturally relevant method to maintain their interest," he says.
