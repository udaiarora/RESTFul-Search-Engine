May 5, 2003


DEAN BERNSTEIN'S NSF APPOINTMENT AFFIRMS GRADUATE EDUCATION AT ASU

The appointment of Graduate Dean Bianca L. Bernstein to a director's position with the National Science Foundation (NSF) is viewed as a significant inroad for ASU and an affirmation of the university's commitment to graduate education. Dean Bernstein will serve for the next two years as director of the NSF's Graduate Division. Maria Allison has been appointed Interim Dean of the ASU Graduate College.

Having a representative from ASU in one of the key positions of NSF is a major coup for the university, Dean Bernstein said: "I will have the opportunity to influence the national agenda on graduate education, which will surely help ASU, as well as all other universities. Expanding my contacts in the national and international networks of decision makers, academic and agency leaders will be advantageous for ASU during my tenure at the NSF and upon my return to the university. I look forward to participating in a very exciting phase of re-conceptualizing graduate education, working among a group of extremely talented and accomplished national leaders and learning much more about the world of competitive funding."

Milton Glick, Executive Vice President & Provost, said of Dean Bernstein's appointment to the NSF, "This is very good for ASU, just as the Woodrow Wilson National Fellowship Foundation Responsive Ph.D. initiative was two years ago. This positions us very well in Washington. It's a very exciting time for Dean Bernstein and for ASU."

Graduate programs have greatly expanded during Bernstein's eight years as dean. ASU has added 12 new degree programs, seven certificate programs and seven concentrations in that time. ASU has created 17 new interdisciplinary programs and is tied for the national lead with three Integrative Graduate Education Research and Traineeship (IGERT) programs. The Graduate College has continued to make program quality a major priority, evidenced by rewarding outstanding doctoral mentors, coordinating TA orientation, funding the Research Conference Award Program and the Millennium Interdisciplinary Dissertation Fellowship awards.

"I am very proud of what we have accomplished in these eight years and remain appreciative of the opportunity to serve the university in this capacity," Dean Bernstein said. "We have raised the visibility of graduate education in a national context, and I look forward to bringing ASU's best practices to Washington, DC"

Several themes emerge in a review of Dean Bernstein's leadership in the Graduate College. ASU raised its level of support for graduate students through the addition of health insurance and partial resident tuition remissions. The university introduced and institutionalized a broad range of professional development programs for graduate students — Preparing Future Faculty (PFF), Preparing Future Professionals and Strategies For Success. In recent years, the Graduate College has helped ASU attract a broad range of national awards and initiatives, including three IGERT awards from the NSF, and participation in the Carnegie Initiative on the Doctorate and the Responsive Ph.D. initiative. ASU has expanded the number of fellowship programs available to graduate students, and increased graduate minority enrollment and community outreach programs.

Dean Bernstein will direct a budget of $140 million and oversee numerous graduate award programs at the NSF. She said her appointment "is the result of all the things we have done in the Graduate College over the years and having represented ASU at various national venues. We are viewed as champions for IGERTS, PFF, and other vital programs."

The National Science Foundation now encourages universities receiving IGERT awards to follow ASU's practice of placing those interdisciplinary programs under the umbrella of their graduate colleges. Several universities have emulated the Outstanding Doctoral Mentor Award and Preparing Future Professionals programs practiced at ASU.

In addition to the NSF appointment, Dean Bernstein is also the incoming president for the Western Alliance of Graduate Schools (WAGS), for 2003–2004, and she has previously served on the executive boards for the Council of Graduate Schools, the Council for Research Policy and Graduate Education, a division of the National Association of State Universities and Land-Grant Colleges, and other organizations. As president of WAGS, Bernstein will chair the executive board, represent association members in matters concerning graduate education, and serve as program chair for the 2004 national meeting in Arizona.

The Western Association of Graduate Schools works with several agencies in disseminating information, improving standards of graduate education, encouraging research, and assisting institutions in beginning graduate programs. Next year's WAGS conference will be the second held in Arizona. The first was hosted in Tempe under Bernstein's predecessor, former graduate dean Brian Foster. ASU will host the 46th Annual General Meeting of WAGS March 5–7, 2004. The conference will feature workshops, topical discussions and presentations, among other activities.
