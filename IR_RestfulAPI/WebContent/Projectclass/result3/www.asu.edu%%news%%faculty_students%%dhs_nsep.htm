August

ASU students receive top security-related awards

Two ASU students have won highly competitive scholarships through the U.S. Department of Homeland Security (DHS), and a third has received a National Security Education Program (NSEP) grant to study overseas. All three will be involved in studies to increase national and international security.

Brent Satterfield, a bioengineering junior, and Tristan Weir, a computer science senior, are among fewer than 100 students nationwide selected for the new DHS scholarships. The awards are $9,000 stipends plus full tuition and fees, and both students will complete research internships at a DHS-designated facility next summer.

Kyle Burns, a political science and history junior who interned this summer in Washington, D.C., will attend Charles University in the Czech Republic in the spring 2004 semester to study ethnic conflict and to become fluent in the Czech language.
Satterfield and Weir are in the first class of DHS scholars in a program intended to ensure a diverse and highly talented base of scientists and technology experts who can help the DHS achieve its objectives. These include preventing terrorist attacks and reducing America's vulnerability to terrorism, protecting the rights of citizens and enhancing public services such as natural disaster assistance.

Students must be majoring in the sciences, engineering, mathematics or computer science and must be willing to consider competitive employment offers from DHS or its affiliates following graduation.

Burns, recipient of the NSEP grant, plans to research the Czech government's policies toward ethnic minorities, visiting local communities and studying newspaper accounts and other documents. He hopes to earn a master's degree in international relations and a law degree, eventually entering a career with the U.S. State Department.

The NSEP awards provide funding for study outside western Europe, in countries of strategic interest to the United States. Awardees are expected to learn a critical foreign language and to work later for a government agency involved in national security affairs or higher education.
