GENERAL OVERVIEW


Executive Summary

This document provides you with the basics of transitioning (moving) your computing work from one computing system to another. You would normally do a transition because better resources for doing your work are available on another system or because the system you're currently using is being phased out.

Only two systems are being phased out at ASU: the VM/CMS system and the Academic VAX/VMS system. The other systems (UNIX, IBM MVS/TSO, and the PC and Mac sites) will remain.

Limitations of UNIX Systems

At the time of writing of this document, an effective means on the UNIX systems to handle large amounts of data on tape is not available. If you use tape, you should move to or remain on the IBM MVS system (or another system) which has tape facilities.

If the amount of data on tape is small, you may want to copy the files to disk and then eliminate tape usage. If the volume of data you need to move (including tape and disk files) is less than 200 megabytes you should be able to successfully transition to another computing platform without complications.

What is a "Transition"

The term "Transition" is defined as moving your computing activities from one computing system to another, such as to a UNIX server (Research , Stats, General, AI), to the IBM MVS mainframe, or to your own personal workstation.

A transition is required for only two systems. ASU's VM/CMS system and Academic VAX/VMS system are being phased out. All computing activities on these systems must be moved elsewhere. At this point in time, the transition does not include moving e-mail use from VM/CMS because a suitable alternative has not yet been located, although an IMAP based e-mail package is available for use (see section on E-mail Choices).

What do I need to transition?

As you create your personal transition plan, you should consider in what order you might want to transition programs and data. For example, work in progress that must be completed by a certain date is probably not a good choice. Whereas a new project or research, which depend very little on existing programs, would most likely be a good choice.

Caution! - Protect your data!

As you move from one computing system to another, you should be careful to keep backup copies of files until you have verified the integrity of the data on the new system(s).

When should you start?

Once you've identified a need to transition, there is no time like the present to start. The most frustrating part of a transition is the learning curve associated with a new environment. By waiting until the last moment to do a transition, your ability to meet deadlines, to be productive, will be hampered by the time required to learn the new environment. Beginning early allows you to become familiar with the new environment without unnecessary pressures and to do so with minimum interruption to your other ongoing activities.
