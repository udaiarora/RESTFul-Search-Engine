"A Prescription for Murder "
by Carl Labbe RPh


 "Where were you on the night of October second?" is not the usual question asked by the person standing at your pharmacy counter. In the new mystery, Rx For Murder, featuring pharmacists as protagonists and suspects, more serious questions are posed and answered. How would you react if one of your patients was murdered and you were a suspect?

That is the scenario in this novel, by Renee B. Horowitz, filled with true to life anecdotes from actual pharmacy practice. From the older lady who can't believe that the pharmacist is a woman, to the young girl who loudly asks where the condoms are kept, to the clueless young lady without a refill number for her oral contraceptive yet wants it to be ready "like, now," and to the infrequently encountered physician who appreciates a pharmacist's call questioning dosage, the pharmacy in this book is an accurate portrayal of current retail practice.

Mrs. Horowitz, the author, is a professor of Industrial Technology at Arizona State University. Knowing that, you are probably wondering how she is able to write a book which incorporates so many true-to-life pharmacy practice situations. The truth is she's been surrounded by pharmacists almost all her life. She's been married to a practicing pharmacist, Arthur, for many years. Renee's father, Hyman Braunstein was a pharmacist practicing in New York City. Husband Arthur's dad, Sam was also a pharmacist. This lady knows what pharmacy practice is like. When her Dad and Arthur became partners in a retail pharmacy, Renee often helped out. With a pharmacist father and pharmacist husband, she was constantly provided with play by play highlights of the day. She likes to joke that Arthur always says that she doesn't listen to his nightly reports of his pharmacy activities, but this book proves that she was indeed paying close attention. Her main character could easily be a real pharmacist working in a real pharmacy.

Ruth Kantor Morris, a widow, is Pharmacy Manager for the local Food Go Pharmacy in Scottsdale. She's like a lot of us, over-worked, under-staffed, and working a weird schedule. She runs a busy pharmacy with a staff pharmacist and a 20 year old technician named Joey. It's reminiscent of many retail operations where the pharmacist often goes head to head and toe to toe with the store manager. Too often our patients (customers?) go running to the store manager if they can't get their way with the pharmacist. Closing the pharmacy at 9:00 PM in a 24-hour grocery store also can be difficult as you are more accessible to that last minute customer. Endless questions, constant interruptions, ceaseless phone calls, and demanding clientele; most of us have been there. Ruth, or Ruthie, handles it all with an experienced attitude and a good facade of professional calm. As readers, we get to hear her inner commentary, sometimes in sympathetic humor, sometimes just in sympathy.

Ruthie is "old enough to appreciate a man in his sixties," in her own words, and sometimes has daydreams about an attractive patient by the name of Harry Stokes. Harry has become a topic for employee break room gossip since he has just married a much younger woman. The word is that Harry has a lot of money and his children from his first marriage are unhappy with this recent marriage. They had perceived no roadblocks to a healthy inheritance for all.

Before you know it, Harry is dead, possibly due to an adverse drug interaction, possibly a suicide. Harry's prescription history may hold the answer. Ruth knows that Harry has just refilled his Rogaine prescription so she truly doubts that suicide is the case. The police have plenty of suspects, the new wife, Harry's kids, and even the pharmacy staff. Ruth doesn't like being a suspect, she becomes defensive and wary when questioned by the police. Her attempts to second guess the reasons for the detective's questions often serve only to increase her appearance of guilt. Although adept at handling questions from patients and prescribers, she falters when questioned about the deceased and their relationship.

The suspect list grows to include the newly arrived Michael, an old college sweetheart of Ruthie. This development thickens the plot and adds to Ruth's inner turmoil. We're along for the ride as suspicions darken the relationships between family members and friends, and yes, even college sweethearts. Things grow more desperate when another murder is committed. This time the victim is one of the players in the tangled web of suspects and armchair detectives. In the grand tradition of good mysteries, we are inclined to select and discard suspects as the plot unravels and new evidence is revealed. The murderer, of course, is eventually exposed and you can then judge your own detective skills.

With over 175,000 pharmacists in the U.S., many of them in a similar situation or with some exposure to a similar environment, this book has a solid niche audience. More importantly, it is a good book; the characters are believable, the motives realistic, and the plot moves along at an agreeable pace. This book is what is called a "cozy" by publishers, meaning that it avoids the violence, sex and foul language found in much contemporary fiction. Our patients will be seeing this book on the store book racks where many of us work. Perhaps some will come to have an appreciation of the importance of our expertise and knowledge. Anyone who reads this book will certainly see that we do more than "throw a few pills in a bottle," a positive aspect for pharmacists. All readers will meet a friendly, knowledgeable pharmacist and enjoy a satisfying murder mystery.

Carl Labbe R. Ph.
10-96
Appeared in the December 1996 issue of Pharmacy Times.
