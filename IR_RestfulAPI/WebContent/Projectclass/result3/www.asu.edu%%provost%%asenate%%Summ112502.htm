3.B  University President?s Report (Michael Crow).

 

Thanks, George.  Milt will be talking on matters specifically relating the 2003 budget adjustments.  I will talk a little about the 2004 fiscal year, as being a transformational year in rethinking the University?s budget formulation process.  It will indeed be that, in every possible way, as we begin the process, which will take some time of reshaping, to some extent, our own fiscal destiny.  In the 2004 year, we already know that we will see dramatic increase in undergraduate resident tuition, moderate increases in out-of-state undergraduate tuition, increases in fee structures for professional school students, and a range of other adjustments, all of which are linked to our process of trying to move away from significant dependence on our primary investor.  Our primary investor is the state of Arizona, as Milt will detail for you that we have just completed the plans, working with the deans and others, to return $18 million to the primary investor in fiscal year 2003, with possibility for fiscal year cuts in 2004, ranging anywhere from $15 million additional dollars to $51 million additional dollars.

 

Relative to fiscal 2004, two things are simultaneously happening: controlling the fiscal crisis and complying with the Changing Directions initiative.  With regard to changing directions, we are enhancing our cooperation with our sister institutions, while remaining independent on setting the level of our tuition and fees differentially, and in geographically positioning our programs.  This represents the most dramatic move, by the Regents, away from tightly administrating the universities in the past 40 years.  Milt confirms this, and he has been attending Regents meetings for the last eleven years.  I believe that under this new less controlled scenario, success or failure can be ours.  We also plan to promote a new message in 2004.  To the legislature we will say, fund all new students--fund them, or we won?t let them in!   This is the direction that we are moving in.  I am meeting with our new political leaders in the legislature currently, and it is worth noting that I believe we have a friend and a potential ally in the Governor-elect and after spending two hours with her, I found out that we are the single largest contributor to the state?s financial shortfall.   Having said that, I  remain very optimistic that we have the capacity to put together the kinds of plans and resources to continue to advance the University.  Toward that end, we have been engaged on a very large range of fronts, from tuition to other financial models that are quite elaborate, to help us think about how to move forward in 2004 and beyond. 

 

Senator Dan Landers:  If we tell the legislators that if they don?t fund enrollment growth, we will not admit students?isn?t that likely to anger the legislators and for us to get on their bad side?and may in the long run not do us any good?

 

President Crow: We are in a situation now, given the forces that are on us, where it is impossible for us to tread a path that does not anger someone.  The someone might be the middle class or upper middle class family that will realize that 30 to 40 percent of the tuition that we will ask of them in a tuition increase, we will turn around and give to another family immediately.  They might be angry about that.  It might be the lower income family, which believes that the increased tuition in the University that we will be asking for will scare away a lot of able minded students from the University.  I can go through each category in the legislature of who might be irritated by any individual thing that we will do.  However, we are to the point that we cannot continue to ask the faculty to take on more students with no additional resources.  In fact, we have passed that threshold.  We believe that if we were to receive 22 to 1 support, if we were to receive the tuition increase, and if then, we were to receive a flat budget as opposed to a budget reduction, that we could make things work around here relatively well given that scenario.  We have not been receiving 22 to 1 dollars for student increases on the East or West campuses.  There is now 9,000 students on those two campuses.  They are growing at a very rapid rate.  We are talking about the whole university when we talk about this.  We have not definitively decided how we will communicate this, or when we will communicate it but I am saying to you as faculty and staff that the University has reached its limit of the amount of additional energy it can extract from its faculty and staff, and still deliver the quality product that we want to try to deliver.  In terms of irritating people, it will not be possible to move forward?the tuition increase being considered by the Regents right now as President Leingang knows from the Regent?s meeting, is quite large, $1,000 per year, which for many families is high. 

 

3.C  University Provost?s Report (Milton Glick).           

 

Let me begin by talking about the budget reduction exercise we just completed, and then move on to changing directions and the tuition discussion.  First of all, we now have four simultaneous processes, either in play or will be put into play in the next few weeks.  1) How do we deal with the 2003 budget cuts--that is the budget reductions from our already reduced budget that hit last year, that we have already taken in the last two weeks.  2)  Developing a new budget process, which fits Michael?s enterprise system and allows more incentives and opportunities for units to make their own way.  3) Figuring out what the strategic budget injection will be for 2004 and how to implement that.  This year because we took it in mid-year, we know we were not as strategic as we must be next year.  Next year we will be figuring out a strategic budget scenario and  4) Planning the components of the entire University, because for the first time this year we have adopted an all University budget fund.  So, those are the four things going on.  The one that I am going to talk about mainly though is the mid-year general fund budget cut of 5%, which for ASU, the three campuses, reduces our amount by $16.5 million?in making this reduction we used the following strategies and principles: 1) Protect undergraduate instruction to the best of our ability and progress toward graduation  2) Disproportionately hit administration service and support programs.  Protect the colleges and then everybody else.  That is sort of a restatement of the first principle.  3) Protect priority research programs, especially those that have the ability to leverage external funds, because the long-term solution of this University?s problems means that we have to leverage all of our state dollars better.  4) Eliminate state funded subsidies over the next few years, for all units not central to the core mission of the University; we intend to move them to self-sufficiency over three years. 

 

President Crow: Can we mention a few of those?

 

Provost Glick:  Yes, for example, over the next two years we will completely eliminate the subsidy support for the Sundome.  We hope that the Sundome continues to function; we have no desire to close the Sundome, nonetheless, it is presently taking $5-600,000 in subsidy.  In good times it is an appropriate thing for the University to subsidize, but it is hard to justify that subsidy when we have classes we can?t offer and departments that may have to be completely revised in there mission and scope.  That is one example.  Another example is we have been subsidizing the Arizona Center for the Book.  The Arizona Center for the Book is a very valuable organization.  It is appropriate that we subsidize it, to the extent that we can afford to, but in times when we are questioning academic program support, we are not in a position to subsidize it.  There will be others that will be identified later.  Moreover, we intend over several years to reduce subsidies to organized research centers that are not leveraging their money at the level we expect of organized research centers.  I should tell you that this 5% reduction will be met by a combination of central funds (because we held back money from last year?s tuition increase, knowing this was likely to come)--and disproportionate cuts to non-academic units.  We were able to keep the cuts to colleges under 2%.  When you are actually making a 2% cut, it is very difficult, but we think given a 5% overall reduction, we know that we are not yet doing draconian things to those units.  We also know that over the last decade our priority has been to prioritize academic units.  Therefore, in years when there was discretionary money more of that went to the colleges than went to the support groups.  This is the second year in a row we are cutting.  We are cutting heavier on the administrative units again and that catches up to all of us, in that there will be floors that will not be as clean as you like them; there will be services that you used to get regularly that you will get less of them; and remember that this is because we protected the colleges above everything else.  We probably have roughly 120 to 150 positions that should have been filled but will not be.  Now that doesn?t mean that we have 150 fewer people, because some of these were growth positions from the increase in students and in tuition.  We have some student to advisor ratios that are 500 to 1 and we have to find a way to address that. 

 

I know that many of you understand the problem of our bandwidth from off campus to on campus.  The fact is that we have run out of bandwidth and now we have to upgrade our routers to get more bandwidth in and out.  I think, with the help of Bill Lewis, we are going to be able to find the money to greatly increase the bandwidth from off campus to on campus, probably by a factor of four.  However, there will be a cascade effect because as soon as we increase the bandwidth at the borders between off campus and on campus, it simply pushes the problem onto the internal band width problem.  We have not convinced ourselves we know how to identify the funds to do that.  Therefore, there will be a short term improvement when we free up the bandwidth on and off campus, but that will quickly just create a secondary effect and that affects especially those colleges that have put a great emphasis on integrating information technology into their curriculum.  Painting that should have been done two years ago will not be done.  However, we will fix roofs.  By the way, all these reductions do not come directly from the fact that we did not get any building renewal money for the last two years.  We have roughly six thousand more students on our three campuses.  We have about the same number of faculty that we had before those six thousand students.  Having said that, thanks to the hard work of faculty and staff, we are continuing to do a good job of undergraduate education and research, but not at the level that we are going to have to have it if we are to progress in the next five years. 

 

We had a five-year plan to retrofit classrooms, with every classroom having appropriate mediation?that is stretched out to ten years now.  The President had funds set aside for strategic investment this year, most of which we have taken off the table at this point.  We have done things to protect core academic units; we believe that the deans are handling these in appropriate fashions.  We have had to provide special support to East and West because with huge growth in students at both East and West, they received zero enrollment growth dollars.  We received about 85% of the enrollment dollars that we should have gotten on the main campus, but that is about where we have always been.  We have these other two campuses rapidly growing and we need to support them.  I can stop for questions here or move on to my next item.

Provost Glick: Thank you and that leads to my next issue.  The President said that in his brief time here that this was one of the greatest changes in direction, by a Board of Regents that he has ever seen.  As one who is a perpetual Board watcher, 40 years of it, I agree with that.  Boards deal with lots of things, which in the end do not have a great change associated with them.  In this case, however, I think the Board has really made a major directional course change that will have large implications, most of which I believe will be positive for the State and for ASU.  If we go back six months, last March and April, there was a vote within the Board of Regents to raise tuition 12% at that time.  That vote failed by a vote of six to five.  The dissonance between those in favor of 12% and those in favor of  4% was so intense over this, that the Board under the leadership of its then president-elect, now President, Jack Jewett, said we have to find a better way to do this.  They asked what are the two issues driving us?  The first issue was how do we get enough money to run the universities in a quality way?  The second issue, which for most of them actually was a higher priority, had to do with affordability.  We have very low tuition in Arizona?s universities, 49 out of 50 states.  Nevertheless, every national report card gives us poor scores on affordability.  Why is that?it is because there is no state financial aid program.  The bottom line is low tuition helps the middle class and upper middle class.  It does not make it affordable for lower income people because just having low tuition does not solve their problem.  They need low tuition plus.  So, by having low tuition but relatively low financial aid, with all the state financial aid coming out of our budgets, we were unaffordable (even though cheap!)  A paradox of sorts.  I think the Regents over the summer came to understand that paradox and said what we are going to do is deal with that and make a move on tuition, having as its top priority how to make the universities more affordable (rather paradoxical in that we are going to raise tuition so the universities are more affordable).  They asked us to do some modeling of what might you do with a combination of financial aid and tuition at different levels.  Our Institutional Research people worked with Kathy Church, to put together a database of all 21,000 of our students that are on financial aid, including what kind of financial aid, federal, state, or institutional.  We included their level of family income, their ethnicity, and their grade point, etc.  We tried to create a model that allowed us to say we used a clear set of rules and that can illustrate what will happen to each individual student.  The idea was, how do we make it more affordable and provide more funding for the University.  The Board asked us to construct three models, one where we increased resident undergraduate tuition because that is the target audience, by $500, $750, and $1,000.  Then we increased non-resident tuition by $1,000.  How do we provide enough additional income beyond that to help the universities meet their responsibilities?  Here are our baseline financial aid goals.  The goals were to do two things: 1) For all disadvantaged students, financially?there are different ways to say this?you could say all Pell eligible students, you could say all students whose family income is under $40,000.  Let?s just use the term ?the most needy students.?  Our goal was to first hold harmless financially disadvantaged students, so whenever we raised tuition, they did not pay any of that tuition.  Our second goal was to reduce what is called in the federal guidelines, unmet needs.  That money that they have to meet by loans or work reduced by ten percent.  To do that, if we go to the $500 scenario, we have to set aside 12.5% of total tuition revenue to generate net revenue of $17 million.  If instead we go to $1,000 we have to set aside 14% to get $27 million, after you have achieved those financial aid quotas.  Another constraint was that we would not move our tuition above the top of the lower third of the nation.  We are now 49th in the nation and we cannot go above 34th.  It turns out that scenario 3--$1,000 more moves us to 34th position.  So, that is the highest we can go.  If we reduce the unmet need by 20%, generating $22 million, with $1,000 increase, but we have to increase our set aside to 19%.  For non-resident students, we will hold them half harmless, so they only absorb half the increase and we absorb half of it.  The bottom line is that the policy goal for the Regents is that what ever we do, we are not to go above the top of the bottom third.  Secondly, for all whom we will call the greatest need students, we would hold them harmless from the tuition increase, and reduce their unmet need by 20%.  It turns out that to do this you have to take 40% of new tuition and put it into financial aid.  For every dollar of new tuition, 60% goes to support academic programs and 40% goes to help reduce that unmet need.  At the end we have actually made the University more affordable.  At the $1,000 level, we still are generating less than we have lost in the last two years.  In the last two years, counting the cut we are making right now, we have lost about $35 million, and at the $1,000 level increase we can generate about $27 million.  Having said that, it would go a long way toward helping us out.  I am convinced that if we do our job well, we can do this in a way that does not harm students.  My greatest concern and the President?s greatest concern, and we have had it expressed to us by students, especially minority students, is that the newspaper just runs a headline, Tuition Goes Up by 44% or Tuition Goes up by $1,000 and families out there say, well, I guess that means my kids are not going to college!  We cannot afford to let that happen.  We have to find a way that we can get through that we have ways to help the most needy students; toward that end we are having meetings with community groups, we are going to be on Spanish-language radio and we are going to create a swat team that goes out, instead of waiting for the student to come to us to get financial aid.  We will go to that student, at their school, have a computer with us and be able to tell them that they are eligible for financial aid.  It will be an enormous undertaking but one that we must do well, because if we do not do it well this will have very negative results for the state of Arizona.  If we do it well, I believe we can raise tuition and serve the state even better, particularly those needy students.  I will be happy to take your questions or comments at this point.

 

Let me just comment to Mike Leingang, that the student leadership is very thoughtful in understanding the competing forces and are willing to be in a dialogue.  We have not asked them to come out and take an oath, but they are actively in a dialogue, as are our student regents and I think that is a real tribute to the leadership of our student organizations that they are willing to have a dialogue and look at the bigger picture, not just how much more tuition will be.

3.J  (3.H)   Report of the Faculty Athletic Representative (Jerry Kingston).

      

I will be brief in my comments today, but I will be happy to answer questions now or to follow-up later if you have them.  There is a one-page hand out that was distributed and you may wish to follow along.  There are three major issues that I will cover today:  (1)  Changes in initial and continuing academic eligibility requirements recently enacted by the NCAA; (2) changes in NCAA rules related to the length of playing and practice seasons that will be intended to provide additional time for student-athletes to meet the higher academic requirements; and (3) the annual academic report of the Faculty Athletics Representative related to the academic preparation, performance and graduation (and persistence) rates for each of our sports teams. 

 

The NCAA has embarked  on a significant reform agenda that is being led by the Division I Board of Directors of the NCAA.  This group has now taken complete control of the leadership of the NCAA .  They approve all the legislation that is passed, and they chart the course for much of that legislation.  This last October, a change was implemented in initial eligibility requirements for students entering in the fall of 2003.  The high school core GPA will now be calculated on the basis of 14, rather than 13 core courses. You may have read in the paper about the elimination of the SAT or ACT test as a part of the initial eligibility requirement.   That is only partially correct.  It is that lower test scores can now be compensated for by much higher core GPA requirements, but in order to get all the way back to virtual elimination of the test score requirement would take a core GPA of about 3.50 (computed on the basis of 14 core courses).  It is debatable whether this change--allowing higher core GPA values to compensate for lower SAT or ACT scores--actually constitutes a reduction in initial eligibility requirements.  The change was introduced primarily to create additional access for student athletes who are now being screened out by ten-point test score differences. For example, under the present rule, a student-athlete who has earned an SAT score of 820 can be eligible for competition as a freshman, but a student-athlete who has an SAT score of 810 cannot be eligible for competition as a freshman. This change also was in response to a number of lawsuits that have been filed against the NCAA..  So far the NCAA has prevailed on retaining the initial eligibility requirements, but their legal counsel has indicated that they may not prevail in future lawsuits because of the overwhelmingly negative impact of the test score requirement on minority student-athletes.

 

The major changes in eligibility standards, however, are coming in the continuing eligibility requirements. Higher grade point average rules are going to go in effect for students who enter next fall.  These GPA requirements will affect our students less than those in many other states because our Board of Regents has had a very active GPA policy which we have been administering on a semester by  semester basis for the last ten years.  I think the greatest impact for us is going to be that the NCAA is significantly increasing the progress  towards degree rules.  Right now after two years of full-time collegiate enrollment, student athletes must have completed twenty-five percent of their degree credits:  with a 120-semester hour program, student-athletes must have completed 30 hours of course work towards their degree and have 90 hours remaining to complete the degree.  The new eligibility rules will require that 40% of a 120 semester hour degree program--48 hours--must have been completed prior to the beginning of the third year of collegiate enrollment. This  is a significant increase, and I think it will have an impact on our students.  At the end of three years, that number goes to 60% (of degree completion) ; at the end of four years the number goes to 80% (of degree completion).  In addition, the Board of Directors has a implemented a new term-by-term requirement.   We have had a term-by-term GPA requirements for a number of years, but the NCAA now will require  that a student must pass at least six hours in one semester, in order to be eligible for the next regular semester.  Six hours may not seem like a lot but there was strong debate whether that  number should be six or nine, and the Division I Board of Directors may not yet be done with that particular requirement.  For the fall of 2003, new legislation may yet be enacted that would require even continuing student-athletes (not just those initially enrolling in college) to meet this requirement as well.

 

The  NCAA is also developing what they are now calling a new academic performance index or measure.  No one knows exactly what  is going into this formula but it is not going to be based on six-year graduation rates that the NCAA annually publishes.  Instead, it will most likely capture the academic experience of team members in the three most recent years.  Every team is going to  be required  to have a minimum value  on that academic performance index (the value is yet to be determined) as a requirement:  (1)   for that team to participate in NCAA post competition play ; (2)  for the athletics department to  receive revenue sharing distributions from the Men's Basketball Tournament; and (3) for the number of athletics scholarships that team would be allowed to give the following year. 

 

The second major initiative of the NCAA is one that focuses on giving additional time back to student-athletes to meet the higher academic standards.  This will involved reducing the length of playing and practice seasons, and reducing the number of contests within the playing season.  I am serving on the NCAA committee that has been given the responsibility to develop proposals for shortening the length of playing and practice seasons and reducing the number of contests within those shortened seasons.  This will be extraordinarily difficult because we have a number of exempted contests --the pre-season NIT is going on right now and that is a good example of what I am talking about -- that do not count against the number of contests permitted under NCAA rules.  A number of these events have been exempted by the NCAA for a number of years, and several of the groups that sponsor them have come to believe that they have a legal property right in those exemptions.   Therefore, this is going to be a difficult issue for us to deal with over the next several years but it is all a part of  the package. But, if we shorten the length of the playing and practice seasons, and do not reduce the total number of contests, schedule "compression" will occur, and student-athletes will be required to compete in the same number of events in a shorter time period.  That would not be consistent with giving student-athletes additional time to meet the higher academic requirements.

 

Also related to the academic reform agenda, I would note that the NCAA has selected a new president, Miles Brandt, who is  the president in Indiana University and who was formerly President at the University of Oregon.  This will be the first time that the NCAA has been led by a college or university president, especially a Division I president who has experience in both the PAC10 and BIG10 conferences.  I believe that his agenda is going to be to continue to work with the Board of Directors to further enhance the academic reform movement. 

 

The changes in the academic standards that have been enacted will impact our athletics program at Arizona State University.  I believe that the most significant impact will be on number of two-year college transfer student-athletes who will l be coming in to our athletics program.  The requirement that these students must have completed 40% of their ASU degree programs after two years of community college work will constitute a significant barrier for at least some transfer student-athletes.  This may result in a shift in the pattern of athletics recruiting, away from two-year college student-athletes and towards those who would enter ASU as freshman.  A second impact is that, once enrolled at ASU, student-athletes may tend to select less challenging degree programs in order to retain their athletics eligibility.  Some have expressed their concern about that. In my view, all of our degree programs are accredited by the faculty and if the faculty or other groups have concerns about student athletes moving into one degree program or another, they should direct their concerns towards the requirements or content of those degree programs, rather than restricting the choices of student-athletes in terms of which degrees they choose to pursue.  A third implication of the more stringent academic eligibility rules is one that is very close to the work that I perform, and that is trying to maintain the very highest degree of academic integrity in our programs.  I can tell you that when standards go up, and student-athlete preparation does not go up accordingly, and when you add into that mix loss of scholarships, revenue sharing and access to post season championships, the pressure for students to remain eligible is going to be substantial.  This could lead to requests for grades, or grade changes, designed to enable a student-athlete to become or remain eligible.  This would not be appropriate, of course, because athletics eligibility should have no role in determining course grades. I think it will mean that we will need to be even more vigilant about ensuring the academic integrity of our athletics programs as we move forward with the new eligibility standards and the new academic performance measures.

 

The third portion of my report today focuses on the academic preparation, performance and outcomes of the student-athletes on each of our teams.  Please refer to the one-page handout that you have at your seat.  [Editors note:  As this report involved sports team statistics and academic performance data for student athletes, the discussion of this table is not included here.  Please contact Dr. Kingston,  as a courtesy, to obtain a copy of the handout and to discuss any questions you may have from the Senate  meeting (965-7457)].
