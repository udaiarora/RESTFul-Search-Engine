January 17, 2003

Presidents share vision of higher education

The presidents of Arizona's three universities and the Arizona Board of Regents (ABOR) are sharing their vision of how the Regents' Changing Directions initiative will enhance the future of higher education in Arizona.

On Jan. 6 the three presidents, and ABOR President Jack Jewett, participated on a panel discussion hosted by the University of Arizona Foundation at the Phoenix Country Club. The audience included club members, university donors, and other supporters of higher education.

Crow said, "Changing Directions is an immense opportunity for the university system to mature to a level of greatness that is not only possible, but doable at this time."
He said the process is enabling the universities to grow up and take responsibility for their own destinies, moving from the notion of squabbling siblings fighting over limited resources to becoming adults, each with its own identity.

"We must distance ourselves from an agency model and move toward an enterprise model, where we are energetic, quick, responsive and adaptive," Crow said.

The most pressing areas of focus are tuition, admission issues and creating individual identities for each institution.

University of Arizona President Pete Likens said, "The Arizona university system is in jeopardy without significant change. You often can improve utilization of resources by relaxing the rules that bind us to be more alike than different."

Likens said the presidents recognize they are a team and are committed to working together to ensure each individual university is most successful, which, in turn, results in the entire system becoming more successful.

Together, the presidents are determined to improve financial access to a university education in Arizona. Currently, there is no substantial financial aid policy in Arizona. The regents have indicated a significant tuition increase is needed to dramatically ramp up financial aid for qualified students.

On Jan. 10, the Arizona Board of Regents and the three university presidents met, via closed-circuit television, with State Board of Education leaders for the first of several planned joint meetings. Educators vowed greater efforts to enhance collective collaboration and ingenuity as a way to strengthen education statewide.
State Board of Education Vice President Wade McLean said he believes the two-way communication between K-12 and higher education officials could offer valuable solutions to teachers in elementary and secondary education to better prepare school children for college-level courses.

The question and answer session was designed to communicate to State Board of Education officials the significant changes underway within the Arizona University System. The Regents' Changing Directions Initiative, which seeks to loosen operation constraints for universities, was at the forefront of the conversation.

ABOR President Jack Jewett told the state education leaders that its Changing Directions initiative was born out of a necessity to address the state legislature's reduced investment in education. He said as the board contemplates significant tuition increases in March, it will, for the first time, be addressing financial aid as part of the process. He asked State Board of Education members, including Superintendent of Public Instruction Tom Horne, to support the tuition increase.

ASU President Michael Crow detailed his vision for transforming ASU into "the greatest comprehensive metropolitan research university in the nation." He said the university would embrace the notion of size and would work proactively to make growth an asset rather than a deterrent. Through the Changing Directions Initiative ASU's multi-campus institution will increase its graduation rates because students will be matched to programs in which they can achieve success.

Crow told the crowd of about 50 that ASU will seek ways in which students in substantive subjects could become teacher certified while obtaining their degree in other areas. Horne praised Crow for addressing the current state teacher shortage and encouraged Arizona's other universities to pursue similar programs.
