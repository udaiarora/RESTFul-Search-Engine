The Changing Landscape of Graduate Education

Since 1999, the Division of Graduate Studies at Arizona State University has invited nationally recognized leaders in the field of graduate education to engage the campus community in lecture and discussion on changes and trends in graduate education. Watch for announcements of upcoming speaker events.

The following individuals have previously participated as invited speakers in "The Changing Landscape of Graduate Education" speaker series:

  • George Walker, vice president for research/dean of the gradute school, professor of physics, Indiana University. "The Evolution of Graduate Education." Dr. Walker is a Senior Scholar at the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching, which is in the second year of a five-year study of the Ph.D. known as the Carnegie Initiative on the Doctorate, and has actively participated in many of the national organizations related to graduate education and research administration. The focus of Dr. Walker's presentation was to address desirable changes in graduate education and successfully introducing them into the graduate experience. A Fellow of the American Physical Society, Dr. Walker has twice received the Graduate Teaching Award for "outstanding contributions to graduate education" — awarded by graduate students in the Indiana University Department of Physics.

  • Susan Fitzpatrick, vice president, James S. McDonnell Foundation. "The Allure of Interdisciplinary Research and Training: Old, New, Borrowed, Blue." Dr. Fitzpatrick has lectured and written on issues concerning the role of private philanthropy in the support of scientific research and on issues related to the public understanding of science. She has served on the faculty at Yale University, is currently an adjunct professor at Washington University, St. Louis, and directs all public outreach, educational and grant making efforts for the McDonnell Foundation.


  • Jody Nyquist, associate dean, Graduate School, University of Washington, Seattle. "The Ph.D.: A Tapestry of Change for the 21st Century." Jody Nyquist provided a major impetus for the Responsive Ph.D. initiative through her funded project, "Re-Envisioning the Ph.D." She chairs the sectors' group of the Responsive Ph.D. initiative, sponsored by the Woodrow Wilson National Fellowship Foundation.


  • Earl Lewis, dean, Rackham Graduate School, University of Michigan, Ann Arbor. "Disciplines, Multidisciplines and Interdisciplinaries: What's in a Name?" Dr. Lewis serves as national chair for The Responsive Ph.D. initiative and chair of the Council of Graduate Schools. ASU is one of 14 universities participating in the Responsive Ph.D. initiative. Dr. Lewis has taught in both the History Department and the Center for African and African American Studies (CAAS), where he served as Director from 1990–93.


  • Clifford Adelman, senior research analyst, U.S. Department of Education. "Goodbye, Pipeline: You Were Never of Much Use Anyway." Dr. Adelman writes frequently for the general and trade press, most recently on degree completion rates and academic paths of students, among other topics.


  • Wyn Jennings, program director, National Science Foundation (NSF), Division of Graduate Education/manager, Integrative Graduate Education Research and Traineeship (IGERT) program. "A Conversation on Graduate Education in the 21ST Century." On behalf of the NSF's Division of Graduate Education, Dr. Jennings manages the foundation's traineeship programs. This division created the Integrative Graduate Education and Research Traineeship (IGERT) program. Arizona State University has three IGERT programs.


  • Brian Coppola, associate professor, Department of Chemistry, University of Michigan, Ann Arbor. "Faculty 2.0: Embedding the Scholarship of Teaching and Learning in Future Faculty Development." Dr. Coppola is a faculty associate at the University of Michigan Center for Research on Learning and Teaching, and he was selected as part of the first group of Carnegie Scholars affiliated with The Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching's CASTL program (Carnegie Academy on the Scholarship of Teaching and Learning).


  • Maresi Nerad, director, Graduate Research, University of California, Berkeley. "Career Paths of Ph.D. Graduates—10 Years Later." Dr. Nerad's groundbreaking study of 6,000 Ph.D. graduates from 61 institutions—ASU included—who received their doctorates has made her a highly-sought speaker. She has authored books on doctoral education, comparative higher education, and women's higher education.


  • Christina Gonzalez, dean, Graduate Studies, University of California, Davis. "Graduate Education in the Interdisciplinary Setting: Innovations at UC—Davis." Dr. Gonzalez has served on the Council of Graduate Schools Board of Directors and on the Humanities and Arts Dissertation Awards Committee. Her examination of non-traditional learning structures at UC—Davis provided a fertile discussion of the institutional strategy of formulating faculty groups to enhance learning in the interdisciplinary setting.


  • Jules LaPidus, past president, Council of Graduate Schools. "National Trends and the Future of Graduate Education." Dr. LaPidus is a former faculty member who served on numerous committees involved with graduate education and research, among them the National Association of State Universities and Land-Grant Colleges, the American Council of Education, the Association of American Universities, Graduate Council on Education, African-American Institute, the GRE Board, National Academy of Sciences, and the American Association for the Advancement of Science.


  • Steve Crow, executive director, North Central Association of Colleges & Schools. "Quality assurance in education during the rapid emergence of postbaccalaureate certificates." Dr. Crow discussed the rapid emergence of postbaccalaureate certificates and the challenge thus presented to accrediting agencies and graduate colleges. He outlined trends and discussed possibilities for effective quality assurance in the new educational environment.


  • Leonard Peters, vice provost for research/dean, Graduate School, Virginia Tech University; and Gail McMillan, professor, University Libraries, Virginia Tech University. "The Next Journey in Graduate Education: Excursion or Expedition?" Dr. Peters and Dr. McMillan highlighted their school's requirements for electronic submission of theses and dissertations, and addressed key issues, such as security, patenting, and resistance.
