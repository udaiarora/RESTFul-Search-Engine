October 24, 2006

CRESMET spearheads math, science conference

When low-skill jobs were plentiful, sending some Arizona high school graduates into the work force possessing only minimal skills in math and science was tolerable. But with high-skill workers in greater demand than ever before, a strong academic background in these disciplines is rapidly becoming a necessity for all students who wish to compete for jobs in today's marketplace.

The U.S. Department of Labor projects that, by 2008, some 6 million job openings will exist for scientists, engineers, and technicians. But the number of students earning college degrees leading to careers in these key professions has declined over the past decade.

Since 1990, the number of U.S. bachelor's degrees in engineering dropped by 8 percent, and the number of mathematics degrees conferred in the United States declined nearly 20 percent from 1990-2002, according to a recent science and engineering indicators report released by the National Science Foundation.

?The situation in the nation at large is mirrored in Arizona,? says Marilyn Carlson, professor of mathematics and statistics and director of ASU's Center for Research on Education in Science, Mathematics, Engineering and Technology (CRESMET). ?The industries that Arizona has targeted to drive the state's economic growth ? bioscience, semiconductors, electronics and aerospace, advanced mining and manufacturing ? require that employees at all levels have a solid education in math and science.?

This is why Carlson is leading a team of researchers, practitioners, government and industry leaders in establishing Arizona's first-ever initiative to engage school counselors in a statewide discussion focused on what they can do to encourage more Arizona students to take and persist in challenging courses and pursue careers in science, engineering and technology. The 2006 Math and Science Conference for Arizona's Guidance Counselors took place Oct. 24 at ASU's West campus.

The conference, titled ?Competing in a ?Flat World' Economy: Getting and Keeping Arizona Students in the Math and Science They Need to Succeed,? attracted nearly 300 school counselors, teachers, principals and school district leaders and roughly 100 other guests from academia, industry and community organizations to share in the day-long event. Special guests included Arizona Gov. Janet Napolitano, who delivered the opening keynote address. William Harris, president and chief executive officer of Science Foundation Arizona, offered closing remarks.

CRESMET's research focuses on high school math and science teachers. Through two grants from the National Science Foundation, the center's researchers are working over a period of five years with teachers in six school districts. Their overarching goal is to refine a model that all Arizona schools can use to provide meaningful, sustained professional development in which science and math teachers deepen their knowledge and hone their pedagogy over their entire careers.

?The research and national reports have repeatedly identified teaching as the key to improved outcomes,? according to Patrick Thompson, CRESMET's research director, and an ASU professor of mathematics and statistics.

Among the presenters at the conference were representatives from Boeing, Intel, Raytheon, Salt River Project, Pinnacle West Capital Corp., Maricopa Workforce Development Center, ASU Polytechnic campus, the Biodesign Institute at ASU and Resolution Copper Co. Dennis Hoffman, director of the Seidman Institute and Center for Competitiveness and Productivity Research Center at the W. P. Carey School of Business, is leading an economic outlook panel.

The Virtual Counseling Center (VCC) at ASU is among the many resources that conference attendees will have the opportunity to learn more about and begin using at their schools to assist and fuel their students' interest in STEM fields (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math). John J. Horan, a professor of counseling psychology with ASU's Mary Lou Fulton College of Education, is constructing the VCC with a mission to design and evaluate programs and instruments for helping students and graduates develop life and career plans.
