March 17, 2005

Cronkite School names 4 finalists for founding dean position

Four finalists have been selected for the founding deanship of the Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication. They are:

• Mitchell Stephens, professor and chair, Department of Journalism, New York University. Stephens is the author of numerous books, including “A History of News.” He has written articles on media issues and modern thought for the Columbia Journalism Review, Los Angeles Times, New York Times, Washington Post and other publications. In 2001, Stephens traveled around the world, reporting on globalization for FEED and Marketplace and on travel for Lonely Planet. He has degrees from Haverford College and UCLA.

• Christopher Callahan, associate dean, Philip Merrill College of Journalism, University of Maryland. Callahan graduated from Boston University and earned a master’s in public administration from Harvard University. He is senior editor of American Journalism Review and has worked for the Associated Press. He is the author of “A Journalist’s Guide to the Internet” and writes regularly about journalism for a number of publications. He directed and redesigned the Annapolis Bureau of Capital New Service, the University of Maryland’s capstone program in journalism.

• Joel Kaplan, assistant dean for professional graduate studies in the S.I. Newhouse School of Public Communications. He also is a contributing editor for Chicago magazine and has worked as a reporter for the Chicago Tribune and the Tennessean. He was a Journalism Fellow at Yale Law School and a Nieman Fellow at Harvard University. Kaplan has been a reporting coach for several newspapers, and was an invited lecturer last year for the National Writers’ Workshop in Fort Lauderdale, Fla.

• Ruth Walden, associate dean for graduate studies, University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. Walden earned her doctoral degree in mass communication from the University of Wisconsin-Madison. Her books include “Insult Laws: An Insult to Press Freedom” and “Mass Communications Law in North Carolina.” She is on the editorial boards of several publications, including Communication Law and Policy and Journalism and Mass Communication Quarterly.

The Cronkite School is being restructured from a school to a college, but the name will remain the same, says Stephen K. Doig, interim director of the Cronkite School, who holds the Knight Chair.

The founding dean of the Cronkite School will have many challenges to face. In addition to moving the school to the new downtown Phoenix campus, the new dean must work to raise its level of diversity in students and faculty to address a “problem area” cited by the school’s accreditation team last fall.

The announcement of the new founding dean is expected to take place in late May or early June.
