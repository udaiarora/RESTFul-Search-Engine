May 2, 2003

ASU faculty continue Guggenheim success

ASU has won three of the 184 Guggenheim Fellowships awarded this year, a number that generally only the elite, private universities surpassed - Princeton, University of Chicago, Yale (each winning five) and Columbia and Harvard (each winning four).

In the last three years, eight ASU faculty members have won John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation Fellowships. The Guggenheim is a prestigious academic award that ranks as one of the highest honors in the humanities, social sciences and natural sciences.

The university's strong track record in winning the award is evidence of a strong senior research faculty, since the Guggenheim Fellowship is generally given to established scholars and researchers "who have achieved prominence in their fields."

"Our recent record in winning these important awards is a testament to our past track record in recruiting strong researchers to the faculty," said David Young, dean of ASU's College of Liberal Arts & Sciences. "As the university's research enterprise continues to move forward, I expect that we will see more and more of this kind of national recognition."

Winning 2003 Guggenheim Fellowships are History Professor Monica Green; English, German and Theater Professor Gitta Honegger; and Microbiology Professor Kenneth Mossman.

The Guggenheim Fellowship is awarded to scholars, artists and scientists and typically supports release time for work on a major project of importance in the field involved.

Green, a medieval historian who specializes in the history of medicine, won the fellowship to study "Medicine and Culture fellowship to study "Medicine and Culture in 12th Century Salerno." Green's project will focus on the School of Salerno, the European starting point of medical science, inspired by the area's past Arab culture and imported Arab learning.

Though Salerno was crucial for the development of Western medicine and learning, very little historical study has been devoted to it.

Honegger is a theater professor and a literary scholar and has interests in theater, performance, German and comparative literature, critical theory and women's studies. Her fellowship project is titled "A Biography of Helene Weigel."

Honegger's project will take her to Berlin to research and write the life story of Weigel, a prominent German actress and theater director whose history has been somewhat eclipsed by the fact that she was the wife of the famous playwright Bertolt Brecht.

Mossman is a health physics professor in ASU's microbiology department and also director of ASU's office of radiation safety. He is a specialist in the area of radiological health and safety, with interests as well in risk management.

Mossman's fellowship is to study "Risk Dimensions and Precaution." His proposed project is to study the substantial uncertainty that exists concerning the applicability and requirements of the precautionary principle to the management of technological risks.

His research has implications for a broad range of risk issues, including such diverse activities as biomedical research, use of cellular telephones and genetically modified foods.
