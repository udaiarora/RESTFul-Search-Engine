Buy a rug, help make music at ASU

Psst. Wanna buy a rug?

The Arizona State University Carillon Society has a deal for you.

David Adler, owner of David Adler Oriental Rugs in Scottsdale, and father of ASU music student Jacob Adler, has donated a Tabriz Persian rug, valued at $28,500, to the ASU Carillon Society to help bring back the ASU Symphonic Carillon.

The rug is now available for purchase. Prospective buyers may view the rug at Adler's Scottsdale shop, or see a picture on the Carillon web site, www.asu.edu/carillon.

Woven in Tabriz, Iran, the 8-by-11-foot hand-knotted rug is made of silk and wool with a black field, floral design and red border.

The rug, Adler said, was created by two artisans over the course of eight months to a year. "It will last forever," Adler said. "It can become a family heirloom."

Though some people prefer to hang their Persian rugs on the wall, the Tabriz rug will stand up to everyday wear and tear on the floor. "It's not a delicate wallflower," Adler said.

Persian carpet weavers have been plying their craft for centuries - just how many will never be known, says A. Cecil Edwards, author of The Persian Carpet.

Edwards suggests that the first Persian rugs were made by early Persians to cover the floors of their tents. "The Persian people are by nature skilled and artistic craftsmen," he wrote. "Such a people would not for long remain content to cover their tent floors with the skins of beasts. .

"The urge to fashion something closer to the need, more varied, and above all more colourful was there. The materials, too were there - for the sheep is an indigenous animal in Persia.

"Thus, the hand-knotted carpet may well have been evolved by art out of the sheepskin rug - the primal floor-covering of the pastoral nomads of the plateau."

Persian rugs went through a decline in the early 1700s, Edwards said, but a modern revival began in the late 1800s and continues to this day.

Funds from the Tabriz rug will be used to help restore ASU's 148-bell Maas-Rowe Symphonic Carillon.
Associated Students of ASU bought the carillon in 1966, and Roland Pomerat, Rice University carillonneur, gave the dedicatory concert on Jan. 4, 1967. Also on the program were remarks by ASASU President Robert McConnell Jr., who now lives in the Washington, D.C., area, ASU President G. Homer Durham, and College of Fine Arts Dean Henry A. Bruinsma.

 The carillon was used a few years, then put into storage in Matthews Center, where it languished until it was re-discovered in December 2002.

Last August, Maas-Rowe moved the carillon to its factory in Escondido, Calif., where it awaits renovation and modernization. The project is estimated at $45,000.

For more information about the rug or the ASU Carillon, call Judith Smith at (480) 965-4821.
