April 18, 2006

Former ambassador to discuss national security

Lecture part of ASU's School of Global Studies launch activities

Former U.S. ambassador Dennis Ross will deliver a keynote lecture on how to think about national security in an era of globalization at 7:30 p.m. Thursday, April 20, at Arizona State University's Coor Hall, Room 170. The lecture, which is free and open to the public, is part of two days of activities to celebrate the launch of the School of Global Studies in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences.

A ceremony is scheduled for 11 a.m. Friday, April 21, in Coor Hall's East Portico. ASU President Michael Crow and China's Sichuan University President Xie Heping are among dignitaries speaking at the ceremony. They will be joined by David Young, ASU vice president and dean of the college, and David Jacobson, director of the new school.

Ross, a former Middle East negotiator for the United States who served in both the George H. W. Bush and Bill Clinton administrations, is now a counselor and distinguished fellow at the Washington Institute for Near East Policy. A scholar and diplomat with more than two decades of experience in Soviet and Middle East policy, Ross has played a prominent role in U.S. policy toward the former Soviet Union, the unification of Germany and its integration into NATO, arms control negotiations and the 1991 Gulf War coalition.

During the Reagan administration, Ross served as director of Near East and South Asian affairs on the National Security Council staff and deputy director of the Pentagon's Office of Net Assessment. The UCLA graduate has published extensively on the former Soviet Union, arms control and the greater Middle East. He is a frequent contributor to the "Financial Times," "Washington Post," "Los Angeles Times" and "U.S. News and World Report." He also is the author of "The Missing Peace: The Inside Story of the Fight for Middle East Peace," published in 2004.

"It's an extraordinary opportunity to have someone like Dennis Ross on campus, not only because of his remarkable career, but because he embodies the global and systemic approach to international issues that is being taken by the School of Global Studies," Jacobson says.

In addition to the evening lecture, Ross will participate in a panel discussion on "New Challenges and New Strategies in Global Governance" with faculty specialists from the School of Global Studies. This discussion will be held from 2 to 3:30 p.m. Thursday, April 20, in the ASU Fulton Center, 5th Floor Conference Room.

"We have an extraordinary, unusual array of scholars who will work not just as individuals but in a highly collaborative, coordinated way to address broad global challenges that range from terrorism to mass atrocities, from environment and urban crises to health epidemics," Jacobson says. "Universities have become rather insular and we need to break that pattern."

ASU's School of Global Studies is not bound by models of the past, in institutional and intellectual terms, according to Jacobson. "ASU is rethinking the university's role globally. We are as a university at the cutting edge, creating the framework for global engagement."

To address this new role for universities, a panel discussion "New World, New Thinking" is scheduled Thursday morning with the deans and directors of international programs from the University of California at Berkeley, the University of California at Santa Barbara, the University of California at Los Angeles and the University of Washington. This discussion will be held from 10 to 11:30 a.m. Thursday, April 20, in the ASU Fulton Center, 5th Floor Conference Room.

Additional information about the School of Global Studies and these events is online at www.asu.edu/clas/globalstudies .
