April 26, 2005

Research program fuels undergraduate success in engineering

Sarah Kennedy, an undergraduate student in ASU’s Harrington Department of Bioengineering, is working to develop a more effective, longer-lasting and lighter prosthetic for rehabilitation purposes.

Kennedy is advancing the research in this area by developing a system of wires that will mimic the contraction of skeletal muscles. With this research, she hopes to find the best design for maximum prosthetic effectiveness.

This research, and more like it, is being sponsored by the newly minted Fulton Undergraduate Research Initiative (FURI) in the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering. This new program gives undergraduate students the unique opportunity of designing their own research topic and choosing a faculty member to assist the project instead of the other way around.

This novel approach allows students to explore areas of interest at a depth not usually attained through classes alone, as well as to pursue worthwhile contributions to the community and their chosen field of study.

“I have found that FURI has bestowed much needed structure and support for undergraduate engineering research at ASU,” Kennedy says. “As a direct result of the collaborative staff support from my department and the FURI program, I have been able to experimentally demonstrate the viability of my research that would have not been possible otherwise.”

“We are teaching our students the hands-on research and development skills they will need to be successful,” says Norma Hubele, director of strategic initiatives in the Fulton School. “The result is that Fulton engineers are better prepared to tackle problems they will face in industry and in advanced research, and our community is better for it.”

FURI funded more than 30 students in its first semester of operation, and the program is expected to grow in the coming year. In addition to faculty support, FURI also provides graduate student mentors who volunteer to help the undergraduate students in every step of their research.

This semester’s research will be on display at the inaugural FURI Symposium from 1 – 3 p.m., April 22, outside the Engineering G-wing at ASU’s Tempe campus. This event is free and open to the public.

For more information about the FURI program, go to the Web site (www.fulton.asu.edu/furi).

Koldoff, with the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering, can be reached at (480) 965-0213 or (jess.koldoff@asu.edu).
