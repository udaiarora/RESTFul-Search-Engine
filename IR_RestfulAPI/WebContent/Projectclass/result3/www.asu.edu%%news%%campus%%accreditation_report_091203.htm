ASU receives high praise, continuing accreditation for 10 years

More than two years of effort - capping a decade of careful planning, attention to priorities and excellent leadership - have paid off in a big way for the university. ASU Main and East have received continuing accreditation until 2013 from the Higher Learning Commission of the North Central Association of Colleges and Schools (NCA).

A glowing report by the evaluation team that visited last spring commended ASU for many things, including its student development, outreach to the community, improved external support, attractive campuses, cooperative relationships to other Arizona institutions and its strength.

"ASU has a strong sense of itself as a major metropolitan research university. Its plans for the future rest on this secure base," according to the report.

ASU received accreditation without further follow-up progress reports or focused visits, unlike 70 percent of NCA institutions throughout the country. NCA accredits colleges in 19 states.

"The outcomes of the institutional review are extraordinary. We didn't just barely make it over the bar for accreditation, we leaped over the bar," says Sarah Lindquist, assistant dean for academic accreditation in the Graduate College. "This report gives high accolades for the way the university has been managed over the last 10 years, and shows we are on a sharp trajectory upward under President Crow's leadership."

The commission also indicated ASU may open new international sites without prior approval, a rare authorization given to few institutions.

"This shows that the evaluation team found convincing evidence that ASU has built an extensive internal review process that works to sustain quality assurance in its programs, and that it has confidence in our ability to sustain this quality in the future," says Milton Glick, executive vice president and provost of the university. "It is a vote of confidence in the health and direction of the university and a tribute to the hard work of the steering committee, headed by Self-Study Coordinator Dan Jankowski."

Much of the success can be attributed to planning and preparation, says Maria T. Allison, interim dean of the Graduate College and university accreditation officer. A 40-member steering committee and more than 200 people on 10 working teams oversaw a comprehensive and candid self-study, and held open forums for input from faculty, staff, students and community leaders.

"ASU's commitment to its students is evident in the myriad of programs it has developed to serve its various student populations," the report said. "The team was impressed by the support that ASU enjoys among its minority graduates, and suggests that they might serve as mentors to encourage students to enroll and persist."

The report addressed strategies of increasing quality while also increasing size. Having a larger student body can help, not hinder, the increase in quality among the faculty, since the size of the student body will provide the base on which to expand the expertise of the faculty, the team said.

"ASU has wisely tailored its aspirations to the environment for higher education within the State of Arizona," the report said. "For example, it seeks to cooperate with the University of Arizona in building up the health care education and research capabilities of the state, rather than embarking on a path to create duplicative programs. ASU's urban location means it can bring to the table other resources that are not available to the other two state universities."

Other noteworthy accomplishments and progress over the past decade cited in the report were:

. Achieving Carnegie "doctoral/research universities extensive" status, increasing grant expenditures and expanding philanthropic activities.
. Committing to students through student development, living and learning communities.
. Impressive growth at the downtown campus, which will continue through joint biotechnology enterprise with UA.
. Community outreach - faculty and staff are active in a broad array of regional programs.
. Being a leader in developing cooperative relationships with UA and NAU.

The evaluation team found evidence of innovative or exemplary practices as far as morale and collegiality, effective technology, data warehouse for planning and policy making, Native American programs, Honors College and self-supporting international programs.

The team was made up of 12 chancellors, deans and professors from several universities, including UCLA, Ohio State, University of Illinois, Purdue, University of Colorado and University of Wisconsin. The final evaluation team report and the ASU self-study report are available online at (http://hlc2003.asu.edu).
