December 5, 2003

Wright brothers' flight centennial celebration at ASU

It's been 100 years! The Wright brothers, Wilbur and Orville, found success in December 1903 with the "Wright Flyer," the first powered, heavier-than-air device to achieve controlled and sustained flight with a pilot on board.

To celebrate this achievement and how far aviation has come, the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering at Arizona State University, with a grant from Alcoa Foundation, is hosting Dr. John McMasters, Boeing Technical Fellow and AIAA Distinguished Lecturer, on Friday, December 5, 2003 at 2:40 p.m. in Murdock Hall room 101 on ASU's campus, for a lecture on the "Origins and Future of Flight."

The day's events include a paper airplane tournament from 9:45 a.m. to 11:30 a.m. at the Student Recreation Center, where ASU students and the public will compete for prizes on the basis of duration, distance and desired target; a tour of student aviation-related projects from 1:30 p.m. to 2:15 p.m.; and a public reception to meet the lecturer and network with industry, university and student representatives in the aerospace field from 4 p.m. to 6 p.m. in the Memorial Union Ventana room.

Alcoa is the world's leading supplier of primary aluminum, fabricated aluminum and alumina, and also played a role in the Wright brother's success. Alcoa provided the aluminum for the engine of that first powered flight at Kitty Hawk, North Carolina.

"For Alcoa Foundation, Alcoa's history in flight also is a celebration of Alcoa's interest in diversity and access to educational opportunities, especially in the area of science and technology," said Kathleen Buechel, president and treasurer of the Foundation. "We are proud of our grant to Arizona State University for the Centennial of Flight activities, which may spark the curiosity of the next generation of aerospace engineers and technicians."

"The Wright Brothers were innovative in developing design practices that form the foundation of aerospace education. The Alcoa Foundation recognizes that pioneering spirit and has chosen to support this event with a corporate outlook that adds value to our entire community," said Robert Peck, mechanical and aerospace engineering department chair at ASU.

The Fulton School of Engineering provides a transformative educational experience for engineering, computer science and construction students, giving them the knowledge and skills they need for success in a technically oriented career. Through the creation of a highly educated, innovative workforce and the advancement of technical knowledge, the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering drives sustainable growth and improved quality of life for the communities it serves.

For more information on the Wright brothers, go to the Smithsonian Institute's website at <http://www.nasm.si.edu/galleries/gal209/wrights.htm>.

For more information on Alcoa and its pioneering role in aerospace innovation, go to <http://www.alcoa.com>.

For more information on the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering, go to <http://www.fulton.asu.edu>.
