October 20, 2003

Initiative coordinates, promotes sustainability at ASU

As the Valley grows into a thriving metropolis in the harsh desert, its relationship with the natural environment is becoming increasingly important. With sustainability - the concept of creating economic and human vitality while being environmentally sensitive - such a vital issue for ASU and the state's long-term well-being, the university is launching a series of efforts in the area.

"We live today with the realization that human impact on natural systems has long-term outcomes - both positive and negative - for our social, economic and cultural futures," says ASU President Michael Crow. "The dynamics of understanding sustainability and the development of knowledge and tools to advance economic and environmental well-being will be a critical area of interest to academic communities, as well as to the broader public and private sectors."

Crow says the region must begin "a transition toward a sustainable future." With that in mind, ASU is approaching the concept through academic teaching and research, administrative actions and educational efforts for the university community.

One of the key efforts is the new University Sustainability Committee. The committee, led by Chairwoman Lee Overmyer, associate vice president for Administration and Finance, will focus on promoting sustainability at ASU through community awareness.

A major role of the committee will be to coordinate education efforts and serve as a bridge between many current groups and programs on campus, such as ASU Clean and Beautiful, the Campus Arboretum, the Travel Reduction Program, the Energy Conservation Committee and the ASU Recycling Program. All of the individual efforts focus on a single area, but all relate to the overarching concept of sustainability.

"These programs and committees all have charges related to important sustainability concepts," says Overmyer. "ASU has started down the path toward sustainability but there is considerable distance to go."

Each of the groups will have representation on the committee. Geography Professor Michael Pasqualetti and Architecture Professor Harvey Bryan also will add expertise to the committee.

To open the educational effort, the committee sponsored a live telecast Oct. 9 of "Got Sustainability? Plan For It!," a national production of the Society for College and University Planning. The telecast, to recognize the first national Campus

Sustainability Day, featured a panel of recognized sustainability experts discussing the role of higher education in the process. ASU had the largest attendance of the 150 universities participating in the event, with more than 150 students, faculty and staff attending.

On the academic side, the university launched the Consortium for the Study of Rapidly Urbanizing Regions this summer in the Center for Environmental Studies, in part to look at the role of urban environments in the environment as a whole. Within the umbrella of the consortium is Greater Phoenix 2100, which looks at the long-term consequences of policy and development decisions. Additional research efforts are being developed and will be launched in the near future.

Overmyer says everyone on the ASU campuses can participate in the efforts through their daily actions. For information, call Overmyer at (480) 965-3201 or (lee.overmyer@asu.edu).

Campbell, with ASU Media Relations & Public Information, can be reached at (480) 965-7209 or (garycamp@asu.edu).
