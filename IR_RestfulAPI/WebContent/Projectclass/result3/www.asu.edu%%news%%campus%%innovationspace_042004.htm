InnovationSpace to bring socially responsible products to market

When a design student comes up with an idea for a terrific new product, there's a good chance that the product will never hit the market.

Starting next year, that may change, thanks to a new - and unique - program at Arizona State University designed to support and accelerate the transfer of design concepts that create value for business while also contributing to a socially responsible and sustainable future.

InnovationSpace is an entrepreneurial joint venture between the ASU College of Architecture and Environmental Design (CAED), Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering, and W.P. Carey School of Business.

Through InnovationSpace, senior-level students from the three colleges will work with internal and external partners to create new product concepts, complete with engineering prototypes, business plans and visual materials to communicate the function, appearance, manufacturability and value of the concepts, said Paul Rothstein, associate professor of industrial design.

"With these materials in hand, the students and faculty will work with Arizona Technology Enterprises to transfer these concepts to the private sector for further development."

The idea for InnovationSpace grew out of a five-year relationship between the W.P. Carey School of Business and ASU's School of Design (CAED), where students from the two schools participated in an Innovation Studio each semester.

"This past year, engineering joined the team and set the stage for the development of InnovationSpace as a more powerful entrepreneurial resource at ASU," Rothstein said.

To support the venture, Rothstein joined forces with James Hershauer from the W.P. Carey School of Business and Mark Henderson from the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering.

As a team, they obtained seed money from the three colleges totaling $450,000 over the next three years.

"Our goal is to be self supporting and generating income for ASU by the end of that period, " Rothstein said.

"In the past," Rothstein noted, "manufacturers, corporations and funding agencies have supported research and educational initiatives in engineering, business and design at ASU. With InnovationSpace fully developed and promoted, partnerships with these groups are expected to expand in scope and significance."

InnovationSpace also will collaborate with inventors, venture capitalists, intellectual property consultants and applied researchers working in academic institutions where design and/or business programs do not exist, Rothstein added.

Though InnovationSpace will begin its work in earnest next academic year, at least one product idea is being carried over: iCARE, a project in Computer Science that allows individuals who are blind or visually impaired to access textual material by using a variety of inventions.

The motivation for creating InnovationSpace came from both faculty and students, Rothstein said.

"We, as faculty, have seen students' incredible work, and we've seen students who want to make an impact. I think students have always wanted to make a contribution to a better world."

Put the two together, and you have a program that will set ASU apart from other universities.
