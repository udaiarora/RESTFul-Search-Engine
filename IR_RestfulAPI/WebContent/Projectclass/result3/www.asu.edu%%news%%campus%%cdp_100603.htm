October 6, 2003

Serving Arizona's needs key as ASU evolves: Year-long planning process underway

Becoming "One University - In Many Places" is the goal as ASU embarks on preparing a comprehensive development plan to support the eventual doubling of the population of metropolitan Phoenix.

"With ever-increasing quality students coming to ASU, we're having a difficult time keeping up with demand for what ASU has to offer," says President Michael Crow. "There are about 100,000 people per year moving into central Arizona, and they are coming with children, relatives and expectations for a quality education."

The process of building a comprehensive development plan for ASU will involve detailed analysis of all four campus sites, focusing on issues related to transportation, university access, blending university learning facilities with non-university facilities, and blending the university into its surrounding towns and cities.

Crow kicked off the process with a presentation Sept. 26 to about 400 faculty, staff and students. The endeavor will take about a year and will include some 80 focus group sessions with various internal and external constituencies. The planning process also has been presented by Crow to the Arizona Board of Regents. Crow says it's important to define ASU as a niche so it becomes something other than one of the 200 or so indistinguishable higher education institutions that people have a hard time remembering. ASU, Crow says, will be remembered because we have been able to accomplish in the next decade or two the following three things:

. Increasing the university in size and enhancing its quality at the same time;
. Expanding ASU's research position dramatically to benefit both this region and the state; and
. Embedding the university as intimately as possible with our community beyond what we have already accomplished.

This comprehensive development planning process is an effort to help lay the physical foundation for accomplishing those three goals.

"The thing we are trying to avoid in the evolution of this university is a multi-tiered university system," says Crow. "Our desire is to build one university - all of our campuses will be ASU, not 'ASU light,' 'ASU junior,' or 'ASU catching up.' The university has to be high quality everywhere.

"If you attend Arizona State University you will have access to high quality schools on any of these campuses. Each school will be competitive nationally, within the realm of what that school has to offer. So, we will take a school-centric approach, as opposed to a campus-centric approach, as we advance the preparation of our comprehensive development plan."

In the end, Crow says ASU needs to end up as a unique, one-of-a-kind institution. He wants ASU to be the one university that found a way to grow and meet the challenges of dealing with a major metropolitan area's population growth, and population and cultural diversification. He says the challenge of ASU's comprehensive development planning process is a tough one. Most people, when they think of ASU, think about the central core of the Main campus, or the central core of the other campuses.

"We need input, perspective and ideas about how we can build the entire university, not just the individual campuses or units," says Crow. "We're trying to work on the notion of having soft boundaries, not hard boundaries. It's the only way we believe that we can move toward success."

Rough estimates of enrollment growth patterns suggest the Main campus would grow to 50,000 students, the East and West campuses would grow to 20,000 students each, and Downtown to 10,000-12,000. Crow says if ASU doesn't find a way to accommodate this growth, other institutions will be built to serve them.

"I am of the strongest opinion that if we were to allow that, we would lose our central strategic position as The Arizona State University, the public university for this metropolitan area, too early," he says.

The architecture firm Ayers, Saint, Gross is under contract with the university to complete the comprehensive development plan under the direction of Mernoy Harrison, vice president of Administration and Finance; Scott Cole, deputy executive vice president for university services; and Ron McCoy, university architect. They already have begun conducting focus groups to gain input from key constituents.

"This team will help us grasp something we haven't been grasping well enough - how do we make ASU the most successful university that we possibly can," says Crow.
