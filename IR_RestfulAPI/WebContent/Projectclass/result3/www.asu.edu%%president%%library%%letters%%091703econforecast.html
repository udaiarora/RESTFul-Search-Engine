Outlook for Health, Wealth, and Social Fabric: ASU’s Connection to Regional Well-Being

SRP 2004 Economic Forecast Breakfast
A special publication of The Business Journal of Phoenix  
September 17, 2003

By Michael M. Crow, President, Arizona State University

One of the fundamental premises of my vision for ASU is that it must assume major responsibility for the economic, social, and cultural health of the metropolitan area and region.   This is a daunting task, but it is essential because ASU is integral to the well-being of our region.

Arizona has fought throughout its history to build economic prosperity, and we cannot stop now.   We have secured the physical infrastructure that makes life in the desert possible, but in the new knowledge economy the fight is for knowledge infrastructure.   There is a direct correlation between success in the new economy and the research infrastructure of a region’s research universities.   Research capacity has been pinpointed as the single most critical factor in shaping regional economic success–as Federal Reserve chairman Alan Greenspan has argued, investment in university research structure is key to building economic resiliency.

During the past few years the people of Arizona have made an unprecedented commitment to university science and technology research.   The approval of Proposition 301 in November 2000 represented public recognition of the need to invest in the future prosperity of our region by providing a long-term funding stream for science and technology investments.   And the 2003 legislative session ended June 19 with the state legislature passing a bill that will enable ASU to build $185 million in new research infrastructure.   A broad coalition of support from the business community, city government, and state political leaders, such as Gov. Janet Napolitano, Speaker of the House Jake Flake, Speaker Pro Tempor Bob Robson, and Senate President Ken Bennett, helped the bill pass against considerable odds.   It is a pivotal acknowledgment by government leaders that investing in higher education is critical to the economic success of the region.

This public investment opens the door to a knowledge-based Arizona economy in which our universities will play a critical role.   The potential of new technology-based industries–biotechnology, nanotechnology, and information technology–to generate wealth and bring prosperity to the state, however, depends both on our ability to prepare our students to become globally competitive innovators, and to attract and retain knowledge workers–a “creative class,” as it has been termed–from across the nation.   Arizona must become a knowledge producer, rather than a knowledge importer.   With growing competition, continued investment, both public and private, is essential to the success of our plan.

In a metropolitan region with a population that exceeds three million, a population, moreover, that includes a significant number of aging adults and families with inadequate medical care, quality healthcare is a major social issue.   But along with economic prosperity, university research promises to bring state-of-the-art healthcare to the region.   Improving the human condition is at the core of research at ASU, and without question it is the prime motivation behind its research in the life sciences, and the fields of bioscience, biotechnology, and biomedicine.

The April groundbreaking for the Arizona Biodesign Institute was a signal event in ASU’s research enterprise, marking the advent of a pioneering collaborative effort in the life sciences dedicated to improving human health and the quality of life through biodesign–the intentional manipulation of biological systems.   Arizona Biodesign combines many of the universitys leading programs related to biomedicine to form a use-inspired and interdisciplinary world-class research institute committed to real-world impact, such as enhanced pharmaceuticals, the development of biomechanical prostheses, plant-based vaccines that can be easily grown and distributed in third-world countries, and new rehabilitation techniques for spinal cord injuries.

The hallmark of Arizona Biodesign is a research agenda that emphasizes the application of discoveries to commercial uses and societal benefits.   The research programs are clustered in two focus areas of increasing contemporary relevance:   biologics and therapeutics, and nano-biosystems and devices.   And with the critical mass attained through the establishment in Phoenix of the Translational Genomics Research Institute (TGen) and the International Genomics Consortium, Arizona Biodesign has the potential not only to advance knowledge and improve healthcare, but to reconfigure the economic portfolio of the region.

But ASU seeks to have even broader impact, and I would describe our approach to public service as nothing less than comprehensive.   Our programs and initiatives in local schools and neighborhoods build communities.   Our very presence is a leading force in the local economy–the total annual economic impact of ASU on spending in the state is estimated to be $2.1 billion, and this is to say nothing of the higher productivity and earnings that are made possible because of the knowledge and skills students acquire.   We tackle local and regional problems through research and analysis, and provide a forum for public debate.   We bring intelligent and insightful programming to Arizona through our public television station, KAET, and, with our libraries, museums, and innovative programming in the visual and performing arts, ASU's contribution to the cultural depth of our region requires no elaboration.  

Universities are powerful engines for societal transformation–no institution has more potential to improve the human condition, provide us with the tools we need for better planetary stewardship, and foster sustained economic growth.   Never before has the impact of knowledge been greater, and as one of only 300 major research institutions in the world, ASU has the potential to generate new knowledge that may influence almost every aspect of our future–and bring new economic prosperity to the region and state.

I appreciate the interest of the business community in ASU, and look forward to meeting many more of you in person to hear your ideas and share our vision.
