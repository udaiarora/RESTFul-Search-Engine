The key to efficient library research is a systematic search strategy that progresses from general background material to specific detailed information. Use the following guidelines to find information in the earth sciences including geology, physical geography and environmental topics. Although the research strategy is described in a linear sequence, students may need to repeat steps as the paper topic is refined.

Research Strategy

  • Select a Topic

    Keep three things in mind when selecting a topic:
    1. Pick a topic that is interesting,
    2. Before committing to a topic, do a quick preliminary search to make sure that the library has materials on that topic, and
    3. Be flexible; during the research process, you may find it necessary to broaden or narrow the topic to fit the requirements of the assignment. If the topic changes, even slightly, during the research process, it is usually necessary to repeat the research steps using the terminology specific to that topic.

  • Find an Overview

    An encyclopedia is the best place to start if the topic is unfamiliar or if a basic overview is needed. General encyclopedias have information on science topics, however, earth science encyclopedias cover this field in depth and they usually provide longer articles, lengthier bibliographies, and more numerical data. Be sure to copy down/printout all appropriate references at the end of encyclopedia articles; these references will lead to books, journal articles and/or Internet sites that provide more information on the topic.

  • Find Books

    Books provide a comprehensive look or an in-depth review for a specific topic. There are several ways to find books on a topic:

    • To determine what books the ASU Library own on a particular topic, search that topic as a keyword in the ASU Online Catalog.
    • To locate specific books (such as those found in the bibliography of an encyclopedia or journal article) in the library, search for the title in the ASU Online Catalog.

    For more detailed instructions on finding books see:

    • SunTutor: How to Find Books
    • SunTutor: Evaluating Sources

  • Find Journal Articles

    The most current earth science research is found in journal articles. Journal articles can be identified by using an Indexing or Abstracting Database; use one or more of the recommended databases listed below. For more detailed instructions on finding journal articles see:

    • SunTutor: How to Find Journal Articles
    • SunTutor: Evaluating Sources
      Most instructors request that scholarly journals rather than popular magazines be used. This tutorial demonstrates how to determine if a journal article is scholarly.

    The following indexes are recommended for Earth Science topics:

    1. General Science Abstracts/Full Text.
      Library Network Database (1984-)
      Q 1 .G4x Science Reference (1978-1983)

      This is an excellent place for undergraduate students to start researching science journal articles. Journals range from popular magazines such as Discover to scholarly journals such as the Bulletin of the Geological Society of America. All of the journals are in English; many articles have the full text available within the database. The ASU Libraries own most of the covered journals.

    2. Applied Science and Technology Abstracts/Full Text.
      Library Network Database (1983-)
      T1 .A652x Science Reference (1958-1983)

      This is an excellent place for undergraduate students to start researching applied science or engineering journal articles. Journals range from trade magazines to scholarly journals. All of the journals are in English; many articles have the full text available within the database. The ASU Libraries own most of the covered journals. For years prior to 1958, see Industrial Arts Index (T 1 .I52x Science Reference).

    3. GeoRef. 1785-
      Library Network Database

      The database covers North American geology from 1785 to the present and geology outside of North America from 1933 to the present. Most of the covered titles are scholarly publications. Links to article full text, Libraries' E-journal List, and Libraries' Catalog are available. This is the best index to use for finding geology material; recommended for faculty, graduate students and geology majors.

    4. GeoBase 1990-

      This database covers the areas of geography, environment and some geology topics. Links to article full text, Libraries' E-journal List, and Libraries' Catalog are available. Recommended mostly for geography faculty, graduate students and undergraduate majors.

    5. Environmental Policy Index (1973-)

      This databse covers all areas of environmental topics. Many articles have the full text available within the database. Recommended for all levels.

  • Find Government Documents

    There are many government agencies, for example the United States Geological Survey or the former United States Bureau of Mines, that are prolific producers of earth science information. The Government Documents Department on the third floor of Hayden Library contains an extensive collection of publications from both federal (United States) and Arizona agencies. Many U.S. agencies are making their recent publications freely available on the Web.

    To find government documents for earth science topics other than geology (which is covered sufficiently by GeoRef), try these sources:

    1. Monthly Catalog of United States Government Publications.
      Library Network Database 1976-.
      GP3.8 1885-1976 Document Services (Hayden Library 3rd Floor)

      Indexes U.S. federal government publications. Cites books, reports, studies, serials, maps, & other publications.

    2. Government Periodicals Index. (1994-)
      Library Network Database

      Indexes 170 key federal periodicals for articles with substantive research, reference and general interest value.

    3. Government Agencies' Home Pages

      • U.S.
      • Arizona (State)
      • Arizona (Local)

  • Find Internet Resources

    The Internet contains many resources on a wide variety of topics however, the internet cannot be used as the sole source of information for a college-level paper or presentation. Before searching the Internet, be sure that your instructor will accept this type of resource. Also note that Internet resources vary in quality and accuracy and should be evaluated closely (see: SunTutor: Evaluating Sources).

    Because the Internet can quickly disperse news and images, this is a valuable source of information on current topics. For example, the Internet would be a ready source of information on volcanic eruptions or earthquakes that may have happened in the last couple of days.

To search the Internet using keywords, use one or more Internet search engines such as Google (http://www.google.com)
