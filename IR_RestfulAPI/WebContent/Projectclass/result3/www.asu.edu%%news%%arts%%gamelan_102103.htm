October 21, 2003

Indonesian gamelan finds home at ASU

Ted Solis, ethnomusicology professor in the Katherine K. Herberger College of Fine Arts School of Music, spent spring semester unwrapping packages of instruments and then spreading them out on every available flat surface. Individually, the instruments were impressive by their total number. Now assembled, they have become something much more to behold - a traditional Indonesian gamelan.

Sometimes incorrectly referred to as a single instrument, a gamelan is actually a percussion orchestra that is used in both traditional Indonesian and Balinese dance and theater. The gamelan orchestra that Solis set up is from Central Java and was associated with court dances. It was brought to the U.S. by a musicologist at the University of Illinois 17 years ago and was recently purchased by the ASU School of Music.

"The gamelan produces an endlessly challenging, wonderful sound," Solis says. "All the instruments behave in their own way, but all the layers relate to a central theme or melody."

In order for the gamelan to produce melodies, two sets of all the instruments are necessary: one provides the major key, the other set produces the minor one.

A number of gamelan instruments, such as the various kinds of drums, are most prized when crafted of bronze. "Bronze drums are the best because the older they get, the better the tone," says Solis. A gamelan orchestra also includes glockenspiels, gongs, strings, sitars and flutes. Singers, both female and male, provide a vocal component.

He explains that learning to play is an oral tradition, one learned by ear.

"Learning to play the gamelan is available to anyone, although it takes years and years to become a master because there is an enormous amount to learn. It's much like learning a complex language."

On sabbatical spring semester, Solis used the time to study the gamelan with trips to the University of Hawaii, Dartmouth College and Grinnell College to study with accomplished gamelan musicians. This fall, he is introducing the gamelan orchestra in a new ethnic ensemble course offered by the School of Music.

"Teaching the gamelan provides a wide variety of skills for students, from simple to complex. There is something for everyone in the class. My goal is just to stay ahead of the students."

Solis and his students will put what they have learned to use and will host an open house "Gamelan Taster," from 2 to 3:30 p.m., Oct. 22, in Gammage Auditorium room 206 as part of Southeast Asian Culture Week, Oct. 20 - Oct. 24. The event is sponsored by the Program for Southeast Asian Studies (PSEAS) and Students' Association for Southeast Asia.

Solis, who received his Ph.D. from the University of Illinois, Urbana, has carried out field research in India, Mexico, Puerto Rico, Hawaii and the San Francisco Bay area. He is the director of the School of Music's Marimba Maderas de Comitan, which is a Mexican/Latin marimba ensemble. In addition to his current interest in the gamelan, Solis' research interests within the field of ethnomusicology include music/dance relationships, acculturation, the Caribbean, urban Hispanic American musics and diasporic relationships among musical traditions. His field recordings have been released by Original Music and Smithsonian/Folkways, and he has published in the Journal of the Society for Ethnomusicology, the World of Music and the Latin American Music Review. Solis is also one of the core faculty members in the PSEAS at ASU.

Brennan, with the Herberger College of Fine Arts, can be contacted at (480) 965-3587 or (mary.brennan@asu.edu).
