Mirapex® may Hook you on Gambling
By Judy Blaskie, PharmD candidate

Gambling has become popular today in our society, with several outlets including Internet web sites, casinos, sports betting parlors, and so on. A drug that has been prescribed to millions of patients in the U.S. for the treatment of Parkinson's disease has allegedly turned some patients into gambling addicts. A Mayo clinic study from the July 2005 issue of Archives of Neurology found nearly a dozen Parkinson's patients who developed unusual gambling behaviors. The study reported a 68 year old male, with no history of gambling, losing over $200,000 at casinos in 6 months. The patient also became hypersexual and left town for days without anyone knowing where he was going. After a 50% dosage reduction, his impulse to gambling was substantially reduced. Another patient in the study was a 41 year old married pastor who lost over $5000 within a few months while on Mirapex. There was no urge to gamble after the Parkinson's agent was abruptly discontinued.

The risk of gambling behaviors in a Parkinson's patient seems to be minimal according to many physicians. It may be appropriate to inform anyone you know who is on Mirapex (pramipexole) of this potential risk.

In related news.
Applications for trademarks containing the word "poker" increased by 266% in 2004. A 42% and 28% increase for gambling and casino-related trademark applications was also noted respectively.

Something Fishy Smells Good
By Judy Blaskie, PharmD candidate

Did you know that eating fish at least once a week decreases sudden cardiac deaths by 50%? The American Heart Association recommends most people to eat fish at least two times a week. Suggest eating fatty fish.tuna, salmon, herring, halibut.
For people who don't eat fish, recommend fish oil supplements. Fish oil supplements may be less contaminated. There may be lower levels of polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) or other toxins.
Fish oils in higher doses can reduce the production of "bad fats" called triglycerides, in the liver. Doses of 2-4 grams/day can lower trigs by 45%. Fish oils also have other beneficial effects such as preventing blood clots and reducing inflammation. They may have some blood-pressure lowering effects as well. They may have some blood-pressure lowering effects as well.
Soon to be out in the US market by prescription only is Omacor, which contain omega-3 fatty acids found in fish to help lower triglycerides. The manufacturer is also trying to get approval for its use to prevent recurrent heart attacks.

Herbals for Headaches
(and vitamins too)
By Judy Blaskie, PharmD candidate

There's been a lot of discussion about supplements to prevent migraines. Some of the supplements that may be "possibly effective" according to the Natural Medicines Comprehensive Database include butterbur, coenzyme Q10, feverfew, magnesium, and riboflavin.

Butterbur seems to be beneficial. Butterbur is a plant with incredibly huge leaves, up to 3 feet in diameter usually found in wet marshes. These large leaves were once used to wrap butter to prevent it from melting in warm weather. A study in Neurology suggested the herbal supplement may decrease migraines by about 50%. Warn anyone taking butterbur that it may cause stomach upset and burping.

Coenzyme Q10 taken three times a day may decrease the number of migraine attacks by one-third. Feverfew and magnesium may reduce the frequency of migraines in some people. These agents may be worth a try in some people.

Riboflavin, or vitamin B2, has been studied in a number of trials. It has been shown to be effective in reducing the frequency and severity of migraine attacks. Suggest 400mg of riboflavin daily.


Silent Bloodthirsty Creatures of Flying Death
By Brian Bersbach, PharmD candidate

That's right! I'm talking about mosquitoes carrying West Nile Virus (WNV). Although only 391 cases of WNV have been reported in AZ, about 4% of those resulted in death. Worse yet, there is no known proven treatment of WNV. So, what do we do? Protect ourselves from those pesky buggers that carry it! How do we do it? First we can mosquito proof our homes. Draining standing water, installing screens, and even reporting dead birds to the authorities can help in preventing the flying critters of death from getting near your home. We can also mosquito proof ourselves by wearing clothing that covers most of our body.

We can also protect ourselves with chemicals. One is insect repellants, like DEET and picardin. Interestingly, as the percentage of DEET goes up, so does the time it repels mosquitoes (not the number of mosquitoes). The CDC suggests DEET up to 30% (roughly 7 hours of 95% less bites) for children above 2 months. Adults can apply up to 100% DEET, but extra DEET after 50% doesn't have much added benefit. Also important: the outside temperature, and humidity decreases DEET protection time. Picardin 10 and 19% has been shown to be as effective as DEET 15 and 30%, respectively. Finally we can paralyze the bugger by putting premethrin on our cloths. Premethrin should not be applied to skin or used in children less than 3 years old. However, DEET and premethrin in combination can reduce mosquito bites up to 100% for 8 hours. Citronella, IR3535, soybean oil, and other forms can also repel mosquitoes, but their effect last less than 30 minutes!
Ye Olde History
By Carl Labbe, RPh

In merry olde England in the 1700's betting men would hang out at Edward Lloyd's Coffee House in London wagering on just about anything. They would bet on the deaths of celebrities, outcomes of battles or wars, Louis XV's next mistress or the outcomes of trials. Historian Marieke deGoede writes that in 1771, Londoners bet over 60,000 pounds on whether French diplomat Charles de Beaumont was male or female. "Charles" declined to provide definitive evidence, but a judge ruled in favor of female settling all bets. Patrons of the coffee house could also bet on when they would die or if his ship and cargo might be lost at sea. This win-by-losing sort of gambling lives on today and is called insurance. The coffee house eventually evolved into one of the world's most notable insurers, Lloyd's of London.

Time zones in the U.S. were originally the domain of the railroads. Different railroads ran on different clocks in the 1800's. By 1870 there were more than 70 time zones in the country as declared by various railroads. Traveling across the country could cause you to reset your watch 20 times. We can thank a principal of an all-girl boarding school for coming up with the simplified four zone system. Each zone is an hour apart in time, a 24th of a day, 15 degrees apart in longitude (a 24th of the earth's rotation). The railroads adopted the suggestion of Charles Dowd in 1883. Principal Dowd was granted lifetime free passage on every major rail line in the country. Charles Dowd was struck and killed by a train in Saratoga Springs in 1904.

When Edgar Allen Poe was rejected for a Philadelphia customs job in 1842 he publicly wrote about "the low ruffians and boobies who have received office over my head." Trying to help, a friend arranged for Poe to visit the White House and make his case for federal employment directly to President John Tyler. However, upon reaching D.C., Poe promptly got drunk and stayed that way for days, staggering around the city, begging money, insulting people and wearing his coat inside out. The meeting was canceled and Poe eventually made his way back to Philly apparently nevermore to seek a government job.

Young Hans Christian, the son of a law professor, was born in Copenhagen in 1853. He got interested in plants at a young age which led him to the study of pharmacology and the use of the microscope. He obtained his BA in 1871, interned with a zoologist named Steenstrup and decided to pursue the study of medicine, obtaining his MD in 1878. He studied across Europe and was a renowned expert on human red blood cells, becoming one of the first to note macrocytes as a characteristic of pernicious anemia. But that's not what really made him famous. In 1884, Hans Christian Gram published his method for staining bacteria and the Gram Stain was born. Gram understated his discovery in that original publication, stating, "I have therefore published the method, although I am aware that as yet it is very defective and imperfect; but it is hoped that also in the hands of other investigations it will turn out to be useful." The Gram stain is now the most widely used bacteriological staining method, it is almost always the first test performed for the identification of bacteria.


Holy medical malfunction, Hitachi!
By Brian Bersbach, Pharm D candidate

On July 13th, the FDA issued a letter warning Hitachi about 4 complaints of burns, hearing loss and ringing due to the Hitachi medical branches MRI equipment. The FDA had been watching the reported complaints as well as attempting to contact the company about parts/changes in the MRI equipment that had not been reported to the FDA.


Gradient safety coils were found to be arching electricity, which caused one MRI to catch on fire. Bad welds on a Type A absorber could have also caused a helium compressor to crack and resulted in "an explosive force that bent the compressor covers and popped the screw heads making them "projectile". (Complaint # 1-C834T/1) Also some problems with the Hitachi software seemed to be switching/mixing patient and scan information.

The FDA has put Hitachi on hold for approval of any other medical devices until these matters are resolved.

What's all the Heat about Mexoryl?
By Judy Blaskie, PharmD candidate

Mexoryl, a sunscreen ingredient, has been grabbing a lot of attention to the importance of UVA protection. This UVA filter is available in Canada and Europe, but not in the US. Most of the sunscreens note "broad spectrum" coverage to indicate they block UVA and UVB rays. However, SPF ratings apply only to UVB rays. There are no standard ratings for protection from UVA rays.

- UVA rays lead to wrinkles, sagging, discoloration, and photosensitivity reactions.
- UVB rays cause sunburn and skin cancer.

Zinc oxide and titanium dioxide are effective in blocking both UVA and UVB rays; however, they can leave a white film. Not only is mexoryl effective and photostable, it is also cosmetically appealing (no white film left on skin).

Broad spectrum sunscreens with SPF 15 are recommended. Sunscreen should be applied approximately 30 minutes before sun exposure and reapplied throughout the day if out in the sun. Sunscreen should be reapplied frequently after swimming or strenuous exercise.

UVA protection is important for people who take medications that can cause photosensitivity. Skin damage can occur when the drug reacts with UVA light. Some drugs that can cause increase in sun sensitivity include the following: Cipro, Levaquin, doxycycline, minocycline, tetracyclines, Bactrim, Accutane, Retin-A products, Tegretol, Hydrochlorothiazide, Neurontin.
