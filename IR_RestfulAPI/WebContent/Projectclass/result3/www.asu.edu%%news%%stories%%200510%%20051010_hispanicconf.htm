October 13, 2005

Hispanic education conference draws national attention

Thousands of educators, politicians and civic leaders will converge in Phoenix for the 19th annual conference of the Hispanic Association of Colleges and Universities (HACU) Oct 15 – 18 at the Phoenix Civic Plaza . U.S. Secretary of Education Margaret Spellings and Phoenix Mayor Phil Gordon will be among the speakers at the event, which is co-sponsored by ASU.

Conference highlights

Friday, Oct. 14 - Eugene Garcia, dean of the ASU College of Education, will host a special session for university presidents and school district superintendents on how they can work together better. Noon - 2:30 p.m., Cassidy Room, Hyatt.

Saturday, Oct. 15 - Phoenix Mayor Phil Gordon and U.S. Rep. Raul Grijalva will speak at the opening session. 4 - 6 p.m.

Sunday, Oct. 16 - ASU President Michael Crow will discuss the importance of education in economic development, and the crucial role of the Hispanic community in the health of the region, at 7- 7:30 p.m. Networking reception hosted by ASU, 6:30 - 8:30 p.m., Hyatt Regency

Tuesday, Oct. 18 - Town hall meeting with key university leaders to examine how the growing Hispanic population will impact higher education, what the community can do to prepare, and what is the role of higher education in helping Hispanics succeed. Free to the public. 10 a.m. - noon, Hyatt Regency.

For more information, visit www.hacu.net.

Media may attend by checking in at the central registration desk. For additional media assistance, contact Joan Sherwood, ASU College of Education, (480) 965-2114 or (602) 576-1346 (cell); or HACU communications director Cynthia Vela, (210) 860-6681 (cell)

The conference will provide a powerful platform to promote the college and career needs of the country’s largest ethnic population and play a vital role in shaping the future of higher education, according to Eugene Garcia, dean of the ASU College of Education.

Garcia will lead a special preconference workshop on university-school partnerships Oct. 14 for university presidents and school district superintendents, discussing how the two entities can work better together. ASU President Michael Crow will be the host of a reception and networking session Oct. 16, welcoming the group to Phoenix.

Other participants from ASU include Manuel Avalos, social and behavioral science professor who was named a HACU Leadership Fellow; several students sponsored as conference scholars; and other faculty members.

"Given Arizona’s demographics, a growing state with a large Hispanic population, it’s significant that such a substantial organization is coming here,” Garcia says. "HACU will be an important ally as Arizona addresses the challenges of higher education for its Hispanic students.

"The majority of Arizona ’s high school graduates will be Hispanic in 15 years. They are our future, the key to our economic viability and competitiveness. Big companies really understand this, and that’s why they support HACU. It’s not just a Southwest issue anymore.”

The conference will focus on preparing tomorrow’s leaders and building new support for increasing private- and public-sector investments in Hispanic students. HACU is working to stop the decline of local, state and federal support for institutions of higher education.

Other goals for the conference are to demonstrate successful college and university programs, create partnerships, foster graduate education and scholarship opportunities, and discuss policy issues.

Mario Bosquez, a New York television anchorman and author of “The Chalupa Rules: a Latino Guide to Gringolandia,” will speak at a power lunch at noon, Oct. 17.

As part of the conference, the public is invited to attend a free town hall meeting, at 10 a.m., Oct. 18, at the Phoenix Convention Center . Leaders from two prominent universities will speak: Mark Yudof, chancellor of the University of Texas system, and Matthew Goldstein, chancellor of the City University of New York.

The town hall meeting will look at key issues, such as:

• How the growing Hispanic population will affect higher education.

• What the community can do to prepare.

• What is the role of higher education in helping Hispanics gain access to an education and succeed.

With national headquarters in San Antonio, HACU has grown in 20 years from an organization of 14 institutions to one that represents more than 400 colleges and universities serving three-quarters of all Hispanic students in the United States.

For more information on the conference, contact HACU at (210) 692-0823 or visit the Web site (www.hacu.net).
