Faculty prove ASU's research prowess with continuing strength in Guggenheim Competition

For the third year in a row, Arizona State University faculty have won multiple John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation Fellowships - a prestigious academic award that ranks as one of the highest honors in the humanities, social sciences and natural sciences.

In 2003, ASU won three of the 184 Guggenheim Fellowships awarded, a number that generally only the elite, private universities surpassed - Princeton, University of Chicago, Yale (each winning five) and Columbia and Harvard (each winning four) and ASU's three-year total of eight fellowships is larger than similar totals for some of these. The university's strong track record in winning the award is evidence of a strong senior research faculty, since the Guggenheim Fellowship is generally given to established scholars and researchers "who have achieved prominence in their fields."

"Our recent record in winning these important awards is a testament to our past track record in recruiting strong researchers to the faculty," said David Young, dean of ASU's College of Liberal Arts and Sciences. "As the university's research enterprise continues to move forward, I expect that we will see more and more of this kind of national recognition."

Winning 2002 Guggenheim Fellowships were Professor of History Monica Green, Professor of English, German and Theatre Gitta Honegger, and Professor of Microbiology Kenneth Mossman.

Previously, the Guggenheim Fellowship had been awarded 11 other members of the ASU faculty: Regents Professor of Chemistry Sheng H. Lin in 1971, Regents Professor of English Norman Dubie in 1977, Regents Professor of English Alberto Rios in 1988, Regents Professor of Geography William Graf in 1993, Professor of Geography Ronald Dorn in 1997 and Regents Professor of History Peter Iverson in 1999. Winning 2001 Guggenheim Fellowships were William Fagan, assistant professor of biology; Beverly McIver, assistant professor of painting and drawing and Asuncion Lavrin, professor of history. Winning 2002 Guggenheim Fellowships were Professor of Religious Studies Anne Feldhaus, and Professor of Biology Richard Satterlie.

The Guggenheim Fellowship is perhaps the best known national award given to support scholarship in the humanities, social sciences and natural sciences. The fellowship, which is awarded to scholars, artists and scientists, typically supports release time for work on a major project of importance in the field involved.

Green, a medieval historian who specializes in the history of medicine, won the fellowship to study "Medicine and Culture in 12th Century Salerno."

In the 11th and 12th Centuries, Salerno, a city in southern Italy in the Kingdom of Sicily, was home to the School of Salerno, Western Europe's first real medical school. Then ruled by the Normans, Sicily had recently been Muslim, Green explains. Europe's intellectual life had largely faded during the early Middle Ages, as Greek, the intellectual language of antiquity and of many of the great ancient texts, had disappeared from common usage. Across the Mediterranean, Muslim culture was far more sophisticated. Salerno was a multi-cultural place where superior Arab learning got a foothold in Western Europe.

In the late 11th Century, medical knowledge in particular got a big boost in Southern Italy with the arrival of Constantine the African, a scholar from North Africa with an interest in medical learning.

"He probably came from Tunisia," said Green. "The story is that he realized what a dearth of medical texts they had in Italy, and he started translating all these Arabic medical texts into Latin. This transformed Western medicine - there's a lot of new information about different kinds of drugs, but even more important is the philosophical structure behind the learning."

Green points out that the introduction of this sophisticated view of health and the human body was responsible for forging the fundamental institutions of Western learning. "When universities develop in the13th Century, what happens is that medicine, because of this influx of philosophical medicine, is able to take its place with theology and law in the higher faculties of the institutions," she said. "It could claim this prestige because medical scholars could say 'we're as philosophical as you are - we can do the same kinds of intellectual analysis.'"

Green's Guggenheim project will particularly focus on The School of Salerno, the European starting point for the new Arab-inspired medical science. "What they able to do was to establish a curriculum for medicine - a set of books to be studied, much of it from Arabic. This remained intact until the 16th Century. It became the curriculum at Paris and at Oxford and at Montpellier - at all the medical schools," she said.

Though Salerno was crucial for the development of Western medicine and learning, very little historical study has been devoted to it. "There's only a local history that was written in the middle of the19th Century," she said. "If you ask any medical historian they'll tell you that it was very important to the history of medicine, but that's all they can say. It's an important institution that needs to be better studied.

"What has always fascinated me both about Constantine and about Salerno is the way in which the Kingdom of Sicily has seemed so open to Arabic influences . What I'm really looking at is a multicultural society and the ways in which something very distinctive can happen in a multicultural society that won't happen elsewhere," she said.

Honegger is both a professor of theatre and a literary scholar and has interests in theater, performance, German and comparative literature, critical theory, and women's studies. Her fellowship project is entitled "A Biography of Helene Weigel."

Honegger's project will take her to Berlin to research and write the life story of Weigel, a prominent German actress and theatre director whose history has been somewhat eclipsed by the fact that she was the wife of the famous playwright Bertolt Brecht.

"She's one of the most fascinating people in 20th Century drama," said Honegger. "Weigel actually ran the theater - the famous Berlin Ensemble - from 1949 to 1971. What began to interest me is how do you write the biography of someone who is mainly an actor -- because acting is so passing -- and how can I write her story from her viewpoint and not always make her just the wife of Brecht."

Honegger is also interested in the scope of 20th Century history that Weigel's life encompasses, and how, in turn, that history affected her as an artist. Weigel was born in 1900 in Vienna to Jewish parents, and spent her childhood in anti-Semitic pre-war Austria. Though she came from a bourgeois background, she became a fervent communist and ran away to become an actor. Her travels as an artist took her to the Weimar Republic, then to Hollywood, then back to East Germany, following the war.

"Weigel's was a fascinating life that spans almost a century -- almost until when the wall came down," she said. "I want to find out what was going on during those years. So this biography is politics, theatre, cultural studies and it's an ethical question - how do you run a theatre in very difficult times? Her life helps us understand the century - the whole spectacle of the tragedy of the 20th Century is basically there."

Mossman is a professor of health physics in ASU's department of microbiology and also director of ASU's office of radiation safety. He is a specialist in the area of radiological health and safety, with interests as well in risk management.

Mossman's fellowship is to study "Risk Dimensions and Precaution." His proposed project is to study "the substantial uncertainty that exists concerning the applicability and requirements of the precautionary principle to the management of technological risks."

Mossman will study what elements of risk are important for effective risk management decision-making by analyzing a variety of activities and products with different "risk profiles." His research has implications for a broad range of risk issues, including such diverse activities as biomedical research, use of cellular telephones, and genetically modified foods.
