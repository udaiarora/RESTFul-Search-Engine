What's in a name?
McClintock Hall memorializes former student

Students who live in McClintock Hall, like most students at ASU, live busy lives - some juggling a full load of classes and working every day.

But some believe their hall's namesake was the busiest student at ASU. In addition to studying for his classes during his last year at the Territorial Normal School, James Harvey McClintock owned a large farm near Tempe, served as Tempe's justice of the peace, published a newspaper - the Tempe News - and helped supervise county road building.

McClintock, a member of the first graduating class of the Territorial Normal School in 1887, was founding president of the Territorial Normal School alumni association, taught school, was a newspaper editor, worked as a correspondent for the Los Angeles Times, served as Phoenix postmaster and was appointed Arizona state historian in 1919.

He was selected to be in the scouting party in the early 1900s that was searching for possible locations for dams as the Salt River Project was being formed.
McClintock even found the time to write a three-volume history of his adopted state, "Arizona - The Youngest State."

McClintock, born Feb. 2, 1864 in Sacramento, Calif., began his hard-working ways as a young man. At the age of 15, he moved to Arizona and worked as a carnival roustabout - a worker who put up and took down the tents and cared for the animals.

Then he became a printer's devil (apprentice) for his brother Charles, who was publisher of the Salt River Herald, getting the first injection of printer's ink in his blood.

History doesn't record why, but McClintock, who later would become Colonel of the First Arizona Infantry, was asked to help recruit young Arizona men to fight in the 1898 Spanish-American War. He went to combat, landing in Cuba's Santiago Harbor as captain of the B Troop in the Roosevelt Rough Riders. He came back from the brief war with leg wounds that would bother him the rest of his life.
He died in California May 10, 1934.

He was memorialized with McClintock Hall when it opened in 1951. At the time it was a dormitory for women. Today, the quadrangle building houses 168 undergraduate students, both men and women.
