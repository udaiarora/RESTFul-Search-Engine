September 9, 2005

Arizona Technology Enterprises helps protect intellectual property

Arizona Technology Enterprises at ASU (AzTE) has implemented a new review process designed to help the university’s inventors and researchers manage and protect their intellectual property.

In its role as the technology commercialization arm for ASU, AzTE provides faculty members with thorough investigations of all invention disclosures, which are the lifeblood of the university’s technology commercialization efforts. This service protects those ideas that have the greatest chance of commercial success.

The new intellectual property review process provides more information on the status of discoveries, greater visibility and participation in the intellectual property decision-making process, and more services to assist faculty in their technology commercialization efforts.

“The transparency of information and open communication that the new process promotes helps us to build a strong partnership with faculty to jointly make better intellectual property investment decisions,” says Peter Slate, chief executive officer of AzTE.

The processing of a potential invention, which can take up to two weeks, begins with the completion of an invention and discovery disclosure form that is submitted to AzTE. An AzTE representative then contacts the researcher to give an overview of the review process, list key dates in the process and answer questions.

The next step is a technology evaluation, which can take up to 60 days to complete. The researcher is asked to become part of the intellectual property review team that evaluates the invention in several categories, including market size and growth potential, existing competitive presences, market risks and hurdles, and technical merit. Industry experts and patent counsel participate in the evaluation of the invention to provide a thorough view of market needs.

All information obtained during the course of this evaluation process is reviewed at a scheduled monthly review meeting. A decision then is reached at the meeting on whether to file a provisional patent on the invention.

If the decision is made to proceed with a patent filing, a provisional patent application is filed concurrently with the preparation of materials used to market the invention. The researcher will be an important part of the technology marketing team. After about 10 months, a second decision is made to determine whether to continue pursuing the patent. This decision is made based on market input received while marketing the invention.

AzTE officials hope this new process will help ASU inventors realize the greatest chance of success for their discoveries and enhance the university’s opportunity for commercialization.

For questions regarding the review process or other invention disclosure questions, contact AzTE at (480) 965-5670. Forms and associated materials can be found at (www.azte.com).
