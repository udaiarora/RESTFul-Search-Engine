January 31, 2003

Western states facing budget crises

The number of states with budget problems - as well as the scope of these problems - has been growing in recent months, according to an analysis in the January/February issue of Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast.

Exacerbating these budget headaches is slow economic growth coupled with lagging employment gains, according to Dawn McLaren, analyst with the Bank One Economic Outlook Center. "Added to the mix is disappointing news as the president's economic stimulus plan excluded any mention of action that would help the states with their budget crises," McLaren said. "The benefits of any federal stimulus package may be offset by layoffs, budget cuts and increased taxes at the state level."

Thirty-one states reported declining revenues during 2002 and, of the states showing a positive change, at least nine enacted changes in the tax code to produce a net increase in revenues. Twenty-six states used their budget stabilization ("rainy day") funds to reduce budget gaps for the 2002 fiscal year, and 12 plan to use them during 2003. Over the last year, 24 states have enacted changes to their tax structures for the 2003 fiscal year which are expected to increase revenues, according to the November 2002 Fiscal Survey of the States done by the National Governors Association.

"The Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast panel sees a generally brighter picture for 2003 than last year," McLaren said, "but the problem of state government finances won't be solved quickly - even amid an improving economy."

Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast publishes the consensus forecasts of panels of economists in 10 Western states. Panelists are drawn from leading firms, universities and state agencies. The Western Blue Chip also publishes historical data for each state.

The Western Blue Chip is published 10 times a year by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the Seidman Research Institute in the Arizona State University College of Business. The cost is $110 for 10 print issues ($99 for e-mail subscription). For information, call (800) 448-0432.
