Hobby looms large for researcher Chapuis

By day he examines cells under a microscope. By night - and on weekends - his attention is focused on fibers.

Charles Chapuis isn't a workaholic forensic pathologist, but a scientist who weaves.
Chapuis, an associate research professional in the Cancer Research Institute, studies cancer cell biology. He is devoted to discovering new anticancer drugs that may become, ultimately, treatments for cancer patients.

He hadn't considered weaving until he met a young woman in his native Switzerland who had just started taking classes in the ancient art.

"I knew what I wanted to do for a career - I wanted to be a biologist," Chapuis said, "but I thought I needed a new hobby."

Chapuis was drawn to weaving both by Chantal, the young woman, who now is his wife, and the beauty of the loom.

"I love wood, and most looms are made of wood," he said.

The Chapuises now own three looms, and in fact, purchased their current home to accommodate their biggest one. "It's huge, nearly a professional one," he said. "We also have a spinning wheel."

And, they have shelves full of fibers - rich colors in silk, wool, cotton and linen.
"There are so many colors, fibers and patterns," Chapuis said.

Chapuis and his wife, who have been weaving for 15 years, belong to a local weaving guild and continue to learn from the other weavers. And, they both teach at the Fiber Factory in Mesa.

Chapuis finds teaching the most rewarding part of weaving, he said.

"In my latest class, the students range in age from 7 to 85 years." He said. "Some people have wanted to weave for many years, and some take the class because they've just retired. The classes bring the generations together."

Setting up the loom is the most time-consuming part of weaving, according to Chapuis, so he tries to plan two or three projects that can be made with the same warp.

There's no family competition over who's going to sit down at the big loom and weave, since "one weaver should work on one project," Chapuis said. "The fabric is created by inserting a second thread in place using a shuttle. The thread is then set in place with a beater," he explained.

"A good weaver will beat with an even pressure all along his project and get an even pattern. No two weavers will beat exactly the same, so they would have to measure constantly, or the transition from person to person would be visible."

Chapuis has no plans to quit his "day job" to concentrate on the loom, and weaving is simply a shared interest for the couple. "It's not an inexpensive hobby," Chapuis said. "It would be quite hard to make a living at it."

Most of their projects, such as a lace-like baby blanket trimmed with pink ribbon, are destined to be gifts for friends.

"We just enjoy doing it," Chapuis said.
