Dennis Durband

April 26, 2002


GRADUATE COLLEGE AWARDS BEAKLEY MILLENNIUM FELLOWSHIPS TO BERG, EDMONDS

Arizona State University doctoral students Jennifer Edmonds and Michael Berg are the recipients of the Dr. George C. Beakley, Jr. Millennium Interdisciplinary Dissertation Fellowship, awarded annually by the Graduate College. The Millennium Fellowship Program funds students who are involved in cutting edge interdisciplinary research. Edmonds and Berg will each receive $15,000 to support their work during the dissertation year.
 
Jennifer Edmonds
  
Michael Berg

Edmonds is a biology student and senior fellow in the Integrated Graduate Education Research Traineeship (IGERT) program on urban ecology. Her dissertation title is, "The Influence of Dissolved Organic Carbon Quality on Microbial Activity in Desert Lotic Ecosystems." Nancy Grimm, professor of biology, serves as her adviser. Berg is a student in the Molecular and Cellular Biology Program. His dissertation title is, "The Role of Colanic Acid in Escherichia coli Biofilm Development." Valerie Stout, associate professor of microbiology, and Anneta Razatos, assistant professor in chemical and materials engineering, serve as Berg's mentors.

Berg's studies of bacterial biofilm hold substantial importance for biomedicine. An estimated 65 percent of human bacterial infections involve biofilm, which is a specialized microbial community that forms on soft tissue and inert surfaces in living organisms. Urinary tract infections (UTIs) are the second most commonly acquired infection, and the incidence increases 5–10 percent each day in patients with indwelling catheters. Biofilm can block the flow of urine from the bladder, and contamination of implanted biomaterials, such as catheters, is an important area of research, especially because of the growing resistance of bacteria to antibiotics.

Berg's interdisciplinary approaches to the study include biophysical, physiological, biochemical and molecular biological technologies designed to assess the development of biofilms and the potential interference with biofilm formation. He is researching how biofilms develop in a mechanical sense and whether there are materials that could be used in the manufacture of medical devices that would be less prone to serve as substrates for biofilm formation. If methods can be developed that will prevent or impede biofilm formation, that development will have enormous potential both in its humanitarian aspect and in its possibility for commercial development.

Professor Stout said, "Mike has been working on understanding the mechanisms by which Escherichia coli is able to cause urinary tract infections. This is a new and potentially far-reaching area of research that could result in novel treatments of normal UTIs as well as those caused by catheterization of patients. His collaboration with Anneta Razatos from the Department of Chemical and Materials Engineering is a key to his research. He is not only learning how to manipulate the bacterial strains through both traditional and molecular bacterial genetics techniques in my lab, but he is also learning how to engineer surfaces and measure the interactions of bacteria with those surfaces."

According to Razatos, "Berg's project is unique because it focuses on both biological and non-biological factors involved in surface-associated infections. His research may possibly lead to the development of materials resistant to bacterial infection."

James Collins, chair in the Department of Biology, said, "Jennifer Edmonds exemplifies a new generation of ecologists who recognize the need to understand human-dominated ecological systems, and who act on that recognition by seeking collaborations with others. Her research project requires that she make regular field trips to collect water samples, analyze these samples using a series of technically difficult techniques, and devote long hours to analyze the data."

Edmonds contributed to the creation of a nitrogen budget for the City of Phoenix, which involved quantifying the inputs and outputs of nitrogen to and from the city. Nitrogen is an important nutrient for plant growth, but is a human health hazard at high levels. With her co-authors, she determined that the city retains approximately 20 percent of the nitrogen flowing through its boundaries and suggested changes in agricultural practices that might reduce unnecessary nitrogen accumulation.

Edmonds has collaborated on her research with students in the disciplines of geography, geology, plant biology, sociology, anthropology and urban planning. She monitors the quantity and quality of dissolved organic matter delivery to a native desert and urban stream ecosystems, and then determines how dissolved organic matter from different sources in the watershed influences bacterial growth. "My research focuses on the activity of non-pathenogenic bacterial communities living in stream sediment biofilms," she said. "Biofilms on stream sediments contain the bulk of all bacteria in the system, and because bacteria are important for the breakdown of dead plant and animal material, these biofilms are of interest to ecologists. Bacteria are essential for the cycling of plant nutrients. My research focuses on how the quality of the organic matter influences the ability of the microorganisms to break down the matter and release nutrients back into the ecosystem."

Westerhoff said, "The proposed research by Jennifer will improve our understanding of the unique fluxes of organic carbon in arid systems and along urban flowpaths. This understanding is critical as populations in these areas grow and demand both a safe supply of drinking water and amble environmental protection. Better understanding the sources and chemical nature of organic carbon will reduce health risks and lower operating costs."

Grimm said that Edmonds has undertaken an ambitious research project, one that integrates knowledge from environmental engineering, biogeochemistry, microbial ecology and ecosystem ecology. "She has also been a model graduate student for interdisciplinary scholarship. She has absolutely no fear of methods that are new to her, and often will have them worked out within record time. Jennifer shows tremendous promise as a scientist."

The late George C. Beakley Jr. was a professor in ASU's College of Engineering from 1956 until his retirement in 1992. Mr. Beakley died in 1998. His son, Bill Beakley, and his family established the fellowship to "continue on with Dad's greatest love of helping others with the gift of teaching and education."
