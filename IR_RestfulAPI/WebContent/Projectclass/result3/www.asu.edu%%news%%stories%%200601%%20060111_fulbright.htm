January 11, 2006

ASU hosts three faculty Fulbright Scholars

Long regarded as the world’s premiere academic exchange program, the U.S. Fulbright Scholar program attracts exceptional faculty and researchers from more than 150 countries worldwide. ASU is fortunate to host three outstanding faculty members from abroad who have received grants to teach and do research at ASU this year.

Ronald Smith, writer, publisher and founder of Oolichan Books in Canada, recently took up the inaugural ASU Chair in Creative Writing at the Virginia G. Piper Center for Creative Writing. He hopes to nurture new writers and to explore with them developments in Canadian literature over the past 40 years.

Smith will give a free public reading of his work at 7 p.m., Jan. 26, at the Piper Writers House.

Jose Carretero-Gomez, assistant professor of management at the University of Illes Balears in Spain, is at ASU developing a research project on the acceptance by managers of human resource management innovations. He is working closely with ASU management professors Luis Gomez-Mejia and Elizabeth Cabrera, trying to determine the factors that determine practitioners’ acceptance of innovative managerial tools.

Max Schachner is a tourism expert, as head of practical training and overseas programs at the IMC University of Applied Sciences in Krems, Austria. He is collaborating with community resources professor Victor Teye on an Austria-Holland summer school program for ASU tourism students, and he is lecturing on international tourism and the European convention industry. Teye also teaches in Krems. Both scholars have started comparative research on tourism students’ motivations and career perspectives on both sides of the Atlantic.

During his stay at the Piper Center, Smith says he hopes to share with his students the cultural diversity of literature written by new Canadians. He also hopes to offer a comparative study of short fiction and poetry from both sides of the border, exploring these works geographically and aesthetically.

“Ron Smith’s residency is the inaugural event in the Piper Center’s five-year commitment to showcase North American literature and strengthen bonds between North American literary communities,” says Jaime Dempsey, program manager at the Piper Center. “We are thrilled to have the opportunity to host Smith, as he is an enormously accomplished writer, publisher and educator. Having hosted him for several weeks now, we also know him to be a generous and worthy ambassador of his country and literary community.”

Smith was designated the first Honorary Research Associate of the Arts and First Nations Faculty at Malaspina University College, where he taught literature and creative writing until 1998. As an editor of more than 200 books, Smith has played an essential role in the growth of literary, historical and public policy publishing in British Columbia. In addition, he is the author of six books and has received four Canada Council Grants – and three BC Arts Council Awards – for his writing. He is working on a number of plays, as well as a novel and a new collection of poems.
