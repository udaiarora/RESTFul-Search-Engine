August 8, 2005

State’s top students get early boost toward success

One hundred of Arizona’s brightest middle-school students are participating in a challenging educational program this summer at ASU’s Tempe campus.

They have joined their peers from other states and countries, including Virginia, California, Washington, D.C, Washington, Maryland, Oregon, Connecticut, Idaho, New York, Texas, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Michigan, Hong Kong SAR, Thailand and Japan, who also are participating in the ASU program.

The students, who arrived on campus July 17 for the three-week residential experience that concluded Aug. 5, are part of the Center for Talented Youth (CTY) program run by Johns Hopkins University. More than half of the students in the program at ASU are attending on scholarship, thanks to the generosity of numerous individuals and businesses.

“What makes our program unique is our primary effort to recruit students from many traditionally underserved populations,” says Nina Rhodes, CTY outreach coordinator. “These scholarships make it possible for the best students, no matter what their socio-economic status, to participate.”

Barbara and Craig Barrett, for whom ASU’s Barrett Honors College is named, pledged 50 CTY-AZ scholarships, while 20 students are supported by ASU under President Michael Crow’s initiative.

Barbara Barrett had a chance to meet with some of the students on their first day of classes. For an hour she engaged them in discussions about their academic aspirations and goals. They talked about what inspired them and whom they most admired. Chandler Bator mentioned Winston Churchill and Margaret Thatcher. Yaeren Hernandez talked about her grandmother who, when widowed, raised 14 children by herself in a two-bedroom apartment in Mexico.

“She wasn’t famous, but she raised her kids the right way and steered them into adults,” Hernandez says. “That was a lot to take on – and if she can do something that hard, I can do anything.”

Barrett said the CTY-AZ program was something she wanted to support because it “allows high-achieving students to form a community and learn from each other, and challenges and motivates them for future leadership roles and academic achievement. This experience will change the lives of these students.”

For more than 20 years, CTY has identified America’s top academic students in grades two through eight and provided them with the opportunity for intensive study at colleges and universities throughout the nation.

The students participating in the program have scored at or above the 97th percentile on standardized tests such as the SAT, PSAT and ACT.

During the program students are immersed in a subject of their choosing. Courses range from languages, history, writing and the arts, to mathematics, science, ethics and philosophy. All courses are taught above the grade level and place emphasis on active learning and putting knowledge to use in independent and creative ways.

In addition to classroom work, the students participate in activities ranging from sports, to arts and crafts and music, and take part in field trips.
