Herberger College partnerships impact numerous schools and youth organizations

Integral to the mission and activities of the Katherine K. Herberger College of Fine Arts, the college's community programs office, together with the academic units, participates in more than 90 community partnerships that provide arts experiences for children and adults in the Phoenix metropolitan area. More than a dozen ongoing, multi-faceted, "signature" programs bring fine arts experiences to the community and provide a framework for arts education and the exchange of fine arts resources and expertise.

Two of the college's "signature" programs that emphasize K-12 partnerships are the well-established Dance Arizona Repertory Theatre (DART) and the new Maestra! Maestro! These are tailored to fit the needs of each particular partner, says director of community programs for the Herberger College, Melanie Ohm.

Dance Arizona Repertory Theatre (DART) is the community partnership dance company in the Herberger College Department of Dance. It builds collaborative and creative relationships with after-school programs that strengthen community ties between ASU and culturally diverse area schools. The community partners participating in DART are the inner-city Herrera School for the Arts, the Pappas School for Children of Homeless Families, and the Boys & Girls Club of Gilbert.

Mark Yslas, principal at the Herrera School for the Arts, says that the school's DART partnership is an integral part of its success.

"Partnerships with arts organizations such as DART have helped us become a first rate school," Yslas says. "It is a relationship that I hope continues for years to come."

DART involves students and faculty of ASU Dance joining with national and local guest artists to work with schools and organizations, creating opportunities for youth to discover their creative voices and experience the joy of dance. Dance provides them with a new mechanism for self-expression and creative exploration.

After participating in a DART program, renowned dancer and choreographer Arthur Aviles said, "This partnership has helped make dance accessible - the way dance should be."

Through collaborative dance workshops and performance opportunities, youth participants learn leadership and problem-solving skills while developing a sense of teamwork. ASU students obtain training in community dance methodologies.

The DART collaboration with the Boys & Girls Club has been "tremendous," according to Vic Cordts, Youth Development Director for Boys & Girls Clubs of the East Valley.
"The opportunity for our members to interact and spend quality time with the young adults who instruct the program contributes significantly to their interpersonal and social development," Cordts says.

Among the college's newer partnership programs is Maestra!Maestro!, a program now in its second year, whose aim is to promote the growth of after-school arts programming in the Greater Phoenix area.

This partnership trains Herberger College arts students as community teaching artists by placing them with teaching artists already active in the community. The student apprentices and their mentor teachers are then paired with after-school programs in areas where access to arts education is limited. This is done through a second level of partnerships with the Phoenix Arts Commission and the City of Phoenix's Parks and Recreation Department.

The teaching artist and student apprentice spend two afternoons a week teaching the arts to children in the after-school programs. The concept also is being expanded to pair academic tutors with the artists and their apprentices as the program expands to new locations and partner programs in its second year. Tutors and artists will work together to address the same educational concepts in their programs, tying the art lessons to the academic subjects being taught in the after-school programs.
Such change and development of the community partnership programs offered by the Herberger College is constant, says Ohm.

"Our programs are always in flux, always morphing into new 'pilot programs' every time we start working with a new partner," Ohm says. "That's the nature of partnerships. Our programs never stay the same because we work together to meet the specific needs of each community partner."
