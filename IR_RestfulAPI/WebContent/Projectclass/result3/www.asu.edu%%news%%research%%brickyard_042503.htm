April 25, 2003

ASU creates new computer, information sciences institute

ASU has created the Institute for Computer and Information Sciences and Engineering (ICISE) to centralize the university's key academic and research programs in computer science, and to help build its computer science department to world-class stature.

The new institute will be housed in leased space at the Brickyard complex on Mill Avenue in Tempe. The move to the Brickyard will give the university an additional 130,000 square feet of academic and research space for its computer science & engineering department (CSE) and provide the facilities ASU needs to create a top-ranked program.

"ASU intends to build a host of worldclass academic and research programs as we pursue our goal of creating the new American University," said ASU President Michael Crow. "The Institute for Computer and Information Sciences and Engineering is our newest initiative, joining others including the Arizona Biodesign Institute and the Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict."

The creation of the institute and approval of the lease was on the agenda for the Arizona Board of Regents meeting April 24 in Tucson.

Peter Crouch, dean of the College of Engineering & Applied Sciences (CEAS), said, "Establishing ICISE and moving the department of computer science & engineering to the Brickyard are very exciting developments, made possible by the shared vision of President Crow and the faculty in the College of Engineering & Applied Sciences for a top 25 School of Engineering.

"Our presence in downtown Tempe reinforces the college's commitment to strengthening its relationship with the local community and the broader industrial community in Arizona," Crouch said.

Creation of the institute at the Brickyard location accomplishes several immediate objectives:

. It centralizes the CSE department, with 45 faculty members and approximately 1,850 undergraduate and graduate students, in one location with all of its classes, labs and related activities. Classes and offices for the department are now spread throughout several engineering buildings on the Main Campus, which limits many projects.
. The institute and its new facilities will help attract top computer science faculty to ASU.
. Filling the vacant Brickyard office space with ASU faculty and students will benefit the retail establishments in downtown Tempe.

The Aug. 1 move of ICISE to the Brickyard, is one of several initiatives ASU has launched to solve its critical and immediate need for high-quality academic and research space. ASU will officially break ground April 29 on a $69-million Phase 1 facility for the Arizona Biodesign Institute (AzBio) at the corner of Terrace and McAllister. Phases 2, 3, and 4 of AzBio will be built if a legislative bill authorizing an additional $400 million for research facilities at Arizona's three state universities is approved. The bill, which was submitted to the state legislature April 16, will provide ASU with $185 million to complete construction of all phases of the Arizona Biodesign Institute, as well as a facility for the university's new School of Life Sciences and research facilities at ASU East, ASU West and ASU's Downtown facility near the TGen site.

ICISE, the primary tenant of the Brickyard, will be developed with a model similar to that of AzBio, with numerous core research programs, working together with a common goal, in this case to foster interdisciplinary research, education and entrepreneurship in computing.

"A strong computer science department is critically important if we are to help train a technology-savvy workforce, which we have committed to doing in return for our Proposition 301 funding," said Jonathan Fink, ASU's vice president for Research & Economic Affairs.

At the core of the institute, which will be a part of the CEAS, is the strong academic program of the CSE department. The department and the institute will be led by Computer Science Professor Sethuraman Panchanathan, who is currently the interim chairman of the department.

"The department has really been strapped for space," said Panchanathan. "This will provide the leverage and catalyst we need to grow. Our department is already strong, but this will strengthen us tremendously. We are extremely excited about this opportunity to elevate the prestige and ranking of the department and the college."

Along with CSE, the institute will include the Consortium for Embedded and Inter-Networking Technologies (CEINT), the Partnership for Research In Stereo Modeling (PRISM), the Research Center for Ubiquitous Computing (CUbiC), Information Integration, Information Assurance, Bioinformatics, and the ASU Software Factory.

Panchanathan says the structure of the institute will allow for additional collaboration with other computer-related centers on campus. Additional efforts will be aimed at encouraging collaboration with individual researchers throughout ASU in a variety of disciplines with either projects related to or interest in computer sciences.

ASU Executive Vice President for Administration & Finance Mernoy Harrison said the lease of the Brickyard makes sense for the university on a variety of levels, the biggest of which is a need for space.

"We need space and we need it fast," said Harrison. "By acquiring the Brickyard, we get high quality space 1 1/2 years faster and for less than if we had to build it."

The terms of the lease call for 130,000 square feet in the Brickyard, Orchid House and Artisan Courts at $23 per square foot, over a five-year term, with two additional five-year options, a tenant improvement allowance of $1.9 million, 3 months of free rent and free parking for the first year.

ASU will use the tenant improvement allowance to offset much of the first year lease cost. Tenant improvements of $7 million and related costs will be funded from previously issued certificates of participation for a project that has been postponed. The debt service for the tenant improvements in FY 2004 will be approximately $350,000 and funded from funds already included in the ASU operating budget. Lease expenses in subsequent fiscal years will be covered by a combination of tuition, local funds and indirect cost recovery.
