443. EISENHOWER, DWIGHT. MINUTES AND DOCUMENTS OF THE CABINET MEETINGS (1953-1961).  CALL NUMBER: University of Arizona Microfilm 2940  NUMBER OF REELS/FICHE: 10 R  INDEX OR GUIDE: Minutes and Documents. Microfilm Guide 2940-2941  SCOPE AND CONTENTS: No recent President has used his cabinet more fully than Dwight Eisenhower, who relied on it as a vital component of his policy-making machinery. Agenda, minutes, cabinet reports, records of action and memoranda to the President offer a documentary basis for understanding and evaluating Eisenhower’s political leadership. The reports are often detailed enough to enable the researcher to follow major areas of administrative concern. Some of these areas are: the domestic economy (employment statistics, cost of living surveys and other economic indicators mark the onset of the so-called “Eisenhower recession”; civil defense (consideration of potential nuclear war with Soviet Union); atomic testing; social security, health and welfare matters; congressional relations; immigration; labor relations and strikes.  SUBJECTS: Eisenhower, Dwight; Cabinet Meetings; United States – Presidents; Politics

444. EISENHOWER, DWIGHT. DIARIES, 1953-1961.  CALL NUMBER: Northern Arizona University (uncataloged Backlog).  NUMBER OF REELS/FICHE: 28 R  INDEX OR GUIDE: Printed guide.  SCOPE AND CONTENTS: Film editioin of the diaries located at the Eisenhower Library in Abilene, Kansas. Consists of more than 28,000 pages of material, arranged chronologically. The diaries and supporting documents shed light on virtually every issue that concerned Eisenhower during his presidency and reveal much about his political philosophy and his private vieews on subjects such as McCarthyism, Indochina, civil rights and disarmament. Also includes dictated correspondence; memos of telephone conversations with officials such as John Foster Dulles, Walter Bedel Smith, Herbert Brownell and George Humphrey; memoranda of conversations in the White House; summaries of activities of executive branch agencies (prepared daily); and intelligence briefings dealing with CIA and State Department information on international affairs.  SUBJECTS: Eisenhower, Dwight D., 1950s; 1960s; 20c; United States – Foreign Affairs; Presidents; Politics

445. FBI FILE ON THE COMMITTEE FOR PUBLIC JUSTICE.  CALL NUMBER: Microfilm 8802  NUMBER OF REELS/FICHE: 1 R  INDEX OR GUIDE:  SCOPE AND CONTENTS: The Committee for Public Justice (CPJ), a self-supporting affiliate of the American Civil Liberties Union, was formed in 1970 by a group of prominent liberals and activists to voice concern over the political repression they perceived the United States to be entering. One of their targets was the FBI, the subject of a conference sponsored by the committee at Princeton University in 1971. After the conference, J. Edgar Hoover started the surveillance of the group’s activities. The file continues after Hoover’s death in 1971, when, beginning a trend toward increasing openness within the Bureau to criticism andn accountability, Directors L. Patrick Gray and Clarence M. Kelley met with CPJ members to discuss their concerns and recommendations.  SUBJECTS: FBI; Committee for Public Justice; 1970s; American Civil Liberties Union; United States – Civil Rights

450. JOHNSON, LYNDON. POLITICAL ACTIVITIES OF THE JOHNSON WHITE HOUSE, 1963-1969.  CALL NUMBER: Northern Arizona University (uncataloged Backlog).  NUMBER OF REELS/FICHE: 67 R  INDEX OR GUIDE: Printed guide for both Parts I and II.  SCOPE AND CONTENTS: PART I: White House Central and Confidential Files. These groups include internal memoranda, reports and working papers circulated between the President and his advisers, as well as important correspondence with key individuals and groups outside the government. These files contain information on national state and local politics and include the following: public opinion polls, Democratic Party organization, fund raising, operation of state parties, elections and candidates. Part II: White House Aides consists of the office files of Marvin Watson, Special Assistant to the President and Administrative Chief of Staff of the White House, 1965-1968. These files serve as a virtual textbook on the planning and implementation of a presidential campaign. They also contain reports and memoranda on the political situation from many of Johnson’s top aides. Because it was not intended for wider circulation, this material contains frank and incisive commentaries on the political state of the nation.  SUBJECTS: Johnson, Lyndon; United States – Presidents; Politics; 1960s

454. MAP ROOM MESSAGES OF PROSIDENT TRUMAN, 1945-1946.  CALL NUMBER: University of Arizona Microfilm 6422  NUMBER OF REELS/FICHE: 5 R  INDEX OR GUIDE: Map Room Messages. Microfilm Guide 6422  SCOPE AND CONTENTS: Reproduces the key files from the White House Map Room, which was the central station for most classified correspondence during Truman’s first two years. Contains correspondence with such leaders as Chiang Kai-shek, Winston Churchill, Charles de Gaulle, Joseph Stalin, Dwight Eisenhower and Douglas MacArthur. These heretofore top-secret materials present an inside look at American wartime strategy and postwar diplomacy. The collection’s primary research value lies in the postwar communications, which clearly document the crucial departures then leading to the Cold War. Other highlights: Palestine issue; George Marshall’s special mission to China; attempt to maintain atomic energy secrets.  SUBJECTS: Truman, Harry; United States – Foreign Policy; Presidents; 1940s; 20x; Soviet Union; China, Palestine; Great Britain; France

455. MAP ROOM MESSAGES OF PRESIDENT ROOSEVELT, 1939-1945.  CALL NUMBER: University of Arizona Microfilm 6434  NUMBER OF REELS/FICHE: 9 R  INDEX OR GUIDE: Map Room Messages. Microfilm Guide 6434  SCOPE AND CONTENTS: Reproduces the confidential messages from President Roosevelt’s wartime communications office at the White House. Included in these records are the files of correspondence with leading politicians, diplomats and military leaders. A major resource for the study of the presidency and of World War II, these materials consist largely of decoded transcripts of communications between Roosevelt and Winston Churchill, Joseph Stalin and Chiang Kai-shek. Includes lengthy Background papers and incisive interdepartmental memos that openly discuss major diplomatic issues between the Allied powers, as well as military operations. Selected highlights: concern over isolationism in America; flight of Rudolf Hess to Scotland; Stalin’s anger over delay in opening a second front; Casablanca Conference; D-Day; major battles preceding final assault on Berlin.  SUBJECTS: Roosevelt, Franklin; United States – Presidents; Politics; World War II; 1940s; 20c

468. U. S. BUREAU OF THE CENSUS. DECENNIAL CENSUS, 1790-1960. CALL NUMBER: Documents Microfilm 3748 Record Group 29 – T825  NUMBER OF REELS/FICHE:  INDEX OR GUIDE: Bureau of the Census. Catalog of Publications, 1790-1972. Documents Table 5B. C56.222/2.2. Contains description of each census. Has reel numbers in margins.  SCOPE AND CONTENTS: The printed Census Reports are particularly valuable for students who are studying patterns of immigration and emigration, westward expansion, regional history, occupational distribution and slavery. The value of these censuses increased as more informatiion was required from those listed. Between 1790 and 1840, only family heads were listed, with all others grouped statistically only by age groups, sex and race. Since 1850, all members of the family were listed, with the exception of slaves, who were designated in the 1860 census by number, age, and sex, next to the name of the owner. The 1870 census noted if one’s parents were of foreign birth. The 1880 census reveals the relationship of each person to the head of the family. Most of the 1890 population schedules were destroyed by fire.  SUBJECTS: United States – Census; 18c; 19c; 20c; Population; Slavery; Immigration; United States – Social History
