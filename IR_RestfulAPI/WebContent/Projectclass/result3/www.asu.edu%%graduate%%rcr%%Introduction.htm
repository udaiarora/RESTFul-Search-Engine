Introduction
As university research has expanded, becoming not only more complex and cooperative but responding to the needs of society, the issues of research integrity are even more salient. The research mission is founded on trust, trust with other researchers, trust of the funding agencies of research, and ultimately the trust of the public. To be worthy of trust, research must be conducted with the utmost integrity.

Responsible Conduct of Research (RCR) has a number of dimensions. RCR involves questions about the ethical use of human subjects in biomedical research, as well as issues that arise when using human subjects in social and behavioral research. Ethical use of animals in research raises different but still important concerns. Other topics include: responsible authorship in scientific publications; responsibilities to report scientific misconduct; scientists as citizens; conflict of interests; data management including data sharing and data secrecy; intellectual property and technology transfer; ethical, legal, and social implications of genetic research and technologies.

National Academy of Sciences, On Being a Scientist (1995)
“The scientific research enterprise, like other human activities, is built on a foundation of trust. Scientists trust that the results reported by others are valid. Society trusts that the results of the research reflect an honest attempt by scientists to describe the world accurately and without bias. The level of trust that has characterized science and its relationship with society has contributed to a period of unparalleled scientific productivity. But this trust will endure only if the scientific community devotes itself to exemplifying and transmitting the values associated with ethical scientific conduct.”
