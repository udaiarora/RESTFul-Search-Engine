December 9, 2003

Arizona Leading Index moves up in October

The Bank One Arizona Index of Leading Economic Indicators rose slightly in October to 122.6. This is 0.7 percent above September's revised index reading of 121.7, and 4.1 percent above the October 2002 number of 117.8. (1987 = 100).

"The good news is that evidence of a strengthening economy continues to grow," said Tracy Clark, associate director of the Bank One Economic Outlook Center at Arizona State University's W. P. Carey School of Business. "The bad news is that employment growth remains relatively weak."

In this most recent recession, the difference was that a large number of those who lost their jobs were not just laid off - their jobs disappeared permanently," Clark said. Arizona, which has fared better than most states in the struggle for job growth, is hoping for an improved picture next year. "Employment growth nationally should turn positive on a year-over-year basis early in 2004, while Arizona is likely to be experience a more normal pace of job growth by the middle of 2004."

"The most encouraging news from the Leading Index components is the fairly broad-based improvement that can be seen in business activity," Clark said. "The slowdown in business spending was the primary cause of the recession, and it hit Arizona disproportionately hard. Arizona should benefit on the upswing as businesses starts to spend. The indications nationally are positive, with a jump in business spending contributing significantly to the high rate of growth in the third quarter."

Leading Index components with positive numbers in October included sensitive materials prices, materials inventories, new orders, the inflation-adjusted value of Maricopa County residential building permits, and production. The inflation-adjusted value of the M2 money supply, employment from the Business Conditions Survey, and hours worked in manufacturing were negative. Delivery times were neutral.

The Bank One Arizona Index of Leading Economic Indicators is based on nine indicators, combined and weighted to project the pace of economic activity several months ahead. Five of the components come from a monthly survey of Arizona purchasing managers conducted by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute in the W. P. Carey School of Business at Arizona State University.
