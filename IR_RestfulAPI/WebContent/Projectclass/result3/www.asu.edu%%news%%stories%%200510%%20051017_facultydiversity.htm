October 18, 2005

New faculty reflect quality, diversity at ASU

ASU not only has attracted top-notch faculty in the past two years, it has gathered a diverse group of scholars who bring strength to the university through the breadth of their scholarship.

Twenty-nine percent of the 326 tenured and tenure-track hires in the past two years are minority faculty. This brings the overall proportion of minority faculty at ASU to a record 22 percent, the highest in the university’s history. In addition, 41 percent of the new hires are female.

ASU has a higher number of Hispanic faculty (129) than UCLA, and three times the number of American Indian faculty (26) as either UCLA or the University of Texas at Austin , both peer institutions that serve high minority populations.

"This has been a strategic effort to attract clusters of faculty so we can build our intellectual strength in key areas and simultaneously increase diversity,” says Marjorie Zatz, ASU’s vice provost. “The Southwest Borderlands Initiative has been a major element in drawing top Hispanic faculty to ASU, and we’re also working toward creating a Native American policy center.

"We’ve been experiencing rapid growth over the past two years, to meet the needs of our expanding student body and also to enable us to conduct high levels of research and creative activities. We’re attracting extraordinary faculty who are highly qualified – not just in the hard sciences, but in the social sciences, humanities and other areas as well.”

ASU faculty members include 20 members of the prestigious National Academy of Engineering, the National Academy of Sciences, the American Academy of Arts and Sciences and the Royal Society, and one Nobel laureate. Of these, eight were hired in the last two years.

During the same period, ASU hired 30 tenured or tenure-track Hispanic faculty, 11 American Indians, eight African-Americans and 43 Asian faculty. Overall, 326 faculty were hired, for a net increase of 125.

"Prospective faculty are excited by the opportunities they see here for transdisciplinary research, and for collaboration with other talented researchers and scholars,” Zatz says. “They’re excited by the momentum they see at ASU.”

Eddie Brown, director of American Indian Studies, says ASU has perhaps the largest number of American Indian faculty in the nation, representing many disciplines. It’s important for ASU’s growing American Indian student population to see themselves reflected in their professors – and for tribal communities to look to ASU for expertise.

"We’ve made a very determined push to increase the number of faculty and to recruit the very finest and most diverse scholars,” Zatz says. “We must meet the teaching and the research demands of the university as we grow. We also want to meet the expectations of our communities, to help find solutions to the most pressing societal demands locally, regionally, nationally and on a global scale.”
