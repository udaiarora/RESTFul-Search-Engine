January 27, 2004

Crow uses global platform to highlight ASU

ASU President Michael Crow took a slight detour on his way to China this month for a brief stop in Mexico to speak at the "Future of the Americas" conference in Monterrey, Mexico.

The conference preceded a special meeting of the Summit of the Americas, which brings together the heads of state and government of the Western Hemisphere to seek solutions and develop a shared vision for their future development of the region.

Crow was invited to the conference to share his vision of a New American University with a global audience of about 500 people. His speech focused on the similarities of the Americas and cited knowledge development and use as catalyst for regional integration.

"Knowledge knows no borders but, rather, is strengthened by diversity and competition," Crow said.

He provided an outlook of trends in science and technology, including a movement away from a silicon-based economy to a molecule-based economy. Crow also pointed out the advantage of collaboration and integration of disciplines in advancing science and technology. "We know the greatest excitement in research often occurs at the border of disciplines, where they interface with each other," he said.

Conference participants included other high-level academic, political and business figures from all over the Americas including Larry R. Faulkner, president of the University of Texas at Austin; Rodrigo Carazo, former president of Costa Rica; Thomas McLarty, former adviser to President Bill Clinton; Rafael Rangel, Tec de Monterrey president, and Jose Natividad Gonzalez Paras, governor of the Mexican state of Nuevo Leon.

From Mexico, Crow headed to China, where he spent five days in Beijing, Shanghai and Taipei, to develop relationships, discuss potential partnerships and meet a prospective donor.

He met with several education leaders, including the president of Inner Mongolia University and scientists from the Plant Biology Institute; the president of People's University, the president of Peking University; the Ministry of Education; the Ministry of Science and Technology; and Shanghai Municipal People's Government and agencies.

Crow also met for the first time with participants in ASU's Executive MBA program in Shanghai, which launched last September with a class of 65 CEO-level executives from some of China's largest state-owned enterprises and government officials.

Following his business in China, Crow returned to Monterrey, Mexico, where he met with the president of Tec de Monterrey, toured the university campus, and participated in work sessions on several topics, including biotechnology, distance education, technology transfer, and sustainability and architecture.

Crow returned to ASU in time to welcome students and faculty back for the start of spring semester on Jan. 21.
