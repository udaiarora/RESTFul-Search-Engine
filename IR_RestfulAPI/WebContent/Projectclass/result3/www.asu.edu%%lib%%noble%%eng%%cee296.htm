Starting your topic

If you are uncertain of where to begin, the selective list below is a good place to start because they provide a survey of Civil Engineering as a discipline and break it down into the different practices within it. Use sources like encyclopedias to get the history and scope of your subject.  Use the handbooks to get a more detailed breakdown of your subject and a survey of current techniques and practices.

Finding Books in the Online Catalog

The online catalog can be used to locate books, video and sound recordings, special collections materials, and government documents available in the ASU libraries. To access the catalog, choose "Catalog" from the main library web page.

The easiest way to start searching for materials on a particular topic in the catalog is to do a Keyword search. You can search for alternate endings by truncating with an asterisk (*). See examples below:

  • tunnels and construct*
  • clean and hazardous and waste

When you find the information in the catalog for a book you'd like to read, note the following information to locate the book on the shelf in the library:

  • Location: This will let you know which of the ASU libraries has the book and where it is located in that library.
  • Call #: This number indicates the book's exact location on the library shelf.
  • Status: This will indicate if the book is available to be checked out, if it can be used only within the library, or if the book has been checked out and when it is due back.

 

Getting books not available on the Tempe campus

You may find a book that is checked out of the Science Library, or another Tempe campus library, but is available at another campus library such as WEST or EAST. If this does happen you can request that it be sent to you.

 

Just click on the button, enter your name, ID and library PIN. Then follow the instructions on the next screens. Items requested this way usually arrive in two to three working days.

 

Finding journal articles

Finding journal articles is usually a two step process:

  1. search an index for citations to articles that match your research needs
    • note author name, article title, and journal name, volume and pages
  2. search the catalog to see which journals are owned by the library 
    or search  Electronic Journals to locate the article electronically

Constructing your search statement

As you begin to search for articles, you'll need to come up with a list of keywords. Background information obtained from reading books and reference sources can be effective sources for these keywords.

When you search for articles in indexes you may need to combine your keywords into one search statement. The following are common connectors:

Connector What it does Example
And Requires all terms to appear. Use to narrow or limit your search. tunnels AND construction
Or Either term may appear. Use to broaden your search or combine synonyms. design OR construction

You can use more than one connector to design a complex search statement. If you choose to use both an And and an Or in your search statement you'll need to enclose the terms being "or'd" in parentheses. See example below:

asphalt and rubber and (pavement* or road* or highway*)

Truncation allows you to search for alternate forms of words. Different indexes and databases use different symbols to accomplish this task. Both the online catalog and the Applied Science and Technology Abstracts index recommended for your assignment use the asterisk (*) to truncate.

Finding journals available at ASU

Search the online catalog by Journal Title to find out if the library has the journals you need, either in print or electronically. Current journals in print are located in the Current Periodicals Room on the south side of the first floor of the Noble Library. Older journal issues can be found either in bound volumes on the east side of the second floor or on microfilm or microfiche in the northeast corner of the first floor.

Search Electronic Journals to find out if the article is available electronically. Some of the links in the electronic journals list take you to the journal and some of the links take you to a search box where you can search for the article by the title or author of the article.

 

Getting articles not available at ASU

You may want to read items that are not available here in the ASU Libraries. Items that the library does not own can be obtained for you by filling out an Interlibrary Loan request.  Estimated time for an item to arrive is a couple of days to a week.



Off Campus Access

Many databases and journal articles are available off campus.
1. You must have an university ID and Library PIN.
2. Whenever you use an index or database to search for a journal article, or when you need to access and electronic/online journal, you will be prompted to sign in with your PIN.



 For more information see Off-Campus Access to Electronic Resources

Internet Sources

The Internet allows access to a wealth of information but it can be difficult to establish the reliability of its information.  Here are some of the pitfalls that you need to be aware of:

  • It does not organize information in a useful or orderly way. So often searching for relevant information may be more time consuming than using the library.
  • Anybody can publish on the Internet so be critical of what you read.  Compare the information or the source using other sources.



Citing Your Sources

The library has a variety of style manuals that will aid you in correctly citing the resources you use in your research project. These are all located near the Information/Reference Desk in the library. See the page on Style Manuals for a complete list. You can also look at the references from Civil Engineering journal articles for examples.

  • Sample reference page from the American Society of Civil Engineers
  • How to Cite Electronic Resources - provides information on how to cite a variety of electronic resources, including web pages

Tips for Good Research

  • When in doubt, ask a reference librarian Take advantage of the work of others: use the bibliographies and reference lists of articles and books you find. Do your research early so you are sure the library materials you need are available for your use.
  • Keep all references until you know you have enough to complete the assignment.
