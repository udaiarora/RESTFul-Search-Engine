July 10, 2003

Van Duzer to explore architect's work with Fulbright award

When Leslie Van Duzer goes to her office this fall, she'll be working in an architectural landmark where communists once stored their archival material.

Van Duzer, associate professor of architecture, has won a Fulbright Research Scholarship to develop a major exhibition and publication of the Czech work of early 20th century architect Adolf Loos, and her office in Prague will be in the Villa Müller, once home to the Marxist-Leninist Institute of Czechoslovakia.

The Villa Müller, Loos's most notable building in Prague, was built in 1930 as a private home, and later occupied by the Marxist-Leninist Institute.

"I had the opportunity to teach in Prague in 1991," Van Duzer said. "When I arrived, the institute had been ordered to vacate and the future of the villa, Loos's seminal work, was uncertain.

"I was granted permission to be in the house nearly every day for two months, and I documented it with the help of my Czech students. This work resulted in a building monograph co-authored by Kent Kleinman. When, following restitution, the house was about to be sold to a private financier, we started an international press campaign to stop the sale. Finally, the City of Prague Museum agreed to buy it," she said.

In 2000, the city completed a thorough restoration of the Villa Müller, and it's now a museum, Van Duzer said.

"The new Adolf Loos Study and Documentation Center in the house will be my office in Prague," she said.

Van Duzer and her collaborator, Villa Müller curator Maria Szadkowska, will have little physical material to work with.

"There is very little extant documentation about Loos's Czech projects," Van Duzer said. "Loos did not design in models, and there are precious few drawings."

In addition to doing archival research, Van Duzer and Szadkowska will document Loos's buildings and apartments, some of which are in danger of being destroyed out of inattention.

Loos's villas can best be described as a series of cubes in variable heights stacked together to form an almost perfect cube on the exterior.

"Loos believed that every programmatic element of the house had an appropriately-sized volume of space," Van Duzer said. "The complex space of the interior might best be described as a Chinese puzzle."

Loos, an "enigmatic figure" in modern architecture, practiced from about 1900 to 1933, Van Duzer said.

"With the exception of the Villa Müller, his projects for the Czech Republic are little known," she said. "No research on this body of work has been conducted for 20 years. For architects and Loos scholars, we hope this will be a significant contribution."

Van Duzer and Szadkowska's exhibition, "The Czech Oeuvre of Adolf Loos," will be launched in 2005 at the Prague Castle (Hradcany) in the Czech Republic and will then travel internationally.
