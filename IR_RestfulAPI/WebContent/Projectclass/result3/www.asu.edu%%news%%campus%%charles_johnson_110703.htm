November 7, 2003

Award-winning author Johnson to give ASU Centennial Lecture

The prolific and wide-ranging intellect of Charles Johnson, one of America's leading writers of novels, short stories and essays, will energize the ASU community during his residency at the Barrett Honors College Nov. 16 - 21.

Johnson will present the 2003 Centennial Lecture on "Mindfulness and the Beloved Community" at 7:30 p.m., Nov. 19, in the Carson Ballroom of Old Main. Tickets are required for the free lecture and are available at ASU Bookstores, Borders Books and Changing Hands Bookstore.

He won the National Book Award in 1990 for "Middle Passage," a novel which embodies his controversial version of black literature as a "fiction of increasing artistic and intellectual growth, one that enables us as a people - as a culture - to move from narrow complaint to broad celebration."

His perspective on mindfulness is evident in story lines from his novels, which also include "Dreamer" and "Oxherding Tale," and in the careful crafting of all his works. He will discuss the impact mindful thoughts, words and actions can have on any community.

Johnson's balance of philosophy and folklore has been praised since the publication of his first novel, "Faith and the Good Thing," in 1974. He was named by the University of Southern California as one of the 10 best short story writers in America.

Having begun his career as a cartoonist, Johnson also will be featured in a panel discussion on black humor and its political ends at 5:30 p.m., Nov. 20, in Agriculture 250, along with ASU English Professor Neal Lester and David Hemphill, executive director of Phoenix Black Theatre Troupe. In addition to speaking to classes as guest lecturer and meeting with faculty and students, he will participate in a brown-bag discussion at 11:30 a.m., Nov. 21, in the Memorial Union Mohave Room. Johnson's outspoken views on the direction of black literature have been criticized as being unfair to such authors as Alice Walker and Toni Morrison, whose works challenge racism more directly.

He has been a practicing Buddhist since 1980, and says his work is informed by both Eastern and Western philosophical traditions.

He has received both a National Endowment for the Arts grant and a Guggenheim Fellowship, and his collection of short stories, "The Sorcerer's Apprentice," was one of five finalists for the PEN/Faulkner Award. He has written more than 20 screenplays and continues to draw and publish cartoons. Johnson is the Pollack Professor of English at the University of Washington in Seattle.

He also will speak on Buddhism and writing at 7 p.m., Nov. 17, at Changing Hands Bookstore, 6428 S. McClintock Dr., Tempe.
