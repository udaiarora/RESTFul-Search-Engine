ASU President Hosts Fifth Annual Science Coalition Media Roundtable

The Science Coalition hosted its fifth annual media roundtable on March 9, 2004 at the New York City Press Club.  Eleven university Presidents and Chancellors from public and private institutions, along with Al Diaz, Director of NASA's Goddard Space Flight Center, attended the event to discuss federal funding for university-based research in science, as well as other policy issues for science and higher education.

President Michael Crow of Arizona State University hosted the event, with President Karen Holbrook of the Ohio State University serving as co-chair.  They were joined by Rensselear Polytechnic Institute President Shirley Ann Jackson, University of South Carolina President Andrew Sorensen, University of Maryland President C. Dan Mote, University of Notre Dame President Edward A. Malloy, Purdue President Martin Jischke, University of  California - Riverside Chancellor France A. Cordova, Pennsylvania State University President Graham B. Spanier, Stony Brook University President Shirley Strum Kenny, and Georgia Institute of Technology President G. Wayne Clough.

Media outlets present at the event included The New York Times, Los Angeles Times, Columbus Dispatch, Arizona Republic, Nature, Press Enterprise, Business Week, ABC News, Newsday, CBS News, WNBC, Chronicle of Higher Education, CNBC, The Christian Science Monitor, and others.

The dinner discussion was both lively and substantive and focused on the following major themes:

    · The next generation of scientists.  There was considerable discussion on the outsourcing phenomenon, immigration policy and foreign student visas, and getting more American students, particularly women and minorities, into scientific fields.  The topic of the evolution of the US as a creative economy was also explored.

    · The politicization of science.  The participants were asked their thoughts on whether the Bush Administration was politicizing science through regulation and funding.  There was discussion about new restrictions and reporting requirements as well as classified research.

    · The funding of science.  In addition to discussing support for science funding on Capitol Hill and in the Administration, the participants explored the issue of research following money that is available and the fields which might be under-supported as a result.  Congressional appropriations earmarks were also discussed.

    · The ethics of private support of university research.  The participants took on the topic of gifts to universities to fund research and their impact on science overall.

    · The future of space exploration.  The panelists commented on the debate over the future of manned space exploration and whether funding for unmanned missions would suffer from any new manned initiatives.
