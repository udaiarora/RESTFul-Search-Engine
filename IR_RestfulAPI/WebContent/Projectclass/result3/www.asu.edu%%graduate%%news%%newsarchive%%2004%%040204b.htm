Aid to Heat Up Competition for Best Students

For the first time, ASU will offer full tuition remission for graduate teaching and research assistants, beginning in fall 2004. The university also will provide more grants-in-aid to needy and meritorious graduate students.

This will help the university better compete with peer institutions for the best and brightest students, according to Maria Allison, vice provost and dean of Graduate Studies. To further bolster support for these students, ASU is taking an aggressive approach to establish a permanent funding source for an internal fellowship program.

“We have to compete against strong support packages these top students are offered at other institutions,” says Allison. “Some of our peers can offer multi-year, very attractive packages, while we have had very limited resources.

“ASU's research faculty are at such a high level that they need high quality RAs, and we need good TAs to help us move to the next level as a university. These are our future scientists, artists and teachers.”

The enhanced grant-in-aid program will soften the effect of the tuition increase for students who are struggling financially, many carrying loans or working long hours in their jobs. Needy and meritorious students will be nominated by their departments or units.

It was only five years ago that ASU started offering student health insurance to RAs and TAs who were working 20 hours a week, the maximum hours allowed. In 2002 TAs working 20 hours a week received a 25 percent tuition waiver, and in 2003 this benefit was expanded to include RAs and the waiver was increased to 55 percent, with students working 10 to 19 hours a week receiving a 27.5 percent reduction.

In fall 2004, RAs and TAs working 20 hours a week will pay no tuition, and those working 10 to 19 hours will pay half of their resident tuition.

“We've wanted to do this for years,” says Allison. “This has been a large effort by many people, from the president, the provost and the vice president for research to the Graduate and Professional Student Association (GPSA).”

Brian Collier, GPSA president, said offering full tuition remission shows that ASU values graduate students who are working in many areas of the university.

“It's a full acknowledgement of our roles as teachers, researchers and students,” says Collier, a doctoral candidate in history. “And it puts ASU on the map with the best universities in the country that offer full remission, so this will help us to continue to be nationally competitive.”

Collier says he is excited about the enhanced grants-in-aid, as they “will continue to help foster an environment of academic excellence.”

The Graduate College proposes launching next fall an internal university-wide fellowship program to recruit top quality students, including multiyear doctoral fellowships and competitive dissertation fellowships. Another group of enrichment fellowships will focus on diversity recruitment and retention, drawing students from underrepresented populations including men and women in certain programs and needy students as well as minorities.

“We have a long way to go with regard to fellowship funding compared to our peers,” Allison says, “but we are beginning to build for the future, and we hope within five years it will grow into a substantial program.”
