9 February 2006

Chad Gaffied Talk "Unilingualism, Bilingualism, Multilingualism: What does the Canadian experience suggest?"
Room: Coor 4403
Tuesday, February 21 3:00 to 4:30

Chad Gaffied, a prominent Canadian historian, formerly President of the Canadian Historical Association, will be visiting ASU on February 20 and 21 . His biography is attached below. Gaffield´s pioneering historical work on language in Canada should provide a refreshing perspective for us and for our students, given the growing salience of language conflicts in Arizona and the United States.

Chad Gaffield is a Professor of History at the University of Ottawa (Canada) where he holds a University Research Chair focused on socio-cultural changes in 19th and 20th century Canada. He received his B.A. and M.A. from McGill University and his Ph.D. from the University of Toronto. His publications have included work in historical demography, the history of schooling, regional history, language and identity, and computer applications for historical research. Gaffield is currently the Principal Investigator of the Canadian Century Research Infrastructure project, a $15 million interdisciplinary and multi-institutional initiative to enable systematic research on the making of modern Canada in an international comparative context. The Royal Society of Canada awarded him the J.B. Tyrrell Medal in 2004 and elected him as a Fellow in 2005. He has served as President of the Canadian Historical Association (2000-1) as well as President of the Canadian Federation for the Humanities and Social Sciences (1996-8) representing 25,000 scholars across the disciplines of the human sciences.

AMERICAN LYNCHING presentation
A Documentary Feature
By Gode Davis and James M. Fortier

American Lynching is slated to become the first feature-length documentary examining lynching in the United States. Lynch mobs have victimized African Americans, Mexican Americans, and other expendable members of American society. Gode Davis, director and co-producer, will discuss the ongoing project and the phenomenon's sordid history. ASU professor of history, Arturo Rosales, author of ¡Pobre Raza!, a pioneering study of violence against Mexican Americans, will add to Mr. Davis' presentation.
