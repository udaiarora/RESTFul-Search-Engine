March 28, 2003

ASU scientists grace cover of 'Nature' twice in March

Most universities would be happy to get this kind of attention once every few years, but research by ASU scientists has made the cover of "Nature," one of the world's leading science journals, twice in a single month.

A research article by Korrick Professor of Geological Sciences Philip Christensen was the cover story of the British magazine's March 6 issue. The research, revealing the possible existence of recent liquid water on the surface of Mars, was also featured on the front page of the Los Angeles Times, as well as in the New York Times, the Washington Post, the Boston Globe, the Boston Herald, and in other newspapers and over wire services around the world.

Now, three weeks later, an article co-written by ASU Regents Professor of Physics & Astronomy Sumner Starrfield has the university back in the limelight again. Starrfield and a group of co-authors that includes scientists from the Space Telescope Institute, the University of Arizona and the U.S. Naval Observatory in Flagstaff report on a dramatic set of Hubble Space Telescope photographs that capture the eruption of a totally new type of nova, or exploding star.

The photographs, featured in the March 27 issue of "Nature," show light reflecting off increasingly larger shells of gas and dust - apparently debris from past explosions - surrounding the exploding star. But these rare photos reveal something even more unusual: the star did not simply blow off its outside layer of white-hot gas like a "classic" nova, but instead suddenly swelled into a cool, red supergiant star.

According to Starrfield, it is a phenomena that modern astrophysics currently has no way of explaining and may prove revolutionary for the study of exploding stars. News coverage, even with the war going on, is likely to follow.

"These articles are great examples of what we're doing and something the university should be proud of," says David Young, dean of the College of Liberal Arts & Sciences. "This kind of attention is a reflection of the caliber of work that is being done at ASU."
