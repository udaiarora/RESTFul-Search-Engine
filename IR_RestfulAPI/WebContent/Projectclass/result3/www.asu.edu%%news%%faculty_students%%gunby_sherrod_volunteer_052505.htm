May 25, 2005

Volunteer work earns praise for 2 ASU students

Two ASU students, John Gunby Jr. and Maya Sherrod, have been chosen to receive volunteer service awards from the city of Tempe.

Gunby and Sherrod were selected from a pool of more than 3,600 volunteers in 2004.

The students’ achievements reflected the diverse nature of volunteering in Tempe, but both shared a commitment to improving the community.

Gunby, the son of ASU employee Jean Gunby, volunteers for a job that many criminals would consider an unenviable position: helping train police attack dogs. The difference between the bad guys and Gunby is that he wears a protective suit while submitting
to the mauling.

Gunby is part of a training corps that helps hone the skills of police dogs. Though the suit protects against scratches and bites, he still ends some days black and blue.

In addition to his work with the canine unit, he also volunteers with the bait vehicle and motorist assistance programs. The bustle and bruises reflect Gunby’s dogged determination to someday become a Tempe police officer.

Sherrod’s experience with black and blue has more to do with their coordination as fabrics and textures. She was the recipient of a special projects award for her efforts in redecorating two rooms with historical accuracy at the Petersen House Museum.

Sherrod was charged with the task of renovating two parlors – on the condition that each room be adorned with furniture and accessories appropriate to either a Victorian or bungalow period. She researched the periods and interpreted the lifestyle of the would-be occupants before coming up with a design plan for each room.

Not content to stick to the planning, Sherrod also assisted with painting the rooms.

“We always enjoy working with students from ASU,” says Maryanna Bastin, the coordinator of Tempe’s volunteer resources. “They are intelligent and have wonderful, up-to-date skills, great ideas and untapped energy. They bring us new perspectives and ideas, and they challenge us to examine how we do things.”

Bastin says last year the dollar amount conserved by volunteer work in Tempe was close to $1.8 million, easing the burden of taxpayers and expanding public services.

The two students were honored in March at an awards luncheon with Mayor Hugh Hallman and Tempe City Council members.
