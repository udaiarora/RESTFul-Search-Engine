July 8, 2003

ASU program addresses critical need for rural social workers

A collaborative effort among faculty at Northern Arizona University and ASU's Main and West campuses will help alleviate a severe shortage of master's-level social workers in northern Arizona. The program, which welcomes its first cohort of approximately 25 students this month, is designed to provide a more highly educated workforce of social workers in rural and American Indian communities.

The two-year Master of Social Work program targets social work professionals with bachelor's degrees who aspire to improve their ability to intervene effectively with clients and work in supervisory roles. Students will take weekend classes on campus at NAU and complete field practicum work in the rural areas in which they live and work.

"With severe shortages of social workers and increasing work loads, we are in need of several hundred master's-level social workers and counselors every year," says Susan Morley, director of human resources for Flagstaff-based Northern Arizona Regional Behavioral Health Authority. "This collaborative project will enable more northern Arizona residents to further their career goals and will help meet the shortage of behavioral health professionals."

"We saw a need and worked together to find a way to meet it," says Melissa Lavitt, chairwoman of ASU West's social work department.

Lavitt collaborated with Karen Pugliesi, chair of NAU's department of sociology & social work, and Karen Gerdes, associate professor in the ASU Main School of Social Work, in designing the program.

"The unfortunate fact is that social, psychological and health problems are prevalent in rural areas of northern Arizona, including reservation communities," Pugliesi says. "This program is designed to make a tangible positive impact on the quality of life for residents of tribal and other rural areas."

The program's format, offering weekend classes, is designed to make a master's degree accessible to social workers on reservations and in other remote areas who would not be able to travel to Flagstaff to take classes on weekdays.

"This collaborative approach among faculty at ASU Main, ASU West and NAU enables us to expand our academic program offerings in an efficient way during a time of limited university budgets," Gerdes says.

For information, call the ASU West social work department at (602) 543-4679 or the NAU department of sociology and social work at (928) 523-6791.
