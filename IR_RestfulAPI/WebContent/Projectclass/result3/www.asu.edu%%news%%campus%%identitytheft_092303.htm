Identity theft, don't get caught in the web

According to a survey released by the Federal Trade Commission, close to 10 million Americans became victims of identity theft this past year. The cost to consumers, businesses and financial institutions has been close to $48 billion.

Identity theft encompasses a variety of crimes, but generally involves the misuse and exploitation of a credit card, bank account or Social Security number.

While identity theft is reaching overwhelming proportions, there is still much consumers can do to protect themselves, says ASU Chief Information Officer and Vice Provost Bill Lewis.

The most crucial steps to avoid becoming a victim are to be aware of the problem and to be cautious with personal information such as a social security number or credit card information, he says.

"Identity theft is a real problem and it is everyone's responsibility to do what they can to protect themselves from becoming a victim," Lewis says. "Some people make the mistake of thinking that identity theft will happen to someone else."

The latest hoax is an e-mail message indicating a partial shipment from a retailer was sent but they needed additional credit card information to complete the shipment.

"They asked you to go to a Web site to provide the data," Lewis says. " The rule of thumb is that if you are not positive that you have requested something that this is in direct response to, don't do it.

"E-mail is no different from any other information request - if it isn't expected, don't respond without checking it out," he says.

Lewis reminds individuals to make sure they use a secure Web site when using their credit cards to make purchases online.

Lewis stresses how essential it is for individuals to make a point of protecting their own private information.

"It is the individual's responsibility to do everything they can to protect themselves," Lewis says. "No one is going to do it for you."

For information about identity theft, go to (www.asu.edu/it/fyi/menus/id_theft.html) or go to the Federal Trade Commission's Web site at (www.consumer.gov/idtheft)

Zajac, with Media Relations & Public Information, can be reached at (480) 965-8145 or (izajac@asu.edu).

What can you do to protect against identity theft?

. Minimize where you use your Social Security number. Make sure your driver's license number is not your Social Security number.
. Shred all documents that have any personal identification information on them before throwing them away.
. Check all bank and credit card statements promptly upon receipt to ensure charges are legitimate. If not, report errors immediately to your financial institution.
. Be assertive and question why people "need" your information.
. Always be on the lookout for unexpected or unusual events.
