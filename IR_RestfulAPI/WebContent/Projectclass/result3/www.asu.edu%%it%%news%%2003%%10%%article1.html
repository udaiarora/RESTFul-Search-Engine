The State Of IT At ASU

From the recently-completed multi-ton, multi-million dollar supercomputing system, to a $22 million campus technology upgrade, measurable changes are happening now and on the horizon for ASU. The IT department is moving ahead in 2003-04 with several projects to strengthen the campus communications and computing structure, according to Dr. William Lewis, ASU's Chief Information Officer and Vice Provost.

Lewis spoke to the Computer Consultants Cooperative and the Windows Networking Users Group in September regarding ASU's present and future technology plans. The "State of IT at ASU" speech is an annual tradition for the CCC networking group.

Lewis described the proposed campus infrastructure changes as including an upgrade of telecommunications wiring. On the Main Campus, old network cabling would be upgraded to current specifications, and additional duct banks would be added to acommodate campus expansion. The project will be paid by bond issuance.

Recently completed by ASU IT was the installation of a massive new parallel processing computing system for TGen, the Translational Genomics Research Institute, and the International Genomics Consortium (IGC). Lewis said because of the size and complexity of the system, it was immediately ranked among the world's top 500 most powerful supercomputers. The system will be used in research on the human genome in support of developing new treatments for diseases such as cancer and Alzheimer's disease.

The Arizona Board of Regents now requires a review process for technology project funding. Lewis said an advisory committee reporting to him is expected to take an active role in the process. Approval requirements will vary in four steps, based on the dollar levels of expenditure. Exceptions to the approval requirements are expected to include campus construction, projects funded by Proposition 301, and technology purchased as part of grant-funded research. However, notification to the CIO of technology projects would be appreciated even when reviews are not required.

The IT administrative advisory committees are being restructured. The restructure will eliminate inactive groups and combine others, leaving three committees including a newly-formed CIO Advisory Committee. The changes will not affect campus computer networking groups such as the CCC.

Lewis said he wants to encourage continued communication from faculty regarding classroom concerns. He praised the work of the Classroom Support teams, saying that with preventative maintenance the teams have dramatically reduced the number of complaints compared to previous years. He said ASU faculty are pleased with the responsiveness on calls for service, but he added that receiving no complaints would have him worried, as "no one is that good, that there are no problems."

Questions from the audience prompted discussion on several topics. Asked whether Internet connection speeds would increase after the cable upgrade, Lewis cautioned that the speed of the computer being used would still be a limiting factor, however fast the network system becomes. Regarding recent computer virus outbreaks, Lewis said they have been a problem at ASU as well as elsewhere, but the university has not had as much difficulty containing them as some private corporations. He mentioned there would be increased focus on business continuity and security planning, and these topics would be of concern not just in central IT but across the university. "Security is everyone's concern at ASU," Lewis said.
