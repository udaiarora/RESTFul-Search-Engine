ASU, Maricopa Community Colleges form new partnership to benefit students

ASU and the Maricopa Community Colleges are forming an unprecedented partnership to enhance and increase the number of associate and baccalaureate degrees awarded in metropolitan Phoenix.

The new "ASU-Maricopa Alliance" will move the institutions far beyond the traditional "2 + 2" transfer model by offering new options to eligible students for where and how they learn. By working collaboratively, the institutions will provide these co-enrolled students with programs and flexibility that take advantage of the benefits of each institution.

The direction for the expanded partnership evolved out of discussions between Maricopa Community Colleges Chancellor Rufus Glasper and ASU President Michael Crow.

"We are bringing the forces of both institutions together to enhance higher education opportunities for all Arizonans, particularly the residents of Maricopa County," says Crow. "We live in a unique setting, where we have the nation's most innovative community college district and a growing dynamic university. Working together will dramatically increase productivity."

"Despite the good work that ASU and the Maricopa Community Colleges do, there are still a number of students whose academic needs are not met due to program availability, access and affordability," explains Glasper. "Together, we want to increase educational options and make it financially feasible for students to earn degrees."

Although the partnership is still in the development phase, the initiative could include a variety of academic programs available at education sites that are operated by either institution, or both.

"Embedding an accessible baccalaureate education in every community throughout the Valley will enhance the quality of the work force that Arizona businesses demand," says Crow.

As teams of educators are brought together, discussions will explore how students can earn associate degrees as they strive for a baccalaureate degree.

"We already know that 54 percent of all students who earn a baccalaureate degree from ASU have attended one of the Maricopa Community Colleges," says Glasper. "The ASU-Maricopa Alliance will provide new opportunities for advanced learning while students move smoothly through a program administered by both institutions."

Both Crow and Glasper agree there is a lot of work and consensus building to be done before the initiative is officially launched. An "ASU-Maricopa Alliance Steering Committee," co-chaired by Christine Wilkinson, senior vice president at ASU, and Anna Solley, vice chancellor of academic affairs at the Maricopa Community Colleges, is working to identify pilot programs to advance the program from concept to implementation. The steering committee also is working to develop a timeline and establish working teams to get the pilot running by fall 2004.

The two institutions also will prepare joint principles to address common concerns and goals, focusing on encouraging student retention and success, offering learner-centered degrees and programs, meeting the needs of a diverse student population, making higher education accessible and affordable and expanding opportunities for students that will support the new economy and work force needs.
