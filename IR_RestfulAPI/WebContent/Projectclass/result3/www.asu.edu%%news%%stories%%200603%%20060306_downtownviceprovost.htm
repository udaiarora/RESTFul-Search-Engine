March 6, 2006

ASU names vice provost for downtown campus

Chicano studies and English professor Cordelia Chávez Candelaria appointed to post

Cordelia Chávez Candelaria, nationally renowned for her pioneering work in furthering multicultural understanding of the Americas, has been named vice provost at Arizona State University’s Downtown Phoenix campus.

A Regents' Professor of Chicana/Chicano Studies and English in ASU’s College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, Candelaria in her new post will oversee academic affairs at the university’s newest campus.

“Dr. Candelaria embodies a standard of excellence one needs to lead the downtown campus to academic success. She has demonstrated a strong commitment to ensure the university’s vision comes to life,” says ASU President Michael Crow.

Recently selected as one of the ASU Regents’ Professors, Candelaria is an accomplished author, distinguished scholar and innovative practitioner. Her ASU career began in 1991 when she joined the university as an English professor and researcher for the Hispanic Research Center. Soon after, she founded the university’s Chicana and Chicano Studies Department, which offers students classes that examine the experience, culture, art and current status of people of Mexican descent living in the U.S. The department’s faculty has produced more than 15 published books and operates the “Teaching America’s Latino Legacy,” a summer program for Arizona teachers who incorporate multicultural studies in lesson plans.

“As a new campus faced with budding challenges, we need someone who has passion and energy for what we are trying to accomplish; and Dr. Candelaria has these qualities,” says Mernoy Harrison, vice president and provost of the downtown campus. “As the Chicana/Chicano Studies Department chair for five years, she gained excellent knowledge and understanding of creating new academic enterprises, including developing curriculum and recruiting faculty.”

“I’m honored to join the ASU Downtown Phoenix campus team in building a major, 21st century urban university in Arizona,” says Candelaria. “Being at the very threshold of this institution’s building process guarantees plenty of work and challenges, as well as incomparably rewarding results.”

Recognized for her groundbreaking work in the field of American literature and cultural studies, Candelaria was awarded the 2005 Outstanding Latina Cultural Award from American Association for Higher Education. She also was the third recipient of the Americas Award in 1991, putting her in an elite group including previous honorees Carlos Fuentes, a writer, and U.S. Sen. Daniel Inouye, D-Hawaii.

Through more than 150 published titles, Candelaria has helped redefine the mainstream of American literacy and cultural studies. Her publications include the “Encyclopedia of Latino Popular Culture” (2004), “Chicano Poetry, A Critical Introduction” (1986) and “Seeking the Perfect Game: Baseball in American Literature” (1989).

Candelaria’s academic career includes a professorship at the University of Colorado at Boulder, where she founded the Center for Studies of Ethnicity & Race in America and served as chair of the Chicano Studies Program. She also held a one-year visiting professorship at Stanford University.

Candelaria started her professional career as a program officer in the research division at the National Endowment for the Humanities (NEH) in Washington, D.C. She is credited for initiating the inclusion of women and African-Americans to the NEH database in the 1970s.
