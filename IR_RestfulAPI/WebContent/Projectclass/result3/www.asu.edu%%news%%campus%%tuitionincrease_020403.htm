ASU President Michael Crow makes tuition increase recommendation - Providing quality education and increased access are first priorities

Arizona State University President Michael Crow announced today he is recommending to the Arizona Board of Regents that state resident and nonresident undergraduate students pay a $ 1,000 annual tuition increase (to $3,508 for residents and $12,028 for non-residents) for the 2003-04 academic year. Crow also recommends resident and non-resident graduate students pay an additional $1,200 annually for tuition. If the Board approves the increase at its March 6-7 meeting, ASU's tuition level would remain in the lower one-third of all U.S. public universities.
Crow said revenues generated from a tuition increase would substantially improve the university's ability to provide financial aid, enhance the student academic experience, and provide greater support for faculty.

ASU's overarching goal is to provide access to as many academically qualified students as possible, regardless of financial need, he says. Although tuition at Arizona's state universities is currently ranked near the bottom for large state universities in all 50 states, Arizona fairs poorly (46th) when ranked for its accessibility because so little state financial assistance is currently available for students.

"Any tuition increase will be accompanied by a commensurate increase in financial aid, which will make the state's universities more - rather than less - affordable for the neediest families," said Crow. Approximately 44 percent of the revenue from a tuition increase will be returned to students in financial aid assistance and the amount available for need-based financial aid will more than double under this plan, he said.

Providing the highest quality education to students is ASU's top priority. Increasing tuition, which currently is $2,508 a year for undergraduate Arizona students, will assist in achieving this goal since all additional tuition revenue not going into financial aid will be used to enhance academic programs and provide increased attention to student advising.

"ASU recognizes the difficulty a tuition increase may impose on some families, and the university is committed to do everything possible to mitigate the effects by making more financial aid available, while improving accessibility for students with unmet financial needs, as determined by federal guidelines," Crow said.
Currently, tuition covers only a fraction of the university's cost of educating a student. According to calculations by U.S. News & World Report, at ASU it costs $13,500 to educate each student.

Arizona state law requires that public university presidents announce publicly the university's recommendations for tuition and fees for the coming year at least ten days in advance of the Arizona Board of Regents tuition hearing. The hearing, scheduled for 4:30-7 p.m., Feb. 27, will be held statewide at 12 campus sites simultaneously, via interactive television. The hearings will also be broadcast live on cable TV. Students and other constituents are encouraged to attend and will have the opportunity to share their comments with Regents. The ASU sites include the Main campus W. P. Carey School of Business-C wing, room 328; the West campus Sands Building, room 125; and East campus Academic Center, room 145. The hearing will also be broadcast on Channel 61 and 13 respectively.

During its March 6-7, 2003 meeting to be held at ASU, the board will also set residence hall rates and mandatory fees. Graduate program fees and other fees will be set during the board's April meeting in Tucson.
