July 29, 2003

National Science Foundation recognizes six rising stars with CAREER Awards

ASU researchers have once again received strong recognition from the National Science Foundation (NSF) in the form of six 2003 Faculty Early Career Development Program (CAREER) Awards.

Receiving the recognition are assistant professors Alyssa Panitch in bioengineering, John Shumway in physics and astronomy, Teresa Wu in industrial engineering and Dong-Kyun Seo in chemistry and biochemistry. Rob Gray, in applied psychology at ASU East and Junshan Zhang in electrical engineering have also received official notice of the awards but are awaiting funding approval.

The awards are given annually by the NSF to young faculty members, within the first five years of taking a tenure-track position, considered most likely to become the academic leaders of the 21st century. With this year's six awards, ASU researchers have received 24 since 1999.

"CAREER Awards are extremely important to a growing institution like ASU - they show the recognition of the National Science Foundation that our young faculty members have the potential of becoming the future leaders of the chosen fields," says Jonathan Fink, ASU vice president for Research & Economic Affairs. "ASU in recent years has done very well in CAREER competitions, a great credit to our rising faculty."

From 1999 to 2002, ASU ranked 19th in the nation, tied with Harvard University, University of California - Berkeley, Ohio State University and Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute for the number of CAREER Awards over a four-year period. National statistics for the 2003 awards have not yet been released.

All of the grants include a basic research program for four or five years as well as an educational component designed to apply the knowledge to a classroom or educational setting.

Panitch, with ASU since 1999, sets forth a five-year plan of biomaterials research and community college educational outreach in her CAREER proposal, "Biomimetic Self-Assembling Hydrogels for Delivery of Bioactive Molecules." Panitch is developing soft, pliable materials, known as hydrogels, which can be injected as a liquid into the body, where they will solidify, or spread over a wound like ointment. The material shows promise as a delivery method for medication to aid in treating disease or improving organ transplants.

Shumway, a second-year ASU assistant professor of physics, is applying a novel computer modeling technique to quantum dots in "Path Integral Simulations of the Electronic, Optical, and Magnetic." The simulations could create a better understanding on the nature of atomic-scale quantum dots and lead to new materials and electronic devices.

Wu, who came to ASU in 2001, will pursue collaborative product development research in "Design and Implementation of a Virtual Product Development Environment." Wu's research will focus on creating tools to aid the product development process for collaborators, particularly at multiple companies. A major part of the project is to create a Web-based shared information space that allows all parties involved in a product's development to communicate with one another.
Seo, in his second year at ASU, will focus on developing new ferromagnetic materials using intermetallics, or metallic compounds using two or more types of metals, in his research "New Ferromagnetic Metals." The process has strong potential for new media storage devices.

Gray, associate professor in applied psychology at ASU East, is focusing his research on the concept of collisions - both in initiating and avoiding them - in "An Integrated Approach to Research on Collision Control." Gray, with ASU since 2001, will study visual cues and try to better understand human adaptation to changing circumstances in relation to avoiding automobile collisions. He will also study the actions used in creating collisions in activities such as hitting a baseball with a bat.

Zhang, in his CAREER proposal, "Efficient Resource Management and Multi-Access Protocols for Bursty Traffic Over Wireless Networks: A Cross-Layer Design Approach," plans to improve wireless networking, particularly for multimedia transmission. Zhang, who joined the ASU faculty in 2000, will create theory and design algorithms for cross layer design, which takes advantage of the interplay between protocol layers in a network. This work could lead to better streaming audio and video on wireless networks.
