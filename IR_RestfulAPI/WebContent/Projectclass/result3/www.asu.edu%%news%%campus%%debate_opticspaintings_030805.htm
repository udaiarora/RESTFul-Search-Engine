March 8, 2005

Professor debates use of optics in Renaissance paintings

In 2001, celebrated painter David Hockney proposed a controversial theory that, as early as 1420, some artists secretly used optical devices – concave mirrors and lenses – to project images onto their canvases, which they then traced and painted over. This technique, Hockney argued, was responsible for the rise in realism in early Renaissance painting.

Hockney, soon joined by Charles Falco, a physicist and professor of optics at the University of Arizona, asserted that these secret techniques of the great masters explain some of the more interesting attributes of these works of art – the lifelike representations exhibited in them and some of the optical illusions they present, such as fixed focal points in the paintings.

But many historians have defended the paintings as being truly free-form expressions, citing the fact that there exists no documentation that concave mirrors were used as an art technique, or that artists were using optics well before scientists, who didn’t invent similar optical devices like microscopes or telescopes until the 1590s.

David Stork of Ricoh Innovations, who also is a consulting professor in electrical engineering at Stanford University, will present results of his independent analysis of the Hockney/Falco theory during a colloquium at 4 p.m., March 10, in Physical Sciences F173.

Stork, who initially approached Hockney’s theory with an open mind, says that it was only after finding “persistent flaws and simpler alternative explanations for the phenomena” that he began to seriously doubt the theory.

Stork’s illustrated talk – a blend of elementary optics, computer vision and art history for non-specialist artists and scientists alike – will present recent discoveries and the first independent investigations into Hockney’s claims.

He promises that “you may never see master paintings the same way again.”

Stork’s talk, which is free and open to the public, is sponsored by ASU’s Department of Physics and Astronomy.
