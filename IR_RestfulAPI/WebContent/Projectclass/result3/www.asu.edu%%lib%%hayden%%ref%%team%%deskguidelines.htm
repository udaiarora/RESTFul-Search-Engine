Hayden Reference Team
Guidelines for Behavior Towards Patrons of Reference Desk Staff


(accepted on July 15, 1999 for the duration of the Hayden Reference Team structure)


Introduction | Approachability | Responsiveness | Listening/Interviewing | Searching/Answering


Introduction

Our goal was to identify and recommend observable behavioral attributes that could be correlated with positive patron perceptions of reference desk staff performance. The Hayden Reference Team has identified several areas in which behavioral attributes could be directly observed, including approachability, responsiveness, listening/ interviewing, and searching/answering, activities. These guidelines are intended to be used to assist in the training, development, and/or evaluation of librarians and staff who provide information services directly to library users. They are designed primarily to deal with instances in which the patron and the librarian are working face to face. While many of the guidelines also apply to all other reference transactions, some will need to be adapted for remote users and persons with special needs.


1.0 Approachability

In order to have a successful reference transaction, the patron must be able to identify that a reference desk staff member is available to provide assistance and also must feel comfortable in going to that staff member for help. Approachability behaviors set the tone for the entire communication process between the reference staff and the patron. The initial verbal and non verbal responses of the staff will influence the depth and level of the interaction between the reference staff and the patron. At this stage in the process, the behaviors exhibited by the staff member should serve to welcome the patron and to place him/her at ease, recognizing that not all examples below are not appropriate for every culture. Our role in the communications process is to make the patron feel comfortable in a situation which may be perceived as intimidating, risky, confusing, and overwhelming. Examples of approachability are:


1.1 Is poised and ready to engage approaching patrons and is not engrossed in reading, filing, chatting with colleagues, or other activities that detract from availability to the patron. However this does not preclude such activities
1.2 Establishes initial eye contact with the patron.
1.3 Acknowledges the presence of the patron through smiling and/or open body language.
1.4 Acknowledges the patron through the use of a friendly greeting to initiate
conversation and/or by standing up, moving forward, or moving closer to the patron. 1.5 Acknowledges others waiting for service.
1.6 Remains visible to patrons as much as possible.
1.7 Observes the reference area offering assistance when appropriate.


2.0 Responsiveness

Reference desk staff should be responsive to each patron's informational needs by demonstrating a high level of attention to the inquiries of their patrons. Examples of responsiveness are:


2.1 Faces the patron when speaking and listening.
2.2 Maintains or re establishes eye contact with the patron throughout the transaction.
2.3 Establishes a physical distance which appears to be comfortable to the patron, based
upon the patron's verbal and nonverbal responses.
2.4 Signals an understanding of the patron's needs through verbal or non verbal
confirmation, such as nodding of the head or brief comments or questions.
2.5 Appears unhurried during the reference transaction.


3.0 Listening/Interviewing

The reference interview is the heart of the reference transaction and is crucial to the success of the process. The librarian must be effective in identifying the patron's information needs and must do so in a manner that keeps the patron at ease. Strong listening and interviewing skills are necessary to identify the patron’s actual informational need. Examples of listening/interviewing skills are:


3.1 Uses a tone of voice appropriate to the nature of the transaction.
3.2 Communicates in a receptive, cordial, and encouraging manner.
3.3 Allows the patron to state fully his/her information need in his/her own words before
responding.
3.4 Rephrases the patron's question or request and asks for confirmation to
ensure that it is understood.
3.5 Uses open ended questioning techniques to encourage the patron to expand on the
request or present additional information. Some examples of such questions include:


• Please tell me more about your topic.
• What additional information can you give me?
• How much information do you need?


3.6 Uses closed and/or clarifying questions to refine the search query. Some examples of
clarifying questions are:


• What have you already found?
• What type of information do you need (books, articles, etc.)?
• Do you need current or historical information?


3.7 Seeks to clarify confusing terminology and avoids excessive jargon.
3.8 Uses terminology that is understandable to the patron.
3.9 Maintains objectivity and does not interject value judgments about subject matter or
the nature of the question into the transaction.


4.0 Searching/Answering

Many of the aspects of searching that lead to successful results are dependent on the behavior of the librarian. Examples of effective searching/answering behavior are:


4.1 Discusses the search strategy with the patron.
4.2 Encourages the patron to contribute ideas.
4.3 Explains the search sequence to the patron.
4.4 Attempts to conduct the search within the patron's allotted time frame.
4.5 Accompanies the patron when it improves the overall level of service provided
Factors to consider are:


• impact on service to others (in person or telephone)
• impact on colleagues (work flow at desk)
• patron’s learning style and or skill/level


4.6 Explains how to use sources when the patron shows an interest.
4.7 Works with the patron to narrow or broaden the topic when too little or too much
information is identified.
4.8 Asks the patron if additional information is needed after an initial result is found.
4.9 Recognizes when to consult other librarians for when additional subject expertise is
needed. Also recognizes when to refer a patron to a more appropriate library,
librarian, or other resource person.
4.10 Asks the patron if the question has been completely answered.
4.11 Encourages the patron to return to the reference service point.
