Mission of RCR Programs at ASU
To raise the depth of understanding about research ethics at ASU and to integrating and internalizing responsible conduct in research is the goal of this cooperative effort between the Arizona State University's Division of Graduate Studies, the Bioethics Program, the Office for Research and Sponsored Projects Administration (ORSPA), the Consortium for Science Policy Outcomes, and the Lincoln Center for Applied Ethics.

To attain that goal we must approach research ethics from a variety of directions and embed the ethos and practices of responsible conduct in research in the university culture. Faculty must model and mentor responsible conduct in research for their graduate students, have ongoing dialogues about the problematic situations that arise in the course of research, and discuss strategies for mitigating or avoiding such problems. Students must be trained in the norms of integrity in research. Administrators must provide incentives for responsible conduct in research and reward its successes. This initiative aims at moving the university from a regulatory compliance oriented institution to one that expects and exemplifies the highest standards of integrity in research. These are different cultures. One is the legalistic model, merely following rules, that is motivated to avoid sanctions. The other type of institutional culture is one where individual researchers, administrators, and students understand that good research and good researchers have certain core values that guide their decision-making. Researchers with integrity see these values as framing their work and their evaluation of others' research.

Institutional Context
Under the leadership of President Michael Crow, ASU is moving rapidly to establish itself as the New American University, one of the nation's leading public metropolitan research universities. The vision of the New American University includes design imperatives and standards relating to a host of transformational principles including responsibility for active engagement in societal change, an environment rich in knowledge entrepreneurship, Pasteur's principle of use-inspired research, intellectual fusion, and transnational/transcultural engagement (www.asu.edu/president/newamericanuniversity). Reflecting these principles are new major transdisciplinary initiatives that include, for example, academic graduate research and education programs in biodesign, biomedical research, nanotechnology, transgenomics research, and global health studies. These bold new initiatives are layered on the core assumption that the university's mission is to advance knowledge in a manner that is fair, socially responsible, and that demonstrates integrity. In such an interdisciplinary and transdisciplinary environment, the institutional challenge is to create a culture (and the research protocols that support and sustain such a culture) that insures that critical educational principles, such as research integrity, are not lost while pushing these transformative frontiers of knowledge. ASU as an exemplar of the New American University will proactively engage in use-inspired research aimed at applying scientific principles to new and on-going problems facing our communities and the world in which we are a part. This attention to the outcome of our research is one way in which the culture at ASU is shifting to one in which scientists are held accountable for the consequences of their research, whether intended or unintended, and whether short or long-term. Responsible Conduct of Research thus becomes not a barrier to the researchers by our Institutional Review Board (IRB), but rather a central feature of our work as scientists, scholars, and teachers.

Attaining and maintaining a culture of responsible conduct in research must be integral to the mission of our institution, and of the New American University more broadly. Whether we are addressing issues of confidentiality, plagiarism, intellectual property, protection of research subjects, or a host of other issues that come up in doing research and being part of the research community, "doing the right thing" because the rules say so versus doing it because one believes that it is the correct way to conduct research are very different models. This project provides the framework for establishment of such an institutional culture.

Goals
  • Moving the university from a compliance model to a responsible conduct in research model

  • University culture of responsible conduct of research

  • National visibility for responsible conduct of research training and commitment in our graduate programs

  • On-going training for all graduate students in RCR

  • On-going training for faculty in social behavioral and life sciences RCR, particularly faculty who are chairs of graduate committees—leading to thesis chairs as ultimately responsible for mentoring their students in RCR.
