A.S.U. PHARMACY NEWSLETTER                            ISSUE TWENTY-FIVE

Food for thought


Rice fortified with vitamin A, or golden rice, is being made available free of charge to impoverished farmers of nations who lack this vital vitamin in their every day diet.  It was developed by Zeneca, a biotech company.  Vitamin A plays an important role in vision, bone growth, reproduction, cell division, and cell differentiation.  It has been estimated that about 500,000 people world wide will go blind annually from vitamin A deficiency, not to mention millions more suffering from other complications.  The recommended dietary allowance (RDA) for vitamin A ranges from 1000 IU to 4335 IU depending on age, sex, pregnancy, and lactation status.  Golden Rice has the potential to benefit millions.  On the surface it seems like a perfect scenario, a biotech company offering one of it’s innovations free of charge to those in need – all that is necessary is for it to be accepted.  But here’s the kicker -  this product is intended for nations whose diet is primarily rice, and has been this way for ages.  If farmers in these areas are even aware of this product – many are not – how easy will it be to convince these people to accept this product?  The rice is yellow, not the conventional white, and is being offered by foreign means.  Hmm… maybe not the easiest thing to do.  However, this idea and the idea of biotech innovations regarding food should not be dismissed.  Its already being utilized right here at home and there’s definitely more to come…

JeffreyBae, Pharm D candidate

 

A Brave New World


Soma is used for the relief of discomfort associated with short-term painful musculoskelatal conditions.  Although Soma has been getting a lot of attention in the news, it is not a narcotic opiate like Vicodin or Percocet.  Soma is a skeletal muscle relaxant and it is not known whether Soma relieves pain by its muscle relaxant effects or because it causes sedation.  Soma is also sometimes sold as a combination product with codeine to increase its sedation and pain relief effects.   Currently, Soma is only considered a controlled substance in a few states and does not yet have a federal mandate to make it controlled nationally.  Although Soma is not controlled, when the drug is broken down in the body, some of the drug is turned into meprobamate, which is a controlled substance.  Meprobamate is a controlled drug that is used to treat anxiety.  The current federal law states that any drug that breaks down in the body to a controlled substance will also be classified as a controlled substance.  However, despite Soma breaking down to a controlled substance, it still has not been classified as a control substance.  When a drug is classified as controlled, it has stricter dispensing rules and the prescription is not valid as long as other drugs.  This helps to reduce possible drug abuse.  With all the controversy occurring in the news with Soma, perhaps the FDA will take a closer look at requiring Soma to be controlled nationally.

Casey Allen, Pharm D candidate

I’ve got the Medicare Blues 

(WARNING!  POLITICAL COMMENTARY)


The new drug bill passed in the dead of night this past December is beginning to echo through our society.

The new Medicare cards went into effect June 1, 2004.  So far, we’ve had service reps at the call center overwhelmed and we solve the problem by throwing money at it, adding more personnel.  And, despite all the encouragement for seniors to sign up, it appears that the response has been under whelming.  The estimated 2005 cost of the new drug bill was pegged at $540 billion (revised up from $400 billion at passage) and now could go even higher.  The cost of the program, we now know, was severely under-estimated as it was pushed through Congress.  This administration seems to have become more brazen in its free spending of other people’s money. 

The new Medicare bill is just another example of the Bush administration governing by corporate interests.  Take a look around, our energy policy was devised in secret by corporate interests, we are at war to serve corporate interests of defense contractors, Halliburton and oil companies, and the new Medicare bill serves insurance companies, claims processors and pharmaceutical companies at the expense of taxpayers and the seniors that should benefit from this plan.

The Medicare drug bill does nothing about high prescription drug costs, for seniors or anybody else.  In fact, it will tend to increase the nation’s drug bill by funneling prescriptions toward higher-priced “covered” brands.  The Medicare drug bill also prohibits the government from negotiating favorable prices for the 41 million folks eligible for coverage.  Instead, that bargaining power is diluted among the dozens of HMO’s and their ilk that will provide the new coverage.  The VA, which does have bargaining power, typically saves 50% on brand name drugs, while the new Medicare program is expected to save somewhere around 15%, a savings which is already achieved or surpassed by existing programs.

Remember, too, that up to 6 million of the poorest seniors already get superior coverage in their Medicaid plans and that 2 million or more retirees may lose their retirement plan drug coverage as a result of this new Medicare bill.

There is more stink hidden in this bill.  Just a few days before the bill passed UnitedHealth Group paid $500 million for a small family-owned insurance company that specialized in health savings accounts.  The family in question, that of J. Patrick Rooney has given over $3.6 million to political candidates (90% to Republicans) and another $2.2 million to Republican organizations.  Mr. Rooney is also a registered lobbyist and has spent over $2 million working “The Hill.”  The tax rebate for health savings accounts is included in the Medicare bill passed in December.  It will cost the Treasury about $6.5 billion over the next ten years, it is estimated.  There are more drug company lobbyists in DC than there are members of Congress.  Our old buddy, Dennis DeConcini is a lobbyist whose firm represents Aventis, Novartis, and Pfizer among others.

Oh, I know, some will say “look at the economy, things are perking up,” but take at look at the deficit.  We are doing it all with borrowed money!  That bill will come due.  In the meantime Bush and his cronies will reap the benefits of an administration with corporate greed as its foundation and philosophy.

Look behind the curtain, people, there is no Wizard of Oz.                    Carl Labbe

Sometimes you feel like a nut…

The FDA has announced that packaging for walnuts can now claim heart health benefits.  They are high in omega-3 fatty acids, the good stuff most people get from fish for lowering cholesterol.  A handful or two will satisfy daily requirements.

Almonds are good for us because of their tryptophan content.  Tryptophan is converted to serotonin which is necessary for well being and good mood.  We normally get tryptophan from meat, so attention, vegans – eat your almonds!

Peanuts, hazelnuts, and cashews are a great source of iron.  This is great news for vegetarians and for anyone who could use a bit more iron in their daily diet.

Bug Off!


Getting bug-bit is getting a little more than annoying these days.  Mosquitoes are known carriers of the West Nile virus so you may want to take note of a new development in sporting attire.  Orvis, a fishing and sporting clothing company, partnered with ExOfficio, recently launched its BUZZ OFF line of garments.  These mostly all-cotton clothes have permethrin bonded to the fibers in the cloth.  Permethrin, derived from chrysanthemums, is well known to us in the Rx product Elimite (permethrin 5%) and Nix (permethrin 2%) Cream Rinse for lice.  The clothes are blessed by the EPA and are said to hold up for 25 washings.  The garments performed well in field tests in the woods of Maine and the bayous of Louisiana.

Carl Labbe

Promising news for diabetics


BioSante, a biotech company from Smyrna, Georgia, has announced promising results from a preclinical study regarding an oral insulin delivery system.  The innovation is called CAP nanotechnology, short for calcium phosphate nanoparticle.  This innovation involves casein, the primary protein in milk, being wrapped around CAP, PEG (polyethylene glycol) and insulin.  The preliminary results show promise – this oral delivery system may have comparable glucose lowering effects to its injectable counterpart.  Insulin itself, a protein, is of little utility if ingested orally because enzymes and the acidic environment of the stomach break it down.   Jeff Bae

Encapsulation of pancreatic cells in microcapsules having nano-sized pores will potentially revolutionize the treatment of diabetes. Nano-sized filters (pore size < 10 nm) are essential in order to prevent the body's immune cells from rejecting the implanted cells while providing a conduit for nutrients and insulin.

Xenotransplantation, or implanting pancreatic islet cells from another species into human diabetics is another strategy being researched.  The primary challenge here is to make these foreign cells “immuno-silent” in some way.  Encapsulation using nano-particles may provide an answer.

Speaking of immuno-silent cells reminds me of my days in pharmacy school when I postulated Lyophilized Immuno-silent Macrophages for inhalation.  These would be freeze-dried macrophages that would be inhaled and become active in the lungs to act as “scrubbing bubbles” to clean the lungs of pollutants.  The professors laughed!  Somehow it doesn’t sound so laughable now.              Carl Labbe
