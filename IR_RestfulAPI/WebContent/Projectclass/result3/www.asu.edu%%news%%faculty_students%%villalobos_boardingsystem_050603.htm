May 6, 2003

ASU engineer designs new boarding system

Airline travel nowadays is all about standing in line - at the ticket counter, at the security checkpoint, at the jetway. Airlines don't like the lines any more than passengers, for every minute a plane stays on the ground is a minute it isn't making money.

To reduce boarding time, this summer America West will institute a new group boarding system designed by Rene Villalobos, industrial engineering professor, and graduate student Menkes Van Den Briel.

Most airlines board an airplane's coach section by rows, with rows in the back of the plane boarding first. At America West, gate agents can decide how many rows to board at one time. The airline asked Villalobos to examine if a standardized group boarding system, in which each group includes certain rows, would be beneficial. In addition, Villalobos and his team of students explored alternate boarding strategies such as outside-in, which boards window, middle and aisle seats in different groups.

Van Den Briel and America West personnel filmed passengers at Los Angeles International Airport (LAX) walking down the jetway and boarding aircraft. They collected data on passengers' walk speed, luggage speed and arrival rate. They also looked at the percentage of parties of one, two, three, etc., as well as the amount of time passengers had to wait while someone else blocked the aisle or their row.

Using this data, Villalobos' laboratory developed a computer simulation to compare various boarding methods. They found that neither a true back-front nor outside-in strategy optimized boarding time. Rather, a six-group system a hybrid "reverse pyramid" strategy reduced boarding time the most.

Pilot tests of this strategy at America West gates at LAX showed that the reverse pyramid did indeed reduce boarding time if the gate agents were trained in the strategy. It continues to be used there, and system-wide implementation will occur this summer.

"The students that were involved in this project, they had the opportunity to start with a problem, to develop a model, and to see their solutions applied, which is something I hope we can do more and more at ASU," Villalobos said.
