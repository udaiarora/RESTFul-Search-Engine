January 10, 2006

Symposia explore conflicts at crossroads of religion, science

Conflict at the interface of religion and science is the focus of a series of public symposia to be held at Arizona State University’s Tempe campus Jan. 22-23.

ASU’s Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict, the Metanexus Institute and the Harold and Jean Grossman Chair of Jewish Studies present the series to explore two controversial subjects – evolution and intelligent design, and happiness and the pharmaceutical industry.

The Metanexus Institute is a Philadelphia-based international organization specializing in matters of science and religion.

The first symposium, “Evolution and Intelligent Design: Science, Religion and American Culture,” takes place from 7 to 9 p.m. in the Evelyn Smith Music Theater of ASU’s Music Building.

For more than a century religion and science have appeared to be in conflict, most especially over Darwinian evolution. Religious fundamentalists who seek to preserve a literal, biblical account of the creation of the world and human life have rejected evolution, the foundation of modern biological science. This movement has presented “creation science” and “intelligent design” as alternatives to the neo-Darwinian evolutionary science taught in the public schools. The resultant cultural battle has reinforced the problematic view that religion and science are necessarily antagonistic.

A panel of experts, moderated by Barry Ritchie, chair of ASU’s Department of Physics and Astronomy, will explore the battle over evolution and intelligent design, locating it within its broader historical and cultural contexts and illuminating what is at stake for each side. It will address the challenges of reconciling evolutionary science and religion and, in the process, consider larger questions about the nature of religion, the nature of science, and their interface.

The panel includes Jon Roberts, professor of history at Boston University, Holmes Rolston III, University Distinguished Professor of Philosophy at Colorado State University, and John Haught, Distinguished Research Professor at Georgetown University

The second symposium, titled “The Pursuit of Happiness: Perspectives from Science and Religion,” takes place Monday, Jan. 23, from 9 a.m. to noon in the Old Main Carson Ballroom.

This event will explore the expansive question: what does it mean to be happy and where is happiness found?

Within that debate, a distinguished panel of religious experts and bioethicists will consider scientific discoveries that have led the pharmaceutical industry to design psychotropic drugs that control brain processing and alleviate suffering caused by disease and mental illness – as well as dispensing drugs to alleviate depression. The panelists will assess the benefits as well as the dangers of the pharmacological approach to human longing for happiness and, in turn, consider traditional sources of meaning such as religion, philosophy, art and literature.

The panel features John Haught, Distinguished Research Professor at Georgetown University, Stephen Post, professor of bioethics at Case Western Reserve University, and Laurie Zoloth, professor of medical ethics and humanities and professor of religion at Northwestern University.

Due to limited seating, tickets are required for this free public event. Call 480.727.6736 or e-mail csrc@asu.edu to obtain tickets. Parking information will be included.

ASU’s Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict continues its programming in the spring with two additional lectures: “Interpreting Islam: Politics, the Media, and the Academy,” on Wednesday, March 8, and “Are We Losing Our Humanity?: Religion and Moral Conflict,” on Wednesday, April 5. For more information, visit the center’s
