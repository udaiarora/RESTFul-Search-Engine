June 6, 2005

Pathways to greatness: Espinoza, Gutierrez earn Ohtli Awards

ASU served as co-host for a ceremony and reception in honor of two individuals who exemplify community leadership and dedication.

Tommy Espinoza and Alfredo Gutierrez were honored at the Ohtli Award presentation, co-hosted by ASU and the Secretary of Foreign Relations of Mexico at the ASU Kerr Cultural Center.

“Ohtli” is a Náhuatl word that means “pathway,” and the Ohtli medallion, which has an image of footprints, symbolizes the pathway that women and men will follow. For Náhuatl poets, Melahuac Ohtli signifies the right path people will take to their destiny.

Shannon Wulf, manager of strategic relations and planning with ASU’s Office of Pan American Initiatives, says co-hosting the event is another great example of ASU’s commitment to social embeddedness, “because the event not only recognizes leaders within the Mexican community in Arizona, but it also brings together people that care about that community and want to recognize its leaders.”

The Instituto de los Mexicanos en el Exterior (IME) within the Secretaria de Relaciones Exteriores (SRE) acknowledges the importance of recognizing the altruistic activities of many Mexicans living in the United States, and it offers the Othli Award to those that have devoted their lives to improving the quality of lives of our fellow countrymen and women.

SRE presents the Othli Award to acknowledge and strengthen the relationship among the Mexican communities living in and outside of Mexico, and to recognize their efforts. Wulf says this year’s recipients are well deserving of this recognition.

Espinoza is the chief executive officer of Raza Development Fund, the largest and most successful financial institution for the development for Hispanic communities.

He started his career in servicing the community by organizing extracurricular activities for Hispanic children through Chicanos Por La Causa. Twenty years later, he became the president of this organization.

Recently, he, along with the Consulate of Mexico, collected almost $2 million to fight the Proposition 200 that was approved by the electorate of Arizona. The approval of this initiative was lowered from 76 percent to 56 percent.

Alfredo Gutierrez, a native Arizonan and the youngest of five children, grew up in a labor union family that struggled to make ends meet. Along with labor legend Cesar Chavez, Gutierrez was instrumental in organizing the grape boycotts and mobilizing demonstrations on behalf of the men and women who worked the fields under brutal, unreasonable conditions.

Gutierrez won a seat in the Arizona Senate in 1972. He was then 27 years old and the youngest person who had ever been elected to the state Senate. For 14 years he served Arizona in that capacity. He now leads Tequida & Gutierrez, a consulting company that produces strategic campaigns in English and Spanish. Gutierrez is also the host of the daily radio talk show, “Aqui Estamos con Alfredo Gutierrez,” which is broadcast locally in Spanish.
