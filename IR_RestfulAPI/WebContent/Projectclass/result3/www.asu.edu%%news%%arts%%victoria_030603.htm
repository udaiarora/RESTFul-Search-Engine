March 6, 2003

Acclaimed performer Dulcinea Langfelder presents 'Victoria' at Gammage

Dulcinea Langfelder, an accomplished dancer, actress, singer and performance artist, brings her poignant tragicomedy work, "Victoria," to ASU's Gammage Auditorium at 7 p.m., March 8.

"Victoria" is the story of a woman who has lost everything but her imagination. She has lost her memory, she has lost her pussycat, she has lost control over her life ... and her bladder.

As she succumbs to the effects of Alzheimer's disease, her wheelchair becomes her rocking chair, her prison, her tango partner and her flying chariot. "Victoria" is about life - every single drop of it.

Often compared to the late actor/comedian Charlie Chaplin, Langfelder takes audience members on a journey as she confronts the last part of life. With song, dance, simple technology and a multitude of artistic disciplines, Langfelder pushes tragicomedy to the max.

"Living isn't easy and neither is dying," Langfelder says, "but it is all interlaced with moments of great richness ... little victories. Victoria savors every moment. I was glad to find a bit of myself in her - I hope that you will, too."

Langfelder studied dance for much of her life, then mime with the master, Étienne Decroux, in Paris. She has studied theater with Eugenio Barba and Yoshi Oida (long-time colleague of Peter Brook). In 1978, Langfelder went to Montreal to work with Omnibus, then briefly with Carbone 14. In 1985, she founded her own performance company and has created full-length, multidisciplinary works that have toured extensively in four continents. "Vicious Circle" (1985), "The Lady Next Door" (1989), "Hockey! O.K.?" (1991), "Portrait of a Woman with Suitcase" (1994) and "Victoria" (1999) are her major works.

The recurring thread that intertwines all of her work is her search for lightness as a reaction to the weight of living. Using lightness, she is continually searching for a level of perception in which we find the courage to confront the tragi-comical reality of the human condition.

In addition to the March 8 evening performance, the ASU community can join Langfelder for a free brown bag rehearsal at noon, March 7 at ASU's Gammage Auditorium.

Designed to break down the formalities of theater-going and give audience members a behind-the-scenes glimpse at the artistic process, the brown bag rehearsal is one-hour in length with a question and answer period as time permits.

Ticket prices for "Victoria," at ASU's Gammage Auditorium are $35, $29 and $9 with half-price tickets for students and groups of 10 or more, and $3 off for ASU faculty and staff. All ticket prices include a $2 facility fee. Convenience charges may apply.
Tickets may be purchased by calling ASU's Gammage Box Office at (480) 965-3434.
