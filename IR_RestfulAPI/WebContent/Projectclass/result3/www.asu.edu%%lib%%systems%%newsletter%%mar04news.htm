Workstations Now Support USB Thumb Drives

The public workstations now support USB "thumb drives." What makes these small portable devices so useful is that they have much more storage space than a floppy disk. Most floppy disks hold 1.44 Megabytes (MB) of information, while thumb drives can hold 16 MB or more. The thumb drives have the added advantage of being faster and more reliable than floppies. They also come with handy ways of storing them, such as a built-in clip or attached key ring.

The drive plugs into the USB port, which is located on the front of our black workstations behind the gray panel. On the older white workstations, the USB ports are located in the back. Once the drive is plugged in, a dialog box will pop up notifying that the drive has been found. When the dialog box is finished, the drive is ready and files can be saved just like a floppy disk. Typically the drive will show up as "Removable Disk D:." Before removing the drive, it is important to make sure it is properly shut down. This is accomplished by clicking the "Unplug or Eject Hardware" icon in the lower right (has a green arrow), and choosing to stop the device. A message will pop up on the screen saying the device can safely be removed, and the drive can be unplugged. If this step is not properly taken, all data on the drive may be lost.

Please note that it is not guaranteed that all drives will work, though testing has not revealed any problems. If you are interested in more information about these drives or their technology, please check out http://www.thumbdrive.com.

New Database Available

This new database has been put online (http://www.asu.edu/lib/top-menus/new.html):

Colección Clásicos Tavera (Subscribed)
(1470-1900) An ongoing digitization project of the Fundación Historica Tavera and made available on a 150+ CD-ROM collection. Begun in 1996, this virtual library project aims to include all classic and key works of the history of Spain and Iberoamérica in every field of learning. Full collection includes more than 5,000 full-text works originally printed between the 16th and 19th centuries in Spain, Portugal, Latin America, and the Philippines.


Ask LIST

Q. "What does it mean when LIST staff say they’re ‘building’ a workstation? Can’t they just take it out of the box and plug it in?"

A. New workstations arrive with an operating system (OS) and a variety of software preloaded on them, but these are not library-specific. This is why we have to "build" workstations, by erasing everything already on them, and installing all the essential library programs. On all public workstations, we install our specially-configured Windows 2000 OS, Viruscan 7.0, printer drivers, Adobe 6.0, Quicktime, and local database applications, and set the computer up on the network so it can receive auto-updating patches. The workstation is then ready to use the Library databases and other library tools. The same process is involved in “rebuilding” a workstation after it has been infected with a virus or has stopped working properly.
