March 7, 2003

ACES celebrates decade of impact

From the shoestring efforts of a few dozen ASU students and faculty 10 years ago, a small inner-city tutoring project has grown into a vital community partnership that has touched thousands of lives.

About 650 ASU students each year now tutor reading, math and science to more than 1,500 children in some of the most impoverished areas of the Valley, also teaching literacy skills to their parents. They plant gardens, devise experiments, teach computer skills and mentor the youngsters, who bloom under their attention.
Academic Community Engagement Services (ACES), known for many years as the ASU Service Learning Program, will celebrate a decade of successful partnering with the community with a reception 2:30 - 4:30 p.m., March 13, in the Old Main ballroom. ACES will honor many of its longtime partners, including the Roosevelt School District and Salvation Army.

"It's had a huge impact on our district," says Mark Dowling, assistant superintendent of the Roosevelt District. "We've seen test scores increase as a result of what ASU students have done with our children. Their behavior has also improved significantly, and disruptions in the classroom have gone down. Having mentor relationships has helped steer the children in more positive directions."

Sixth graders at Sunland School who come in for tutoring three times a week have increased their reading scores 75 percent and math scores 150 percent on standardized tests, says Gay Brack, ACES founding director. They also learn science as they plant a garden, and get to take home a 35-pound box of food from the United Food Bank at the end of each week.

Many schools in the district have a 50 percent turnover rate, as parents move from job to job or children are placed in a series of ever-changing foster homes. The children see gang violence and drug transactions in their neighborhoods, and in some areas gunshots ring out every night.

"We have wonderful ASU students who are doing the impossible, working with children who have short attention spans because of parental crack use or fetal alcohol syndrome," Brack says. "They work with these kids, discover what their interests are, then go to the Tempe library to get books that meet their interests. They love these kids. I've never seen more committed students."

The ASU students' work is tied to the classroom, so they apply their learning and use their experiences as a basis for research papers, compositions and lesson plans. Many have gone on to become teachers in the inner city. Others say it's been a life-changing experience.

"This has opened my eyes to a world I had never seen," says one psychology major. "Though I went into the classroom to teach sixth graders about geography, the students and the school were a better educator to me than I was to them. My experiences there were unforgettable and are among the best in my years at ASU."

With support from the ASU English department, Brack designed the program in the fall of 1992, after her son, a Phoenix police detective, told her the kinds of problems he saw as he worked with children. The Salvation Army provided a site where 50 tutors could work with 100 children, and funding for a couple of graduate students to supervise them. The English department linked special sections of composition to internships.

From that tiny beginning, the program has spread its outreach to encompass 34 programs at 16 different sites, including adult GED preparation and literacy classes for the children's parents. More than 4,000 ASU students have given over 600,000 hours of service. Community contributions have totaled more than $3 million, providing transportation, supplies and a computer laboratory for the community.

Working all over the Valley, from Tempe to Phoenix to the Salt River Pima-Maricopa Indian community, ASU students have formed science clubs, taught creative writing, read stories, helped with homework and taught Web page design.

"They will never, ever understand the lives they've touched," says Sandi Gabel of the Salvation Army.

"It's been a tremendous, wonderful thing," says Ruben Duran, principal of J.F. Kennedy Elementary School. "It would be hard to estimate the value of this partnership with ASU, for the children, the parents and the teachers."
