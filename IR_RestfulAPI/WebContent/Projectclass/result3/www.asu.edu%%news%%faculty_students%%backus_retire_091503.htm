September 15, 2003

Happy trails - 'Creative force' of ASU East Backus to retire in June 2004

After three decades of dedicated service that has helped transform ASU, particularly at ASU East, Chuck Backus, university vice president and provost, announced plans to retire June 30, 2004.

Backus has been responsible for ASU East's development from the campus's inception to its current period of dramatic physical and programmatic growth. He became provost prior to the campus opening in 1996, after serving 26 years in the College of Engineering and Applied Sciences as both a professor and administrator.

"By next year at this time, I'll be 67, and that's the time one should be thinking about retiring. I want to exit when we are at a peak and someone else can come in and take us to the next peak." said Backus. "From a campus point of view, we've brought ASU East up to a respectable level of development and set a positive course of direction, and it's time to bring in new leadership and new views to take the campus to the next level."

Under Backus's tenure, ASU East's enrollment has increased by at least 20 percent every year and the number of academic programs has grown from 8 to 24. The campus's enrollment has grown from 1,016 in 1996 to more than 3,550 today.

"Provost Backus established the vision for a great new ASU campus in the East Valley. His energy and decisiveness has served the community and the university well," said ASU President Michael Crow.

"Chuck leaves a great legacy and tall boots to fill," said Milton Glick, ASU executive vice president and university provost. "He had a great deal to do with achievements in the College of Engineering in his early years, and in recent years, he has been the creative force, as well as the soul, of ASU East.

"He has advocated for an East Valley ASU presence for many years, and I believe that the extraordinary progress ASU East has made is in large part due to Chuck's commitment. And we all owe him a debt of gratitude for that," Glick said.

ASU will conduct a national search for Backus's replacement. Glick said the polytechnic focus that Crow has established for the campus will not change with the new leadership.

Backus holds a master's and doctoral degree in nuclear engineering from the University of Arizona and a bachelor's degree in mechanical engineering from Ohio University. He has focused his research on solar energy, and is one of the founders of the photovoltaics field, having secured more than $2.5 million in research and sponsored projects. He has published more than 90 papers, articles and books on solar energy.

While at the ASU engineering college, he was responsible for building ASU's strength in solid state electronics, specifically fine tuning solar cells to make them more efficient. He also created the Photovoltaics Testing Laboratory, one of three such research facilities in the world that conducts applied research and certification of solar panels. The only U.S. lab is now located on the East campus and it serves 70 percent of the world's solar panel market.

He is a fellow of the Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers and is a member of such prestigious scientific organizations as the American Association for the Advance of Science, American Nuclear Society, American Society for Engineering Education and the American Society of Mechanical Engineering.

Backus and his wife, Judy, are also active members of the East Valley community, serving on numerous organization boards. He is also committed to land preservation efforts, serving as president of the Superstition Area Land Trust, an organization devoted to preserving open lands in the Superstition Mountains area. Backus is the owner and operator of the historic Quarter Circle U Cattle Ranch, located east of Apache Junction, and another ranch near Pinetop.

Backus said he plans to continue working on his ranches after retirement. He also plans to dedicate himself to activities in the public service sector, using the skills he developed while at ASU.

"This has been a great culmination of my career at ASU," said Backus. "I have a great sense of satisfaction and accomplishment because I've had a chance to make a major impact on many people's lives, whether student, faculty, staff and the community, and helped set the stage for a much greater impact in the future for our institution."
