ASU President Announces New Technology Venturing Entity -- Names Peter Slate Chief Executive Officer

Taking Arizona State University technology from the research lab to the marketplace more rapidly and with greater ease is the role of Arizona Technology Enterprises, a new entity being developed through the ASU Foundation to serve as ASU's technology venturing enterprise.

The move is further evidence of ASU's rapid shift from an agency to an enterprise model. Through its alignment with the ASU Foundation, the endeavor will have substantially more flexibility to stimulate and bring about new technology driven ideas and companies.

ASU President Michael Crow says creating the new entity is part of ASU's progression as a nimble, fast-paced, and responsive entrepreneurial organization. He already has refocused the university's proceeds from Proposition 301 to support the Arizona Biodesign Institute, and is expanding the university's research infrastructure to create new and high value economic opportunity for the state.

"We need to be as creative as we can, and use our creativity to catalyze economic opportunity," says Crow. "ASU already is Arizona's leading technology transfer enterprise, and, by breaking the traditional mold, we can go further and get even better."

Finding the right person to lead such a dynamic enterprise has been a top priority for Crow. He found that person in Peter Slate, former director of Global Technology Outlicensing with Baxter International, Inc., an international biomedical company based in the Chicago area. Slate, who is relocating to Arizona, will join Arizona Technology Enterprises, as Chief Executive Officer effective April 1, 2003.

Slate specializes as a corporate attorney and business development professional in the areas of tech transfer, venture capital and emerging company development, business acquisitions and strategic alliances.

Slate says, "Arizona Technology Enterprises will be less about licensing patents and more about building value-generating partnerships with the business and venture capital communities around the university's core areas of technology expertise."

"By the time Arizona Technology Enterprises is fully operational, it will be, in a sense, a time shifter, bridging the gap between business culture and university culture so neither has to change to accommodate the other," says Crow.

ASU's technology transfer program is among the leaders in national higher education, according to rankings released by the Association of University Technology Managers (AUTM) and published last year in the Chronicle of Higher Education.

Jonathan Fink, ASU's vice president for Research and Economic Affairs, says the university's tech transfer program has been doing an excellent job of getting faculty and student inventions into the marketplace, especially given ASU's youth as a research university.

"Seven years ago, the emphasis was largely limited to only one type of licensing (related to the Cancer Research Institute), so the university worked actively to diversify its portfolio," he says. "With funding from Proposition 301, the tech transfer program is now able to actively expand into other promising areas of science and technology."

In the AUTM study, ASU's tech transfer program ranked 7th in the category "number of U.S. patent applications filed per $1-million spent on research," ahead of Princeton (8th); Stanford (14th); and the University of Arizona (113th).

ASU ranked 7th for "number of start-up companies formed per $10-million spent on research," falling just behind Stanford (6th), and ahead of Massachusetts Institute of Technology (14th); Iowa State University (19th); and University of Arizona (80th).

ASU was 10th for "number of inventions disclosed per $1-million spent on research," ahead of Columbia University (22nd); Stanford (29th); and the University of Arizona (76th).

With some of the employees from ASU's Office of Technology Collaboration & Licensing moving to the new entity, there will be a seamless transition and a continuation of the group's hard work and dedication. The staff of professionals, with both academic and industry experience, will put an emphasis on supporting ASU faculty members during the invention and technology venturing process.

"We don't want faculty members worrying about writing patents, or thinking about the market or business," Crow says. "We need faculty to be able to focus on great teaching and great research, while this new organization will perform all of the time consuming technology venturing and licensing functions."

Slate, who was with Baxter International since 2000, created a small business development group responsible for marketing Baxter's technologies, both in and out of the healthcare industry. The group successfully licensed Baxter's technology to healthcare organizations and other companies in fields as diverse as outerwear and beverage distribution.

Another important role for Slate at Baxter was to evaluate and develop new business areas for the company. In one deal, he helped Baxter develop the concept for a new nutrient enhanced beverage that is currently being launched nationally.

Slate, who also has experience in business acquisitions and developing joint ventures with both Primecare International (1997), a physician practice management company, and Zenith Electronics Corporation (1997 - 2000), says Arizona Technology Enterprises will focus on building strong relationships within the university and externally.

"Successful businesses are based on strong relationships," says Slate. "It will be a top priority of the organization to build credibility, not only in our ability to get deals done, but also in our dealings with the faculty and the technology community at large."

Slate says the organization will create a capital fund to assist researchers with technology development. He says lack of development support can derail many new concepts and technologies before they get off the ground. By adding a small amount of strategic funding early in the process to allow researchers to prove the viability of their concepts, it can substantially increase the technology's value.

Slate says that one of the keys to success will be to spread the word that ASU is generating valuable technology that addresses identifiable business needs.

"Businesses have many options for who they partner with. Our goal is to create a world-class technology transfer and development organization that will become a partner of choice," he says.

"To do that," Crow says, "service and speed will be Arizona Technology Enterprise's key asset. This will be a unit that will really focus on providing outstanding service to the faculty and to industry."
