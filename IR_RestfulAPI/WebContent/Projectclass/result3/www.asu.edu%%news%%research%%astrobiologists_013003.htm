January 30, 2003

World's astrobiologists to meet at ASU, give public talks

About 500 scientists from around the world, essentially the who's who of researchers in the burgeoning field of astrobiology, will be gathered in Tempe, Arizona for the 2003 NASA Astrobiology Institute General Meeting taking place February 10 to 12 at Arizona State University.

The meeting, only the second international conference of its kind, will feature a host of presentations on leading edge research from the top figures in its field, activities for educators interested in learning how to bring astrobiology into the K-12 classroom, and two significant lectures on topics of popular interest.

The lectures, which are free and open to the general public, will include:

? A presentation on February 11 at 7:30 p.m. in Gammage Auditorium by prominent Mexican astrobiologist Antonio Lazcano, president of the International Society for the Study of the Origin of Life, on "Astrobiology and the Emergence of Life on Earth," and

? A presentation on February 12 at 5:30 p.m. in the Ventana Room of the Memorial Union by world-renowned paleoanthropologist Donald Johanson, director of ASU's Institute of Human Origins, on "Our Evolutionary Journey: From Darkest Africa to Deepest Space."

Antonio Lazcano is a prominent biologist and Distinguished Professor at the National University (UNAM) in Mexico City. An academic committed to public education, he has received several major awards for his contributions to science, scientific journalism and teaching, including the Gold Medal for Biological Research granted by the University of Puebla. He is the author of several books in Spanish, including The Miraculous Bacteria, a collection of scientific essays, The Spark of Life, a popular exploration of the heterotrophic theory of the emergence of life, and The Origin of Life, which has become a best-seller with more than a half-million copies sold. He has been professor-in-residence or visiting professor at universities and research institutes in France, Cuba, Spain, Switzerland, Russia and the U.S. Lazcano has served on many advisory, editorial and review boards, and has been organizer for major scientific meetings in Mexico, the U.S. and Europe.

Currently, Lazcano is the president of the International Society for the Study of the Origin of Life and is considered among the ten most distinguished Latin American scientists. His research focuses on the study of the deepest branches of the tree of life, with special interest in the last common ancestor of extant life forms and the origin and development of metabolic pathways.

Donald C. Johanson is probably the best known American paleoanthropologist. His discovery in 1974 of a 3.18 million-year-old hominid skeleton -- popularly known as "Lucy" -- in the Hadar region of Ethiopia has had an extraordinary influence on our understanding of early hominid evolution. His subsequent discoveries have included "The First Family" -- a unique collection of 13 individuals who died in a single geological moment, found in Ethiopia in 1975; the discovery (1986) of a 1.8 million year old partial skeleton of Homo habilis at Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania; and the 1992 discovery of the first complete skull of Lucy's species, Australopithecus afarensis. Johanson has carried out field research in Yemen, Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Jordan, and Tanzania.

Johanson is a prolific writer. He wrote the widely read book Lucy (with Maitland Edey; 1981), which won the American Book Award in Science; he has also authored numerous scientific and popular articles. Other books include Blueprints: Solving the Mystery of Evolution (with Maitland Edey; 1989), Lucy's Child (with James Shreeve; 1989), Journey From the Dawn, (with Kevin O'Farrell; 1990), Ancestors: In Search of Human Origins (with Lenora Johanson and Blake Edgar; 1994), and most recently, From Lucy To Language (with Blake Edgar, principle photography by David Brill; 1996). The films "The First Family" (1981) and "Lucy in Disguise" (1982) highlight the pioneering work of Johanson and his team in Ethiopia. In 1982 he was also host of the Public Broadcasting Service NATURE series, and narrated the National Geographic Society film "Fossils: Clues to the Past." Johanson is host and narrator of a 3-part PBS/NOVA series entitled "In Search of Human Origins," which first aired in February, 1994 and has now been seen by over 100 million viewers worldwide. He is a frequent lecturer at universities and other forums in the United States and abroad. Johanson is an Honorary Board Member of the Explorers Club, a Fellow of the Royal Geographical Society, and

many other professional organizations; and recipient of several international prizes and awards.

In 1975, Johanson was appointed curator of physical anthropology at the Cleveland Museum of Natural History and, beginning in 1976, developed a laboratory of physical anthropology that attracted scholars from all over the world. In 1981, Johanson founded the Institute of Human Origins, in Berkeley, California. In 1997, Johanson and his team relocated IHO's headquarters to ASU where he currently serves as director of the institute and professor of anthropology.

Founded in 1998, the NASA Astrobiology Institute sponsors scientific research in a wide variety of disciplines aimed at asking and answering large and fundamental questions about life in the universe. Astrobiology is an exciting new discipline devoted to the study of life on Earth and elsewhere in the universe - its origin, evolution, distribution, and future. This new area of multidisciplinary study is bringing together the physical and biological sciences to address some of the most fundamental questions of the natural world.

The NASA Astrobiology Institute is composed of over 700 researchers distributed at more that 130 research institutions across the United States. Its central offices are located at NASA Ames Research Center, in the heart of Silicon Valley, California. Additional information about the NAI can be found at its website: <http://nai.arc.nasa.gov>

For more information on the NASA Astrobiology Institute 2003 General Meeting or the associated public lectures, call 480-965-0833.
