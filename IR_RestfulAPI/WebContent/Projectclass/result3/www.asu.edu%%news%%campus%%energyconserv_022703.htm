February 27, 2003

Energy conservation remains high priority for university

Leetta Overmyer, associate vice president of Administration & Finance, chairs ASU's Energy Conservation Committee and says finding ways to conserve energy is more important than ever.

"The energy conservation committee wants to remind everyone of the university initiative to conserve energy wherever possible," Overmyer says. "Not only do each of us have a responsibility to conserve our natural resources to the greatest extent possible, but every contribution to the conservation effort also helps the university minimize utility costs, which is so very important during these tight budget times."

With this goal in mind, the ASU and APS Energy Services Lighting Project, which will save the university both in energy and money in the years ahead, began Feb. 10. Over the next several months, crews will be replacing existing indoor lighting systems with new energy efficient systems.

In addition to changing over to energy efficient equipment, ASU departments are finding new and innovative ways to save energy.

Superintendent of Facilities Thomas Corwin says the energy consumed during this past Winter Session was 8 percent lower than the same period the previous year.
Several departments, including facilities management, faculty administration support, academic scheduling and facilities staff got together and were able to more efficiently schedule meeting and classroom spaces. This meant fewer buildings needed to be climate controlled.

"The coordinated effort yielded significant savings," Corwin says. "This result can come only when faculty and staff at every level work toward this goal, and this past season proved that we can achieve big results when we cooperate."
Corwin says because his staff shut off HVAC equipment in buildings not being used, the load for campus heating and cooling needs was reduced.

"Our goal is not just about money saved but also in resources not expended needlessly," Corwin says. "In this case the utility cost avoidance realized - above and beyond the previous year - was about $500 for each day."

Overmyer says all employees can help with the ongoing energy conservation effort by remembering to turn off non-essential equipment such as lights, computers, fans and radios, when not in use.

For energy conservation tips, go to (www.asu.edu/fm/energy.htm) or call the ASU Conservation Hotline at (480) 965-4521, from 7 a.m. to 2 p.m., Monday through Friday.

Zajac, with Media Relations & Public Information, can be reached at (480) 965-8145 or (izajac@asu.edu).
