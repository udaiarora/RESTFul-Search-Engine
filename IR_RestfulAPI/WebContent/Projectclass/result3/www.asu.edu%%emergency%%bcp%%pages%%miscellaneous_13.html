Introduction

As a critical component of any Disaster Recovery/Business Continuity Planning effort, a Business Impact Analysis (BIA) is performed to determine the tangible and intangible impacts that would be encountered due to a disruption to one or more of the department's/college's business processes. The general BIA process is to collect relevant information from a variety of sources, analyze the functions performed by the department/college, determine the impacts that would be encountered by a disruption to each business process, document general recommendations for the organization's recovery planning efforts, and present the findings in both a formal and management presentation.

As part of the (BIA) process, each business function is assigned a numerical Recovery Priority based on how quickly the organization would begin to encounter a substantial negative impact if the function were to be disrupted. For each individual process, disruption of varying duration are considered in order to identify any further consequences that would be encountered by the department/college if the function were not reestablished over time. In assessing the impact that the department/college would encounter if a particular business function were to be disrupted, components that are considered include:

Lost Revenue
Loss of public image
Failure to meet legal, regulatory, and/or contractual obligations
Accrual of inordinate recovery expenditures
Danger or health and welfare of personnel and citizens

In order to ensure that each function is assigned the correct Recovery Priority, a list of classification guidelines is defined for each category. In addition to the guidelines, each Recovery Priority is assigned a window within which the corresponding processes are to be recovered following a disaster. The assigned recovery window is then established as the Maximum Acceptable Outage (MAO) for each of the corresponding business functions. By utilizing the recovery sequence defined by the MAOs, the department/college is able to efficiently allocate their recovery planning resources.

Critical Business Function Analysis Instructions

(BIA)

We are undertaking a survey to assess the critical business functions at ASU. Each department/college should complete an assessment on which to base its Recovery Plan. The assessment has two parts: 1) the identification of the department/college critical functions, and then 2) the priority of each function identified within the department/college. The prioritization should be based on a window of time within which the corresponding functions are to be recoveredreferred to as the Maximum Acceptable Outage (MAO).

For the purpose of this analysis, the following four Recovery Priorities will be used to prioritize critical business functions.

Recovery Priority MAO time
Priority 1 None - 24 hours
Priority 2 24 -72 hours (3 days)
Priority 3 4 - 5 days
Priority 4 15 -30 days

Departments/colleges may have more than one business function in each Priority level. If so, the department should also do an internal ranking, to identify the order in which the functions should be restored within each Priority.

The planning methodology ASU will employ utilizes the "worst-case" planning concept to develop plans that provide for the timely recovery of the University/College/Department regardless of the scope or magnitude of the disaster.

The survey procedure is as follows:

  • Review the business functions, which your area is responsible for, to determine their support requirements and recovery time frames.
  • Prioritize these business functions based upon the Priority/Maximum Acceptable Outage (MAO).
  • Return the forms, which will then be sent to VPAF Office for initial analysis.
  • Meetings may be held with specific individual areas to resolve any priority questions or issues.
  • The Business Continuity Management Team will then review surveys.
  • Recommendations for support requirements for critical applications will then be presented in a summary report to the Executive Vice President Administration and Finance and Vice Provost for Information Technology.
