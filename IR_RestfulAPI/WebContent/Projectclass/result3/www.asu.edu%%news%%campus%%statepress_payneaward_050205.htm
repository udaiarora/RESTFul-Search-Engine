May 2, 2005

State Press claims Payne Award for Ethics

The State Press will receive a Payne Award for Ethics in Journalism May 12 for “exemplifying the highest standards of college/campus media in the face of political or economic pressures.”

The awards were established in 1999 by Ancil Payne, former chief executive officer of King Broadcasting, based in Seattle. He established the Payne Awards in 1999 at the University of Oregon School of Journalism and Communication “to honor the journalist of integrity and character who reports with insight and clarity in the face of political or economic pressures and to reward performance that inspires public trust.”

The State Press was chosen for the honor for its response to public comments about a graphic photo illustrating extreme body piercing.

Following the publication of the story, and subsequent reaction from university administration, State Press staff members established formal guidelines for determining what will be published in future editions, and how the staff will communicate with university administrators.

State Press editor-in-chief Cameron Eickmeyer says reaction to the story at first was “not out of the ordinary.”

“We first printed letters to the editor, but when the administration contacted us, we knew something different was in the works,” Eickmeyer says. “Once we knew what was going on, we began developing a process for decision-making that both parties could be happy with in the future.”

The guidelines note, first and foremost, that the State Press adheres to standard newspaper industry practices in determining what goes into the newspaper, and that these decisions are guided by a code of ethics. The State Press follows the Society of Professional Journalists (SPJ) Code of Ethics.

Under the SPJ Code of Ethics, the State Press agrees to “follow the incontrovertible principles that journalists agree upon,” which prohibit editors and reporters from lying, misstating their identities or intentions, fabricating stories, plagiarizing, altering photographs to mislead readers or intentionally slanting the news.
