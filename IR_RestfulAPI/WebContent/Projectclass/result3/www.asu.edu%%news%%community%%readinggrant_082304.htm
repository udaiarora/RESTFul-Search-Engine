August 23, 2001

ASU partnership leads to major education grant

A partnership involving ASU has resulted in a $2.5 million Early Reading First grant for the Navajo Nation to better prepare youngsters from low-income families to succeed academically and socially in kindergarten and beyond.

The federal grant will fund the work of the Navajo Early-Childhood Education Partnership (NEEP), a joint effort of the Navajo Nation, ASU, New Mexico State University and the Southwest Institute for Families and Children with Special Needs. Over the next two years, college-level professional development training will be provided through the project for up to 150 Head Start teachers serving nearly 3,000 Navajo children across three states - Arizona, New Mexico and Utah. Community-school-family teams will also be established to help ensure that Navajo Head Start children have the skills they need to enter school ready to learn.

Additionally, the training for Navajo Head Start instructors will better equip them to provide special-needs children and families the academic and social support they need to succeed.

"This is the first national competitive grant ever awarded to the Navajo Nation Diné Education," says Jay Blanchard, professor of psychology in education at ASU, and principal investigator for the NEEP. "This grant will help thousands of Navajo Nation children reach the success in school they so richly deserve."

Blanchard traveled with Jim Christie, ASU professor of curriculum and instruction and co-principal investigator for the NEEP, to Albuquerque, N.M., in late July to meet with U.S. Deputy Secretary of Education Gene Hickok, where they joined Navajo Nation officials for the formal presentation of the grant award. Billie Enz, associate director of the division of curriculum and instruction at ASU, will also serve as a co-principal investigator.

NEEP will begin its work this fall with the establishment of professional development education centers at each of the Head Start Centers housed at the five agencies within the Navajo Nation, including: Shiprock, Tuba City, Ft. Defiance, Crownpoint and Chinle. The education centers will support professional development classes of 10 Head Start mentor-teachers and 150 teachers/instructional aides. Teachers who complete these classes will earn 15 hours of university credit applicable to Childhood Development Associates (CDAs), and AA/BA degrees.

The classes will provide Navajo educators with a science-based curriculum that emphasizes the development of knowledge and teaching skills in the oral/listening language; phonological awareness; alphabet knowledge; word, story and book sense; early numeracy; special needs, disabilities, Limited English Proficiency (LEP); and social/emotional behavior.

In addition, the curriculum will focus on the integration of science-based knowledge and teaching skills with Navajo traditions and cultures, Blanchard said. 

NEEP will align Navajo Nation Head Start curriculum, instruction and assessment with Arizona and New Mexico Early Childhood Education and K-3 Academic Standards.

"This parallels ASU's efforts in Yuma County on the Mexico border where we have a $3.5 million grant with a similar focus," Blanchard said.

The Navajo Nation selected New Mexico State and ASU to assist in addressing the needs identified by the NEEP because of their extensive experience educating teachers on the Navajo Nation and areas throughout the Southwest that serve children and families from high-need communities, and those with limited English proficiency, disabilities and other special needs, Blanchard said.
