May 16, 2003

French film collection comes to ASU

A variety of classes at ASU will now be a little more visual, thanks to noted French film director Jean Chérasse and renowned musician Angela Morley.

Chérasse and Morley have given the French Section of Languages & Literatures and the Interdisciplinary Humanities Program approximately 80 videos and DVDs in both French and English to start the French Section Film Collection for Media Studies, with the promise of more to come.

The collection of cinematographic art, "or the Seventh Art, as the French call it," said William Hendrickson, professor of French, will be housed in Hayden Library.
Students of French will be able to learn more about the French way of life, and absorb the language while watching the French films. Students of media and culture will have a library of new films to analyze.

"The value of showing French films in class is to give the students an example of authentic French language and culture and a French approach to the cinematographic art," Hendrickson said. "For language courses, French films are a wonderful way to increase language comprehension."

Chérasse, who lives in Normandy, France, is donating films he receives as judge of the César Awards in France. Morley, a Scottsdale resident, is giving films she receives for judging the Oscars.

Michel Sarda of the Institut Français d'Arizona made the donation on behalf of Chérasse.

Sarda said Chérasse agreed to the gift because he is familiar with Arizona and ASU. On one of his visits to the Institut Française d'Arizona, he came to campus to present his 1992 documentary film, "La Marseillaise n'est pas encore enrouee" ("The Marseillaise is not yet hoarse").

Chérasse's film titles include "Un clair de lune à Maubeuge" and "Dreyfus ou l'intolérable vérité." He served as "chargé de mission" in the cabinet of Madame Catherine Trautmann, French minister of culture & communication in 1997 and 1998.
Morley is a noted arranger who has worked with John Williams, former conductor of the Boston Pops, and other composers. She has been nominated for two Academy Awards and six Emmy Awards, and received three Emmy Awards.

She currently is director of the Alliance Française of Greater Phoenix Chorale and hosts monthly Alliance Française Cinéclub Evenings in her home.

Deborah Losse, chairwoman of the languages & literatures department, said the new French film collection will "enhance the cultural context of French classes at ASU and bring French culture to the Valley." Some of the films in the collection include: (English), "The Hours," "Adaptation" and "Frida," (French), Costa-Gavras' "Amen" and Bernard Tavernier's "Laissez Passer" (Safe Conduct).

Sarda said the collection is important because "movies are the expression of our time, more than any other art form. Movies have a very specific way of capturing audiences. You don't see anyone going to an art museum and sitting in front of a painting for two hours."
