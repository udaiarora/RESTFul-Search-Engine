November 8, 2004

Top graduate student researchers named ARCS Scholars

Fifteen ASU doctoral students have won the 2004 Achievement Rewards for College Scientists (ARCS) Scholarship.

The $6,000 fellowships are awarded by the Phoenix ARCS Foundation, one of 12 chapters in the nation. The Phoenix chapter grants the awards to top graduate student researchers at Arizona ’s three state universities in support of their dynamic research projects. For the second year in a row, ASU has the most ARCS Scholars among the three universities.

Fellowship recipients are graduate students of exceptional academic promise whose scholastic endeavors demonstrate potential for widespread benefits:

Jason Ayubi-Moak, electrical engineering, is developing a sophisticated software package to aid the development of the next generation of semiconductor devices for the health care, military and space fields.

Michael Beaumont, bioengineering, is working on a project involved in improving nerve regeneration in the central nervous system.

Charity Coury, chemical engineering, is working on characterizing aerosol particles with Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy to better understand air pollution chemical evolution.

Jennifer Desai, electrical engineering, is conducting research to find new transmitter architectures that are smaller, more efficient and less expensive, for wireless handsets.

Sherry Di Jorio, bioengineering, is working to develop a biomechanical model to further understanding of how fractures heal.

Elizabeth Donarum, biology, is conducting research on the learning and memory deficits associated with neurofibromatosis, a neurological disorder.

James Jancovich, molecular and cellular biology, is exploring pathogenesis in amphibians, which can advance the study of emerging infectious diseases.

Aaron Johnson, biology, is investigating heart development.

Clinton Mason, mathematics, is working on a mathematical model to enhance understanding of the development of diabetes to help find a cure.

Jeffrey Pavlicek, microbiology, is investigating the molecular mechanisms of hereditary colon cancer.

Michael Rothrock Jr., microbiology, is conducting research to develop and apply techniques to rapidly detect and identify bacteria.

Justin Searcy, organic chemistry, is working on the isolation and synthesis of new anti-cancer drugs.

Paula Stice, bioengineering, is conducting research to assist in improving diagnosis and treatment of stroke-related neural injuries.

Corey Tyree, chemical engineering, is focusing on the development of a technology designed to measure toxic and therapeutic aerosol particles in the lungs.

John Werner, molecular and cell biology, is investigating a protein that is essential to the antibiotic resistance of e. coli bacteria.

Coronado, with the Division of Graduate Studies, can be reached at (480) 965-5995 or (nora.coronado@asu.edu).
