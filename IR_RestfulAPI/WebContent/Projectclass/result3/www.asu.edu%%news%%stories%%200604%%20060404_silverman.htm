April 4, 2006

Silverman keys ASU, UA medical partnership

ASU is collaborating with the University of Arizona (UA) on the expansion of the College of Medicine to a four-year program in Phoenix. The program will be located on the Phoenix biomedical campus in downtown Phoenix.

Assisting in the establishment of the campus is Howard Silverman, a special assistant to the vice dean of administration of UA's College of Medicine. Silverman will serve in a joint position between ASU and the UA College of Medicine as a clinical professor of biomedical informatics at ASU, and a clinical professor of family and community medicine at UA.

The downtown campus also is home to the ASU College of Nursing; the Translational Genomics Research Institute (TGen); the Arizona Biomedical Collaborative; and an expansion of the UA colleges of medicine and pharmacy.

Silverman's new responsibilities for ASU will include:

. Advising on the development of the undergraduate and graduate biomedical informatics (BMI) programs at ASU.

. Developing online distance education, executive education and BMI training programs for community physicians, nurses and related medical staff.

. Formulating, implementing and evaluating the BMI curriculum for the Phoenix track of the UA College of Medicine.

Silverman says the Phoenix campus will start an initial class of 24 entering medical students in July 2007.

Silverman's expertise - biomedical informatics - is a relatively new field in biomedicine that deals with the storage, retrieval, sharing and use of biomedical information, data and knowledge for problem solving and decision making.

He says the role of the biomedical informaticist in medicine and research is to help "see the intersections between what is useful and what is doable" when accessing and using biomedical data.

Silverman gives the example of doctors having context-specific access to necessary literature, such as drug interactions or other important patient information.

"Since detailed clinical and genetic information are becoming more and more critical for research, and eventually treatment, biomedical informatics is going to play a huge role in the efficiency of obtaining, organizing and accessing that information," Silverman says.

He says the benefits extend to patients as well as researchers, as better organization of key information can allow doctors to focus more on their patients.

"The less I have to worry about the paperwork, the more time I have to spend with patients," Silverman says. "It frees up humans to do what humans do well."
