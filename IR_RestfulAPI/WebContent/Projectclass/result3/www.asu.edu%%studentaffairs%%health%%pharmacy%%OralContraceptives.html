What You Should Know About Oral Contraceptives

Carl Labbe R. Ph.

What are oral contraceptives and how do they work?

Commonly call birth control pills, or simply "The Pill," oral contraceptives represent a reversible method of birth control.  Oral contraceptives contain various amounts of female hormones.  Two kinds of female hormones are estrogens and progestins.  These hormones regulate the female reproductive cycle.  As you know, pregnancy can occur when a sperm fertilizes the egg.  Oral contraceptives that contain an estrogen and a progestin work by suppressing egg production and fertilization.  Some oral contraceptives may be a progestin alone (the "minipill").  The progestin-only pills may also prevent ovulation but mainly work by thickening cervical mucus and blocking sperm from reaching the egg..  The FDA approved the first birth control pill in 1960 and continuing research has led to the development of a variety of safe and effective pills.  The trend toward lower dosages has led to safer products.  Today, many brands of oral contraceptive are available in the US. 

 How effective are modern birth control pills?

When used properly, today's oral contraceptives are up to 99% effective.  In other words, women who take the Pill correctly every day have less than one chance in a hundred of getting pregnant. 

 How do I know if an oral contraceptive is right for me?

The decision to use any type of contraception is personal.  The advantages of oral contraceptives are very attractive to many women (over seven million women, in fact).  The Pill offers women a chance to control their reproductive cycle while offering several other health advantages.  Oral contraceptives allow for spontaneity in sexual relationships that is unmatched by barrier methods such as condoms or diaphragms.  Remember, though, that oral contraceptives do not provide protection against sexually transmitted diseases.  Like most medications, oral contraceptives may also cause various side effects or adverse effects.  These risks must be weighed against the benefits.  Certain conditions will exclude you from pill use.  Your clinician should take a good medical history to identify any health problems that might rule out the pill as a contraceptive option.

 What are the side effects?

Some of the side effects from oral contraceptives may or may not subside after the first few cycles.  These include nausea, breast tenderness, weight change, spotting, changes in libido, changes in mood and other effects.  Many clinicians will schedule a follow-up appointment after the first three cycles of Pill use to discuss the compatibility of the oral contraceptive that was prescribed for you.  Certain physical or mental changes warrant alerting your physician.  These include:

  • Sudden shortness of breath or spitting up blood
  • Sudden pain in the abdomen, chest or arm
  • Sudden or constant pain or redness in the legs
  • Severe headache
  • Sudden vision changes
  • Severe depression
  • Muscle weakness or numbness

Although these events are quite rare among Pill users, they should be reported to your prescriber.

 Is there anything I can do to minimize adverse effects?

First, make sure that you provide a complete and accurate health history to your health care provider.  Be sure to exercise regularly and have an annual medical check-up.  Good nutrition is important.  Pill users may experience decreased levels of vitamins B-1, B-6, B-12, C, folic acid and zinc.  Most experts recommend a balanced diet over vitamin supplements, while others suggest a nonprescription vitamin formula with folic acid, such a prenatal product.  Also, don't smoke!  Cigarette smoking and age can contribute to an increased risk of pill-related complications. 

 What are the benefits of the Pill?

The convenience and contraceptive benefits of oral contraceptives are easy to understand.  Women using the Pill also tend to have more regular periods, less menstrual flow, less menstrual cramping and less premenstrual tension.  Oral contraceptive use leads to improved acne, less iron deficiency anemia, less rheumatoid arthritis, less pelvic inflammatory disease and fewer ectopic (tubal) pregnancies.  The Pill also lowers the user's risk of ovarian and endometrial cancers, non-cancerous breast tumors and ovarian cysts and helps to prevent osteoporosis (bone thinning).  The U.S. Department of Health and Human Services estimates that the Pill, each year, prevents:

  • 51,000 cases of pelvic inflammatory disease (PID)
  • 27,000 cases of iron deficiency anemia
  • 20,000 hospitalizations for certain non-cancerous breast disease
  • 9,900 hospitalizations for ectopic pregnancies
  • 3,000 hospitalizations for ovarian cysts
  • 2,700 cases of rheumatoid arthritis
Remember that for all women other than heavy smokers over the age of 35, the newer low-dose pills are much safer than pregnancy.

 How do I use the Pill correctly?

Read through the patient information that comes with each package of birth control pills because it contains information about the specific product prescribed for you.  It is very important to take the Pill exactly as directed.  To maximize effectiveness it is important to take the Pill at the same time each day.  Contraceptive failure with birth control pills can be the result of illnesses accompanied by vomiting or diarrhea, starting to take them too late in the menstrual cycle, missing two or more pills, or taking them in the wrong order.  Certain medications, such as some seizure medications and various antibiotics, may diminish the effectiveness of the Pill.  Be sure to consult your clinician or pharmacist if you have questions about the proper use of the Pill that you are taking.

 How long can I continue to take the Pill?

It is not necessary to "take a break" from the Pill, unless advised to do so by your physician.  Many experts believe that the risks identified with oral contraceptive use start to rise after the age of 30, especially if you smoke.  Continued use of the Pill can be discussed with your physician during your annual check-ups. 

 Will using an oral contraceptive change my future fertility?

An estimated 80% of women regain normal fertility within three months after stopping the Pill, 95% within a year.  If normal periods do not resume within six months, see your physician.  Use of oral contraceptives may actually increase fertility by decreasing the incidence of ovarian cysts and pelvic inflammatory disease.

 How much do these pills cost?

Retail prices for oral contraceptives generally range from $15 to $40 per cycle.  Note that there are thirteen 28-day cycles in a year, so an annual prescription requires not twelve but thirteen cycles.  Health insurance plans will often cover birth control pills for a nominal co-pay.

 Any final words of advice?

Many different oral contraceptive products are available in today's market.  The variations in the amount and type of estrogens and progestins offer the prescriber a good chance of selecting a product that is ideal for you.  Remember that it may take up to three cycles to know how you will feel when taking a particular oral contraceptive.  Make sure that your are happy with the way you feel, both mentally and physically, while taking the Pill prescribed for you.  You have choices.

Remember that these are only contraceptives, the Pill will not protect you from sexually transmitted diseases such as chlamydia, gonorrhea, herpes or HIV.

Never hesitate to ask your clinician or pharmacist any questions that you might have.

 Originally appeared in Pharmacy Times, June 2001
