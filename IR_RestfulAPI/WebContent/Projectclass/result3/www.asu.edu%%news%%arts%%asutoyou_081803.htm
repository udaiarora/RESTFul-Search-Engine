August 18, 2003

Free discussion series explores the arts and society

Bring a friend and enjoy a lively evening of coffee and conversation when Arizona State University launches its fall ASU to You: Coffee, Conversation & the Arts series at Tempe Public Library on Sept. 16.

The lecture is the first of a series of three free community classroom events this fall, continuing the popular series offered in previous years by faculty and staff from ASU's Herberger College of Fine Arts and the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, in conjunction with other organizations in the community. This year's partners in presenting the series are the City of Tempe and Friends of the Tempe Public Library.

The series of guided discussions will explore the dynamic dialogue between the arts and society and take place from 6:30-8 p.m. on Sept. 16, Oct. 14 and Nov. 18. Tempe Public Library is located at 3500 S. Rural Road and members of the public may call the Herberger College at (480) 965-6536 for more information about the events.

Gender and Society in Ancient Cyprus - Sept. 16

The last three decades have witnessed the introduction of gendered approaches to the social sciences in general, and archaeology in particular. Nancy Serwint, associate professor of art history at the Herberger College, will lead a discussion about gender and society in ancient Cyprus from the Neolithic to Roman periods. The introduction of gender as a focal point brings vital new approaches to studying the social interactions of the island's rich and dynamic past.

Super Mario, Virtual Professor - Oct. 14

What if the university lecture as it has been known since before the ancient Greeks, were replaced with video game-style learning? Imagine the technical thrills of video games, such as car chases, shoot-outs and navigating through imaginary kingdoms infused with algebra, research science and the arts.

Studies show that visual learning through computer games enables the users to absorb two-to-three times as much information at least twice as fast and retain this information longer than in a traditional classroom environment with a learned professor.

But could the indestructible group of virtual characters, led by the leaping plumber Super Mario, be replaced by educational programs? Would it be possible to teach students twice as much in a semester? And if so, what would this do the existing structure of education that goes back 2,000 years? Spend an informative and lively evening with Ron Newcomer, professor of theatre at the Herberger College, exploring the answers to these questions.

Religion and Conflict: Old Problem, New Initiative - Nov. 18

The role of religion in public life has become increasingly contentious and high profile, at both national and international levels. We are witnessing the rise of extreme forms of fundamentalism as well as a more general expanding quest for spirituality.

The proximity of this talk to the Thanksgiving holiday will enable a reflection on the historic character of religious conflict. Carolyn Forbes, Assistant Director of ASU's Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict will moderate this discussion, which will also explore the nature and character of religion itself - what it is we define as religion, its relationship to political conflict and the paradoxical quality of religion as both a source for conflict and a source for unity.

The discussion will conclude with a look at contemporary conflicts between religion and the liberal state, and a discussion of strategies for ameliorating religious conflict.

ASU to You is a joint program of the Herberger College of Fine Arts, College of Liberal Arts & Sciences, City of Tempe Cultural Services and Friends of the Tempe Public Library.
