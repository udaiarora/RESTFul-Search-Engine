3.  Announcements and Communications.

 

3.A  Senate President?s Report (George Watson).

 

Since our meeting last April, I would like be able to tell you that nothing really much has happened here at the University over that period but I cannot.  A lot has been happening because we have a new president, as you well know, and he is with us today.  We look forward to hearing from him about a lot of the changes that may be in store for us--how we are going to participate in those changes and help to determine what the future of Arizona State University will be. 

 

I am happy to report that over the past few years and continuing last year that ASU?s student profile is looking very good.  Our population of students has grown in terms of diversity over the past decade, doubling the number of Native American and Hispanic students over the past ten years.  Asian American students have increased by 85%, African American students by over 50%.  We have had good increases in those numbers. 

 

I am also happy to report that at the same time, we have experienced good increases in the quality of the student body at ASU.  Last year we had 115 National Merit Scholarship students coming here that ranked us 17th in the nation.  Our neighbors to the South also did well, so Arizona overall did well.  Our neighbors to the South were 3rd or 4th in the nation and they certainly contributed to Arizona having a good quality student population. 

 

Persistence rates are up, graduation rates are up, the numbers looked good in that respect in terms of quality and I hope that we continue that way.  Faculty numbers are remaining fairly constant in terms of the number of faculty we have.  Students are going up in number but the number of us is remaining fairly constant.  We are doing well in recruiting equal numbers of men and women.  We have decent recruitments in the minority areas but not as good as we want yet.  What we want to work on more this year, as much as we do need to pay attention to recruitment, we also need to pay attention to retention and to promotion.   

 

All is not totally good at the University; as you know, health services and health benefits are not?Doug Johnson will fill you in on that.  State finances are a problem; the revenue outlook is not very great.  Now they are talking about no pay increase for faculty, no increase for state employees, I should say, and we hope that will change.

 

Our tuition numbers were 46-47th in the nation, depending on what numbers you are looking at.  It reminded me of North Carolina, where I have spent some time in my graduate days.  North Carolina has a state statute as we do, that higher education will be as nearly as free as possible and they therefore they have always ranked very low in tuition in their state as well.  Last year they ranked one step lower than we did.  They raised their tuition a bit last year and now they are a little bit above us.  A major difference between the two states is that Arizona State appropriations for the past year were about 950 million for higher education and for North Carolina it was 2.4 billion.  Student aid here was about 3 million; student aid there was 149 million.  There are some big differences even when you say you are going to keep your tuition as nearly free as possible, there can still be some big differences in the way that you make that happen and handle that.  I hope we will have opportunities to look into that as well, and work with the Board of Regents and the State Legislature.  Something is going to happen this year on student financing and on tuition.  I believe the Board of Regents is committed to doing that and I hope we will have some input into what the Board is going to do. 

 

I would like to now proceed with a report from the University President.  You had an opportunity at the Academic Assembly meeting and at college assemblies to hear Michael speak, but today you will not only have an opportunity to hear from him but to ask important questions as well.  He will be up to answering them, too.  I believe we have a real find in Michael Crow.  He is going to be a great university president and he is going to be a great university president at Arizona State University.  I am looking forward to that, and I know that you will be looking forward to it, too.  Let me introduce our President, Michael Crow.

 

3.B  University President?s Report (Michael Crow).

     

Thank you and it is nice to see everyone, even though we are in this formal setting.  My intention today is to be primarily informational in my report, as opposed to offering a series of policy statements in the sense that there is a lot going on right now.  I know that some of you have questions, concerns and issues you want to discuss, so, I thought I would try to be informational in setting the context of the stage on which the University?s present, near term and future will be played out.  Before walking you through those activities, let me indicate that I have been working with the Senate leadership--with Ruth, Tony and George and with others--in terms of talking through issues, learning what the important issues are, and beginning a process of what I hope will be an open and constructive dialogue.  I take the role of the Senate very seriously.  I appreciate the fact that each of you take time out of your busy academic lives to be a part the governance of this institution, and I look forward to your constructive input, your constructive critique and your constructive analysis of what we are doing and where we are going and how we are headed in that direction.  I do not mind being reviewed or critiqued; I expect it, desire it, and even welcome it.  It is the only way that I can have any sense of whether or not I am doing the right kinds of things, or not.  What I always hope is that people are least civil in the discussion, because I do have a tendency when treated in a not so civil way--not to become uncivil, but the person on the other end does know that I am not happy.  I like to work through the issues for as long as possible as constructively as we can. 

           

Let me set a framework for the information that I want to pass along.  That is that we have a three-dimensional policy space that we are working in right now relative to the university.  Partly because of things that are happening in the state political structure, partly because of things that are happening within the Board of Regents and partly because of our own activities that we are implementing.  Let me suggest that there are three levels of processes that we are working through.  The highest level, we term ?strategic.?  What I mean by strategic are those things that we are engaged in which are likely to have a significant impact on the university in a three to five year time frame.  Things at the next level are termed ?tactical? and they are nearer term, which are likely to have an impact on us in a one to three year time frame, and those things that are on the third level are termed ?operational.?  I am trying to work on all of these levels at the same time.  If you look, for example, at the Arizona Board of Regents? ?Changing Directions? process, which we are at the early stages of implementing--and you may have picked up on a few news reports, which some are more accurate than others, and you also may have picked up on a few things while talking about the Changing Directions policy discussion--and this is a strategic level discussion.  Those are discussions that are likely to impact the University in a three to five year time frame and they are likely to be more discussions that will take several more months to even know the exact shape of them, and I will come back to that.  Likewise, the role of our own activities on the 301 initiative and the spending of those dollars, some of the things that we are doing with scholarship investments and so forth--and I will come back to that.  Those are tactical; those are likely to have a one to three year time frame as opposed to operational activities, which right now would be dominated by the 2003 budget cuts and the 2004 budget request.  I can take these in any order that you wish Do you have a preference to start with 2003 and 2004 operational issues or to start with the more strategic issues, the ABOR Changing Directions? 

           

Senator Johnson: Operational.

 

President Crow: So, we will start from the nearest term and move our way out.  Operational: three to twelve months.  The Governor has informed us directly and personally that there will be a higher-level expectation for potential return of the University?s state budget to the state, which is non-spending for 2003.  We have been operating over the last few weeks under a 5% reduction model; we are now operating under a 10% reduction model.  The 10% reduction model is probably not just a doubling of the expected amount of financial reduction but it is probably a tripling or quadrupling of the difficulty of meeting that level of financial reduction.  I do not recall the exact number but it is roughly in the zone of thirty million dollars that has to be returned from the present fiscal year allocation.  For those of you that manage your own money, imagine that level of reduction in your resources after you had already made your commitments for things like your mortgage and your car payments and so forth.  It is quite difficult.  Milt Glick and I and several others are working our way through how do we manage this, and what rules of thumb do we follow to govern our decision making as we deal with this level and magnitude of financial uncertainty.  I can tell you that our first rule of thumb is to hold all academic programs as harmless as we can, for as long as we possibly can by focusing our attention on non-central academic activities, non-core mission activities, central activities, central administration activities, dollars not yet spent and dollars not yet allocated.  We are right in the middle of it right now so I am not prepared to tell you unless Milt wants to stand up and give us the list, which I don?t think he is ready to do.  We are not ready to talk about how we are going to do that but I want to give you the general model of how we are going to do it.  I can tell you that a 10% budget reduction in the middle of this year with a possible 10% reduction or even higher in the next year is something that has all of our attention.  Now, we could sit and focus all of our energy on worrying about that or we could move in simultaneous directions, and I am not one to focus my attention only on the fact that the state is reducing out of its own fiscal crisis because they have a constitutional mandate that they cannot deficit spend.  As you all know the state received less revenue than any of their budget models could predict.  Given that, they have no choice but to cut spending.  Universities are the second most significant item of public expenditure, the second largest if you take them all together in the state?s discretionary budget.  If you have to balance your budget due to your constitution, you have balance your budget. You have no choice.  That is what we are facing now, as uncomfortable as that might be.  We can come back with any questions or comments to deal with 2003, but first, let us talk about 2004. 

           

We are also at the same time as we are dealing with these budget realities from the state relative to cutbacks for 2003?preparing, and have submitted to the Regents, who have approved, our 2004 budget request.  In that budget request, rather than continuing the fantasy of submitting a series of items that had zero probability of ever being funded or even ever being considered, with double digit budget increases in an era where we already know there is no probability of success, we have taken a different strategy.  The strategy that we have taken is to focus all of our energy in our budget request for 2004 and subsequently, if we continue to be successful in the process moving along, all of our political energy on two new things.  One is acquiring the resources for enrollment growth that are built into the formulas under which this institution has been core funded for a long period.  It is called ?22 to 1 resources? because those are not automatically available.  Those are not politically unassailable and they must be protected.  There are core means for dealing with both growth and budgetary inputs.  Second, we have concentrated all of the rest of our requests around one item, the acquisition of just under 200 million dollars of new capital for the construction of new research facilities, which will allow the University to move into certain research areas that have the capacity to both improve and enhance the University?s reputation, as well as improve and enhance the University?s revenue generation capacity.  This is a part of a broader strategy of working to seek investments that will enhance the University?s capacity to generate a more diverse set of revenues, from which we will have more money to work with in times of fiscal uncertainty like the one we have right now.  I can come back to that. 

 

Operationally we are focused on 2003 cuts and on the 2004 budget request, and there will be many discussions going forward about both.  Those are two relatively significant elements.  Very often, universities make the bad mistake of focusing all of their energies at this level only.  We are not doing that.  At the tactical level, the one to three year payoff level, there are several things that we have been doing at the same time as we are struggling with realities of the state?s fiscal condition.  We are focusing a fair amount of our attention on the 301 allocations that we have received, focusing on targeted expenditure areas?working with groups of faculty  around the University, working to build new centers around existing well established, highly capable faculty members, as a means of accelerating our process of building our capacity to do what the state has asked us to do with the 301 dollars, which is a complicated message.  At the heart of it is building our capacity to do and perform research and to expand and enhance the University?s reputation and expanding and enhancing the University?s economic development impact.  Second, we have in that same tactical area begun focusing a lot of energy and resources on the issue of making a series of targeted investments in a broad area of scholarship, in the humanities, in the arts, in business, in education and in the sciences and engineering.  We are in the process of working with groups of faculty, schools, departments and others to take these focused investments and move them into areas where we think that we can have the greatest impact and the greatest potential for return.  Lastly, in the midst of this fiscal crisis, we have decided to embark on the building of the Arizona Bio Design Institute building, and we have committed resources to the development and construction of most of this building under the assumption that it will help us to position ourselves to have additional capacity in terms of research.  In addition, for those of you not involved in these areas of research, if you leave this meeting thinking that research dollars are all I think about, you are wrong; it is not all that I think about?it is the closest means that we have now to generate new revenue streams, which helps all of us regardless of discipline.  The resources that come with the research dollars are significant and they help everyone. 

 

At the strategic level, which is the three to five years out impact, we are really focusing on the Arizona Board of Regents ?Changing Directions? exercise, which has moved from more of an exercise into an actual policy transformation process.  Beginning just after the announcement of my appointment in April, and then with subsequent discussions with various Regents between May and July, a series of questions came up, which were basically: What does Arizona State University need to be the most successful that it can be, and secondly, what does the Arizona State University system, that is the all three University system, need to be successful?  Those   discussions became concentrated at the Regents Retreat in August in Flagstaff where we spent a day and one half or so talking through a whole series of issues.  Milt Glick and I put forth several parameters that are important to the future of ASU.  One thing that we felt we needed was a movement of the University away from its box of being viewed by many as an agency--acting and thinking like an agency, highly dependent on the state, highly regulated by the state, highly controlled by the state--moving away from that to the notion of the University as an enterprise, where its destiny was more driven by its faculty and more driven by its leadership than by the legislature.  In doing that, we got a strong positive reaction from the Regents.  In fact, that became a central theme of the Changing Directions policy shift as it has begun to take shape.  We felt the second thing that we needed to argue for was differentiation, and you may have read this term being thrown about by some who were not at the meeting and have been struggling to interpret it.  It can be boiled down to the following:  Each of the three universities have been treated the same, in tuition, in resources and in allocation. In fact it is fascinating to see the lock step way in which the universities have been treated in terms of allocation.  We believe it is in the Arizona state universities? best interest, and I strongly believe that it is in ASU?s best interest, to differentiate at two levels: to differentiate its mission and its orientation from the other two state universities in a complimentary sense, and to differentiate within the University between the schools and the programs and between the campuses sufficiently, so that identity can be developed within campuses and between schools.  We are not going to spend much time talking about that today because that is another talk which we will have plenty of time to talk through.  We are a ways away from being able to present to the faculty an idea for everyone to consider and work on but we are thinking our way through that.  It is unequivocally the case that the notion of enterprise v agency and the notion of differentiation are deeply tied to the realization, by this particular set of Regents, that they are on the precipice of a critical decision relative to the well being of the universities in the long term.  There is a full understanding now that the universities are grossly under funded and these folks are highly focused on how to determine and how to identify ways to begin to remedy that, including tuition adjustments, tuition differentiation, tuition differentiation within graduate and professional programs, between graduate and undergraduate programs, between campuses and between schools within universities.  At the conceptual level we have reached a consensus between the Regents and the Presidents relative to that differentiation alone.

 

One example is the Law School.  We are in the Great Hall of the Law School, with a great faculty and great students and so, someone could ask why is it that our Law School is so under funded and why as a result of its under funding is it not rated anywhere near as high as it should be rated academically and the answer would be that one of the rating conditions is investment in the Law School!  You will find out that its tuition is not in the top 100 of law schools that are out there.  Therefore, differentiation means that maybe our tuition of our law school will be driven by something associated with the market value of the law degree and with the capacity of certain folks to be able to return and pay back the investment that a law degree requires to acquire and so forth.  I will not go through all of the details on that other than to suggest that at the strategic level we are focusing on the Changing Directions policy. 

 

Differentiation relative to the University itself and then we will turn to your questions.  Differentiation on the part of the University itself means establishing a clearly identifiable mission for this University, which is built on the foundation on which the University already rests.  This already is a comprehensive, quality, metropolitan university.  We need to take it one or two steps further down that same trajectory to enhance those descriptors  to comprehensive metropolitan research university of significant, local impact with high levels of local embeddedness.  This is not the time or the forum, because we will have other times and other forums to talk through all that means, or all that might mean.  That is a significant difference from a land grant research university and that is the vision that is beginning to emerge for that institution.  The notion that somehow a comprehensive large scale metropolitan research university would abandon the notion of quality, I have mocked in the following way--by informing the Regents that next year we would be admitting 400,000 students to this institution and shrinking the faculty to seven.  I said to them, we do not need buildings anymore because tents would certainly suffice.  They got the point.  There is no abandonment, no retreat from the notion of quality, because any institution that retreats on quality will not succeed in the end.  We can spend some time talking more about that. 

 

Before going to questions, the three levels of information are--and today is not a policy talk, it is an informational talk?at the operational level we are dealing with the 2003 cuts; we have to find a way to absorb those with minimal academic program impact.  By the way, that will mean that if we get into an actual implementation of a 10% cut, don?t call up and ask me, why have you killed off the Sun Dome in Sun City that this University invests in.  That is not a core academic program.  On the other hand, do not ask why we have cut the University?s contribution to other non-academic programs.  It is because we have no choice.  Would you rather that we come and say that we cut five positions from the English faculty to be able to continue our programs in Sun City at the Sun Dome?  I don?t think so.  Therefore on 2003 and those budget cuts we intend to be as focused as we can, protecting the academic core, and for 2004 we are focusing our energy on the one to three year time frame; we are still trying to make investments.  We obviously will not be able to make as many investments as we had hoped to be able to make but we still continue and are planning to make investments.  Strategically we are focusing on the Changing Directions activity with the Regents and the Regents? staff.  With that, I will stop and ask if you have comments or questions. 

 

Senator Dorman: One of the things that is usually correlated with a great university is a high admission standard.  You [President Crow] derived some pride in saying that I went to Harvard and it is hard to get into.

Can we as a university afford to do that, or are we tied to the dollar per student issue because we need those dollars?

 

President Crow:  I was on the faculty for the last eleven years of an institution like that, where they had a prime directive on how many students could they get to apply and there was great pride gained in turning away as many students as possible.  While that can provide for the selectivity that one wants to fill one?s classes--with only students who are able to express certain forms of their intelligence at highly capable levels, it is not the only way to build a high quality university.  There are other ways.  I happen to think that one of the other ways is to have highly defined programs within the university that have their own admission standards, their own requirements for admissions that make the students most likely to be able to succeed within those programs, and I will provide an example, from our College of Engineering.  In engineering, obviously to be successful in an engineering program requires high mathematical ability.  There are other ways to teach technology oriented activities; there are others ways to teach things that don?t require such high levels of mathematical ability--by allowing students with high mathematical proclivities to be attracted to the engineering school with standard X and those students without high mathematical proclivities (who are in fact intelligent and capable and high achieving) to move into other programs.  I believe if one thinks this through, we have the capacity to actually enhance our overall University record by having a higher quality engineering program attracting higher quality students with a higher average SAT score, while other students who might have been more suited for another program would be applying to and successfully gaining admission to those programs.  I believe because I have worked the numbers, as have others, that we will see a net overall gain in our University?s standing and ranking.  Therefore, there are ways to accomplish this effect and still be large.  The schools that you mentioned (you mentioned one and I mentioned another) are purposefully small.  The total undergraduate student body in liberal arts and sciences at Columbia University is 4,100 students.  It has only grown its class size four times since 1940 and does not intend to grow it again.  We cannot set on a university wide basis those kinds of boundary conditions, but we could on a school wide basis within the university grow our schools to levels of excellence where we attract high quality students to each school, rather than the more averaging effect that we have right now.  It is a long discussion but one that we are giving some concentration to now, as well as what we hope to be able to do down the road, which is the further growing of the Barrett Honors College into its next stage of morphing.  There are some thought exercises underway right now on how we can do that. 

 

Senator Munk: As you consider new sources of revenue, in particular for construction, are congressional earmarks one possibility?  I know there is a lot of controversy associated with those. 

 

President Crow: I think that at our stage of evolution and vulnerability as a research institution, that if we become heavily involved in earmarked activities, we will be punished by the funding agencies more than other more established research universities.  That pathway to funding is not at the moment high on my list.  What is high on my list is convincing the state as we are in the process of having taken the first step to do--we convinced the Regents last week and now we have to convince others to make?are  investments in facilities themselves.  We believe they then can generate sufficient revenue to help us to spurn on the development of other facilities down the road.  Therefore, earmarking at the moment is not a part of the strategy.  Lacking the state?s engagement, one would reconsider that pathway but it also is the case that the state is not well equipped politically to deal with that particular approach to funding.

 

Senator Haynes: How do you see faculty involved in these various enterprises, I realize that is not crucial at this time, but as time goes on how can we become involved?

 

President Crow: I see faculty involvement as central to the shaping, the strategy and the means by which we advance the institution through this forum, by working with Senate leadership, and by working with individual faculty leaders.  I see multiple forums and multiple levels of input.  Faculty send many signals, even organized faculty groups send many signals, so the signals have to sometimes be sorted.  I would be looking to faculty leaders to help with that sorting process, because I don?t have the means to know the history behind lots and lots of things that have gone on or been promised, or promises that were not kept, or believed they were not kept.  One needs a tremendous amount of input as one goes along.

President Crow: I do not remember all the investments.  There were about thirty, and they do run the spectrum of all areas of the intellectual realm that we sit in. among which is Nursing.

President Crow: Each of the vice presidents--I will pick on the one that is here today, Senior Vice President and Provost of the University, Milt Glick, who in his new position as Executive Vice President and Provost of the University--intends to reflect the notion that this is one University with multiple sites that we operate in.  Milt is the Chief Academic Officer for all academic programs, at all places that the University operates.  Therefore, Milt and I have the following agreement: Milt has his core agenda that he has been working on for many years and has been adding to and subtracting from on an annual basis, and he has all the other things that just come his way on a daily, weekly or monthly basis.  He also has a list of about 50 things that he got from me that I am hoping that he can integrate into his agenda.  That list that you have is some facsimile of that part of his agenda.  That is not his comprehensive agenda; it is not my comprehensive agenda.  Those are items that I have put on the agenda that are of particular interest to me, either to test what the system can do, how the system can react to begin moving in new directions and to see how teams can work together or not work together.  That list is reflective of a whole bunch of different criteria.  I do not know if people have seen that or not.  I think David Young and other deans have seen it.

Senator Park-Fuller: I understand. I guess the question I had then is will faculty have a formalized opportunity to provide input on these issues?one was for increasing faculty salaries for high performers?there was another: bringing faculty salaries to the 50th percentile of peer institutions.

Senator Burstein:  This came about because of a case that the Arizona Supreme Court ruled on, commonly referred to as the Kimball Case.  It has given the grievant the right of representation by counsel.  Before this, the grievant was only able to add ?advice by counsel.?  The counsel could not fully represent the grievant.  It is currently the case, even in the hearing that we are doing, that there is full representation by counsel.  The grievant gets an opportunity to decide the level of representation that they want in their grievance.  The person can represent himself or herself, in which case the respondent and the committee get no representation.  The person can be advised by someone, in which case the committee and the respondents get advice, or the person may be fully represented by legal counsel, in which case the committee and the respondents are fully represented by legal counsel.  It also gives subpoena power to the hearing committee and it gives the grievant the ability to appeal the President?s decision based on the committee?s recommendation to the President, on specific grounds. 

 

When I was President of the Academic Senate, all these were incorporated into the ACD Manual of our University, and then this served as the model used by the other two universities in their policies.  What the Regents are doing now is incorporating that into the Conditions of Faculty Service, so this I believe is pro forma more than anything else, in terms of making the Conditions of Faculty Service in agreement with the already established ACD policies of the three universities.  Are there any questions? 

 

Senate President Watson: This is up on the Senate?s web site as well, if you are interested in looking it over and giving it to your faculty as information.  It really serves as an additional option of the grievant; if the grievant chooses to have counsel, then everybody has counsel.  If the grievant chooses to not have counsel, then no one will have counsel.  There are a few other options as well beyond that.  In general though that is the way it works.  We will move forward if there are no other questions.
