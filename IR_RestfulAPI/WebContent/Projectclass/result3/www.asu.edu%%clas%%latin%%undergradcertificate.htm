General Overview

The Latin American Studies Certificate is intended to provide students with a multi-faceted exploration of the histories, cultures, and contemporary status of the people living in the Spanish and Portuguese speaking Americas.

This Certificate is designed for:

    * ASU undergraduates in any major who wish to enhance their degree and employment possibilities by adding expertise in Latin America.
    * ASU Bachelor of Interdisciplinary Studies undergraduates who seek a certificate program for their degree.

The purpose of the Certificate Program is to encourage undergraduate students to develop a broad-based understanding of Latin America--through the study of its history, society and politics, language and literature, environment and culture--while pursuing other degrees at ASU. In order to gain this understanding, students take coursework in a variety of different disciplines and departments. The knowledge and understanding gained from this endeavor will benefit students both in their future academic work and in their employment in the public or private sector. Completion of the Certificate is noted 1) on the student's transcript, and 2) via a Certificate signed by the Director of the Center and the Dean of the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences.

Application Process

Anyone interested in earning the Certificate must make formal application at the Center for Latin American Studies. This Certificate is available to all ASU undergraduate students, regardless of major. Students may begin taking courses before they formally register for the Certificate; however, they should apply for admission as soon as possible. An application form is available--please complete the top portion and bring it to the Center for Latin American Studies in Room 4450 of the Lattie F. Coor Hall.

Certificate Requirements

Requirements for the Certificate Program are satisfied through formal language instruction and by completing upper division courses taught on Latin American topics.

   Language Requirement:

    * The minimum requirement in formal language instruction is SPA313 or POR313.
    * Native speakers, or those with in-field experience equivalent to SPA313/POR313, can have this requirement waived. Equivalence will normally be assessed by a faculty member in the Department of Languages and Literature.
    * While students are encouraged to enroll in language instruction courses (i.e., conversation and composition) above the 313 level, they cannot apply such course work to the Latin American course requirements.

   Latin American Content Course Requirements:

    * Students will take 30 upper division semester hours (10 courses) from the list of Latin American Content courses filed with the Latin American Studies Center. Students are encouraged to select courses both from within their major, and from other disciplines.
    * Language instruction courses dealing with Spanish and Portuguese conversation and composition cannot be used to satisfy these requirements.
    * Courses used to satisfy these requirements must receive a minimum grade of 'C'. ASU Main and West Campus will supply courses for this certificate.

At least six (6) of the ten (10) courses must be taken at ASU Main.

Community Outreach

The Center for Latin American Studies also seeks to enter into partnerships with community organizations and the Arizona business community with an interest in Latin America. Our state's economy is increasingly tied to international trade. Of this overall trade, Arizona businesses are trading more with Latin American countries than ever before.

Mexico has overtaken Japan to become Arizona's primary trading partner. Arizona exports to Brazil have made a dramatic 700% increase in the last few years- growing from $17 million in 1993 to about $125 million through 1997. The copper mining industry in our state is similar to Chile's primary economic activity. High technology industries in Arizona are finding more and more markets throughout Latin America.

There is also heightened interest in the sub regional trading blocs such as MERCOSUR in the Southern Cone, the Andean Pact countries comprised of Bolivia, Peru, Ecuador and Colombia, the Central American Common Market and of course, the North American Free Trade Agreement.

The hemispheric goal of creating a Free Trade Area of the Americas will increase economic contacts between Arizona and the rest of this hemisphere in the years ahead. The CLAS can serve as a focal point for identifying programs that provide opportunities for partnerships for our larger Arizona community in the areas of language acquisition, socio-cultural exchange, and economic development opportunities.

Program Summary

U.S. - Mexico Border Studies Colloquiums: A lecture series featuring renowned experts on issues related to the United States-Mexico borderlands.

Student Exchange and Educational Programs in Mexico, Ecuador, and Bolivia: These programs provide participants with a well rounded educational experience featuring language study, culture, anthropology, sociology, history, politics and geography, depending on program selected.

Special Events

Community events highlight the diverse peoples and cultures of Latin America. The Focus on Brazil Program (Enfase no Brasil), was a day-long activity to acquaint our community with the music, art, cuisine, and film of Brazil.

Friends of the Center

The CLAS outreach efforts includes the Friends of the Center for Latin American Studies. This association comprised of community and business leaders who have an interest in the people and nations of Latin America. It was organized to help identify and plan activities for the community at large. The Friends hold numerous social, cultural and business events to further link Arizona State University and the non-campus community. Through their annual Festival Latino, the Friends raise funds for students' scholarships, faculty research and other programs of the Center.
