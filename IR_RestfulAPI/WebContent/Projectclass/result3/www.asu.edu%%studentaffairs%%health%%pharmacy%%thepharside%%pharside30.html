Pharmacy Student Fights for Contraceptive Coverage

 

            In February of 2001, I became insured through my current employer, Albertson's-Osco. It was the first time I had insurance on my own, independent of my parents. I had taken it for granted that my birth control pills would be covered, but much to my dismay, my statement of benefits booklet told me otherwise.

            I knew this was unacceptable, but I wasn't sure how to make a change in company policy, especially a huge company that employs thousands of people. I sent an email to Planned Parenthood in Tucson, and they referred me to my attorney, Grace McIlvain. She told me about www.covermypills.com.  I used a template available on the website to write a letter to the Albertson's benefits coordinator. My letter was ignored.

            After the first step was completed, Grace helped me to file a complaint with the EEOC regarding their sexual discrimination in health care coverage in October 2001. Four other women came forward during the following months and also filed complaints to make our case stronger. The EEOC decided in our favor, and we began filing the complaint that officially began the lawsuit in November 2002.

            Thankfully, Albertson's was open to negotiating the terms of the class action settlement with Grace McIlvain and a huge team of talented lawyers. The first conciliation meetings between our lawyers and the representatives from Albertson's took place in January 2003.

Finally, in October, the terms of the settlement were finalized. On February 9th, 2004, I was able to watch a federal judge sign our class action settlement that will ensure contraceptive coverage for all employees of Albertson's and their dependents.

        Stephanie Thune, PharmD. Candidate

Feeling Blue?

Have you been feeling sad or out of sorts lately?  You’re not alone.  More than 19 million adults in the United States suffer from depression.  Depression affects women 2x more than men.

Most people experience depression symptoms from time to time.  Help is needed when the symptoms last for over 2 weeks on a daily basis and/or interfere with normal daily activities (going to class or work, talking to friends, bathing, etc).  Some signs of depression include:

·        Mood changes: sad, anxious, or vacant

  • Feeling that there is no hope, lack of self-worth, or helpless
  • No interest in pleasurable activities or hobbies
  • Feeling tired and worn-out
  • Trouble remembering, concentrating, and decision-making
  • Sleeping problems: too much or too little
  • Weight loss or gain 
  • Irritable and restless
  • Headaches, upset stomach, and general pain unresponsive to treatment
  • Suicidal thoughts or attempts

If you have been feeling like this for a few weeks or more, there is help available!!!  ASU has many resources available for students needing assistance.  The Counseling and Consultation Center on campus is available to help you through the difficult time.  Your problems are confidential. 

You can reach them by phone

at 480-965-6146 to set up an appointment or have questions answered.  The fee structure is available on line at              www.asu.edu/counseling_center/eligibilityandfees.  Your first assessment visit is free!!

Please remember that there is help for you.  Depression is not your fault.  You aren’t a weak person because you are depressed.  It is a treatable illness like any other.  No one needs to suffer in silence!!

Kerri Pomaville, PharmD Candidate

Treatments for Depression

            There are many treatment options for people who suffer from depression.  Some people are able to get help from therapy or behavioral modifications alone. However, many people who have depression require medications to control their symptoms.  If you need medication, do not feel as if you are inadequate.  You cannot simply “snap out of it” or “get a grip.” 

            The most popular and safe group of medications are the SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors).  These medications include Zoloft, Prozac (fluoxetine), Paxil (paroxetine), Celexa (citalopram), and Lexapro.  All are equally effective, however people respond differently to medications.  Some common side effects include nausea, insomnia, dry mouth, decreased sex drive and/or drowsiness. 

            Newer antidepressants target multiple neurotransmitters (norepinephrine and dopamine) in the brain in addition to serotonin.  These include, Effexor, Cymbalta, Wellbutrin (bupropion), Serzone (nefazodone), and Remeron (mirtazapine).  These can have all of the above side effects as well as increased heart rate/ palpitations.  More drowsiness is seen with Remeron and may help those that have insomnia as a symptom.  Effexor and Wellbutrin tend to have sleeplessness as a side effect and are best taken in the morning.  People with seizure disorders should shy away from Wellbutrin as this drug increases the risk of seizures.  Serzone also has been reported to cause rare causes of liver failure. 

            Tricyclic antidepressants were used frequently before the discovery of SSRIs and the newer drugs.  They have fallen out of favor due to their unpleasant side effects.  The drugs include amitriptyline, imipramine, desipramine, and nortriptyline.  The side effects include very dry mouth and skin, decreased sex drive, drowsiness, dizziness, constipation, weight gain and urinary problems.  These drugs are still used for in lower doses mainly for painful neurological conditions and sleeping problems.

            The last resort antidepressants are known as MAOIs (monoamine oxidase inhibitors).  The names are Nardil, Parnate, and Marplan. They have side effects like the other drugs mentioned before.  However, there are severe dietary restrictions with these drugs, since foods containing tyramine (cheese, wine, chocolate) may cause severe, life-threatening hypertension.  These drugs are mainly used for patients in severe depression who do not respond to any of the other antidepressants.

            All of these drugs require a 4-6 weeks trial before effectiveness can be determined.  If side effects are experienced, many should subside within a couple of weeks after starting the medication or increasing the dose. 

            Remember, do not give up!!  If one medicine doesn’t work, there are many others to try.  There will be one that works for you!!

Kerri Pomaville, PharmD Candidate

Worts and All

In recent years, herbal medications have become much more prevalent and popular in your local drug store.  Many people seem to think that since herbal medications are natural, they are both safe and better for you than traditional medications.  However, what many people don’t realize is that many herbal medications can actually be harmful to your health and can interact with both prescription and non-prescription medications.  One of the most commonly prescribed medications here at Student Health is birth control pills, or hormonal contraceptives.  Did you know there are some herbal medications that can decrease the effectiveness of your birth control pill? 

The most common herbal medication known to do this is St. John’s Wort.  St. John’s Wort is used to treat symptoms of mild to moderate depression, anxiety, and sleep disorders, and is one of the top-selling herbal medications in the United States.  However, since the FDA does not regulate herbal medications, there is no guarantee that the herbal medications you buy over the counter actually contain any active medication at all.  If taken together, St. John’s Wort can speed up the clearance of birth control pills from your body, which can lead to decreased efficacy of your birth control pills.  If you are currently taking birth control pills and are also taking St. John’s Wort, talk to your doctor or pharmacist to see if there is another alternative to treat your symptoms of depression.

There are many “natural” products on the market and new one seems to appear daily.  Very few of these have actually been studied for drug interactions.  Be careful!

~Jackie Cavanagh, PharmD Candidate

 


Free of Chains in North Dakota

 

Pharmacy has changed drastically over the past decades.  Massive chain pharmacy corporations, giant managed care organizations and, more recently, mail-order pharmacies have serve to alter the image of pharmacy away from the traditional “mom and pop” drug stores of yesteryear.  There is, however, a bastion of independence that supports Mom and Pop as owners of neighborhood drugstores. 

            North Dakota state law 43-15-35-5 says:

“The applicant for such permit is qualified to conduct the pharmacy, and is a licensed pharmacist in good standing or is a partnership, each active member of which is a licensed pharmacist in good standing , or a corporation or association, the majority stock in which is owned by licensed pharmacists in good standing, actively and regularly employed in and responsible for the management, supervision, and operation of such pharmacy.”

This provision may help to explain the evident shortage of chain retail pharmacies within the state of North Dakota.  Essentially, the closest to a chain drugstore would be a few Medicine Shoppes, which are majority-owned by pharmacists and therefore meet the standards of the state law.  You will not find super market chain pharmacies, Walgreens or Wal-Mart pharmacies selling prescription medication in North Dakota.

        - Dominin Hrabe, Pharm. D. candidate

 

Sleep disturbances:

 

Research shows that solitary sleepers sleep for shorter periods and wake up more often than those with bedmates. 

A Mayo Clinic study found that more than half of people seeking help at their sleep clinics were kept awake by dogs or cats.  41% of the patients were bothered by restless pet bedmates and 58% were disturbed by noise of pets sleeping in the same room, including snoring dogs.
