April 24, 2003

ROTC program gives cadets hands-on look at military life

Black Hawk helicopters and M-16's aren't just being used 7,000 miles away - they're being used here in Arizona's own back yard.

Cadets in ASU's ROTC program are being offered a chance to be trained with the same equipment used in Operation Iraqi Freedom with the added benefit of a structured academic program. This two- to four-year program allows students to engage in hands-on military experiences while earning three elective credits per semester.

"We are developing the future leaders of the Army," said Major Herbert Chong, head of the ROTC department. "We like to think of ROTC as the best leadership course on campus."

According to Chong, there are three core elements that ROTC is all about: fitness, academics, and leadership. To be an ROTC cadet is to go to a class twice a week, attend physical training up to three times a week at 6:30 a.m., go to a lab once a week and participate in other activities such as a field training exercise once a semester.

The field training exercises allow cadets to participate in such war simulations as using M-16's, practicing land navigation techniques and undergoing other problem solving tests.

The hands-on applied learning by "going out and doing various activities" is geography freshman Kymberly Boyer's favorite part of being in ROTC. According to Boyer, "It's a very fun, educational experience."

Juan Tabora, an environmental technology management junior, served for eight years in the Army before joining ROTC and therefore was promoted to the 300, or junior, level. This is a critical year for Tabora, because this is the year when he undergoes more scrutiny.

The better he does, the better his chances are at receiving his choice in what he wants to do in the army after graduation. He will be tested in such areas as academics, leadership and physical fitness.

"ROTC gives you the leadership skills to be able to lead soldiers at a managerial level," Tabora said.

Boyer is in her first year in the ROTC program but is no stranger to military service. She was part of JROTC her freshman year of high school, and has many family members that are in the military. However, she still learns by example from older cadets such as Tabora.

"I look up to them and try and learn things from them," Boyer said. "I watched and listened in the beginning of the year when I didn't know what I was doing. It's all about setting goals for yourself. It's all about being committed."

Tabora said that with respect to the war in Iraq, he feels prouder to be in the program.

"We tend to think about our fellow soldiers a lot more. Like any other group or team, you get closer through adversity," Tabora said.

"I think the war has strengthened their commitment to what they're doing," Chong said.
