New Faculty - College of Liberal Arts

Susan Scarberry-Garcia
Assistant Professor, Department of English
 Dr. Scarberry-Garcia is the author of Landmarks of Healing: A Study of House Made of Dawn and of Dancing Spirits: Jose Rey Toledo, Towa Artist. She has also written numerous articles on the literatures and arts of the American Southwest. After obtaining her Ph.D. in comparative literature at the University of Colorado, Boulder, she taught as visiting faculty at Colorado College where she has recently served as Endowed Chair of Southwest Studies. She has also taught at the University of California, Los Angeles; Fort Lewis College; and Navajo Preparatory School . She has served as president of the Association for the Study of American Indian Literatures and chair of the executive committee for the Division of American Indian Literatures of MLA. Dr. Scarberry-Garcia now writes comparatively about collaborations with Navajo and Pueblo artists and about her excursions with colleagues into Western Siberia, Russia to work with native Khanty and Nenets writers. In 2004, Scarberry-Garcia was awarded an Honorary Citation/Gold Medal for Special Services to the Native Peoples of Siberia by the United Nations, International Decade of the World’s Indigenous People. Dr. Scarberry-Garcia joins the ASU Department of English as a specialist in Native American literatures.

 

Christina Schatzman
Assistant Professor, Department of Political Science
Dr. Schatzman received her Ph.D. in the summer of 2005 from the Department of Political Science at the University of New Mexico. Her research, supported by the National Science Foundation and the Tinker Foundation, examines the juncture of domestic and international factors on privatization reform. Her current research interests include market reforms in developing countries and the impact of social protest on policy outcomes.

 

Alexander Smith
Assistant Professor, Department of English
Dr. Smith received his doctorate from Princeton University in twentieth-century American literature, with a specialization in early twentieth century Southern fiction. Prior to coming to ASU, he was assistant professor of American studies in the English Department of Carleton College, an appointment he held for two years. He has also taught courses at Ryder College and at Rutgers University, Newark. Included in his publications are articles on William Faulkner and Eudora Welty, which have appeared in PMLA, Novel, and American Literature. His monograph (Cornell University Press, forthcoming 2005) is entitled Postmodernism Revisited: Faulkner, Welty, O’Connor, and Tolson. He has recently been elected to the Modern Language Association’s Delegate Assembly.

 

Brian H. Smith
Professor, School of Life Sciences
A neurobiologist with a solid background in bee behavior, Dr. Smith left The Ohio State University this year to join the School of Life Sciences and its distinguished and growing group of researchers studying bee behavior. After earning his Ph.D. from the University of Kansas, Dr. Smith completed several postdoctoral fellowships before accepting the faculty position at OSU. He earned a NATO fellowship in science, a National Institute of Mental Health National Research Service award, and most recently became a fellow in the American Association for the Advancement of Science. He has served as a mentor to a number of undergraduate and graduate students, and has employed postdoctoral fellows in his labs for many years. Numerous research grant awards, including those from the National Institutes of Health, the National Institute of Mental Health, the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency, and the National Science Foundation have resulted in publications in prestigious journals including Nature, Evolution, Ecology, Animal Behavior, the Journal of Experimental Physiology, and the Journal of Comparative Psychology. Dr. Smith will assume responsibility for teaching biology for non-majors, as well as courses in behavior, neuroscience, and cell biology.

 

Michael E. Smith
Professor, School of Human Evolution & Social Change
Dr. Smith joins the faculty from the State University of New York, Albany where he taught for 14 years. His Ph.D. in anthropology was awarded by the University of Illinois, Urbana-Champaign in 1983. Dr. Smith is an archaeologist who has directed excavations at a number of Aztec sites in central Mexico . He has published six books on his research. These include a popular textbook, The Aztecs (Blackwell, 2nd edition, 2003), technical archaeological reports, and edited collections on Aztec political and economic organization. He has also published numerous scholarly articles and has established a series of web sites with information for both specialists and the public. In addition to Aztec topics, Dr. Smith’s research interests include comparative early urbanism and imperialism, and archaeological method and theory. He has been director of the Institute for Mesoamerican Studies, book review editor for Latin American Antiquity, and is a founding editor for the book series, “Ancient Cities of the New World ” (University Press of Florida). He serves on numerous editorial boards and professional panels in the United States and Mexico . His next book will be entitled Aztec City-State Capitals.

 

Vibeke Sorensen
Professor, Department of Languages and Literatures/Arts, Media and Engineering
Vibeke Sorensen is an artist and professor working in digital multimedia and animation, interactive architectural installation, and networked visual-music performance. Her work in experimental new media spans three decades and has been published and exhibited worldwide in books, galleries, museums, conferences, performances, film festivals, on cable and broadcast television, and the internet. She taught and developed programs in digital media at Virginia Commonwealth University, the Art Center College of Design, the California Institute of the Arts, and Princeton University. From 1994 to 2005, she was professor and founding chair of the Division of Animation and Digital Arts, School of Cinema-Television at the University of Southern California. She has been a consultant for Disney and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory/NASA, and her research in new technologies has been supported by the National Science Foundation and Intel Corporation. She is a 2001 Rockefeller Foundation Fellow in film/video/multimedia and the 2005 Knight Distinguished Visiting Lecturer at the University of Manitoba , Winnipeg . Sanctuary, her most recent interactive installation made in collaboration with the midi@rte Laboratory of the Federal University of Minas Gerais, Belo Horizonte, Brazil and composer Shahrokh Yadegari of the University of California, San Diego, was premiered in Winnipeg, Canada in October of 2005.

 

Christopher Stojanowski
Assistant Professor, School of Human Evolution and Social Change
Dr. Stojanowski received his doctorate from the University of New Mexico in anthropology, with a specialization in contact period bioarchaeology. Prior to coming to ASU, he was an assistant professor of anthropology at Southern Illinois University, an appointment he held for two years. He also taught courses at Florida State University. His publications include articles on colonial demography and human microevolution and have appeared in American Antiquity, the American Journal of Physical Anthropology, and American Anthropologist. His monograph ( University of Alabama Press, forthcoming 2005) is entitled Biocultural Histories in La Florida : A Bioarchaeological Perspective.

 

Kathryn Frances Sykes
Assistant Professor, School of Life Sciences
Dr. Sykes is a molecular technologist who has been working to apply her expertise to vaccine discovery and development for the past 10 years. She received her Ph.D. from Duke University in the field of eukaryotic gene regulation and transcription. She pursued postdoctoral studies at the University of Texas Southwestern Medical Center (UTSW), in the Department of Biochemistry. She worked in the area of yeast molecular biology, applying it to the field of stress/chaperone proteins. She then became a research fellow within the UTSW Department of Medicine, where she became interested in translational research, as applied to modern vaccinology. During her time as a research assistant professor in the UTSW Center for Biomedical Inventions, she was involved in the development and implementation of a number of molecular vaccine technologies. These led to the founding of a local biotechnology company in Dallas , where she became director of research at MacroGenics, Inc. for three years before returning to the Department of Medicine at UTSW. This past summer she joined the faculty of ASU as an assistant professor in the School of Life Sciences, and she now conducts her research within the Biodesign Institute in the Center for Innovations in Medicine.

 

Kim TallBear
Assistant Professor, American Indian Studies Program
Kim TallBear is a doctoral candidate at the University of California, Santa Cruz in history of consciousness and a National Science Foundation graduate research fellow. Her dissertation looks at the cultural power of DNA and how ancestry DNA testing is being used to create a new category of “Native American.” She is particularly concerned that DNA-as-identity will undermine the governance authorities of federally-recognized tribes. Before attending UCSC, she worked as an environmental policy and program development consultant for several United States tribes, federal agencies, and national tribal organizations. Ms. TallBear is an enrolled member of the Sisseton-Wahpeton Oyate in South Dakota.

 

Patrick Thompson
Professor, Department of Mathematics and Statistics
Dr. Thompson received his doctorate from the University of Georgia in mathematics education. Prior to coming to ASU, he was a professor of mathematics education at Vanderbilt University, an appointment he held for nine years. During that time he served as the chair of the Department of Teaching and Learning from 2000 to 2003. He is the founder of the Vanderbilt Program for Talented Youth. Dr. Thompson is currently an emeritus professor of mathematical sciences at San Diego State University . He has published numerous books and journal articles on mathematics education. He is an associate editor for Cognition and Instruction and serves on the editorial board of the International Journal of Computers in Mathematical Learning and the Peabody Journal of Education. His major areas of research include algebraic reasoning in elementary and secondary mathematics, development of quantitative reasoning in mathematics and science, and development of students’ understanding of calculus.

 

Paul M. Torrens
Assistant Professor, Department of Geography
Dr. Torrens joins ASU from the University of Utah, where he was an assistant professor in the Department of Geography. He earned his Ph.D. in geography from University College, London, in 2004, where he was a research fellow in the Centre for Advanced Spatial Analysis. He also holds bachelor’s and master’s degrees from Trinity College, Dublin, and a master’s degree from Indiana University, in addition to having studied on scholarships at Kings College London and the University of Washington. His research has garnered grants from the United Kingdom Economic and Social Research Council, as well as industrial sponsorship from Alias, Inc. and Biographic Technologies, Inc. Dr. Torrens has interests in geographic information science and geosimulation and their application to the study of urban systems. He has over twenty publications on these topics and has just published a new book with John Wiley & Sons, entitled Geosimulation: Automata-Based Models of Urban Phenomena.

 

María L. Cruz-Torres
Associate Professor, School of Global Studies/Women and Gender Studies Program
Dr. Cruz-Torres received her doctorate from Rutgers University in anthropology, with a specialization in human ecology. Prior to joining ASU, she was an associate professor of anthropology at the University of California, Riverside . Her areas of teaching and research are gender and globalization; gender, sustainability and the environment; political ecology; impact of globalization upon local communities and households; and the environmental and social aspects of natural resource management. Her geographic areas of interest are Latin America and the Caribbean. Currently, she is conducting research on the topic of gender, globalization, and the environment in Mexico, and is also involved in a National Science Foundation bio-complexity, interdisciplinary, transnational collaborative research project focusing on the links between human and biophysical processes in coastal marine ecosystems in Baja California, Mexico. Some of her publications include: Lives of Dust and Water: An Anthropology of Change and Resistance in Northwestern Mexico ( University of Arizona Press , 2004), Local-Level Responses to Environmental Degradation in Northwestern Mexico (Journal of Anthropological Research 2001), and “Pink Gold Rush: Shrimp Aquaculture, Sustainable Development and the Environment in Northwestern Mexico ” (Journal of Political Ecology, 2001).

 

Takeyuki (Gaku) Tsuda
Associate Professor, School of Human Evolution and Social Change
Takeyuki (Gaku) Tsuda will be joining the faculty of the School of Human Evolution and Social Change as associate professor in July 2006. After receiving his Ph.D. in anthropology in 1997 from the University of California, Berkeley, he was a collegiate assistant professor at the University of Chicago and is currently associate director of the Center for Comparative Immigration Studies at the University of California, San Diego. His primary academic interests include international migration, diasporas, ethnic minorities and identity, transnationalism and globalization, ethnic return migrants, the Japanese diaspora in the Americas, and contemporary Japanese society. His publications include numerous articles in anthropological and interdisciplinary journals as well as a book entitled Strangers in the Ethnic Homeland: Japanese Brazilian Return Migration in Transnational Perspective (Columbia University Press, 2003). He is also the editor of Local Citizenship in Recent Countries of Immigration: Japan in Comparative Perspective (Lexington Books, forthcoming in 2006) and co-editor of Controlling Immigration: A Global Perspective (second edition, Stanford University Press, 2004). He has received research grants and fellowships from the University of California (Berkeley and San Diego), Fulbright-Hays, the Wenner-Gren Foundation, the Social Science Research Council, the Japan Foundation, and the Hewlett Foundation among others.

 

Carlos G. Vélez-Ibáñez
Professor and Chair, Department of Chicana and Chicano Studies
Dr. Vélez-Ibáñez joins the faculty from the University of California, Riverside where he was professor of anthropology and director of the Ernesto Galarza Applied Research Center. Dr. Vélez-Ibáñez was also the former dean of the College of Humanities, Arts, and Social Sciences and presidential chair in the Department of Anthropology at the University of California, Riverside from 2000-2002. Previously, he was professor of anthropology and director of the Bureau of Applied Research in Anthropology at the University of Arizona . He earned two master of arts degrees, one in English from the University of Arizona and a second in anthropology from the University of California, San Diego followed by his Ph.D. in anthropology from the latter institution. A fellow of the American Association of Sciences since 2002 and a former fellow of the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences at Stanford University , Dr. Vélez-Ibáñez received the Brownislaw Malinowski Award in 2003 and the Robert B. Textor Family Prize in Anticipatory Anthropology in 2004. He was also named as one of the 100 Most Influential Hispanics in the United States Hispanic Business in October 1997. His book, Border Visions: the Cultures of Mexicans of the Southwest, received the Choice Award in 1998.

 

Ning Wang
Assistant Professor, School of Global Studies
Dr. Wang earned his Ph.D. at the University of Chicago and has research interests that lie in the interface between law, polity, economy, and society, with a thematic focus on international development. He was educated at Beijing University and the University of Chicago where he was a student of Nobel Laureate in Economics, Ronald Coase. He has earned degrees in psychology, international relations, and human development, and conducted research in sociology and economics. The primary concern of Dr. Wang’s research is transition and developing economies, with a geographic focus on China. He has conducted intensive fieldwork in rural China, particularly the rise of commercial fishery, and is now launching a project to examine its auto industry. He grew up in a rice and fish farming village in south-central Hubei, China.

 

Douglas Webster
Professor, School of Global Studies
Dr. Webster received his Ph.D. in city and regional planning at the University of California, Berkeley . His primary research pertains to East Asia. He has worked for and been funded by the World Bank on various projects since 1980. He is currently working on a flagship study as an advisor to a team whose mission is to rethink the role of infrastructure provision in east Asia and to create a new policy paradigm for multilateral and private sector involvement in that infrastructure delivery. He has worked for the United Nations, the governments of Thailand and South Korea, the National Academy of Sciences, the Canadian International Development Agency, as well as other international agencies and for the private sector. He was professor of planning at the University of British Columbia, the Asian Institute of Technology, and the University of Calgary where he directed the urban planning program. He was also a visiting professor of the Asian Pacific Research Center at Stanford University . Most recently he was professor of geography and regional planning at Utrecht University and chairman while at the same time, professor for the International Institute for Geo-Information Science and Earth Observation in the Netherlands. His current teaching and research has focused on comparative global peri-urbanization.

 

Linda Sargent Wood
Assistant Professor, Department of History
Dr. Wood received her doctorate (2002) from the University of Maryland, College Park, in twentieth-century U.S. cultural history. Her research focuses on holistic thinking and practices that gained popularity in the United States between 1945 and 1975 and provided the era with an intellectual and moral motif for that post-war generation. Prior to coming to ASU, she was a visiting assistant professor at Northern Arizona University under a Teaching American History (TAH) grant from the U.S. Department of Education. The TAH grant was collaborative among NAU’s History and Education Departments, the Page Unified School District, and the Arizona K-12 Center. She provided historical content and pedagogical instruction to elementary and secondary teachings in Page, Arizona, administered a master’s program in education with a history emphasis, and coordinated the projects defined under the grant. Her background also includes fours years of teaching in public and private schools where she taught high school students history, literature, and composition. At ASU she will guide the History Department’s program in history education and expand offerings in U.S. cultural history.

 

Heather Harris Wright
Assistant Professor, Department of Speech and Hearing Science
Dr. Wright received her doctorate from the University of Georgia in communication sciences and disorders. Prior to coming to ASU, she was an assistant professor in the Division of Communication Disorders at the University of Kentucky . Dr. Wright is a certified speech-language pathologist. Before receiving her Ph.D., she worked full time as a speech-language pathologist in an adult rehabilitation facility. During her doctoral program and her time in Kentucky, Dr. Wright continued to see patients clinically, on a part-time basis. Her research and clinical interests and expertise are adults with acquired language or cognitive disorders, typically resulting from stroke or head injury. She is a full member of the Academy of Neurologic Communication Disorders and Sciences and is an editorial consultant for the Journal of Speech, Language, and Hearing Research. Dr. Wright’s publications can be found in several language journals, including the American Journal of Speech-Language Pathology, Brain and Language, and Aphasiology.

 

Jeffery L. Yarger
Professor, Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry
Dr. Yarger received his doctorate in physical chemistry from ASU in 1996. Prior to coming back to ASU, he was a professor of chemistry and physics at the University of Wyoming and Colorado State University, an appointment he held for seven years. Dr. Yarger’s research interests include amorphous and glassy systems, solid state nuclear magnetic resonance, battery and fuel cell materials, biopolymers and quantum dot structure. Dr. Yarger is the director of a new magnetic resonance research center at ASU and will continue active research in the field of biological chemistry and physics.
