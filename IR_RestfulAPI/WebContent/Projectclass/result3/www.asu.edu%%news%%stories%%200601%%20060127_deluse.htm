January 27, 2006

Featured Faculty: DeLusé connects with students in online courses

It takes a special kind of teacher to grab and hold the attention of students in an online course. Stephanie DeLusé, a lecturer in interdisciplinary studies, connects especially well with her online students by asking a great deal of them and supporting them with frequent personal e-mails and phone calls.

In her senior seminar, “Money and Meaning,” she gives them highly detailed assignments, and some students panic because they expected an online course to be a cakewalk. When they finish, many say it was one of the best and toughest courses they ever had.

DeLusé has been named the ASU Featured Faculty for January, for cultivating her students’ development inside and outside the classroom. More than 80 faculty members have been nominated by their students.

“Dr. DeLusé responds to my questions right away, and she follows up and shows she is concerned if I’m having a hard time,” says Kelly Berkley, an ASU senior. “She calls me to tell me the best way to go about a problem that I might be encountering in the course – and when I send her an e-mail, she replies with lengthy advice. “She has motivated me to do the best in every aspect of this class, which is surprising considering it is an online course. You can tell she really cares. She calls me and gives me advice, or lets me know how I could do better. She is one of the most effective teachers I have experienced throughout my career at ASU.”

The Featured Faculty award program was started two years ago by Co-Curricular Programs and Activities to recognize and celebrate the contributions of faculty in enhancing student learning outside of class. Three faculty members are chosen by a panel of students each semester, and each of them receives an honorarium.

DeLusé, who joined the ASU faculty in 1999, says she loves connecting with students and finds it possible to do in her online classes, though it takes special effort. In doing so, she still gets to see those priceless “Ah-ha!” moments.

“I design the class to get the student personally involved instead of making it just an intellectual exercise, and I provide plenty of course support and personal attention when needed, via e-mail or phone calls,” she says. “The course asks them for a lot but is well laid out and highly detailed, so typically they feel plenty of support.

“I encourage personal responsibility, but I am always available to help people when they hit a bump. Whether in a face-to-face class or an online class, I love facilitating individual growth, skill building, and broader awareness about challenges and opportunities for students on different levels. The growing complexity of the world in which we live truly requires students to be able to consider multiple levels and perspectives.”

DeLusé also teaches core courses in interdisciplinary studies, as well as another senior seminar, “Money, Medicine and Morals.” She is co-developing a new course, titled “Introduction to Organizational Studies.”
