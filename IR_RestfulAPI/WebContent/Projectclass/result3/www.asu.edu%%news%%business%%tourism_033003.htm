March 30, 2003

Arizona tourism has yet to recover from 9/11 effects

Tourism activity in Arizona plunged after the terrorist attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, and has yet to recover, according to a new Tourism Barometer developed by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center at Arizona State University.

The Arizona Tourism Barometer is an index that provides an aggregate monthly look at the health of the Arizona travel and tourism industry, which represents about $13 billion a year in direct spending in the state. The barometer incorporates both visitor and dollar components, and is seasonally adjusted.

The barometer began a downward trend in the beginning of 2001, as the last recession began. Except for a brief bounce in January 2002, it has remained fairly low. "Worries about unemployment, war and further terrorist attacks appear to have affected tourist sentiment and travel plans," according to Dawn McLaren, research economist with the Bank One Economic Outlook Center. In January 2003, the Arizona Tourism Barometer rose slightly to 94.8 from 92.7 the previous month - a 2.3 percent increase. "This is 4.5 percent higher than in January 2002, but still well below the level of 112.6 seen in August 2001, just prior to the terrorist attacks," McLaren noted.

The barometer reached a historic high of 121.9 in November 2000. The slight upward movement in the barometer was, for the most part, caused by an increase in visitation to Arizona's national parks. A decline in overseas visitation and in air carrier landings, however, offset most of that gain. "It remains to be seen whether overseas and domestic visitation to Arizona will be affected by the war in Iraq, and the increased security screenings for visas and entry into the United States," McLaren said.

For further information on the Arizona Tourism Barometer, call Dawn McLaren at (480) 965-7265, or visit the Web site, http://wpcarey.asu.edu/seid/eoc/atb/.
