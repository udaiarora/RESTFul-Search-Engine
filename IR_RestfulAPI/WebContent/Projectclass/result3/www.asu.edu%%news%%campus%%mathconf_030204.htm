March 2, 2004

Math department efforts add up to success

Exciting things are happening in undergraduate studies in the ASU Department of Mathematics and Statistics. Math majors now number about 450 - up from 350 just two years ago - and the department is hosting the first-ever Arizona Mathematics Undergraduate Conference Feb. 27 - 29, with 120 students coming from as far away as Texas and California.

It's all part of an effort by Glenn Hurlbert, associate chair for undergraduate studies, and department chair Andrew Bremner to make math fun and show students they can "do math" for a living. Hurlbert recruits heavily and provides math-related social activities for math majors once they're at ASU.

"We've been working really hard on it, coordinating with the Honors College to recruit students out of high school and recruiting from existing ASU students who perform well in calculus class," says Hurlbert. "Math can be a tough sell, but we show them the breadth of career possibilities and the fact that they can make good money.

"We'll have industry representatives at the conference, people from Lockheed Martin, General Motors and the National Security Agency, some of the largest employers of mathematicians on the planet. Students will give oral presentations on their mathematics research, and we hope they'll get to know each other and make contacts for graduate school and industry."

Hurlbert applied for and received a grant for the conference from the Mathematics Association of America. The College of Liberal Arts and Sciences pitched in also, along with the department and the provost's office.

About 25 percent of conference participants are ethnic minority students, which is unprecedented for a meeting of this type, Hurlbert says.

In between presenting theorems and soaking up math, students will socialize at a cookout at Tempe Town Lake and a visit to The Improv. ASU students who are members of the Undergraduate Math Club have negotiated deals for donated pizza, raffle items and T-shirts.

"You have to tug and pull them, and you have to make it engaging and fun," says Hurlbert. "Students are starting to realize the role math plays in underpinning much of modern technology. They can work in government research, statistics, actuarial sciences, the computer industry, anything high tech."

Math professor Anne Gelb will give a talk on MRI technology, for instance, and how mathematics can improve its diagnostic ability for professional athletes and others.

"We hope this will be an annual event that will rotate among the three Arizona universities," says Bremner, "although this will depend on UA and NAU having someone with Hurlbert's energy and enthusiasm. This conference is a great chance to bring in bright youngsters to interact with faculty and expose them to the fact that math can also be fun."
