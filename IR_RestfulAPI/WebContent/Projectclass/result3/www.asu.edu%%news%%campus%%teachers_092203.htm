September 22, 2003

ASU prepares to tackle teacher shortage through university-school partnerships

Arizona would need 6,880 new K-12 teachers annually through 2005 to keep up with an increasing student population and retiring educators, according to a report published by ASU's Morrison Institute for Public Policy.

ASU's College of Education, through the University-School Partnerships Initiative, is tackling the state's teacher shortage. Through its initiative, the university plans to train 50 percent more teachers for kindergarten through 12th grade, or a total of 1,500 each year, and 100 percent more than early-childhood education, or a total of 120 annually.

Vice President of University-School Partnerships Eugene Garcia says there's a tough road ahead and the university is preparing to meet the challenges.

"We have the commitment from the president for the faculty and related services needed to expand these programs," says Garcia, who is also the dean of College of Education. "The main challenges include recruiting the students to the programs; ensuring that those students are trained to meet the needs of the students of Arizona; and placing our graduates in the schools in which they are needed."

The report, "Is There A Teacher Shortage? Demand And Supply in Arizona," which can be found online at (http://www.asu.edu/ copp/morrison/teachershortage.htm), is the first comprehensive analysis of supply and demand for certified teachers in Arizona.

Garcia says recruitment and retention of teachers are key objectives in the initiative and are essential for addressing the supply and demand for teachers.

As the university's three campuses begin work this year to achieve these goals by 2005, a top priority will be to recruit graduates with a bachelor's degree and help them get a teaching certificate in 10 months. College students in non-education majors will be targeted for intensive summer programs so they can earn a teaching credential when they obtain their bachelor's degree.

In addition, Garcia says one of the areas ASU is concentrating on is making a comprehensive effort to find the support systems that educators need in order to do their job and do it well.

"They need on-going mentoring - a teacher learns most about teaching while she/he is teaching - therefore ongoing support and professional development is a must," he says. "Specifically, they need mentors who have been successful as teachers and they need to learn new ways of teaching - such as using technology to teach."
Placing teachers where they are most needed is a crucial part of the K-12 initiative. Garcia says there are programs that address this objective and meet the goals of the initiative.

"Developing 'grow-your-own' programs such as the Urban Teacher Corp and the Navajo Native Teacher Program are essential," he says. "We work with schools to take residents of their communities and develop them to be teachers so they can go right back into those schools."

Martha Rader with the ASU College of Education, applauds the university's efforts and also believes more attention should be directed to the teaching profession and its importance.

"We are doing our best to turn out really good teachers," says Rader, associate professor of curriculum and instruction. "We have an excellent program at ASU, and the teachers who come out of our program are very well-prepared; however more needs to be done."

Rader comes from an education family. Her grandfather was a superintendent of schools in Mississippi; her grandmother was a second grade teacher; and her daughter is currently an early childhood and ESL teacher in Litchfield Park. Rader says the key to teacher recruitment and retention is motivation.

"We can try to recruit more people into teaching, but we really need the cream of the crop and the best to go into teaching," Rader says. "The best teachers need to be in the earlier grades because that's when it's more crucial in student development. If you have motivated teachers, then students will achieve."
