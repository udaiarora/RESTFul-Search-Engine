Nora Coronado


September 3, 2004

Workshops Help Scholars Win Fellowships

Bioengineering graduate student Teresa Murray wanted a chance at winning a fellowship that would provide a reasonable standard of living and also look good on her resume. She faced fierce competition, so to gain an advantage she decided to learn exactly what it took to get a fellowship by signing up for a workshop offered by Arizona State University’s Division of Graduate Studies.

Murray credits the workshop, in which she took part in last fall, for helping her earn a distinguished National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellowship.

This fall, the Division of Graduate Studies again will offer a series of workshops designed to help students strengthen their applications for national fellowship programs, such as the National Science Foundation, Ford Foundation, and Jacob F. Javits Foundation.

The workshops are guided by students who are fellowship recipients and faculty members who have served on fellowship review panels. They provide great insight and tips on how to write successful applications. Competitions for national fellowships are open to ASU seniors, first–year graduate students, and in some cases second–year graduate students. All eligible students and their faculty mentors are encouraged to attend the workshops.

“The students at the workshop who had gotten fellowships gave concrete suggestions about the essays that were required,” Murray said. “They also gave hints as to how to get outstanding letters of recommendation.”

Full fellowships are the most prestigious form of funding for students and provide them with the best opportunity to focus exclusively on their scholarly pursuits, including course work, research, and dissertations. But while ASU has a handful of awardees, its aspirational peer institutions have many more, said Marjorie Zatz, an associate dean in the Division of Graduate Studies.

The workshops are a central feature of the initiative launched jointly by the Division of Graduate Studies, the Office of National Scholarships in the Barrett Honors College, and the Office of the Vice President for Research and Economic Affairs to boost the number of ASU students receiving national–level fellowships. As part of the initiative, the Office of the Vice President for Research and Economic Affairs and Division of Graduate Studies also have teamed together and created an incentive program that rewards faculty for their mentoring role in the development of successful fellowship applications.

“We have not had a culture of grant writing among graduate students at ASU,” Zatz said. “This initiative is designed to infuse the graduate community with an awareness of the importance of these fellowships for both the students and the institution. Fellowships provide students with great financial support and they are seen nationally as key indicators of graduate program quality.

“ASU has excellent students who could easily compete successfully for these awards, but they may not have received encouragement to do so or know how to write a successful proposal. That’s where this initiative comes in to play,” Zatz said.

Murray strongly recommends that students interested in fellowships attend the workshops.

“I would not go through this process alone and blind,” she said. “The students and faculty at the workshops have so many good suggestions. I followed those suggestions and got a National Science Foundation Fellowship.”
