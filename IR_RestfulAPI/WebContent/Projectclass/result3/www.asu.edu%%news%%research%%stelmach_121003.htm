Studying motor control deficits in elderly helps identify potential aids

Through their life spans, people face many changes due to the aging process. Whether it's sensory, perceptual or motor performance, the changes can often affect a person's ability to operate in the world.

ASU Professor George Stelmach is trying to gain a better understanding of one of these major declines by systematically studying motor control deficits in the elderly. The main issue for Stelmach, and his colleague Natalia Dounskaia, is to determine why movements typically become slower and more variable in older adults.

Through a four-year, $1.3 million grant from the National Institute on Aging, the researchers will use high-speed motion recorders to analyze reaching and aiming movements.

"It is clear that the population of aging adults 65 or older in the United States is entering a period of rapid growth, as baby boomers and life expectancy increases," says Stelmach. "My overall goal is to use scientific technology to identify the mechanisms that underlie movement deficits. Over the long term, we hope to better understand these mechanisms so targeted interventions to aid the elderly will be successful in the future."

Stelmach says when a person performs a task such as reaching out for an object, the movement has two distinct phases. First, the arm moves out toward the object, generally following a specific "motion trajectory." Then, as the arm nears the object, there is a slight modification of the movement, a sort of fine-tuning to better target the object. This second movement is done in a split second and goes unseen by the eye, but can be spotted using the high-speed data capture.

For many older people, however, Stelmach says the trajectory is often less than that of someone younger. He also has found that an older person will begin making adjustments much earlier and more frequently. These findings suggest that the movement structure of an elderly person's movement is very different from those of the young.

Stelmach says the cause for this decline is currently unknown. He does theorize that it could be due to some form of motor planning or even a slight loss of feeling in the extremities.

He believes finding the root cause of these seemingly subtle changes could have long-term quality of life implications. Given the importance and high costs of health care for elderly adults, technology that could improve health maintenance and health care, or reduce the associated costs, would be especially valuable.

"I think this could be an important breakthrough," he says. "The better we understand these declines, the better we can account for them and improve people's lives."

Stelmach's work with high-speed motion capture continues similar research in the Motor Control Laboratory in the ASU Kinesiology Department. Stelmach is currently working on several other research projects supported by the National Institutes of Neurological Diseases and Stroke and is the principal investigator for grants totaling more than $7 million.
