QUALITATIVE METHODS - APSA

PETITION PROPOSAL

 
        

        

 

DETAILED STATEMENT OF THE PROPOSAL

FOR A NEW APSA ORGANIZED SECTION

ON QUALITATIVE METHODS

 

This document summarizes the proposal to form a new

APSA Organized Section on Qualitative Methods. The

following paragraphs discuss: (1) the goals of the

proposed section; (2) the two pre-existing organizations

that are sponsoring this initiative; (3) the kinds of

activities the new section would sponsor; and (4) the

relationship of the new section both to the existing

APSA Political Methodology Section, and also to a new

proposal to expand APSA's coverage of methodology

within the annual program.

 

 

1. STATEMENT OF GOALS

 

The APSA Organized Section on Qualitative Methods seeks

to promote research and training focused on the several

branches of methodology broadly associated with

qualitative methods. These include: the case study

method; small- N analysis; the comparative method;

concept analysis; methodological work concerned with

the logic of inquiry; the comparative-historical method;

the ethnographic tradition of field research;

constructivism, which recently has been a strong focus

of attention in the field of international relations;

interpretive methods; and relevant branches of

social and political theory. The goal is to advance

the understanding of these different approaches,

to explore the commonalities, as well as contrasts,

among them, and to find productive ways of integrating

them with other branches of methodology, including

quantitative methods.

 

Within this framework, the section would devote special

attention to the following objectives.

 

A. RESEARCH AND TRAINING IN QUALITATIVE

METHODS. The section seeks to promote research and

training in qualitative methods, broadly understood. Although

qualitative research methods are widely employed in our

discipline, systematic attention to improving and teaching

these methods has been inadequate. A central goal is to

address this deficit. In offering training in qualitative

methods, the section would strongly encourage the

participation of scholars who are applying qualitative

methods in their own substantive research. The section

thus seeks the participation of users of qualitative

methods, as well as of scholars who have a more

specialized interest in methodological research on

qualitative techniques.

 

B. BRIDGING METHODOLOGIES. The section is centrally

concerned with integrating insights and research tools

offered by the several methodological traditions enumerated

above. At present, these different approaches are to a

certain extent balkanized. For example, the comparative

method is closely identified with the fields of comparative

politics and international relations; comparative-historical

methodology has strong links to the discipline of sociology;

important new debates on constructivism are centered in the

field of international relations; and some branches of concept

analysis are linked to the field of political theory. These

different approaches in fact often address similar analytic

problems, and we are convinced that they constitute a shared

intellectual and methodological enterprise to a greater degree

than is sometimes recognized. The new section would be actively

concerned with the commonalities and complementarities

among these several qualitative traditions, on the one hand,

and quantitative methods on the other. Thus, while we have

adopted a short, compact name for the section that identifies

a central aspect of our focus, we are also very much concerned

with exploring alternative ways of integrating different methodologies.

 

C. LOGICAL FOUNDATIONS. In addition to the goals

described under (A) and (B), and in the framework of the

pluralistic approach to methodology reflected in these goals,

another objective of the section is to strengthen these interrelated

branches of qualitative methods by drawing on statistical theory

and mathematical reasoning (above all, mathematical probability

theory) as a source of logical foundations. It is sometimes

mistakenly assumed that statistical theory and mathematical

foundations are distinctively connected with quantitative research,

and/or that they inherently serve as a rationale solely for doing

quantitative research. However, such foundations can also

provide important justifications for distinctive practices of

qualitative researchers, as well as pointing to potential

refinements in these practices. One of the priorities of the

section is to explore such justifications and refinements.

 

 

2. SPONSORS OF THE INITIATIVE TO FORM AN

ORGANIZED SECTION

 

This initiative is undertaken by a group of APSA members

who are actively concerned with broadening the discussion

of methodology within the political science discipline. At the

same time, this initiative also represents the combined effort

of two pre-existing organizations. We now discuss these two

organizations and their future relationship to the proposed section.

 

A. APSA COMMITTEE ON CONCEPTS AND METHODS

AND CQRM. The initiative to form the new APSA section is

being taken by a group of scholars closely associated with

(i) the APSA Committee on Concepts and Methods, an

official APSA Related Group; and (ii) the Consortium for

Qualitative Research Methods (CQRM), a legally incorporated,

non-profit organization, located at Arizona State University,

which supports scholarly communication and training workshops,

with a primary (though not exclusive) focus on the discipline

of political science. CQRM collaborates closely with various

APSA committees and organized sections, and its officers and

board members are all political scientists. (Further information

on CQRM may be found at <http://www.asu.edu/clas/polisci/cqrm/>.)

 

B. PROPOSED RELATIONSHIP OF NEW SECTION TO

CQRM AND TO THE APSA COMMITTEE ON CONCEPTS

AND METHODS. In the event that this organized section

is approved, the new section and CQRM would remain

separate entities, but would work closely together in

carrying out most of their activities. At its business

meeting at APSA 2003, the Committee on Concepts and

Methods adopted a motion that in the event that the

new organized section is formed, the present Committee

would be dissolved and its activities would be

incorporated into the new section. At the same time,

the Qualitative Methods Section would maintain

a close association with the IPSA Committee on

Concepts and Methods (IPSA Research Committee No.1).

 

 

3. ACTIVITIES TO BE SPONSORED BY

THE NEW SECTION

 

The activities of the new section would include:

 

A. Sponsoring panels at the annual APSA meeting. The

Committee on Concepts and Methods has recently

sponsored or co-sponsored five to seven panels per year,

covering a broad spectrum of methodological topics. The

new section would plan to expand these offerings.

 

B. Continuing the tradition of sponsoring short courses at

the annual meeting that was started by the Committee on

Concepts and Methods. These earlier short courses focused

on measurement, comparative method, field research methods,

and problems of bridging quantitative and qualitative methodology.

 

C. Jointly administering CQRM's Annual Training Institute.

In its inaugural session in January 2002, the Training Institute

brought together a talented group of 45 graduate students and

junior faculty from across the United States. These participants

were drawn from nominations made by the universities which

are members of CQRM, as well as from a national pool

of 200 applicants.

 

D. Jointly administering, and further expanding, projects

that have already been initiated or planned by CQRM,

including an electronic newsletter, a listserv, a syllabus

collection, and a working paper series.

 

 

4. RELATION TO THE EXISTING APSA POLITICAL

METHODOLOGY SECTION AND TO THE SHIVELY-

SIKKINK PROPOSAL ON APSA'S COVERAGE OF

METHODOLOGY

 

A. COOPERATION WITH THE POLITICAL

METHODOLOGY SECTION. The new section intends to

maintain cooperative relations with the existing APSA

Organized Section on Political Methodology. We hope to join

forces with that section in broadening the discussion of

methodological issues within APSA, and we have kept the

Political Methodology Section fully informed of our plans.

Over the past three years the APSA Committee on Concepts

and Methods, which is closely involved in this initiative, has

co-sponsored panels and short courses with the Political

Methodology Section, and we intend to continue that

collaboration. Future cooperation might include, for example,

working together in providing electronic access to working

papers, syllabi, and other scholarly material.

 

Regarding the division of labor between the two sections,

the Political Methodology Section has made an outstanding

contribution in sponsoring research and training in quantitative

methods, and the new section would not seek to duplicate this

effort. However, given that insights about quantitative research

can play a valuable role in the kind of work on qualitative

methods with which we are concerned, strong links between

the two sections are important to achieving our intellectual goals.

 

B. RELATIONSHIP TO SHIVELY-SIKKINK PROPOSAL.

In April 2002, W. Philips Shively and Kathryn Sikkink,

the Co-chairs of the Program Committee for the 2002 APSA

Meeting, submitted a proposal on APSA's coverage of

methodology to the APSA Standing Committee on the Annual

Program. Shively and Sikkink argue that the APSA needs to

augment both the number of methodology panels offered at

the annual meeting, and the breadth of topics - including

qualitative methods -- covered in these panels. They see

expanded coverage of methodology as a "public good" that

is of broad benefit to the profession, and they also note that

many recent discussions of methodology among political

scientists have taken place almost entirely outside of the

APSA program. Correspondingly, Shively and Sikkink

recommend that panels on methodology be given a higher

priority within the official program. We believe that our

proposed section would directly advance these dual goals

of enlarging and diversifying the APSA's role as a locus

of methodological discussions.

 

 

5. CONCLUSION

 

We wish to conclude by observing that for the purpose

of evaluating proposals for new organized sections,

the APSA Organized Section Handbook stipulates

that new sections should "increase intellectual

community within APSA and not lead to greater

fragmentation of the discipline." Given our combined

objectives of cooperating constructively with the

existing Political Methodology Section, at the same time

that we broaden the offerings on methodology of the

APSA, we are convinced that our proposed organized

section on Qualitative Methods meets these criteria.

PROPOSED BY-LAWS OF THE ORGANIZED SECTION

FOR QUALITATIVE METHODS, AMERICAN POLITICAL

SCIENCE ASSOCIATION

 

I. GOALS OF THE QUALITATIVE METHODS SECTION

 

The goals of the Section are to promote research and

training focused on the several branches of

methodology associated with the qualitative tradition;

and to strive for an integrated understanding of these

diverse methods and of their relationship to quantitative methods.

 

II. MEMBERSHIP IN THE SECTION

 

A. All members of the American Political Science

Association (APSA) are eligible for membership.

 

B. Membership in the section is established by current

payment of annual section dues to the APSA. The amount

of the dues will be established by the Executive Committee.

 

III. OFFICERS

 

The section will be governed by an Executive Committee

consisting of the President, Vice-President, Secretary- Treasurer,

four at-large members, and the Program Chair. The

President-Elect will also serve as a member of the

Executive Committee, and in the event that the Section

extends its publications to the point of having a

newsletter that requires an Editor, the Editor

would also be a member. All officers must be

members of the APSA and of the Section.

 

A. PRESIDENT. Chairs the business meeting of the section

at the APSA annual meeting; chairs meetings of the

Executive Committee; in consultation with other

officers, appoints the Program Chair; appoints

officers to fill unexpired terms; administers

the policies of the section; and performs other

duties that are in the interest of the section.

The president serves for a two-year term.

 

B. VICE-PRESIDENT. Assists the President in all

functions specified in Article III-A. and in the

absence of the President, may chair the annual

business meeting or executive committee

meetings. The Vice-President serves for a two year term.

 

C. PRESIDENT-ELECT. Likewise assists the President

In the above functions and becomes closely familiar with

the organization and functioning of the Section, with

the goal of ensuring coherence and continuity in the

leadership and administration of the Section. The

section member who is nominated and elected to be

President-Elect will serve for two years in that role,

and then for two years as President.

 

D. SECRETARY-TREASURER. Directs and produces the

publications of the section, as established by the Council;

maintains roster of members; administers mailings to the

membership; manages income and disbursements, and

keeps financial records, of the section. participating

in all aspects of section governance. The

Secretary-Treasurer serves for a three-year term

to ensure that (1) there is greater continuity in the

administration of the Section, and (2) the transition

between two Secretary-Treasurers will only

coincide with the transitions between Presidents half

of the time.

 

E. ANNUAL MEETING PROGRAM CHAIR. Organizes

panels at the annual meeting of the APSA and in other

settings as appropriate. The Program Chair serves for

one year.

 

F. AT-LARGE EXECUTIVE COMMITTEE MEMBERS.

Participate with other officers in governing the section.

The at large members will serve for two-year terms,

staggered to ensure that only two are rotated off the

Committee in any given year.

 

G. OTHER OFFICERS. In the event that other offices

are established, such as Editor of a newsletter, the

Executive Committee will establish appropriate

procedures for selecting these officers.

 

H. For all of the offices above except At-Large

Executive Committee Members, two people may

jointly fill the position. In such cases, they must

be nominated together for election. And in any

instance where the officers of the section make

a collective decision, each office will have only one vote.

 

IV. ELECTION OF OFFICERS

 

A. NOMINATING COMMITTEE. Every year, roughly

between December and February, the President, in

consultation with the Executive Committee and members

of the Section, will appoint a five-person nominating

committee. Based on wide consultation within the

Section, this committee will nominate candidates for

the offices to be filled at the business meeting at the

end of the following summer. The combination of

officers nominated will depend on differing lengths of

the terms of the officers being replaced.

 

B. ELECTION OF OFFICERS. At the annual business

meeting, members of the Section will vote on the candidates

who are nominated. By a written petition signed by 25

members and submitted at least one month in advance of

the business meeting, Section members may nominate

additional candidates for the offices voted upon at the

business meeting. Election will be by majority vote

of members attending the meeting, and any unresolved

issues about the procedures for, or outcome of,

this vote will be resolved by the Executive Committee.

 

V. SECTION FOUNDING AND TRANSITIONAL OFFICERS

 

A. TRANSITIONAL OFFICERS. In the period before the

Section has been approved by the APSA, the group of

scholars initiating the formation of the section has

designated a group of Transitional Officers to

coordinate the effort to form a new section.

The Transitional Officers are a President,

Vice-President, Secretary-Treasurer, and

six additional at-large members of a nine person

Executive Committee.

 

B. PROCEDURE FOR THE INITIAL ELECTION OF

OFFICERS IN 2003. In the event that the Organized Section

is approved, these Transitional Officers, after appropriate

consultation with members of the section, will appoint

in the winter/spring of 2003 a five-person Nominating

Committee that will make nominations for the first

set of regular section officers. These nominees then

be voted upon at the first annual business meeting

of the Section at the 2003 APSA meeting,

following the same procedures indicated in III-B above.

These nominees may or may not correspond to the

initial group of transitional officers.

 

These nominations will be for the positions of President,

Vice-President, Secretary-Treasurer, and a four at-large

Members of the Executive Committee. Two of the

at-large members will be nominated and elected for

one-year terms, and the other two for two-year terms,

with the goal of achieving the rotation of half of

the at-large members each year.

 

C. INITIAL NOMINATION AND ELECTION OF THE

PRESIDENT-ELECT. The initial nomination and election

of the President-Elect will be delayed by one year and

will take place in 2004 as specified in IV. A. above.

This first President-Elect will therefore serve for only

one year, 2004- 05, before becoming President for

the period 2005-07. The goal of this procedure is

to avoid having two successive Presidents selected

at the same time. Subsequent President-Elects

will serve a two-year term before becoming President.

Thus, the second President-Elect will be nominated

and voted upon in 2005.

 

D. EVENTUAL DELETION OF ARTICLE V. Following the

second business meeting of the section, Article V will cease

to be relevant and shall be deleted from the by-laws.

 

VI. APPROVAL AND AMENDMENT OF THE BY-LAWS.

 

A. These By-Laws will become official upon approval by

a majority of members attending the annual business meeting

at the 2003 APSA meetings.

 

B. Amendments to the By-Laws may be proposed by

the Executive Committee or by a written petition

signed by 25 members, and submitted to the

Executive committee at least one month in advance

of the annual business meeting. Ratification of

amendments requires a majority vote at the

business meeting.
