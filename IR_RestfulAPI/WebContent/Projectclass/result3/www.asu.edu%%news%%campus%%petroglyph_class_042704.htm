April 27, 2004

New course to catalog, analyze rock art history

If you have ever hiked the largest municipal park in the world, Phoenix's South Mountain, you may have noticed petroglyphs or carvings of images and symbols in rock. The petroglyphs, believed to be made by the Hohokam people, date back between 800 to 3,000 years ago and maybe even longer.

As the city grows, the petroglyphs, or rock art, are threatened by encroaching developments. Before this history is lost forever, an anthropology faculty member and doctoral student have created a course, titled "Petroglyphs in the Valley of the Sun," through the Department of Anthropology at ASU. Beginning next fall, the course will help catalog what exists currently using graduate and undergraduate students to gather information.

"This is probably one of the few courses an undergraduate can take that involves actual hands-on research and the research process," says Michael Winkelman, anthropology lecturer.

Students will learn to photograph, sketch, identify exact locations and other spatial relations of petroglyphs through a survey using geological information and global positioning systems, and other software programs to help with collecting and analyzing data.

According to Rex Weeks, the doctoral student who is helping with the course, some petroglyphs relate to spiritual rituals or astronomy as well as tell stories.

"This course brings together and requires interdisciplinary talents to fully study and analyze petroglyphs," says Weeks. "The trails that the general public is able to hike in South Mountain are actually ancient trails that have been modified. Along those trails you can find petroglyphs that may have represented the birthing places, hunting sites and observation points for the winter solstice. Some of the things we have found even lead us to believe that areas were used as amphitheaters based on the structure and carvings."

Winkelman himself has experienced thousands of petroglyphs within 100 yards of each other when hiking South Mountain.

"Just to catalog these images will take years," says Winkelman, who stresses that the class will not be doing any excavating or removing materials from the park.

"We have permission from the city of Phoenix to go off the trails, which is normally illegal, so that we may conduct this course and record our observations," says Winkelman.

"People who take this course should consider this a privilege," Weeks adds.

If this course is successful, the plan is that other parks and mountain ranges in the Valley will be studied in the future.

For information, contact Winkelman at (480) 965-3087 or Weeks at (480) 965-6215.
