April 14, 2006

Student environmental advocates win Udall Scholarships

Two ASU students have won prestigious national scholarships, adding to the impressive number of undergraduates receiving academic honors this spring.

Taylor Jackson, a senior in biology and society, and Arianne Peterson, a junior majoring in anthropology, have won Udall Scholarships for their service and research in environmental policy.

Both have been active in environmental advocacy since they were freshmen, founding student groups that have made an impact on their communities. This is the second year for Jackson to win the Udall award. Each will receive a $5,000 scholarship to help them pursue eventual careers in environmental public policy.

"Taylor and Arianne are outstanding students who perform well beyond their academic level," says Elizabeth Corley, assistant professor of public affairs who has worked closely with both students, having employed them as her research assistants. "In addition to being highly engaged in research and courses at ASU, both of them are concerned about social issues and have tried to make a difference on campus."

Jackson, 22, founded Students for Environmental Action, organizing an Earth Day celebration and workshops for students. He also led a team of ASU students in performing thousands of dollars of structural renovations in the homes of low-income families, helping them reduce their energy use and learn about conservation.

He lived in a tiny village in Malaysian Borneo two summers ago, helping develop a low-impact ecotourism development plan for a non-governmental agency.

Jackson is in a combined bachelor's and master's degree program in biology and society, taking advanced science and economics classes while conducting research on the connections between environmental policy, technology and development. He plans to get a degree in environmental law and work on international environmental policy.

"Taylor has the ability to think about research problems in a new and creative way," Corley says. "His analytical ability is more similar to a doctoral student than an undergraduate. Arianne is a highly intelligent student who goes above and beyond the research tasks that I outline for her. I'm always impressed when she brings back analyses that contribute to the research project in ways that I had not previously thought of. It's been a pleasure to work with both of them."

Peterson, 20, plunged into activism her freshman year by organizing Campus Greens at ASU, an issue-based group that promotes ecological knowledge, social justice, grassroots democracy and nonviolence. By setting up a table on the mall and holding several meetings a week, she built a group that invites speakers to campus, makes presentations to high school classes and lobbies for renewable energy on campus.

She will study in Thailand next fall, researching social issues of development and its effects on the environment. She hopes to join the Peace Corps following graduation, eventually earning a doctorate in environmental anthropology and working as a consultant for a resource management agency.

Both Udall winners originally came to ASU as National Merit Scholars - Jackson from Hattiesburg, Miss., and Peterson from Luck, Wis. Both are in the Barrett Honors College, and the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences.
