January 30, 2003

ASU English hosts literary notables

In the coming week, Arizona State University's Department of English will host public presentations by two distinguished visitors, African-American literature scholar Trudier Harris-Lopez and poet David St. John.

Trudier Harris-Lopez, J Carlyle Sitterson Professor of English at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, will lecture on "William Melvin Kelley's Real Live, Imaginary South" on Friday, January 31 at 3:15 p.m. in the Pima Room of the Memorial Union.

David St. John, Professor of English at the University of Southern California and winner of the American Academy of Arts and Letters' Academy Award in Literature, will give a reading of his poetry on Wednesday, February 5 at 4:30 p.m. in the Architecture Auditorium, room 60 of the Architecture and Environmental Design Building.

Both events are free and open to the public.

Trudier Harris-Lopez is one of the nation's most distinguished scholars in the areas of African American women's literature and folklore. In addition to lecturing throughout the United States, she has lectured in Jamaica, Canada, England, France, Germany, Poland, Spain, and Italy.

A ground-breaking scholar in the modern field of African-American Studies, Harris-Lopez has published articles and book reviews in such journals as Callaloo, Black American Literature Forum, Studies in American Fiction, and The Southern Humanities Review. Her authored books include From Mammies to Militants: Domestics in Black American Literature (1982), Exorcising Blackness: Historical and Literary Lynching and Burning Rituals (1984), Black Women in the Fiction of James Baldwin (1985, for which she won the 1987 College Language Association Creative Scholarship Award), Fiction and Folklore: The Novels of Toni Morrison (1991), and, most recently, The Power of the Porch: The Storyteller's Craft in Zora Neale Hurston, Gloria Naylor, and Randall Kenan (1996). She edited three volumes of the Dictionary of Literary Biography series on African American writers and co-edited three additional volumes. She edited (1996) for Cambridge University Press and co-edited The Oxford Companion to African American Literature (1997). Two other volumes for which she has served as co-editor, Call and Response: The Riverside Anthology of African American Literary Tradition and The Literature of the American South: A Norton Anthology, were also published in 1997.

David St. John is one of the Southwest's premier poets, and is the author of 13 collections of poetry, as well books of essays, translations, and edited works. His books of poetry include Prism (Arctos Press, 2002), The Red Leaves of Night (HarperCollins, 1999), Study for the World's Body: New and Selected Poems (1994, nominated for the National Book Award in Poetry), No Heaven (1985), and Hush (1976). His awards include the Discover/The Nation prize, the James D. Phelan Prize, and the prix de Rome fellowship in literature. He has also received several National Endowment for the Arts Fellowships and a Guggenheim Fellowship. He is a graduate of the Iowa Writers Workshop and has taught writing on the faculties of the University of Iowa, Oberlin College, Johns Hopkins University and USC, where he has served as the director of the creative writing program.

The Harris-Lopez and St. John presentations are sponsored by the ASU College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, the Department of English and the Creative Writing Program. For more information on these events, call 480-965-7611.
