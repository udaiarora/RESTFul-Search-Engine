May 30, 2003

Home affordability keeps sales soaring in first quarter 2003

Record low mortgage rates and high rates of appreciation continued to be a boon to the Greater Phoenix home buyer in first quarter 2003, according to the Arizona Real Estate Center.

"Ongoing economic concerns have greatly benefited home buyers, because funds have flowed from the uncertain equity market to the bond market, driving down interest rates," according to Dr. Jay Q. Butler, director of the Center at Arizona State University's W. P. Carey School of Business. "The average 30-year mortgage interest rate has declined from 6.7 percent in first quarter 2002, to 5.7 percent at the end of the year, to 5.5 percent at the end of the first quarter 2003."

In the Greater Phoenix resale market, the median home price rose to $148,750, compared to $139,000 in the same period last year. The previous record of $147,000 was set in fourth quarter 2002. For new homes, the median price decreased from $164,540 (fourth quarter 2002) to $163,545, but still emerged ahead of the $158,015 for a year ago. "The slower rise in new home median price is due primarily to the increasing role of the lower-priced housing market in the West Valley," Butler said.

As lower interest rates and a slightly higher median household income offset a higher median resale home price, the Greater Phoenix resale affordability index increased from 128 in fourth quarter 2002 to 131 - much more favorable for home buyers than the 122 reported a year ago (but short of the record level of 139 reported in first quarter 1999). An index value of 100 means that the typical home buyer - based on the current median resale price and household income - would be able to afford a median-priced home at the effective interest rate. Since home prices and incomes vary throughout the Valley, so does the affordability index. With the decline in the new home median price, the affordability index value for new homes improved slightly from 115 to 119, which is well above last year's 108.

Since 1985, the Arizona Real Estate Center has computed affordability indexes for the Greater Phoenix area. Recently, it has begun to develop affordability indexes for cities and even smaller geographical areas. The following provides some examples of affordability indexes.

· Even though Chandler and Gilbert have some of the most expensive homes in the Valley, their respectively high household incomes support a strong affordability index. Based on current estimates, the median household income in Chandler is $62,665, while it is $72,800 in Gilbert. Thus, the resale affordability index in Chandler - based on a median price of $158,000 - is 159, while the new home median price of $227,375 set the affordability index for new homes at 111. In Gilbert, the resale index is 173 (median price $170,000) and new home affordability index is 142 ($207,040).

· Based on an estimated median household income of $48,290, Glendale has a resale index of 137 (median price $141,900) and a new home index of 125 ($155,675).

· Mesa, with an estimated median household income of $45,930, the resale index is 136 (median price $135,700) and new home index of 109 ($169,410). Much like the Greater Phoenix area, affordability here can vary throughout the city. For example the resale index is 160 in East Mesa and 131 in North Mesa.

· In North Scottsdale, with a median household income of $78,650, the resale index value is 93 (median price $340,000) and the new home index value is 83 ($381,060). In South Scottsdale, the resale index value is 120 based on median home value of $162,000 and median household income of $48,270.

· In the city of Phoenix, the resale affordability index is at 139, ranging from 98 in the East Camelback area to 160 in Deer Valley area. In the new home market, the index value is 109 with a range spanning 81 in Union Hills, to 111 in South Phoenix, to 123 in Deer Valley.

· The West Valley communities are known for their affordable housing. For example:

o In Avondale, with an estimated median household income of $52,725, the resale affordability index is 150 (median price $141,375) and new home index is 160 ($132,420).

o Based on a median household income of $36,270, El Mirage has a resale index of 125 (median price $117,000) and new index of 119 ($122,910).

o Goodyear, with an estimated median household income of $61,675, has resale index of 151 (median price $164,500) and new home index of 153 ($161,540).

o Surprise, with an estimated median household income of $47,370, has a resale index of 136 (median price $140,500) and new home index of 125 ($152,695).

Further analysis of the first-quarter data for Greater Phoenix home sales and affordability will appear in the June issue of AZB/Arizona Business, a publication of the Center for Business Research. The Seidman Institute is an affiliation of six research centers that serves as a link between the local, state, national and international business communities and the creative and intellectual resources of the nationally ranked School of Business.
