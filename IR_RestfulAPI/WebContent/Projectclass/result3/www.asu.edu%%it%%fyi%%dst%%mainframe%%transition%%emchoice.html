E-MAIL CHOICES

You can migrate your use of electronic mail (e-mail) on the VM/CMS or Academic VAX/VMS systems independently of when you migrate other applications. In fact, you should take steps now that will simplify your transition later.

You should first configure your Post Office account and advertise your Post Office E-mail Address (e.g. Jane.User@asu.edu) as your new e-mail address. This will allow you to have an e-mail address based on your name, rather than on the system you happen to be using at the time. By doing this, you will (probably) never have to change your e-mail address at ASU, even if you change e-mail systems again.

You can initially configure the Post Office so that when your correspondents send e-mail to your new Post Office address, it will be delivered to your current e-mail system. Once you are ready to transition e-mail systems, you can tell the Post Office to deliver your e-mail to the new system, and none of your correspondents will know the difference. In case some of your correspondents aren't aware of your Post Office E-mail address, you can have the mail they send to your old address forwarded to your new address.

ASURITE E-mail Systems

ASURITE E-mail systems are based on a mail server protocol known as IMAP. There are several e-mail "clients" you can use to access e-mail on IMAP.

UNIX Pine

Available on all ASURITE UNIX clusters, including Research, Stats, General, and AI. UNIX Pine is a character based application that allows you to send and receive local and Internet e-mail, maintain folders and address books.

PC-Pine

Just like UNIX Pine except that it runs on Windows workstations. There is no need to log on to the UNIX clusters first.

Simeon

Windows and Mac based e-mail clients for accessing IMAP mailboxes. Allows you to store address books and personal configurations on a server so that you can easily move between workstations.

If you have access to PPP then you can run PC-Pine or Simeon when dialing into ASU from your workstation at home. Otherwise, you will have to dial into one of the UNIX clusters and run Pine. Also, if you are on an Internet connected host outside of ASU, you can telnet to one of the UNIX clusters and run Pine.
