January 27, 2005

Report highlights Arizona's national standing

Which state is wealthiest or healthiest? How do Arizona’s crime, tax, and home ownership rates compare to other states’? Which metro is the least stressful to live in?

“How Arizona Compares: Real Numbers and Hot Topics,” a new report from the Morrison Institute for Public Policy at ASU, answers these questions and offers enough other comparisons to keep Arizonans quizzing each other for weeks.

But this fifth edition of Morrison Institute’s “Arizona Policy Choices” is more than just fun and games. “How Arizona Compares” is a reference tool to help leaders and residents see their state and metropolitan areas in competitive terms.

Some 90 years ago, when Arizona was the “baby state” among the nation’s 48, its first governor – George W. P. Hunt – presented an enduring challenge: He wanted Arizona to “represent the best things in statehood, and to typify the highest ideals in human brotherhood.”

So have we lived up to the challenge? How do we stack up today among our competing states and metropolitan areas?

“Just fair” is the answer in this unique combination of data and policy analysis. Based on widely accepted data, “How Arizona Compares” ranks Arizona against other states on dozens of measures across 10 major public policy areas, and ranks the Phoenix and Tucson metropolitan areas on some of the same measures. It also provides results from a new opinion survey of Arizonans. Overall:

• Arizona is among the national leaders on some measures, in areas such as business vitality, education and housing.

• Arizona does not lead the nation in a positive way overall in any of the 10 public policy areas, which are: crime and punishment, health and health care, education, business futures, families and incomes, government, arts and culture, housing, transportation and environment.

• Arizonans do not think their state and major metropolitan areas compare favorably on many issues.

“The data and the issues contained in ‘How Arizona Compares’ are more than important for Arizonans to read,” says Cathy McKee, senior vice president of General Dynamics C4 Systems and chairwoman of the Morrison Institute’s Board of Advisers. “They are an urgent call to action for Arizona’s leadership. Appraisals like this one help us to overcome our weaknesses and enhance our strengths.”

Comparisons are basic to human nature; people love to evaluate things side by side. In addition, McKee says, comparisons highlight areas that warrant attention and offer reality checks on our assumptions.

“We can’t forget that other states, metropolitan regions, and countries are making decisions every day to improve their comparative positions,” says Rob Melnick, director of Morrison Institute for Public Policy and associate vice president for economic affairs and public policy at ASU. “Like it or not, when they get better on a key issue, Arizona becomes relatively worse.

“This report is more than numbers, though. It highlights numerous policy choices that are being talked about now and could either move Arizona up or provide a springboard for improvements. We hope Arizonans talk about this information and use it as the basis for wise public policy choices.”

“How Arizona Compares” is available online at (www.morrisoninstitute.org).
