March 8, 2005

Group honors Dix, plans library of African-American pioneers

Clay Dix, one of the original ASU faculty to go to the West Valley to teach when ASU’s West campus opened in Phoenix, was honored Feb. 25 at the fourth annual Pioneer Award dinner and celebration.

The evening was marked by a formal processional with many participants in traditional African dress, dramatic readings, dance entertainment and a presentation of an oral history project featuring Dix. The retired professor was the first chair of the social work program in ASU’s College of Human Services .

The Pioneer Award is given by the West campus’ Black History Month Committee to a community person who makes strides in improving African-American life, community and culture and has a minimum of 20 years of service. Dix is well known in the Valley as a community leader and activist, a mentor and a trainer-speaker on diversity, and a motivational speaker.

Dix retired in 2004 from his position at ASU as senior lecturer and director of field education.

He is now an entrepreneur with his company, New Choices, providing alcohol and drug treatment services. He continues his work in diversity training and is a popular speaker on topics such as fatherhood and community needs.

Once a Pioneer awardee is selected, an oral history process begins, which includes formal research, videotaping of interviews with the honoree and other related persons, and the editing of the oral history. Excerpts of the oral history were presented at the award dinner.

The committee intends that this award and oral history will mark the beginning of a library of African-American pioneers to be housed at the West campus, as well as a research library yet to be determined.

Dix earned his master’s degree in social work from the University of Chicago and was the chair of the social work department of Valparaiso University before moving to ASU’s Tempe campus as field director and lecturer in 1979.

As part of the evening’s events, Carolyn Jones, an administrative associate in the office of the provost, received an award in recognition of her many years of exemplary service to campus and community.

Black History Month at the West campus kicked off this year with a gallery installation of “African-American Vibes of the City” and continued with a variety of events, including a presentation of Pulitzer Prize-winning playwright Suzan-Lori Park ’s “Top Dog/Underdog” and book discussions of Anthony Grooms’ “Bombingham.”

Veihdeffer, with the West campus, can be reached at (602) 543-5209 or (jim.veihdeffer@asu.edu).
