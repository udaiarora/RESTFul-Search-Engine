Fall 2006 Asian Film Classes

CHI 294-FMS 294-FLA 294: Introduction to Chinese Cinema

Class meeting time MW 440-555 PM
Class meeting venue Architecture 321
Film screening time W 6:00 PM
Film screening venue NUR 101
Instructor John Zou, Visiting Assistant Professor
Office LL 645D

The class provides a survey of Chinese cinema for students who may or may not have previous exposure, but are interested in acquiring a relatively systematic understanding of its history, genres, major styles, and global reach. Outside the Hollywood and European canon, China has developed, over the course of the 20th century, one of the longest continuing and most vibrant film traditions in the world. Since the 1980s, films from the People?s Republic, Hong Kong, and Taiwan have scored considerable success at international film festivals as well as via commercial releases. The works by Zhang Yimou, Wang Kar-wai, and Ang Lee cultivated such large followings that they have earned for Chinese language films and the Chinese film aesthetic a very prominent position among today?s national cinemas. In the art house circuits, these masters often command as much respect and attention as their best known western colleagues. This class situates the recent boom of Chinese cinema within the history of a hundred years of filmmaking that went through several episodes of massive social upheaval and across three politically splintered regions. It features ten films organized into five segments. We begin with the so-called Fifth Generation of mainland directors who catapulted Chinese cinema into the international arena with their large stories and ebullient styles. Then we go back to the long episode before the 1980s to consider the film culture of a semi-colonial Shanghai and Maoist communism that predated and in a way prepared the Fifth Generation for its international debut. After that, in the third segment of the class, we look beyond the Fifth Generation masters and discuss two excellent works partaking in the vast industrialization and urbanization process in contemporary China. Hong Kong?s displacement of Shanghai as China?s film city in the mid to late 20th century is the subject of the fourth segment, where we examine the continuation of commercial movie making in the martial arts genre and an artistic fascination with Shanghai as Hong Kong?s nostalgic past. The class concludes with movies from Taiwan, where again the film industry developed as mainland evacuees arrived after the communist triumph, but where the Japanese film legacy also delivered an important impact. China?s extraordinary history in the last century, i.e., its transformation from a semi-colonial territory to a nationalist party state, a communist system, and then a booming robber baron economy, affords extraordinary stories to be told in the cinema and emotionally charged stylistic virtuosity that accompanies their telling. In this class, we will also attempt to account for the rise of historical drama as the dominant film genre on the mainland, with which the family romance of Taiwan and escapist works of Hong Kong may be seen as engaged in a generic, stylistic, and of course political dialog.
