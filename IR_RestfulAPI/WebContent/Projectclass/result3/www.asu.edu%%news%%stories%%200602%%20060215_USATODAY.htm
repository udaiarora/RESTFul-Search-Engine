February 15, 2006

Two top ASU students win academic honors from USA Today

Two outstanding ASU students have won national academic honors from USA Today, named in the newspaper's Feb. 15 issue for their exceptional intellectual achievement and leadership. Taylor Jackson, a senior in biology and society, has been chosen for the All-USA College Academic Third Team and Elizabeth Dreeland, a December graduate, has received honorable mention.

Eighteen ASU students have been chosen for the top three academic teams in the past 14 years, one of the best records of any public university in the nation.

A national team of judges selects top students based on grades, leadership, activities - and, most importantly, how students extend their intellectual talents beyond the classroom. More than 600 were nominated by colleges and universities across the United States .

Jackson, who grew up in Hattiesburg, Miss., has a deep interest in protecting the environment and revitalizing communities. He has been able to do both by developing a plan to improve the energy efficiency of homes in a low-income area of Phoenix. Last year, he led a team of students that performed thousands of dollars' worth of structural renovations in homes, educating residents in the process.

Winner of a 2005 Udall Scholarship given to top students in the fields of environmental policy and science, Jackson also spent several weeks in Malaysian Borneo, living in a bamboo hut while writing an ecotourism development plan for a rhino protection group. Two years ago, he founded the ASU Students for Environmental Action.

Dreeland, of Corona, Ariz., is a literature and history student who co-founded Lux, the undergraduate student literary magazine published by the Barrett Honors College and the Virginia G. Piper Center for Creative Writing. She also is a champion fencer, serving as captain of the ASU women's foil team.

Her honors thesis on how revolutions and governments claim past heroes to legitimize themselves has taken her halfway around the world, to Cuba and Hungary, looking for statues and talking to residents. She studied in Budapest, Hungary, for seven months last year on a National Security Education Program Boren Scholarship.

Both Jackson and Dreeland are in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences and the Barrett Honors College.

USA Today chooses 20 students each for the first, second and third teams, and another 20 receive honorable mention.
