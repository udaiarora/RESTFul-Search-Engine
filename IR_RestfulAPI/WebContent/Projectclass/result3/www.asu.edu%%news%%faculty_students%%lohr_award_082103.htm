August 2, 2003

Lohr receives Gertrude M. Cox award for survey research

Professor Sharon Lohr, with ASU's mathematics and statistics department, has been chosen as the first recipient of the newly inaugurated Gertrude M. Cox Award.

Funded by the RTI (Research Triangle Institute), the award acknowledges mid-career statisticians who are making significant contributions to statistical practice. Lohr accepted the award at the annual dinner of the Washington Statistical Society June 10.

Lohr says she was surprised and deeply honored to learn she had been selected for the award.

"I am really thrilled that the awards committee and RTI consider my work to be having an impact on statistical practice," Lohr says. "Gertrude Cox really was one of the greatest statisticians of the last century, and was equally well known for her encouragement of students to go into the mathematical sciences. She was one of my heroes when I was a student, and she remains so today."

Lohr joined ASU in 1990 as an assistant professor, and since then her primary research area has been survey sampling.

"I develop statistical methods for analyzing data from large sample surveys with complex structure," she says. "I like the freedom I've had at ASU to work on research projects that I think are important, and feel fortunate that my students and I can contribute to studying these new problems."

She says while at ASU she has had the opportunity to teach a wide variety of statistics courses, both applied and theoretical.

"Because of the flexibility at ASU, and the encouragement to try new ideas, I've been able to develop five new courses here, including graduate courses in statistical consulting and sample surveys as well as an undergraduate course in bioinformatics."
Lohr will be on sabbatical next year, visiting Carleton University and Statistics Canada in the fall to work on problems of how to combine information from different surveys to obtain more accurate estimates without having to increase the cost of collecting data. She will also be collaborating with Associate Professor Doug Sylvester in the ASU College of Law on research about legal, ethical and statistical issues involved in maintaining the confidentiality of survey data.

She is a member of the U.S. Census Advisory Committee of Professional Associations, which gives advice to the Census Bureau on issues such as whether to adjust the census.

In 2004, she will chair the Survey Research Methods Section of the American Statistical Association and is looking forward to coordinating national activities and proposing new programs for improving research in survey methodology and encouraging students to enter the discipline.

Gertrude M. Cox was born in Iowa in 1900 and enjoyed a long, productive and influential career as a statistician. In 1940, she began working as a statistics professor at North Carolina State University at Raleigh, founded the department of experimental statistics at North Carolina State University, the Institute of Statistics of the Consolidated University of North Carolina, and ultimately the Statistics Research Division of the Research Triangle Institute.

In 1949, she became the first female elected into the International Statistical Institute, and served as president of the American Statistical Association in 1956. Cox remained active even after retirement, and was elected to the National Academy of Sciences in 1975. She died in 1978.
