Donald Fixico

Distinguished Foundation Professor

Department of History

Ph.D., University of Oklahoma

Prior to coming to ASU as a Distinguished Foundation Professor in the Department of History, Dr. Donald L. Fixico was the Thomas Bowlus Distinguished Professor of American Indian History, CLAS Scholar and Founding Director of the Center for Indigenous Nations Studies at the University of Kansas. His area of expertise is American Indian History, especially 19th and 20th centuries involving U.S.-Indian relations, ethnohistory, and oral history.

After earning a Ph.D. from the University of Oklahoma, Fixico received postdoctoral fellowships at UCLA and the Newberry Library in Chicago. He has been a Visiting Lecturer and Professor at the University of California at Berkeley, UCLA, San Diego State University and University of Michigan. He was an Exchange Professor at the University of Nottingham, England and Visiting Professor in the John F. Kennedy Institute at the Freie (Fry) University in Berlin, Germany.  He also served on the faculties at the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee and Western Michigan University at Kalamazoo.

Fixico has published numerous articles and 8 books, Termination and Relocation: Federal Indian Policy, 1945-1960; Urban Indians; The Invasion of Indian Country in the 20th Century: American Capitalism and Tribal Natural Resources; The Urban Indian Experience in America; The American Indian Mind in a Linear World and Daily Life of Native Americans in the 20th Century. He has edited, An Anthology of Western Great Lakes Indian History and Rethinking American Indian History. He has also worked on more than a dozen documentaries. He is currently working on a book on American Indian leadership.

In 2000, President Clinton appointed Dr. Fixico to the Advisory Council of the National Endowment for the Humanities and in 2002, he was the John Rhodes Visiting Professor of Public Policy in the ASU Barrett Honors College. Recently the Organization of American Historian gave an award to him to teach for two weeks in Japan in November.
