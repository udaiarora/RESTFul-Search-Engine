Essayist Rodriguez tackles ‘Religion in the New Century’

Richard Rodriguez, a prominent American essayist, author and editor, will deliver a free public lecture at 7 p.m., March 8, at ASU’s West campus. His topic will be “As the World Turns: Religion in the New Century.”

The lecture will be held in the University Center Building, La Sala Ballroom, at ASU’s West campus, 47th Avenue and Thunderbird Road, Phoenix. For more information, call (602) 543-5311. To R.S.V.P., call (602) 543-5300.

Rodriguez often is compared to literary figures from Albert Camus and James Baldwin to Montaigne and Emerson. He is the fifth recipient of the John M. and Hellen S. Jacobs Endowed Visiting Professorship in Ethics at the West campus.

In addition to the public lecture, Rodriguez will participate in a number of classroom discussions, as well as a special student discussion and a faculty and staff book discussion during his weeklong visit to the campus.

“Richard Rodriguez is a person of faith, very much of an individualist,” says Thomas V. McGovern, professor of psychology in ASU’s New College of Interdisciplinary Arts and Science. “He articulates the conflicts and the contradictions of living in contemporary America.”

Rodriguez is an editor for the Pacific News Service in San Francisco and a contributing editor of Harper’s Magazine and the Sunday “Opinion” section of the Los Angeles Times. He has published numerous articles in the New York Times, the Wall Street Journal, the American Scholar, Time, Mother Jones and the New Republic. He also has written several books, including “Brown: The Last Discovery of America,” “Hunger of Memory: The Education of Richard Rodriguez” and “Days of Obligation: An Argument With My Mexican Father,” as well as two BBC documentaries. Rodriguez appears regularly on “The NewsHour with Jim Lehrer.”

His awards include the Frankel Medal from the National Endowment for the Humanities, the International Journalism Award from the World Affairs Council of California and a George Foster Peabody Award for his “NewsHour” essays on American life.

The Jacobs Endowed Visiting Professorship in Ethics was established by Edwin K. and Margaret Jacobs Delph in honor of Mrs. Delph’s parents. The donors wanted to bring attention to the importance of ethical perspectives and values in the education, research and service mission of the programs at ASU’s West campus. Each year, the professorship allows ASU to host a distinguished ethics scholar at its West campus for a thought-provoking dialogue.

“The purpose of a university has always been to be a gathering place where people come to work through difficult and challenging ideas,” McGovern says. “Contemporary American life is fraught with dilemmas and the purpose of this series, as intended by the donors, is to provide us with the means to bring an intellectual to campus … and to reflect deeply and respectfully about those dilemmas.”

Information about past visiting scholars is online at: (www.west.asu.edu).

Hughes, with the Office of Public Affairs at the West campus, can be reached at (602) 543-5220 or (carol.hughes@asu.edu).
