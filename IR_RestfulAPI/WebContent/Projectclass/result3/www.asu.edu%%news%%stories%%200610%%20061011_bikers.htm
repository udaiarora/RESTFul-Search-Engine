October 11, 2006

Bikers provide ecological study input

Mountain bike enthusiasts and recreation managers have a new ally in preserving places to ride: scientific research.

Land managers, policy makers and cyclists have faced the challenges of crowded mountain trails, irresponsible trail use, increased demands for more trails and degradation of existing trails caused by the explosion of mountain biking as a popular national pastime. In the absence of sound scientific information, managers often have chosen to take regulatory action ? or to restrict use of recreational resources.

In an effort to understand the ecological impacts of mountain bike use on trails, the Arizona State Office of the U.S. Bureau of Land Management and Shimano American Corp. launched and funded a collaborative research project led by recreational ecologists Dave White from ASU and Pam Foti from Northern Arizona University.

Their efforts provide managers with assessment guides that inform their decision-making, and help sustain the trail systems as well as this increasingly popular outdoor activity.

?The future health of our business is directly related to environmentally sustainable recreational uses of public lands,? says Phil Morlock, director of environmental affairs at Shimano. ?When we discovered that limited research had been conducted about the ecological impacts of mountain bikes, we felt it was critical to initiate a credible effort to learn more.?

The comprehensive, three-year study focused on the ecological significance and physical impacts of mountain bikes across five common ecological regions in the West.

White, from the ASU School of Community Resources and Development and Foti, along with graduate students M. Troy Waskey and Grant P. Brodehl, sampled 31 trails encompassing nearly 200 miles that ranged from alpine to desert terrains. The peer-reviewed Journal of Park and Recreation Administration published the team's article, ?A Comparative Study of Impacts to Mountain Bike Trails in Five Common Ecological Regions of the Southwestern U.S. ? in its summer 2006 issue (volume 24, No. 2, pages 21-41).

The good news from the research is that, when properly planned and managed, mountain bike trails are an environmentally sustainable use of the back country. Results indicate that the effects from mountain bikes are similar to ? or less than ? those that occur on hiking and multiple use trails, and appreciably less than those occurring from equestrian and off-highway vehicle trails.

?As a scientist with a commitment to creating usable knowledge, I was interested in developing a decision-making model that would be practical and scientifically sound,? White says. ?Our study contributes to the growing consensus that mountain biking can be a sustainable activity on properly managed trails.?

The team also published a guidebook released at the recent Interbike International Bicycle Expo in Las Vegas . The publication, ?Planning and Managing Environmentally Friendly Mountain Bike Trails,? is a model for resolving natural resource use problems, written specifically for land managers, policy makers, mountain biking associations and individual enthusiasts. It includes recommendations about design, zoning, rehabilitation, technical ratings and monitoring of trails, as well as tips on promoting proper riding techniques, responsible rider behavior and outdoor ethics.

?These new trail monitoring techniques are defendable methods to sustain trail systems that respect the natural environment as well as the need for high quality recreational opportunities,? says Don Applegate, recreation program lead, Arizona, U.S. Bureau of Land Management.
