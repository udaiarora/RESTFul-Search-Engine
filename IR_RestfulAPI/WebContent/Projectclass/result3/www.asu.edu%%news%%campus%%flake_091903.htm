September 19, 2003

Flake carries lessons learned at ASU through life, state politics

Arizona Speaker of the House Franklin "Jake" Flake has been a determined advocate for ASU for nearly 50 years.

Flake first realized political success when, as a young college student, he joined an effort that, literally, made ASU the university it is today.

While attending what was then the Arizona State College in the late 1950s, the young, fourth generation cattle rancher from Snowflake, Ariz., rallied fellow students and worked to get university status for his college.

"It was my first political endeavor," Flake says. "We went out, had people sign petitions and spoke in front of the Board of Regents for our cause. It was exciting to see we were bringing about change. We worked hard and were finally able to get university status."

When Flake graduated with a bachelor's degree in agriculture in 1960, he took pride when receiving his degree, which read Arizona State "University."

After graduation, Flake returned to his family's ranch in Snowflake, which left some wondering "what was the point of college?"

Flake says the answer to that question has always been easy.

"My time at ASU may have been the best four years of my life," he says.

"While there, I opened my mind to all of the possibilities. I was introduced to new cutting edge ideas in genetics and nutrition, that I could bring back to the ranch."
Flake also carried the lessons he learned while at ASU as he entered the world of state politics.

"No matter what the issue, it is good to remember that there are always new and better ways of doing what you want to do," he says. "My time at ASU is what instilled this in me."

Flake admits state lawmakers still have some challenging budgetary decisions ahead, but says he is confident the outlook is bright for the state and for ASU.

This past spring Flake was instrumental in the passage of House Bill 2529, a bipartisan effort to stimulate the state economy by funding more than $400 million in high-tech research infrastructure construction at ASU, the University of Arizona and Northern Arizona University.

"I am a strong advocate for investing in our universities," he says. "I strongly believe in the infrastructure bill, and feel it is already bearing fruit."

Flake says he will support legislation calling for additional state funding for university enrollment growth.

"I think we have to find a way to get the enrollment growth money back to universities," Flake says. "Especially for ASU where there is so much growth. I really want to work with President Crow and the other university presidents to work out this issue."

Flake says he is looking forward to working with Crow on a variety of university endeavors.

"I have known ASU presidents all the way back to Grady Gammage," Flake says. "And I am very taken with Michael Crow. He is dynamic and exciting, and he has a wonderful vision for the future - a vision I'd like to help with."

Through the years, Flake and his wife Mary Louise have encouraged their 13 children to enrich their lives by attending college. Several have attended ASU, including their youngest child, Kayla Flake who is currently an ASU student studying nursing.
Flake says he is proud of his children, grateful for the role ASU is playing in the state's future and very pleased to call himself an ASU graduate.

"I believe it is important to make a success of whatever you do," Flake says. "I am so thankful for what I learned at ASU."
