March 19, 2003

Degree long-term investment for college students, families

Financial aid experts say higher tuition rates should not discourage current and potential students from pursuing a college degree.

"The financial aid process has become a lot easier," says Diane Stemper, director of ASU's Student Financial Assistance Office. "The real challenge is making sure you complete your courses in order to earn your degree."

On March 6 the Arizona Board of Regents voted 9-0 in favor of raising ASU tuition $1,000 for resident and nonresident students and $1,200 for resident and nonresident graduate students for the 2003 - 04 academic year.

ASU President Michael Crow said the revenues generated by the increase will increase financial accessibility by making more funds available for needs based financial aid.

ASU has received more than 14,000 applications for next year so far and Stemper says more funds were obtained for students with financial need.

Stemper says the most frequently asked question made by families seeking financial aid is "What is the most money a household can make and still receive financial aid?" The answer, she says, "There isn't one."

A family that brings in a total of $80,000 could have the same opportunity to receive financial aid as a family that makes a total of $40,000.

"Each family situation is different," Stemper says. "A family that makes $80,000 could have a total of nine people in the household with five children in college. This family would be eligible for some sort of financial assistance. Each family should make sure they look at their situation closely before eliminating their options of financial aid."
Stemper offers the following advice to students and families seeking financial assistance:

Try not to let the financial aid form intimidate you. It can be very overwhelming for people who have never filled one out. Keep in mind that it becomes easier when you begin answering the questions.

File your financial aid form early. Many people don't realize that they can use estimated information regarding their income taxes.

Students and their parents should work on filling out the financial aid form together. It's a joint venture that will ultimately affect both the student and the parent.

The priority deadline date for the Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA) form was Feb. 15, but Stemper says there are still funds available for students who decided to turn in their forms after that date. In addition to obtaining a FAFSA form online, there are deadline updates available at: (http://www.fafsa.ed.gov/) and suggestions on how to fill out the form, including instructions in Spanish.

The General Scholarship Application for the 2003 - 04 academic year is also available online at (http://www.asu.edu/fa/scholarships/general.html). Priority deadline is March 15.

Notification of awards will begin in March and continue on a rolling basis throughout the year. Awards made through the General

Scholarship Application are not automatically renewable. Students must submit a new General Scholarship Application every year by the priority deadline in order to be considered for an award.

A list of additional outside scholarships is available online at: (http://www.asu.edu/fa/scholarships/index.html). Scholarships are listed in five categories: freshmen scholarships, tuition waiver scholarships, departmental/college scholarships, private scholarships and community scholarships. Deadlines for applications may vary.

Taking out a loan is another option that students may consider. Information on loans is available online at: (www.asu.edu/fastt/loans/dirinfo.html).

"Even though we would all like to complete college without having to pay back any loans," Stemper says, "I think as long as you are careful not to take out too many loans, then you should be fine.

"I can't think of anything that a person can invest in that will have a greater pay off in the long run," Stemper says. "The important thing to consider is making sure that you finish, because that's when the payoff comes. A college graduate has more opportunities to make a better salary that someone without a college degree."

For information on the ASU Student Financial Assistance Office visit the Web at (http://www.asu.edu/fa/).
