October 1, 2003

ASU researchers advance fuel cell technology by going small scale

The power outages that plagued the northeast this summer have many people re-examining the idea of electrical generating systems that would be more flexible, cleaner and less prone to major catastrophic failure. One such energy source that could fill these electrical power needs is fuel cells.

Fuels cells, which cleanly and quietly generate electric power by passing fuels like hydrogen over one electrode while passing air over a second, have been around for more than 100 years. But their development has long been dogged by costs of the technology as well as safety concerns.

Now, researchers at ASU are applying advanced principles of nanotechnology to the design of fuel cells. Their work could lead to "several new ways of dealing with shortcomings of conventional fuel cells while advancing the technology into new markets and uses," says Frederic Zenhausern, director of the Center for Applied Nanobioscience (ANBC) at Arizona Biodesign Institute.

"What we are doing is incorporating microfluidics and nanotechnology in the fuel cell design to help develop cells that operate at room temperatures and yet have a very high energy density," Zenhausern says.

At ANBC, Professor Don Gervasio and his team are developing micro-fuel cells for portable applications. Gervasio, who has worked on a variety of fuel cell technologies for 20 years, says his team's goal is to develop a complete micro power system, envisioned as a hybrid device made up of a fuel cell subsystem, a battery and controller. Pound for pound, it would supply more energy (application lifetime) than batteries alone, he said.

Presently, micro power means batteries, and these batteries, which cost around $10 per Watt, have always been significantly higher in cost than grid ($0.25 per Watt) and automotive ($0.04 per Watt) power applications. The high cost of batteries means battery-replacement micro-fuel-cell systems are the most likely first practical fuel cell products. They are targeted at personal portable electronics for consumer and military markets, to power a vast new array of cell phones, sensors, radios and laptop computers.

Gervasio says the team also is exploring novel materials, as well as working out systems issues for making the fuel-cell power supply systems. Applying nanotechnology to make inexpensive, yet effective, fuel-cell and fuel-processing-catalysts and using plastic microfluidics and micro-ancillaries (like low-power miniature pumps, fans and controllers) to effectively shrink the fuel cell systems will help get a foothold on some problems that are encountered with larger-scale fuel cell technology.

One key material is the fuel. Hydrogen is the most desired fuel but is troublesome because its storage is too hazardous and bulky to be attractive for commercial portable uses. For automotive applications, a new water-based hydrogen storage solution developed by Millenium Cell, Eatontown, N.J., solves these problems. The new aqueous borohydride fuel has comparable energy density to gasoline and methanol, and yet it is safer to handle because the borohydride solution is not flammable.

The ASU team is taking the borohydride hydrogen storage scheme and using a microfluidics reactor to get the hydrogen out. By using this approach, at any given instant, less than a milliliter of cool, low-pressure hydrogen gas would be present during fuel cell operation. This is much safer than operating conventional fuel cells, which typically involve liters of high-pressure hydrogen or flammable hydrocarbons.

Other key fuel cell materials under study at ASU are: low-cost, lightweight plastic microfluidic housings for the fuel cell sub-system; low-cost, high surface-area nano-catalysts; and advanced proton conductors. In particular, advancing the proton electrolyte membrane materials could greatly simplify the system. Gervasio is collaborating with ASU Chemistry Professor C. Austen Angell to exploit the ion-conduction concepts that Angell's ASU team has developed to make improved lithium ion conductors.

Because the materials issues are still not completely resolved, the ASU researchers expect that a practical room temperature fuel cell prototype is probably still three years away, and that it would take an additional two years to bring a pre-commercial prototype to market. Several consumer product manufacturers have already expressed interest in ASU's technology.

While it is reasonable for micro-fuel cells to displace batteries, costs must be reduced if fuel cells are ever to displace grid power and the internal combustion engine, and keep alive the promise of clean and quiet alternative power supplies for larger applications.
