Recommendation for Backing Up Data

Are you adequately backing up your data? Performing a backup consists of more than merely making an extra copy of the important files. You should consider the number of copies, their storage locations, and the media on which the copies are written.

The importance of the data to you, including the cost in time and money it would take to replace the data (assuming it were replaceable), determines how elaborate your backup procedure should be. Your backup data is just as important as the original data, and is just as susceptible to loss.

Backing up your data is especially important if you are using a desktop workstation (PC, MAC, etc.), but it is no less important if you are using a service bureau (e.g., computer center) for your computing. Any backup performed by a service bureau or other third party is an adjunct, and does not lessen your responsibility for backing up your data.

Multiple Copies

Therecommended number of backup copies is "more than one". A general guideline is to have multiple copies of the data at each of several storage locations.

Multiple copies are needed of every important file. How many copies depends on the data's value, the cost of replacing the data, and your comfort level. Even inconsequential data may deserve backing up if it's more convenient for you to restore the data than to recreate it.

Backups should be made of any new file as soon as practical after creation. However, if the new file is built in stages over a period of time, interim backups should be made at each stage of creation.

Additional copies should be created of any modified file after the modification is complete. If the modification occurs over an extended period of time, interim backups should be made at convenient intervals.

Due to changing technologies and deterioration of media over time, even if a file has not been used or modified recently, you should make backups of the file on a regular basis.

As new backup copies are made, the older copies should be "grandfathered", i.e., retained. The number of generations to keep depends on your needs. Some people like to keep, for example, three generations of backups. The following are two variations of grandfathering three generations: (1) keeping a set of three generations at each of several locations; (2) keeping the most recent generation at the primary location, the next most recent at a second location, and the oldest generation at a third location.

Storage Locations

Having multiple backup copies at the same location as the original data is a great convenience if you need to restore quickly, but is inadequate as the sole location of your backup copies.

An adequate backup requires copies of your data be stored in multiple geographically distinct locations. A minimum of two, preferably three (or even more if your need is great), locations are recommended. Multiple locations within the same room or building do not qualify as geographically distinct locations. A more realistic example of three locations would be, for example, your office, your home, and the computer center.

The environment of each location is also an important consideration. The media on which data is stored is sensitive to temperature, humidity, magnetism, and dust. If the environment of one or more storage locations is less than ideal or is not within your control, you should increase the number of storage locations or the frequency of backups or both.

Media

There are many different types of media. Some are more durable than others, more resistant to environmental changes, or more suitable for electronic processing.

Paper is a useful storage media, but is not generally useful as computer input medium. It is possible to scan printed matter and recreate the data, but special equipment and software are required and mistranslations are possible. Other media, such as disk, tape, CD-ROM, and optical devices, are better suited for storage of data.

The table at the end of this document lists several media types and their life expectancies. It is important to remember that these life expectancies are not guarantees. Data loss on the media can occur prior to life expectancy expiration.

The storage environment has a substantial impact of media preservation, as does the condition of the equipment used to read or write the media. A brand new tape in perfect condition can be damaged beyond repair during the first read attempt if the tape drive is in disrepair. Tape in particular is susceptible to wear because of the physical contact between the tape and the read/write mechanism. Proper maintenance both of equipment and the environment will help ensure the longevity of the backup storage media.

Also of importance, particularly for data that is to be used for a long time, is obsolesence of the hardware and software used to create the backups. You may have backups stored on 5.25" double density floppies, but already the 5.25" double density floppy drive is obsolete. To avoid problems associated with obsolescence, new backups should be created using the newer technologies as the technologies enter the mainstream of computing, i.e., are affordable, commonly used, and readily available.
