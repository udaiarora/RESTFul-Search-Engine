Who should take this study:  Students interested in the linkages between interdisciplinary humanities, business, computer science, art, and marketing.
Purpose/Benefit:  To prepare students for a diverse range of occupations - corporate, business, academic, non-profit, and government - requiring a knowledge of digital culture and digital tools.

Area of Study:

Additional Units:  21 hours

HUM 350:  Introduction to Digital Culture and Media Technology Studies
This course introduces students to postmodern digital culture studies by exploring how digital technologies, telecommunications, and the digital arts are transforming contemporary culture and everyday life.  Topics for reading and discussion include transformations of identity in on-line communities, generational differences in media culture, the political economy of information and telecommunications, digital ethical issues, and new forms of political organization and cultural resistance (jammin).  In addition, we will explore the unique rituals, re- presentations, ideological constructs, sites, information theories, economic models and aesthetics (media technologies) of digital culture.  Secondly, the course explores media technologies that enable the construction of digital culture.  We apply a number of post structural theories to a reading of the digital culture concentrating on such issues as visual media, micro/macro technologies, post capitalist society, in the post human.  This course will also explore how digital culture creates contemporary meanings through various kinds of technologies and various strategies of representation (e.g. video technologies, computer interfaces, robotics, nanotechnologies, neuro-networks, fuzzy logic, being "wired" and "jacked in," and virtual technologies).  Finally, the course will explore how technology contributes to the unique nature of the postmodern world, including such topics as post industrialism, national and global literatures, globalism, and new forms of world communications, economies, and cultures.

HUM 452:  Information Theory, E-Commerce, and Digital Culture
This course explores the nature and status of traditional forms of discourse - the "literary/the aesthetic" - and then places them against the dominant paradigm of post-modern discourse-information.  What kind of information is art?  What kind of art is information?  Is literature?  Is business discourse?  The course explores the biocultural nature of information and then traces the history of the relationship between culture and technologies of information.  Other areas explored are information and hypertextuality, the evolution of literary theory in the past few decades; sociology of and the "new information capital" and knowledge work in popular culture and new kinds of information.
