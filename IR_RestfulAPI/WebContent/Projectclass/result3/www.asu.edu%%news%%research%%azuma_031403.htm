March 14, 2003

Azuma aims to find link to early dementia diagnosis

For many, slight memory loss is a normal part of the healthy aging process. But for others, this loss could signal a much more serious dementia-related disorder.

ASU researcher Tamiko Azuma is taking a closer look at how one specific type of memory problem, Mild Cognitive Impairment or MCI, can be used in the early detection of serious dementia.

MCI is a new classification of cognitive impairment in older adults thought to be a possible pre-clinical stage of dementia. The primary characteristic of MCI is cognitive impairment in one area, usually memory, with other areas intact. As an early diagnostic tool, however, MCI is limited because while many patients with MCI later develop Alzheimer's disease, many others don't.

"This group of people with Mild Cognitive Impairment tends to convert to dementia at a greater rate than seniors who aren't classified as MCI," says Azuma, an assistant professor of speech & hearing science. "This classification could help us with early treatment. But why do many convert to Alzheimer's disease, while others stay the same?"

Working in collaboration with Sun Health Research Center in Sun City, Azuma, is seeing if she can find a clue to the use of MCI by examining the working memory of adults 65 years old and older.

Azuma is taking a group of patients with MCI and healthy older adults and evaluating their working memory with a number of different tests. Working memory, by definition, is the memory used to temporarily store and retrieve "working" information such as grocery lists or phone numbers. In the working memory tests, people may be asked to remember words or numbers for a short period of time or asked to reorder those lists in their heads.

Azuma says it is possible that the patients who end up developing dementia could have specific memory or attention problems in a certain area, while the people who do not progress could have other types of memory problems. If specific types of working memory problems can be classified, researchers would know what to look for as an early warning.

"The experiments are really fine tuned, looking at error rates and response times," she says. "We're trying to get a better handle on the different kinds of memory issues that may be present in these individuals."

In a related study, Azuma will take a longer-term look at many of the same patients. Azuma will monitor changes in the patients in coming years and see how those who do later progress to more severe stages of dementia correlate to the test results of the short-term studies.

Azuma says patients with MCI, when compared to their peers, may have a relatively small difference in their ability to retrieve information. Some memory loss as a person ages is actually part of the normal healthy aging process and occasional forgetfulness should not be a cause for panic.

"Losing your car in the parking lot once is one thing," she says. "That happens to everyone. Losing it every day, that's a bad sign."
