October 5, 2004

Program offers Mexican educators professional development

José Gómez Cruz and Alelí Morales Díaz are two of the 20 educators from the states of Jalisco and Chiapas, Mexico, who are participating in a new program that focuses on learning innovative teaching methods in a multicultural setting.

Both say they welcome the challenge and hope for an enriching experience, which they plan to share with their colleagues and students back home.

“We appreciate this opportunity to learn how we can become better teachers,” Cruz says. “We are very grateful to have been given this chance to experience the American culture and learn other methods of educating our students.”

Cruz and Díaz are from the states of Jalisco, Michoacan, Nayarit and Chiapas, Mexico, and they are participating in the Cooperative Association of States for Scholarship (CASS) Program, sponsored by ASU’s Center for Indian Education (CIE) in conjunction with Georgetown University.

The CASS Indigenous Teacher Professional Development Program is a yearlong professional development program in Multilingual/Multicultural Education, which began in August and runs to July 2005. This program, “Strengthening Education for Indigenous Children,” is multifaceted, targeting primary school teachers and administrators working with indigenous students and their families in Mexico. The program is delivered through a combination of university level coursework, internships, seminars and focused workshops, job shadowing and professional and community linkages. Coursework is provided in Spanish, and a one-year certificate will be issued to the students (educators) upon completion of the academic program.

Sally Gonzales, CIE program coordinator, says this is the first year ASU has offered this program, and she says the citizens from Guadalupe will serve as the base community for the program.

“Participants will shadow jobs not only in school settings, but also with Guadalupe and Pascua Yaqui Tribal employees and elected officials,” Gonzales says. “CASS participants will be paired with friendship families to allow participants to interact with members of the community outside of the classroom.”

Other community collaborators include South Mountain Community College Guadalupe Center , Centro de Amistad, Frank School , Holdeman School (Tempe School District No. 3) Creighton School (Creighton School District No. 14), ASU College of Education Pre-school and Las Fuentes Clinic.

After completing their first year, the educators will be given the opportunity to continue the program for three more years. Cruz and Diaz hope to complete the four-year program.

In addition to Cruz and Díaz, the other Mexican educators who arrived on Aug. 11 are: José Isabel Sánchez Bautista, Juventino De La Cruz Bautista, Héctor Francisco Anselmo, Martín Gómez Culebro, Fernando Gómez Hernández, Indalecio Manuel Gómez López, Marcelo González Carrillo, Oscar H. Hernández, Juan Miguel Hernández Montes, Pascual Jiménez Hernández, Gusatvo Núñez Gómez, Ariosto Robledo Muñoz, Enrique Roblero Zunun, Edgar Romero Sánchez, Juan Salvador Díaz, Joaquín Sanitz Gómez, Leonardo Santiz Gómez, and Ernesto Torres Burguete.

Between all 20 educators, eight indigenous languages are represented. The visiting educators are learning English as part of the program.

“The American way of doing things has been a learning experience, from learning how to prepare and become accustomed to new foods, to learning directions,” Gonzalez says.

After completing the program, the educators are expected to return to their communities and become advocates for indigenous children.

For more information about the program, call (480) 965-9785.
