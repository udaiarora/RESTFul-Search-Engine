ASU/UA CONSORTIUM

ASU and UA Recipients of $859,000 Grant To Enhance Latin American Studies

ASU's Center for Latin American Studies, together with the University of Arizona's Latin American Area Center, have been awarded a Title VI grant from the U.S. Department of Education, to enhance the Latin American Studies programs at both institutions.

The three-year grant totaling $859,000 includes support for graduate student fellowships, library acquisitions, outreach to the teaching and business communities, language instruction and scholarly exchange with Latin American universities. In addition, funds will also be available to match the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences contribution toward a new Assistant Professor position in Latin American Studies.


Tod Swanson, Director, ASU Center for Latin American Studies

Scott Whiteford Director UA Center for Latin American Studies

This is the first time that Arizona institutions have received Title VI funding for Latin American Studies. The designation of "National Resource Center" recognizes the high quality programs at the two state universities where more than 170 faculty teach more than 300 advanced courses on the languages, society, history, culture, environment and other aspects of the Latin American region.

The fact that an Arizona consortium received this prestigious designation is also indicative of the significant increase in demand for information, teacher training, economic and cultural activity, and informed public policy relating to Latin America.

Tod Swanson, Director of ASU's Center for Latin American Studies praised the academic excellence and commitment of the over 102 faculty affiliates of the Center that through their combined instruction and scholarly pursuits, have made it possible for us to receive this grant. Swanson also praised the vision of the strategic ASU/UA alliance in Latin American Studies as a benefit for the entire state of Arizona. Swanson and his counterpart, Scott Whiteford, Director of the Latin American Area Center at the University of Arizona are optimistic that each university can enhance its own instructional and research strengths in particular areas within the Latin American region.
