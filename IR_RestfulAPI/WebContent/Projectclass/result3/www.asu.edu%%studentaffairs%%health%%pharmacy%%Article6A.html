"The Use of Carbon Monoxide Testing in Smoking Cessation Programs" (Background)

The Student Health Center of Arizona State University (ASU) serves a population of approximately 45,000 students. The facility is an ambulatory care center and is accredited by the Accreditation Association for Ambulatory Health Care (AAAHC). College campuses can be a difficult setting to implement and maintain a successful smoking cessation program. Nearly 75% of college students nationwide have smoked at least one cigarette. It is estimated that 29% of college students smoke one or more cigarettes on a daily basis. Of these current smokers, 34% smoke eleven or more cigarettes per day and 16.5% smoke twenty or more cigarettes per day. Among students who are current smokers, 67% have tried to quit smoking. Smokeless tobacco is estimated to be used by 5.4% of college students.1

In April 1998 I became a Certified Smoking Cessation Specialist through the University of Pittsburgh's National Certification Program. Shortly thereafter a smoking clinic was established at ASU Student Health where patients were seen at half-hour appointments. The overall six-month quit rate for the first eight months of the clinic was 51%.

In January 1999, SmithKline Beecham Consumer Healthcare made a Smokerlyzer brand carbon monoxide (CO) monitor available to the clinic. It has been demonstrated that the End Expired Carbon Monoxide (EECO) can be directly related to the percentage of carboxyhemoglobin in a patient's blood.2 This information provides a numerical value that relates to the impact of smoking or second-hand smoke exposure. Working with staff physicians, pharmacy students on rotation, and other volunteers, carbon monoxide testing was offered at various sites on campus. Data was accumulated at these events and shared with Student Health administration. Arizona State University declared all freshman residence halls as smoke-free effective August 1999.

Carbon monoxide testing was also integrated into the counseling process. CO levels are documented at initial visit and on subsequent visits. This tool has allowed us to promote our smoking cessation efforts by increasing awareness of the effects of smoking in a very personal way. It has also allowed us to document CO levels of patients at the beginning of counseling and to show improvement when patients quit smoking.

The use of carbon monoxide testing has significantly improved our smoking efforts. It provides patients with a dramatic and meaningful assessment of their smoking behavior. This model for carbon monoxide testing can be adapted for use by community or clinic based smoking cessation programs. The pharmacist is well positioned to lead these programs. When properly conducted, these programs can provide important therapeutic benefits to the patient and create new revenues for the pharmacist. In June 1999 the Internal Revenue Service declared that the cost of smoking cessation programs may be deducted as a medical expense for smokers trying to quit. The deduction does not include products, at this time. Nevertheless, this ruling should make smoking cessation programs even more attractive to those attempting to quit smoking.

Carl Labbe R. Ph.
Pharmacy Manager
Student Health Services
Arizona State University
