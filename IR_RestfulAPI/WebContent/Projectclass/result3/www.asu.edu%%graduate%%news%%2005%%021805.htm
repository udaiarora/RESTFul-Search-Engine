Kintigh, Montgomery Named ASU Outstanding Doctoral Mentors
Pictured, left to right: Ira A. Jackson, ASU Foundation President and Chief Executive Officer; Dr. Maria T. Allison, Vice Provost and Dean of Graduate Studies; Dr. Keith W. Kintigh and Dr. Douglas C. Montgomery, 2004 ASU Outstanding Doctoral Mentors.

Professor Keith W. Kintigh and Douglas C. Montgomery have been named ASU Outstanding Doctoral Mentors for 2004 in recognition of their unbridled commitment to the learning, preparation, and intellectual and professional growth of their students.

Kintigh, a professor of anthropology, and Montgomery, a professor of industrial engineering, will be presented with the award by the Division of Graduate Studies and the ASU Foundation at a reception on Feb. 10. They are the twenty-first and twenty-second recipients of the award, which includes a $5,000 award.

Both professors have demonstrated stellar records of chairing doctoral committee and student success, as measured by external grant funding, publications, conference presentations, and career placement, said Maria T. Allison, Vice Provost and Dean of Graduate Studies. Many of their graduates now enjoy productive professional careers in academia as well as industry.

"Dr. Kintigh and Dr. Montgomery are distinguished scholars and researchers in their fields, and have also garnered a reputation by peers and students alike as the consummate mentors," Allison said. "They have played a critical role in the success of their doctoral students, from their graduate career, to degree completion, to beyond."

Added Ira A. Jackson, president and chief executive officer of the ASU Foundation, "Drs. Kintigh and Montgomery demonstrate every day one of ASU's most important missions—that of teaching. They create an invigorating learning environment that is inspirational for their students to achieve their dreams. The ASU Foundation is proud to salute these distinguished leaders with the Outstanding Doctoral Mentor Award."

Kintigh joined ASU as a professor of anthropology in 1987. His mentoring formula combines socializing students into the archeology profession and helping them to develop strong research skills and to seek research opportunities, while providing professional support and personal motivation and confidence.

"It is my fortune to have worked with so many superb graduate students at ASU," Kintigh said. "To be recognized for that activity which has already brought me so much satisfaction is a great honor, for which I am most grateful."

Montgomery's career as a professor of industrial engineer at ASU also spans nearly two decades. His mentoring philosophy is founded on the belief that effective mentoring begins with effective teaching. However, a good mentor must also be a role model, one with a commitment to scholarship and professional service.

"Dr. Montgomery is the quintessential role model," said James R. Simpson, one of his former students and now an associate professor in his nomination letter. "He has earned an international reputation as one of the finest scholars in applied statistics, has graduated nearly 50 Ph.D.s, served as director of our most prestigious journals, and is a fellow of all four of our professional societies. Most strikingly though, he clearly loves being a professor and a mentor, an affection we find contagious."
