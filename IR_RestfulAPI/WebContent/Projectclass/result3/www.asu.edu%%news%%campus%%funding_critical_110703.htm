November 7, 2003

Funding critical to lift quality, access

As the Phoenix metropolitan population continues to surge - growing from 3.5 million to eventually 7 or 8 million - the college-age population is burgeoning. The Governor's Task Force on Higher Education estimates that by the year 2020, nearly 150,000 students will be enrolled at the three state universities and more than 200,000 students will be in Arizona community colleges.

A healthy university system is vital to the future of the state. For many students, ASU increasingly has become the school of choice, with record enrollments in both size and quality for the past several years. The increased enrollment at ASU comes at a time when the University of Arizona is considering capping enrollment and Northern Arizona University is experiencing slower growth. Since Fiscal Year 2001, ASU has enrolled an additional 7,628 full time equivalent students - with no additional state investment for those students.

"The fact that ASU, in recent years, has managed to increase in size while maintaining high quality is no small feat," says Virgil Renzulli, vice president for Public Affairs. "In fact, ASU has had numerous accomplishments to celebrate, while some universities across the country have experienced layoffs, hiring freezes, enrollment caps and/or program cuts."

This academic year, ASU enrolled 173 National Merit Scholars, the most ever, and up from only 24 in 1992. Last year was ASU's most successful fund-raising year ever - capped by two $50 million gifts from individuals and $10 million from the Piper Memorial Charitable Trust. The university has attracted top faculty, scientists and administrators from some of the nation's most elite institutions. And, the people of Arizona, through their representatives, approved $185 million in long-term financing to build research infrastructure.

ASU President Michael Crow says ASU's transformation is catching attention and causing people to not only stand up and take notice, but also to take a personal interest and make a commitment.

"We have presented our goals in such a way that people see the end result - a stronger Arizona economy, a well-educated workforce, a nationally competitive university, and more high-paying jobs," says ASU President Michael Crow. "We haven't said, 'please help us because we need your help, please help us because we're underfunded, please help us because we lack resources.' These new sources of revenue sensed the energy of an enterprise wanting to move forward and all of those investments were a product of that."

True to Crow's commitment, the university has been very entrepreneurial in its efforts to develop other revenue sources. But as a public institution, ASU still requires its major investor - the state of Arizona - to fund its core operating expenses.

In its Fiscal Year 2005 budget request to the Legislature, ASU, with approval from the Arizona Board of Regents, is requesting an additional $58.6 million to cover unfunded core operating costs, primarily associated with the university's significant enrollment growth.

"We are a very successful state university trying to become a unique national university in a unique place," says Crow. "We have a long way to go. The institution is probably funded at 50 percent of the level that's needed to carry out the mission we have. Tremendous things have been done with the limited funding. The people of Arizona are the most significant contributor to this institution and, person for person, they're also the most generous contributor."

Crow says he and others will be making the case that ASU must have resources to maintain quality and to maintain access.

"There are increasing numbers of qualified individuals who want what ASU has to offer, but we must have the resources to move forward," he says. "At this moment, when the university is becoming more diversified than ever, more accessible than ever, more open to qualified students than ever, more engaged in the community than ever, we have to advance this university by seeking additional resources from our prime investor - the people of Arizona."
