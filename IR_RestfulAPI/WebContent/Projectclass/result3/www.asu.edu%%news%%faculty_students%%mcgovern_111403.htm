November 14, 2003

McGovern earns top professor nod

Thomas V. McGovern, professor of psychology at ASU West, has been selected as the 2003 Arizona Professor of the Year by the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching and the Council for the Advancement and Support of Education (CASE). McGovern was selected from among nearly 400 top professors in the United States.

The U.S. Professors of the Year program salutes outstanding undergraduate instructors who excel as teachers and influence the lives and careers of their students. McGovern and the other awardees were honored at a Nov. 13 luncheon at the National Press Club in Washington, D.C.

"This award is a tremendous acknowledgement of Dr. McGovern's dedication to engaging students in the learning process," says ASU President Michael Crow. "It reflects the university's commitment to excellence in undergraduate education."
McGovern, who has taught at ASU West since 1990, co-founded the campus' Integrative Studies Department. Integrative Studies emphasizes interdisciplinary study in the liberal arts, the development of critical thinking skills, respect for diversity, and the capacity to synthesize problem-solving strategies for life in the 21st century.

"Tom's courses changed my life," says Maren Fagerman, an integrative studies graduate. "He helped me break a lifetime pattern of pleasing others and pushed me into a world where I must be authentically me to be successful."

Fagerman changed her major to integrative studies from accounting after suffering a major stroke that forced her to relearn simple mathematical computations. She and McGovern shared the experience of recovering from a serious illness; in 1997 McGovern was paralyzed by Guillain Barré Syndrome, a rare neurological disease that kept him out of the classroom for more than a year.

McGovern wrote "Patients/Patience: A psychologist's rehabilitation memoir" about his recovery process, published in The Washington Post.

"The memoir documents a time when this professor became a student again, learning how to roll over, to sit up, to eat with a fork, to write with a fat pencil, and to walk," McGovern says. "It was a time when I deepened my empathy for my students who feel helpless about their basic skills, adrift in unfamiliar surroundings, and uncertain about whether to even perform the tasks that professionals prescribe for them."

"Integrative Studies graduates, all of whom have taken at least one of Tom's classes, have later commented to program reviewers about leaving ASU West with an ability to appreciate different modes of thinking, to think critically themselves about real issues, and to feel comfortable in an increasingly diverse world," says Emily Fourmy Cutrer, dean of the ASU West College of Arts and Sciences.

McGovern has provided campuswide leadership at ASU West in establishing learning communities for freshman students and in devising methods to assess outcomes of student learning. He has served as president of the Arizona Faculties Council and representative to the Arizona Board of Regents.

Nationally recognized in the field of psychology, McGovern is a Fellow of the American Psychological Association (APA).

McGovern is the second ASU West professor chosen as Arizona Professor of the Year. Vincent Waldron, professor of communication studies, earned the award in 1996.

The Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching was founded in 1905 by Andrew Carnegie and is the only advanced-study center for teachers in the world and the third-oldest foundation in the nation.

CASE is the largest international association of educational institutions, with more than 3,200 colleges, universities and independent elementary and secondary schools in nearly 50 countries.
