This program provides an interdisciplinary study of Quichua languages and religious life, tropical plant biology, and/or the Shuar and Waorani languages while experiencing the rich culture of the Andes and the Amazon. Students gain a unique perspective by living and working with indigenous communities; members of these communities serve as co-teachers in the courses.

LOCATION

Santo Urcu Amazonian Quichua Community on the banks of the Napo River, a tributary of the Amazon. Here, students become immersed in Quichua life, camping in virgin rainforest, listening to the singing of ayahuasca shamans, clearing forest with machetes to plant manioc, and much more. Other Andean destinations include Quito, Papallacta, Baños, Salasaca, and the Cayambe/Otavalo region. Click here for more information about each session's itinerary.

ACADEMIC PROGRAM

All students must enroll in one language course and at least one credit of other coursework for each session. Spanish Language Classes are offered in four levels (101, 102, 201 & 202). The Quichua Language & Culture Program prepares students for serious research on indigenous culture. It provides immersion in the Quichua language and practical training in contemporary field work methods. Undergraduates may enroll in 300 or 400 level classes only. Biology students taking the "Tropical Plant Diversity" and/or "Amazonian Ethnobotany" courses will learn to recognize major families and genera of the Amazonian region by collecting, imaging, and identifying plant specimens as part of a plant diversity inventory of the Canoa Yacu Reserve. Quichua shamans work with the class to compare biological classifications with Amazonian ethnobotanical traditions. Nursing students will learn about the relationship between cultures of diverse groups and health/illness. This program will emphasize cross-cultural communication, including awareness of the student's own cultural influences and indigenous and complementary healing practices.

FELLOWSHIPS

The U.S. Department of Education for Graduate Summer FLAS Fellowships approves this program. To qualify, FLAS Fellows must enroll in both sessions for 6 credits of Quichua language and 1 credit of Quichua culture.

FACULTY

Professor Tod Swanson is Director of ASU's Center for Latin American Studies. His specialty is Quichua religious history. Raised in the Ecuadorian Amazon, Dr. Swanson is fluent in Spanish and Quichua. Dr. Michael Uzendoski, a Professor of Anthropology at Florida State University, is an expert in Quichua culture and is fluent in the language. Luz María de la Torre Amaguaña holds an M.A. in Political Science and is a native speaker of Otavalan Quichua. She has taught Quichua at the Pontíficia Universidad Católica del Ecuador and the Universidad San Francisco de Quito. Most recently, she has served as an advisor to the Ecuadorian Ministry of Foreign Relations.

LIVING ACCOMMODATIONS

Triple occupancy, thatched roofs, bamboo walls, and verandas while in the indigenous communities; elsewhere, hostels are available. American-style meals alternate with opportunities to sample native cuisine.
