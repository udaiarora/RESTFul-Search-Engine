July 9, 2004

ASU brings awareness about West Nile virus

ASU and Arizona health and environmental officials are collaborating to educate the university community about the mosquito-transmitted West Nile virus.

"There have been no cases of West Nile Virus reported from the ASU Health and Wellness Center," says Mary Ellen Rimsza, director of Student Health. "However, Arizona is the leading state with more than 35 cases, so it's important that everyone becomes aware of this virus."

In the past few weeks, a young wild crow that was found emaciated on the ASU campus near the Hayden Library tested positive for West Nile virus. The disease is transmitted to humans by mosquitoes that have fed on infected birds. Rimsza says while birds cannot directly infect humans, it is best to avoid contact with dead birds.

Because there is no vaccine for the potentially fatal virus, health and environmental officials of ASU and Maricopa County are focusing on eliminating sources where mosquitoes breed.

The Arizona Department of Health Services suggests the following tips to prevent mosquitoes from breeding:

. Eliminate standing water. Check for conditions outside the home such as poorly maintained swimming pools, cans, bottles, jars, buckets, old tires, drums and other containers with water.

. Change water in flower vases, birdbaths, planters and animal watering pans at least twice a week.

. Repair leaky pipes and outside faucets, and move air conditioner drain hoses.

ADHS also suggests using mosquito repellent near dawn and dusk and wearing long-sleeved clothing at these times.

Rimsza says most cases of West Nile Virus are very mild and the illness develops within three to 14 days of the mosquito bite.

"Indeed, 80 percent of patients have no symptoms," she says. "In another 20 percent, the disease is similar to Mono with fatigue, muscle aches and headaches, usually lasting only three to six days. Less than 1 percent of infected people develop a severe disease."

Mosquito infestations and dead birds on campus should be reported to the Facilities Management Service Center by calling (480) 965-3633.

For information on mosquito fogging operations, call the Mosquito Fogging Hotline at (602) 372-3000.

For a complete description of signs, symptoms and laboratory findings, visit the Web at (www.cdc.gov/ncidod/dvbid/westnile/clinical_guidance.htm).
