ASU master's degree program tackles national bilingual professional shortage

As the number of non-native English speakers grows throughout the nation, there is a need for professionals to serve that population. One ASU program is helping to meet those needs with a specialized curriculum to prepare bilingual speech-language pathologists.

The ASU Bilingual Training Program, supported with funds from U.S. Department of Education Office of Special Education Programs, is part of the master's degree program in the speech and hearing science department.

The bilingual training track is one of four curriculum options for graduate students in the speech-language pathology program. Program students who are awarded federally-funded traineeships work as speech-language pathologists in a public school setting for a period of two years for every year they receive assistance.

"We have an incredible group of graduate students preparing to work with the population of ESL (English as a Second Language) children who have speech and language disorders," says Kathy Murphy, coordinator of the program. "Living in the Southwest we are all aware of the increasing number of children who are entering our public schools with limited skills in English. Unfortunately there's a dire shortage of professionals who are truly qualified to meet the needs of these children."

Murphy says in the Valley alone, speech-language professionals in the public schools are working with a diverse group of children with disabilities who speak more than 60 different languages. One of the primary challenges for professionals is to determine whether a child who is experiencing language-learning problems has an underlying speech-language disorder or if their identified problems are related to learning a new language.

"It's the first question a speech-language pathologist needs to address and it's difficult to answer without adequate training and appropriate assessment tools," says Murphy.

Unlike many graduate training programs throughout the nation where the students participate in internships in their final few semesters, ASU's students in the bilingual training track are working with professionals in the field from the first week in the program.

Through partnerships with more than 15 school districts around the Valley, students spend one morning per week training with a local public school speech-language pathologist who has experience working with multicultural children. Every few weeks, students rotate to another school district. By the end of the first year, the students will have trained in a minimum of eight different school districts with more than 10 professionals, gaining experience working with a very diverse group of children in grades K-12. In the second year of the program, the ASU students will complete two full-time internships at public school settings, often selecting a site that prepares them to work with a specific age group or type of disorder.

"I looked into a lot of schools and found that ASU had a strong clinical focus and was one of the only schools in the nation with a bilingual training program," says first-year student Julie Kleinheinz. "Being bilingual alone doesn't mean you know how to work with multicultural populations. Our program coursework and clinical training prepares us for understanding the needs and challenges of working with kids of all cultures and language backgrounds."

Murphy says the training program also targets the needs of Valley speech-language professionals who are already employed. Murphy says these professionals are encouraged to enroll in the specialized academic coursework in the master's program or participate in semi-annual workshops presented by nationally recognized experts.

For information on the program, visit the Web at (http://icrp.asu.edu/ bilingual) or e-mail Murphy at (kmurphy@asu.edu).
