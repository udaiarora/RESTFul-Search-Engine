Announcement: The Amazon and Andes Field School announces its Quichua Language and Culture Program during the Summer and Winter Breaks. To date, over 240 students from various U.S institutions of higher education have participated in the Field School where they are introduced to elementary and advanced Quichua language and culture. It is also possible to take beginning courses in Shuar or Waorani, two other indigenous languages of the Ecuadorian Amazon. Several alumni of the Field School have chosen to return again the next year for additional linguistic and cultural immersion.

Introduction: Quichua is perhaps the most important of the indigenous American languages. It is spoken by eleven million people in five countries straddling the Andes from Colombia to Argentina. Nevertheless, very few Americans are fluent in Quichua even though the language is taught by several universities in the United States. This is because in order to become fluent, most students need to immerse themselves in an environment where Quichua is spoken as the common language. Our program is unique in its ability to place students in rural Quichua communities where the Quichua language is predominant. The Quichua communities included in our program were specifically selected because they foster an environment where language acquisition is complemented by a distinctive cultural immersion. Our program faculty's knowledge of these areas and their relationships to the host communities afford participants a more comprehensive and community-based learning experience.

Location : This program is held during the ASU Winter and Summer breaks in an Amazonian Quichua community located on the banks of the Napo River with shorter stays during the Summer Program also taking place in Andean communities near Otavalo and Salasaca. Students ( which include undergraduate, graduates, and K-12 school teachers and community college professors ) attend classes daily and live within the Quichua speaking communities that they are studying.

 

Academic Program: All students must enroll in one language course and at least one credit of other coursework for each session. The Quichua Language & Culture Program ( in which FLAS recipients are enrolled) prepares students for serious research on indigenous culture and requires that each student must enroll in 7 credits: 4 of Quichua and 3 of Quichua culture class. It provides immersion in the Quichua language and practical training in contemporary field work methods. Undergraduates may enroll in 300 or 400 level classes only. Biology students taking the " Tropical Plant Diversity" and/or "Amazonian Ethnobotany" courses will learn to recognize major families and genera of the Amazonian Region by collecting, imaging, identifying plant specimens as part of the plant diversity inventory of the Canoa Yacu Reserve. Quichua shamans work with the class to compare biological classifications with Amazonian ethnobotanical traditions. Nursing students will learn about the relationship between cultures of diverse groups and health/illness. Emphasis will be given on cross-cultural communication, including awareness of the student's own cultural influences and indigenous complementary healing practises.

Living Accomodations: Triple occupancy, thatched roofs, bamboo walls, and verandas while in the indigenous communities. Hostels elsewhere. American-style meals alternate with opportunities to sample native cuisine.

 

 

Eligibility and Courses: This program is open to adventurous students and alumni from any university willing to be challenged by the differences of indigenous cultures.

FLA 494/590: Beginning Quichua (4 credits): This course provides students with immersion-based instruction in Quichua (Runa Shimi) as it is currently spoken in Ecuador (Napo and Otavalo dialects). Fundamentals of the language are covered. Emphasis on listening and speaking.

FLA 494/590: Intermediate Quichua (4 credits): Continuation of Beginning Quichua. Emphasis the development of the skills of reading, listening comprehension, speaking, writing, and cultural meaning.

FLA 494/590: Advanced Quichua (4 credits): Emphasis on the development of fluency in conversation. Students engage in more advanced study of Quichua semantics to deepen their understanding of the Andean/Amazonian symbolic world.

FLA 494/590: Beginning Shuar (4 credits): Intensive study of beginning Shuar with a native speaking tutor under the supervision of Professor Tod Swanson. Shuar tutor victor Cayapa is an experienced bilingual school teacher who presently works as an administrator for the Napo Province Bilingual School District (Direccion Bilingue). He is Director of the Sacha Causai Foundation. The Shuar language (also known as Jivaro) is spoken by 30-32,000 people in south-eastern Ecuador (SIL 1981, Ethnolgue). It is related to other Jivaroan languages such as Achuar-Shiwiar, Huambisa, and Aguaruna spoken in the Peruvian Amazon.

FLA 590: Beginning Waorani (4 credits): Graduate students work with a native speaker under the supervision of Professor Tod Swanson. Participants must be highly motivated, able to speak Spanish or Quichua, and have taken introductory courses in Linguistics (preferably Phonemics and Syntax). Waorani tutor Nenquimo Quemontare Epanca spent her childhood among the Waorani of the Payamino before the outside word established contact with them in 1969. After that time, her family moved to the Waorani mission in Tonampari. The Waorani language is spoken by approximately 800 people who live between the Napo and Curaray Rivers in eastern Ecuador (SIL 1993, Ethnologue). So far, its relation to other languages has not been established.
