November 4, 2003

Hard work paying off as ASU evolves to national status

ASU President Michael Crow thanked employees for their hard work, dedication and accomplishments over the past 15 months during his Town Hall session on Oct. 22. He said everyone's efforts are a significant part of the process to evolve ASU into a national university.

"A national university is an institution that is competing at the highest possible level in all aspects of its identity - its academic programs, its specialty programs, its athletic programs - everything," Crow said.

Crow said ASU is embarking on its sixth evolutionary step as an institution, growing from a territorial normal school, to a teacher's college, to a state college, to a comprehensive state college, to a state university, and, now, the step from a state university to a national university. "Six steps of evolution in a little more than 100 years is huge," Crow said. "Almost no institution, and, certainly, no university, has had to go through that. It puts a huge amount of stress into the organization when you've just completed step five and someone comes along and says 'hey, let's take step six.'

"But, step six is essential for the success of metropolitan Phoenix and the success of Arizona," Crow said.

To point out ASU's success in moving toward step six in its evolution, Crow introduced the university's first annual report, printed through the ASU Alumni Association in Vision Magazine, which represents a summary of the ASU community's collective efforts over the past year and a half.

"Many of you have heard me talk about the enterprise model, or being more entrepreneurial, and have probably wondered 'what is he talking about.' This report's content helps explain what we mean by academic enterprise - being responsible for ourselves, being responsible for our own destiny, plotting our own course and moving forward and behaving in ways where we don't act and think like an agency of the government.we act and think like a university, an independent creature."

In addition to highlighting successes, Crow answered questions from the audience. The full presentation is available electronically online at (http://is.asu.edu/live).

Following are some questions submitted via e-mail that were not addressed at the Town Hall due to time constraints:

Q. If all of ASU's funding goes to athletics, research in sciences, business and law, what does this say about our commitment to the departments that place emphasis on the human endeavors of art and literature?
A. The ASU Intercollegiate Athletic Department gets minimal funding from the university, relying instead on revenue producing sports and external fund-raising support programs.

There has been a great deal of publicity around our research infrastructure program and large gifts to support specific colleges, such as business and engineering. I can't stress enough the importance of building strong, well-funded science and technology programs, because when a university's scientific programs are world class, those programs bring in new revenues, which frees up other funds to build world class humanities, arts and literature programs. Just last month, we received a $10 million gift for the new Virginia G. Piper Center for Creative Writing.

Q. Are there any plans to add more on-campus housing at the Main Campus, and when?
A. Yes, we are planning for the development of approximately 5,500 new beds over the next four years. The first phase is the McAllister Academic Village, with 2,000 beds. The first 500 beds will be operational for fall 2005, and the remaining 1,500 beds will be available for fall 2006. The Barrett Honors College will follow with approximately 1,700 beds - possibly in Fall 2006 as well. The remaining beds will follow with the redevelopment of the south campus housing area.

Q. Will the salary increase you mentioned be given as a lump sum, is it based on merit, and will the increase address employees with at least 10 years experience who are not at mid-point in salary range?
A. The salary increase will be based on merit, as a reward for high-level performance. There is an opportunity within the merit adjustment process to address market and salary equity issues. The salary adjustment will not be a lump-sum payment, but will be added as a percentage to eligible employees' base salary.

Q. Will the university be prepared to handle enrollment growth in terms of student support, faculty teaching support and crowded conditions?
A. We are a very successful state university trying to become a unique national university in a unique place, while growing at an unprecedented rate. We have a long way to go. This institution is probably funded at 50 percent of the level that's needed to carry out the mission we have. Tremendous things have been done here with limited funding. The people of Arizona are the most significant contributor to this institution and, person for person, they also are the most generous contributor. Alumni, private donors, and students actually contribute very little compared to other state universities. We need substantial increases in financial aid, substantial improvements in access, substantially enhanced salaries for all staff, substantial expansion of faculty, larger numbers of advisers and better repair of our buildings. But the solution to all that does not rest solely with the ability of the administration to sell the Arizona legislature. Our future rests in our ability to dream what we want to be and sell it to everyone - students, families, donors, alumni, everyone - and that's what we're trying to do.
