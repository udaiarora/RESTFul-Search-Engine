Philip Tarrant

Raptors Response to Habitat Fragmentation and Urban
Growth in the Phoenix Metropolitan Area

ABSTRACT

The spread of urbanization is recognized as an inevitable part of modern society in both the developed and developing nations of the world. However, as urban growth continues, the impact on the flora and fauna of the local ecosystem increases. Whether it is the dense vertical development of European cities, or the urban sprawl of the new North American cities, local species have to compete not only with the anthropomorphic effects of urban development, but also with alien species introduced by human residents. While some species adapt to these new circumstances better than others, ultimately all species are affected. 

During the spring of 2006 I surveyed 50 raptor nest sites in the Phoenix metro area to explore the response of raptors to the increased fragmentation and ongoing development of their historical habitat. By modeling these nest site data in a GIS geodatabase along with bird count data and mortality data I hope to produce an integrated spatial view of the response of raptor species to the changes resulting from the meteoric urban growth in the Phoenix metro area. This initial research, and the data it utilizes, could readily be extended to produce a long term view of how well raptors are adapting to the desert-urban environment.
