July 18, 2005

KAET captures ASU journey ‘From Mars to China’

Noted ASU planetary geologist Phil Christensen and a team of scientists journeyed to Beijing in May to participate in the prestigious China Science & Technology Week – and KAET-TV/Channel 8’s cameras were there to capture every historic moment. A 30-minute special about the historic visit, titled “From Mars to China,” airs at 7:30 p.m., July 19, and 4 p.m., July 24, on KAET.

“From Mars to China” offers a profile of ASU’s Christensen. The special takes a look at how Christensen become so integral to the discoveries on the Red Planet.

For the noted geology professor, the fascination began years ago.

“When I was a kid, Mars was this really mysterious place,” Christen says. “We knew nothing about Mars. We’d never sent a spacecraft there. It was a red dot in the sky. That captured my imagination.

“I came to ASU in the early ’80s out of grad school, and the first experiment I proposed was an orbiting instrument that looked at infrared light coming off the surface.”

From that first proposal came a series of successful discoveries. Christensen and his team made the first mineral maps of Mars and uncovered places where there were minerals that formed in water.

Years later, ASU’s Thermal Emission Imaging System (THEMIS) became one of the three main science instruments aboard the Mars 2001 Odyssey orbiter.

Most recently, Christensen’s team developed instruments for the Mars rovers. One of the sites targeted for discovery on Mars, was first identified on the early maps of Mars mineral content.

“So it’s been sort of this steppingstone of an early experiment that led to a really great camera (and) led to participation in these Rover missions … and so one thing led to another,” Christensen says. “We can actually say we have four experiments working at Mars: two in orbit and two on rovers. We’re still mapping the planet. We’re making new discoveries.”

It’s that track record that brought ASU to the attention of the officials at China Science & Technology Week.

“Why China ? China is the most important country outside of the United States for the coming century,” says Jonathan Fink, ASU vice president for research and economic affairs. “Virtually every major university in the United States is trying to find connections to China . So what we’ve tried to do is take our strongest programs and bring those over there.”

“Six, eight months ago, it was, ‘Who is ASU?’ ” Christensen says. “They quickly realized that ASU was a big-time player … in the exploration of space – and suddenly it was, ‘You guys have four experiments at Mars and Harvard has none, Cal Tech has none, MIT has none. We should be working with you.’ ”

Christensen and his team presented “Welcome to Mars!”, a multimedia exhibit designed to introduce Chinese visitors to the Red Planet, as well as to share the results of ASU’s contributions to the exploration of Mars. ASU is the first American university invited to participate in this prestigious event.

“KAET was fortunate to have such incredible access to history in the making,” says Greg Giczi, KAET’s general manager. “I am struck by what an exciting time this is for ASU.”
