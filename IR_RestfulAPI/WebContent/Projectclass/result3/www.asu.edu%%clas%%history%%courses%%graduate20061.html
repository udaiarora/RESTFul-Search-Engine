HST 500 ? Methods of Historical Investigation
TTH, 9:15-10:30AM, EDB L1-36
Professor: Brooks Simpson                                                                                               

This is the second of two courses that together form a year-long introduction to historical research, method, and practice for incoming graduate students.  Each week students will join various faculty members and invited guests in discussing books and articles that illustrate various approaches to historical study; they will also learn about research strategies, methodologies, tools, and resources .

 

HST 525 ? Historical Resource Management
W, 8:30AM-11:30PM, COOR 4581
Professor: Jannelle Warren-Findley                                                                       

Cultural resources?the physical remains of historic sites, landscapes, buildings, structures, historic districts, and artifacts comprise a valuable archive of information for historians.  In this course we will examine the theories that underlie the discovery, analysis, treatment, interpretation, and preservation of cultural resources.  This course will provide needed background for students contemplating careers in resource management, and museums.  The students will investigate, based on readings and guest lectures, innovative and interdisciplinary approaches for evaluating and interpreting various kinds of material culture.

 

HST 527 ? Historical Administration
M, 8:30-11:30AM, COOR 4581
Professor: Noel Stowe

This introductory course for public history students examines methodological practices appropriate to public history work. Short papers generous samplings of readings, oral presentations, and final simulation project.

 

HST 591 ? Seminar: Biography as History
T, 1:40-4:30PM, COOR 4581
Professor: Brooks Simpson

One way to explore history is through biography: using a person's life as a window through which to explore the past. In this research seminar, students will do just that: they will examine the intersection between an individual and an issue in an effort to achieve a broader understanding about a particular historical problem. After reading several sample explorations drawn from American political history, students will embark on their own research projects, grounded in primary sources. This course is open to all graduate students, regardless of geographical field.

 

HST 591 ? Seminar: Gender During the Progressive Era in the US
TH, 1:40-4:30PM, ED 336
Professor: Gayle Gullett

The objective of this class is to provide history graduate students with an opportunity to conduct research and write a scholarly paper that analyzes gender during the progressive era in the United States, 1880-1930.

We begin the class with common reading assignments; we do this to provide a sampling of how different historians have analyzed gender in the progressive era and suggest models of writing in history that are informed by various theoretical understandings.  Students are free to chose and/or develop a model.

To aid students in this process of learning how to create history, the class attempts to take students through each step of the process that historians follow:  designing a research project, conducting research, writing drafts, collaborating with colleagues, presenting their findings at professional meetings, and writing the final paper.  The final paper should be ready or nearly ready to submit to a journal for publication.

 

HST 591 ? Seminar: Public History
W 6:40-9:30PM, COOR 4581
Professor: Karen Smith

This seminar will explore the various research tools and techniques public historians need to use in all aspects of public history, from preparing legislative histories, working on a team land use history, conducting oral interviews to writing a targeted research paper, typically a chapter of a Master's thesis. The seminar offers a pragmatic and ?hands-on? approach to public history, taught by current practitioners. We will have guest speakers on a variety of topics, including using history to prepare documentary productions. Our focus will be on acquiring new research tools and refining writing and communication skills.

 

HST 598 ? Special Topics: US Cultural Institutions and Public Practice
T, 8:30AM-12:30PM, COOR 4581
Professor: Jannelle Warren-Findley

This readings course, open to all concentrations, examines roles that public historians and cultural institutions play in U.S. society.  Students read and discuss texts concerned with museum practices in different kinds of museums, historic preservation, archaeology, cultural landscapes, folklore and ethnography.  The term project is the development of a working bibliography on a topic of the student's choice.

 

HST 598 ? Special Topics: Modern Europe and the World
T, 3:40-6:30PM, COOR 4581
Professors:  Laurie Manchester , Roger Adelson , Rachel Fuchs , & Anna Holian

This team-taught course introduces beginning graduate students to some of the classic and cutting-edge recent historiography on modern Europe, with a particular focus on comparative and theoretical perspectives that take into account Europe's influence on and interaction with the rest of the world. Topics for this year's course include the idea of Europe, the birth of class, revolution and the nation-state, defining secularization, imperialism, migration, decolonization, the impact of world war, gender constructions, and the emergence of universalistic ideologies such as communism, fascism and nationalism. Many of the readings are not specific to any geographic region; some focus specifically on England, France, Russia and Germany. Students will be required to write book reviews, give oral presentations, actively participate in class discussions, and write a historiographical essay on one of the topics covered in the course.

 

HST 598 - Special Topics: Historiography in Latin American National Period
TH, 3:40-6:30PM, COOR 4581
Professor: Lynn Stoner

This course is intended to prepare graduate students for their preliminary exams in thematic approaches to Latin American history during the national period, 1821-2005. It will also train young professionals in the art and science of producing history through instruction in evaluating the methods of analysis used in historical research. Historians present their work at professional meetings, and students in this class will present their thoughts and ideas weekly in a formal format to develop the skill of public presentation and inquiry.

This course is based upon a thematic rather than a chronological examination of Latin American history from 1821-present. While many important themes have been omitted, my purpose is to stress areas that are well developed and demonstrate a mature historiography. Most of the works link specific themes to broader social, economic, cultural and political trends and issues prevalent in the Latin American experience. In some cases I have included authors whose disciplines are other than history, but their work has obvious value for the historian. In this way, we can observe how history itself is linked to literature, anthropology, sociology, political science, demography, geography, and even journalism. Thus, we will contemplate what history is and how it is distinguished from and interrelated with other disciplines.

Like the course on political and economic histories of modern Latin American countries, this course will emphasize Argentina, Brazil, Cuba, and Mexico. In the section on revolution, we will consider other nations as well.

 

HST 598 ? Special Topics: Core Readings in Race, Ethnicity, and Nation
M, 1:40-4:30PM, ED 328
Professor: Angela Wilson , Matthew Whitaker , & Brooks Simpson

This course will provide a conceptual foundation to explore and challenge Eurocentric, essentialist, "conservative," and "liberal" orthodoxies of race and ethnicity in North America.  It is based upon the premise that race and ethnicity lies at the very nexus of American history and life, and that historians should ask several fundamental questions including but not limited to: What are nation, race, and ethnicity?; What historical processes have contributed to the formation and maintenance of national, racial and ethnic hierarchies in North America?; How have revisionist interpretations of history, critiques of liberalism and conservatism, material determinism, structural determinism, biological determinism, postmodernism, decolonization, and popular culture influenced our understandings of nation, race and ethnicity and the ways we continue to negotiate them in this society?; How can we better understand the exigencies and intersectionality of nation, race and ethnicity, especially through the use of inclusive modes of information gathering and analysis (such as language, storytelling, counter-storytelling, personal narratives, material culture, informal communication, public monuments, celebrations, and commemorations)?  The legacies of invasion, conquest, slavery, colonization, immigration, and creolization are inseparable from issues of nation, race, and ethnicity and contribute to the structural foundation supporting the current hegemonic order permeating all of society.  This course, therefore, is based on the supposition that the study and analysis of nation, race, and ethnicity is not mere ornamentation in the construction of North American history: it is instead essential to the achievement of the fullest scholarly understanding. 

 

HST 598 ? Special Topics: Colloquium on the Southwest
W, 12:40-3:30PM, COOR 4581
Professor : Robert Trennert

This course is intended to introduce history graduate students to the general subject on Southwestern history.  Students will be expected to read eleven books (six required books and five from a selected list).  Topics will encompass five major themes: the Spanish period, the Mexican Southwest, the Territorial era, the 20th century, and the Ethnic Southwest.  Students must come to class prepared to make an oral presentation and to discuss the topic of the week.  Written reviews of five books will be required.  Finally, a paper (approximately 10 pages in length) analyzing the literature of a topic within the field of Southwestern studies will be due at the end of the semester.  An expanded definition of the Southwest (Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, California, Nevada, and Utah) will be employed.

 

HST 598 ? Special Topics: Introduction to English Legal History (Cross-listed with LAW 691)
TTH, 9:00-10:25AM, LAW 109
Professor: Jonathan Rose

The origins of the many of the characteristic institutions of the Anglo-American Common can all be traced back to the English medieval Common Law. These institutions include the courts and jurisdictional notions, a professional judiciary, an organized legal profession, the rules of procedure and pleading, and the reporting of cases. In addition, many modern legal doctrines have their origins in the English medieval Common Law. The course will focus on the creation of the Common Law as a legal system during the 12th and 13th centuries and on its subsequent development down to the 17th century. The course will also introduce the basic notions of substantive law in areas such as property, contract, tort, and criminal law and their development during the same time period. The course will make extensive use of relevant original documents in English translation as well as secondary literature. Additional instruction will be available for students who wish to learn how to work with primary sources in their original language and form.

 

HST 780 ? Practicum ? Preparing Future Faculty
F, 1:40-4:00PM, COOR 4581
Professor: Noel Stowe

This class is part of a four semester sequential introduction to the landscape of higher education, to the professoriate, and to faculty roles in higher education's various institutions.  Doctoral students planning to enter the professoriate should enroll in 780 beginning in their first semester of doctoral work.  PFF is based in a support cluster composed of historians at ASU West, ASU East, Grand Canyon University, Phoenix College, Chandler Gilbert Community College, and Scottsdale Community College.  Work begins with an orientation meeting during orientation week before classes begin.  The semester includes visits to the participating campuses.
