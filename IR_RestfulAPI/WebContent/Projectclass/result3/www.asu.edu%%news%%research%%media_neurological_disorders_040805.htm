April 8, 2005

Study explores media’s impact on neurological disorders

On April 12, an ASU graduate student will achieve a first for the Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication.

That morning, master’s of mass communications candidate Joseph Caspermeyer will make a presentation to the 57th annual meeting of the American Academy of Neurology in Miami Beach, Fla., reporting results of a newspaper analysis he undertook with neurologists at Mayo Clinic Scottsdale and Cronkite School mentors.

The annual meeting, expected to draw more than 8,000 neurologists to the world’s largest neuroscience gathering, will mark the first time a Cronkite student’s master’s work has been selected for such a prestigious debut.

Caspermeyer will report findings of a content analysis of nine U.S. newspapers, aimed at determining to what extent stories on patients with such neurological disorders as epilepsy and Parkinson’s disease used stigmatizing language that might increase the burden of the disease itself, and whether stories were over- or under-emphasizing critical information about treatment options and survivability.

Mayo’s Joseph Sirven, an epilepsy specialist who is associate dean of the Mayo School of Continuing Medical Education in the College of Medicine, explained that the study’s questions originated in the neurology clinic.

“The first thing that caught our attention is how very savvy patients are nowadays when they come to talk to us about their conditions,” he says. “They carry newspaper clippings and Internet printouts about the very latest treatment options.”

But the downside, Sirven said, is that those reports often overstate the availability of treatments.

“Sometimes stories would tout treatments that had only been tried in animals, that might be years even from clinical trials,” he says. “In addition, it struck me that health care professionals and journalists may not be aware of the impact that the language of news stories has on patients and families.”

Caspermeyer will present the results of his examination of the nine newspapers’ stories covering 11 neurological conditions for 2003, ranging from those in the New York Times, as a national newspaper of record, to the Arizona Republic, as a large regional paper. Assisted by graduate students in George Watson’s Research Methods class, Caspermeyer analyzed a random sample of stories for stigmatizing language and traced sources to the reporter, patient, family or health professional. Meanwhile, Sirven and fellow Mayo neurologist Joseph Drazkowski studied stories for accuracy across a range of professional issues, such as overstating or understating disease mortality and threat to quality of life.

Caspermeyer notes that many studies have looked into the media’s role in potentially stigmatizing issues such as race, gender and sexual preference or in medical topics including mental health and AIDS.

“But this is the first one to examine stigma over a broad range of neurological issues, which will help us to understand the language that newspapers use to describe individuals with various neurological conditions,” he says.

The study and results were put together by Caspermeyer, along with Cronkite School professor Ed Sylvester, a journalist specializing in neuroscience who teaches science writing, and Watson, an expert in statistical data analysis who teaches courses in media statistics and research methods.
