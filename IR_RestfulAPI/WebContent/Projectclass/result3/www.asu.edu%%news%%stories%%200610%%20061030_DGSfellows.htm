October 30, 2006

Division of Graduate Studies helps students with their academic journey

Pursuing a doctoral degree requires significant professional and personal commitment, and students who enter a doctoral program realize that they must invest a great deal of time and effort before they will be able to embark on careers in academia.

As part of its effort to help those students, the Division of Graduate Studies (DGS) developed a fellowship program a few years ago to help highly motivated students on their academic journeys.

In September, DGS gave out Doctoral Enrichment Fellowships to five top doctoral candidates who contribute to the diversity of ASU's student population. The award allows these students to focus exclusively on their studies and research for an entire year.

?The selection process was very competitive this year,? says Maria T. Allison, university vice provost and DGS dean. ?These five should be very proud of themselves, as they represent the best and the brightest of our Ph.D. students.?

The 2006-2007 Doctoral Enrichment Fellows are selected for their strong record of achievement and contribution to the diversity of ASU. They are:

? Hina Arora, who is pursuing a doctorate in business administration with an emphasis in computer and information systems. She received her bachelor's degree in physics from Women's Christian College in Madras, India, and her master's degree in electrical engineering from the Indian Institute of Science in Bangalore. Arora is interested in taking a modeling and simulation approach to optimize resource allocation across healthcare entities in the event of a public health emergency. Her research will result in a dissertation that makes a substantial contribution to the field of information systems and digital health care.

? Nestor Bravo Goldsmith, who is pursuing a doctorate in theater and performance in the Americas . Bravo Goldsmith received a master's degree in fine arts from the Universidad de Chile and another master's degree in theater from Brigham Young University. As a Chilean national, Bravo Goldsmith studied classical mime in Buenos Aires, Argentina, and he later created and directed the Universidad de la Frontera Theatre Co. His research focuses on theatrical documentation, theater and education, and the implementation of artistic principles and concepts of multimedia theater as a way of understanding theater and performance among Latino populations.

? Mahogany Brown, who is pursuing a doctorate in physical activity, nutrition and wellness. Brown holds a master's of education in educational leadership and educational specialist degree from Middle Tennessee State University . As a top athlete and former journalist, Brown has extensive expertise in teaching activity programs appropriate to underserved populations, and her research focuses on minority health disparities. She produced and starred in ?No More Excuses,? a television show that provides underserved groups with health and fitness options at home, and she believes that this type of community outreach yields the most effective health promotion results.

? Luz-Andrea Pfister, who is pursuing a doctorate in anthropology, received her medical degrees from the University of Chile and the University of Berne in Switzerland, and her master's degree in anthropology from the University of Nevada-Las Vegas. While researching viral pathogenesis, she developed interests in the use of infectious agents as markers of population history, and in host-pathogen co-evolution. Pfister's doctoral research is on the co-evolutionary history of humans and Mycobacterium tuberculosis, which will contribute to our understanding of this major public health problem.

? Lee Zelewicz, who is pursuing a master's in passing and a doctorate in anthropology, received his bachelor's degree in social anthropology and music from Lycoming College. Zelewicz's research focuses on the idea of identity and gender within Native American tribes, and the processes of assimilation ? culturally and behaviorally ? that have taken place within them. He also is an accomplished musician, and his secondary research focus is in ethnomusicology, with a special interest in the function and the role that music plays in Native American cultures.
