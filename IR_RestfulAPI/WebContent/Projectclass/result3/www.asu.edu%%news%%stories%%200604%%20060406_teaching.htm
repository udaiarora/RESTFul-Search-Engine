April 6 , 2006

Distinguished Teaching Academy promotes teaching excellence at ASU

Great teachers bring about superior learning for students. To encourage and promote a culture of teaching excellence, ASU is inaugurating a Distinguished Teaching Academy that will enhance the ability of teacher-scholars to help students learn.

With seed funding from the ASU Parents Association, the academy sponsored its first daylong seminar April 4, “Creating a Culture of Teaching Excellence.” Ten outstanding professors, all of whom have been honored in the past as Parents Association Professors or President’s Professors, have been named as Founding Fellows and are leading the group’s activities.

“We want to be able to tell students and their parents that we care about them and want to help them achieve,” says Margaret Nelson, associate dean of the Barrett Honors College and the academy’s first president. “These goals are at the core of excellent teaching. It’s not just about being clever in the classroom, it’s about engagement with students.”

The academy will begin by identifying practices that foster teaching and learning at ASU, and will develop a vision for creating a culture of teaching excellence as an integral part of the New American University.

“This effort is driven, led and inspired by faculty,” says Jane Maienschein, Regents’ Professor of biology and society and a fellow of the academy. “We all care about students. Pursuing a culture of teaching excellence is something we already do at the university, but on an informal basis. We want to make the effort more organized and more accessible.

“Great teaching is dynamic and exciting, and it is tied to research and discovery. We want to create a highly energetic environment in which our own faculty shares with each other and also learns from outside experts.”

The academy will sponsor a series of events across the university, Maienschein says. Workshops, lectures, brown-bag discussions and mentoring of new teachers may be part of the activities, and the group looks forward to ideas from others with similar commitments.

Kenneth Shaw, chancellor emeritus of Syracuse University, was the keynote speaker at the April 4 event. Shaw led Syracuse through its most comprehensive restructuring in history and set it on a course to become one of the nation’s leading student-centered research universities.

Academy Fellows will advise the provost on matters of teaching and learning excellence and provide leadership and guidance to the university as a whole.

In addition to Nelson and Maienschein, Founding Fellows of the academy are Neal Lester, Department of English; Ronald Roedel, Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering; Kenneth Morrison, Department of Religious Studies; George Watson, Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication; Afsaneh Nahavandi, School of Global Management and Leadership; Alice Christie, College of Teacher Education and Leadership; Randall Cerveny, Department of Geography; and Ian Gould, Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry. Six are in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences.

For more information, visit the Web site (www.asu.edu/dta).
