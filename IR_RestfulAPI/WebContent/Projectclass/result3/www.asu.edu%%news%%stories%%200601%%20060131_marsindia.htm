January 31, 2006

ASU Mars research reaches out to India

Techfest visit pays dividends for ASU

ASU’s Mars research team recently wrapped up an educational outreach mission in India, giving college-aged space exploration enthusiasts and dignitaries from the Indian Space Research Organization a close-up look at the Mars Exploration Rover (MER) project, meteorites and live images of the planet’s surface.

The “Welcome to Mars!” exhibit, modeled after last year’s ASU exhibition in China, was featured at Techfest ’06, the annual international science and technology festival of the Indian Institute of Technology (IIT) Bombay. An estimated 30,000 people attended the three-day event in Mumbai, India, noted to be the largest of its kind in Asia, according to Rohit Hippalgaonkar, an IIT student and Techfest media manager.

In promoting ASU’s participation, Techfest organizers emphasized that India was just the second country outside the United States to showcase the Mars exhibit.

“There was a huge media presence, with coverage in Times of India and CNBC,” Hippalgaonkar says.

Visitors “were engaged, smart, college-age kids,” says ASU geologist Joe Michalski, a postdoctoral research associate with ASU’s Mars Space Flight Facility.

“We were interacting with kids who have the desire and ability to explore space and become seriously involved,” adds Michalski, who also was a member of the ASU Mars team in China in May.

A large Mars globe was the centerpiece of the exhibit, which was surrounded by Mars rover panoramics, rock exhibits, Mars meteorites, rover models, DVD videos featuring rover animations and plasma screens showing live images of the planet’s surface that were taken by the Thermal Emission Imaging System (THEMIS), an instrument on board the Mars Odyssey spacecraft.

“I really think the visitors got a kick out of everything,” Michalski says.

In addition to answering hundreds of questions at the exhibit itself, Michalski and others from ASU gave lectures about the rovers and Mars exploration. At one lecture, nearly 5,000 were in attendance. That talk was delivered in an open-air theater by Michalski and Dale Noss, a computer system programmer with ASU’s Mars Space Flight Facility.

In related events, Steven Ruff, a faculty research associate with the facility, gave a keynote talk about the Mars rover mission, and Greg Mehall, mission manager at the facility, discussed space missions from an engineering and technical viewpoint. Rounding out the ASU research team in India was Shawn Wright, doctoral graduate student in geological sciences. Wright and Michalski also were able to conduct field work at the Lonar Crater – the world’s largest impact crater in basaltic rock.

Exhibiting at Techfest ’06 was part of a two-week outreach effort where ASU researchers visited several cities in India, sharing information about Mars, ASU’s role in space exploration and ASU’s earth and space programs, Mehall says.

This trip was “seed sowing,” says Michalski, referring to ASU’s commitment to form international collaborative efforts.
