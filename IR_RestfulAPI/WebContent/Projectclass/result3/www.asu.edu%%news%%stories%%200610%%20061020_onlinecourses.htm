October 20, 2006

Team produces innovative online courses

As the pace of everyday life continues to quicken, a series of interactive online courses conceptualized and created by professors in the Mary Lou Fulton College of Education are providing increased flexibility for the college's busy students and instructors.

Robert Atkinson, an assistant professor of educational technology, teamed with colleagues in the Division of Psychology in Education to develop the series of courses that combine videotaped lectures, PowerPoint presentations, blogs, electronic testing and assignments and synchronous office hours. Each course is uploaded to an online server by Matt Bitter, the project's database coordinator. Additionally, Matt Bitter provides continuing course support for professors throughout each semester. The result is an innovative online course delivery system that is the first of its kind.

?It's this blend that makes the classes unique,? Atkinson says. ?To my knowledge, no other university has developed anything comparable to this online delivery system.?

The novel format was introduced by Atkinson and his team in the summer of 2005 with a data analysis course taught by Joanna Gorin, an assistant professor of measurement, statistics, and methodological studies with the Mary Lou Fulton College of Education. The course has continued each semester since its debut, and three additional hybrid courses ? computer literacy (EDT 321), instructional media design (EDT 503), and games, simulations and virtual environments (EDT 591) ? were added this summer.

?I felt strongly that video and graphics would make online courses more realistic and interesting for the student,? says Gary Bitter, a professor of educational technology and one of the project's collaborators.

The response from students has been positive.

?I was a little apprehensive about taking my first graduate level course in a hybrid format, but the course was well-designed, clearly structured and effectively supported with technical and content assistance,? says Janet Krause, a graduate student in the college and program coordinator for ASU's University Testing Services. ?I could not have been happier with my experience.?

Frankie Sullivan, a graduate student and research associate at the Center for Research on Education in Science, Mathematics, Engineering and Technology (CRESMET), also enjoyed the online format.

?The combination of the videos and slides made for great notes,? Sullivan says. ?I found many advantages to this course.?

?The most notable thing students have reported to us in their feedback is that they enjoyed being able to revisit portions of the lecture in order to address misunderstandings or misconceptions,? Atkinson says.

He notes that synchronous online office hours included in the course structure allow professors to communicate one-on-one with their students.

The courses also are reaching greater numbers of nontraditional students, according to Matt Bitter, the project's database coordinator.

?By offering these courses, we open new doors for students who cannot attend a traditional classroom as well as working adults whose days are spent working and raising a family,? Matt Bitter says. ?Students can attend these classes at night or on weekends from wherever they can find an Internet connection.?

Atkinson, Gary Bitter and Matt Bitter plan to introduce more pioneering course delivery systems, including iPod video courses. They also are collaborating with their colleagues in the division to implement several graduate certification programs, including one that will allow teachers to receive 15 hours of technology integration instruction beyond their bachelor's degrees. The new programs are scheduled to launch in summer 2007. In addition, they are researching methods for delivering a course as an online game or within a virtual reality environment.

?The future is unlimited,? Gary Bitter says.
