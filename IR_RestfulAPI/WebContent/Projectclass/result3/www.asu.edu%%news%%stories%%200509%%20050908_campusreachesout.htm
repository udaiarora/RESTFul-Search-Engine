Sept. 8, 2005

Campus community reaches out

Since Hurricane Katrina hit, the university — both individually and collectively — has stepped forward to find ways to help the people impacted the most by the tragedy.

David Butler, associate vice president for human resources, is the key contact for coordinating temporary housing, emergency health care services, and primary and secondary education needs for ASU faculty and staff who have family members from the area that were directly affected.

Human Resources established a housing registry where ASU employees can offer housing assistance for faculty, students, and family members of staff, faculty and students who have been displaced by the hurricane. At least one ASU employee with extended family from the affected area has requested help.

"We have 30 offers of housing assistance from ASU employees,” Butler says. “The offers of assistance have been placed in a registry indicating number of rooms, housing logistics, preference for students, faculty or families, etc. We will be able to match needs of housing with available resources.”

ASU established a partnership with the Mayo Clinic in Scottsdale to offer medical care to displaced family members of ASU employees. Mayo recognizes that medical insurance documentation may not be available, and officials there indicated the clinic will offer emergency medical assistance regardless of the insurance situation of the affected individual.

On the child care front, Human Resources established a contact within the Tempe Union High School District and the Arizona State Department of Education to provide assistance to displaced families who need to enroll children into school and/or need pre-school child care. At least two employees have sought assistance enrolling children into school for their extended families who have relocated here from the affected region.

"It is important to remember that ASU draws employees from throughout the country,” Butler says. “We want to provide a variety of services for those who need help and for those who want to help.”

Throughout the university, ASU departments and faculty members are continuing to reach out to their colleagues along the Gulf Coast.

In the College of Design, efforts are under way to bring a group of fifth-year architecture students from Tulane to complete the semester on the ASU campus. Another effort in the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering is offering space for engineering faculty and programs from the region. The Biodesign Institute is offering space for displaced researchers.

In addition to these efforts, ASU is working on a variety of fronts to help students and employees, while reaching out to universities in the area, including:

• The College of Nursing is collecting, packaging and delivering children’s items.
• A student-organized Hurricane Relief Day for the university on September 14.
• The Phi Beta Sigma fraternity is collecting donations for the Red Cross in front of residence halls on the south side of the Tempe campus and in the MU.
• College of Education students and faculty members, with master’s degrees and counseling licenses, are offering mental health counseling service at the Coliseum through the Red Cross.
• Information Technology and University Libraries are working to provide courtesy accounts to enable faculty and students to have remote access to the library.
• W. P. Carey School of Business officials are exploring ways to support the employment needs of the evacuees in Phoenix.
• The School of Health Management and Policy is launching an outreach program to house LSU fans for the football game. An ASU Web site for faculty to register to accept fans is being developed.
• ASU is coordinating with national professional organizations on various measures to assist faculty and students.

Gary Campbell, (480) 965-7209 or (garycamp@asu.edu)
