July 21, 2003

Literature scholar, administrator Irvin takes reins at East College

Glenn Irvin, an English literature scholar and university administrator who has served in a series of senior academic leadership posts, including 11 years at CalPoly, San Luis Obispo, has been named as new dean of East College at ASU East.

The appointment comes as ASU's East Campus continues to implement President Michael Crow's vision for ASU East to become a 21st-century polytechnic university with a technological focus in all of its academic offerings.

Irvin previously was at ASU West, where he was an English professor in the College of Arts & Sciences and served as ASU West's vice provost for academic affairs from August 1997 to June 2002. Prior to that, he served as vice provost of academic affairs at Cal Poly and as dean of the university's College of Liberal Arts.
He also has served in a series of increasingly responsible academic positions, including associate vice president for academic affairs at East Texas State University, now Texas A&M-Commerce.

"Glenn's strong administrative background in the liberal arts and as an administrator at one of the nation's leading polytechnic universities will help guide East College as we implement a polytechnic focus for ASU East," said Charles Backus, ASU vice president and ASU East provost. "This is a critical time for East College, which is experiencing phenomenal growth in its academic offerings. Glenn knows how to lead this kind of college in a polytechnic setting, and I feel fortunate that we have found our next dean within the ASU family."

Irvin succeeds David Schwalm, who has served as dean of East College since its founding in 1997.

In that time, Schwalm also has served as ASU East's vice provost for academic affairs, a role in which he will continue.

"While it has been a pleasure to serve in this dual role, ASU East needs someone in each of these positions to focus all of their efforts on the individual needs of each area," said Schwalm. "I am confident Glenn will lead East College to an even stronger position as I focus my efforts on the campus's overall academic programs."

Said Backus, "We owe David a depth of gratitude for leading East College from its infancy. He is a valued member of the ASU East team, and I look forward to continuing to work with him in his vice provost role."

East College is among ASU's fastest growing colleges, serving approximately 1,000 students in a variety of professional and liberal arts programs. Among the college's offerings are elementary education; business administration; applied psychology; multimedia writing and technical communication; applied biological sciences; nutrition; exercise and wellness; and human health studies.

Irvin said his goal as dean of East College is to nurture existing programs and help create the appropriate new ones as ASU East further develops its academic focus.
"My aspirations for the college are similar to those of Provost Backus and others at ASU East and President Crow in that we become a university campus with a polytechnic mission, and that East College will continue to develop as a unit with professional, technical, and arts and sciences programs that contribute to that mission," said Irvin. "We will focus on developing outstanding programs, research and teaching within this environment."

ASU East has long caught the attention of Irvin, he said, since he came to ASU in 1997.

"Right from the beginning I thought the campus was developing in a distinctive manner, and that a polytechnic mission might be in its future," he said. "This campus is a wonderful place to be. It has outstanding leadership, an excellent faculty and staff, and is on track for an exciting mission."

Irvin grew up in Williams, Ariz., and earned his bachelor's degree in education, as well as his master's and doctoral degrees in English from ASU. His academic interests include Modernism, specifically 19th and 20th-century British literature, British transitional literature, and the British and American novel. Over the course of his career, Irvin has been involved in a number of projects involving the arts, sciences and education, and has taught courses at the undergraduate and graduate levels in literature, composition and rhetoric. He is widely published in the areas of literature, composition, liberal and general education and public education.

Irvin is married and has four children. His wife, Janet, is a technology consultant with the Maricopa County schools district. Their children are Meredith, Kelly, Darcy and Ryan.
