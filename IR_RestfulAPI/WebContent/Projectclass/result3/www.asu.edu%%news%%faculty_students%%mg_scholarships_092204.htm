September 22, 2004

Maroon and Gold Scholarships help students succeed

A record number of students will benefit from ASU’s Maroon and Gold Scholarship program this year, and the Scholarship Retention Committee credits unique student-involvement programs for the remarkably high number of scholarship renewals.

“You can create a community of young scholars,” says Alonzo Jones, a member of the committee. “They develop a habit or pattern of success.”

Maroon and Gold Scholarships are awarded to first-generation college students with high potential and relatively low incomes.

“I just feel this is wonderful,” says Dominique Noble, a pre-business freshman and scholarship recipient. “All of the programs help you get to know the campus. They teach you so much.”

The scholarship emphasizes student retention through a variety of programs, such as mandatory workshops and a one-on-one mentoring program. Students also can participate in a learning-enhancement series, which includes lectures, symposiums and performances.

“The Maroon and Gold Scholarship has been around for a long time,” Jones says. “It’s very powerful and beneficial to ASU. Students that take advantage of the resources here succeed.”

Ken Hollin, a member of the retention committee, says the first year is the most important in retaining students. He says the Maroon and Gold Scholarship is unique because it focuses on giving students the skills they need to succeed.

“The services offered by the program help them in their transition to the university,” he says. “The success of the program can be directly tied to the support from high level administration and a group of committed, dedicated staff members who serve as mentors for the scholars.”

As part of the program, students are paired with a faculty mentors, who encourage the students to use available tutoring services and become more involved on campus. They are available for advice and support.

Students can apply for scholarship renewal each year. To qualify, they must enroll full-time and maintain a competitive GPA. This year, 100 students qualified for renewal, but just 80 scholarships were available.

Working with Tim Desch, dean of undergraduate admissions, and Jim Rund, vice president for undergraduate initiatives, the committee increased funding and provided assistance to all 100 students.

“They both agreed that these students needed to be funded and approved an increase of scholarships … so all of the deserving students were funded,” Hollin says. “The ‘problem’ of having more students qualify for scholarships than are allotted is one every scholarship administrator would be glad to tackle.”

Hollin foresees a bright future for the program.

“I think we will see an increase in scholarship numbers, both for new incoming students and for continuing students who succeed both academically and socially at the university,” he says.

Noble says she hopes the program is continued.

“There are some students who don’t have the financial ability to attend college, and this really helps,” she says. “It helped me enormously.”
