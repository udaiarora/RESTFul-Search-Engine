Phoenix elementary students educate public about ecology

When Phoenix’s Rio Salado Habitat Restoration Project opens in November, students from four Valley elementary schools will be able to say that they played a visible part.

More importantly, they also will know more about the ecology of the restored area than most of its adult visitors.

Over the past two years, students from Greenfield, Lowell, Sunland and Valley View elementary schools have been participating in “Service at Salado,” an after-school science club co-sponsored by ASU and Phoenix. The students learned about science and ecology and developed a variety of practical projects that will be part of the educational program at the Rio Salado Habitat.

The project is a collaboration between two ASU groups: University College and the International Institute for Sustainability.

The students’ work was presented at the South Mountain Environmental Education Center in Phoenix.

The students’ projects were varied and creative. Greenfield Elementary, Lowell Elementary, and Sunland Elementary students created concrete steppingstones with tracks from native Arizona animals and a mural timeline to decorate trash cans.

The idea behind the steppingstones is to increase awareness of native animals and how restoration ecology can have a positive impact. The steppingstones provide a guide to identification of tracks found near the water.

The mural timeline project involved creating a chronology of the Rio Salado that was divided up into seven sections and painted on 55-gallon drum trash cans. The mural begins 200 million years ago and leaps forward to the first human occupation. Successive periods are spaced at 50-year intervals.

Students were guided in their historical research by the ASU interns and facilitators to create murals on paper. A composite time period mural was created and painted onto trash cans.

Lowell and Valley View Elementary also created a variety of informational signs for the habitat. In addition, Lowell students created petroglyph paintings to inform visitors about local Native American art, as well as birdhouses and batboxes. The batboxes, the youths noted, “will show the visitors that bats are not scary and that they are good because they eat lots of bugs.” Valley View students are also building an “interactive spin-o-rama,” which features information about Rio Salado and includes elements such as history, animals and plants.

For more information on Service at Salado, contact Charlene Saltz at ASU’s International Institute for Sustainability at (480) 965-1961 or Nancy Crocker at ASU’s University College at (480) 965-5694.
