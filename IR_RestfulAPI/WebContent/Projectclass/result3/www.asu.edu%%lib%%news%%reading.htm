Reading Culture: Texts and Textiles of Southeast Asia


(posted 2/20/05)



Loven textile worker in Muong Sayasila, Attopeu Province,
Laos, 1970. The William Sage Collection, Special Collections,
ASU Libraries.

February 7 – April 8, 2005
Hayden Library, Upper Concourse


Christopher A. Miller
Southeast Asian Studies Bibliographer and Exhibit Curator


The purpose of this exhibit is to present Southeast Asian culture through the very visual traditions of textiles and texts in the vernacular languages. The exhibit itself benefits from a combination of the knowledge of Southeast Asian community members from the Phoenix area; local faculty affiliated with the Program for Southeast Asian Studies (PSEAS) at ASU; and the extensive holdings on Southeast Asia in the regular stacks, the Southeast Asia Collection, and the Special Collections of ASU Libraries. This exhibit hopes to highlight and celebrate the myriad resources on Southeast Asia available to the academic community at Arizona State University.

Divided into five cases each with a specific focus, the exhibit seeks to present Southeast Asian texts and textiles in context in order to demonstrate how these objects may connect with social structure, mirror cultural identity, and define social space in Southeast Asia. Textiles from the region frequently contain texts or can themselves be “read” as cultural or historical documents. Southeast Asian vernacular scripts in turn are employed in the transmission of religious and cultural heritage or may be creatively arranged into spiritually potent or visually pleasing designs. This exhibit searches for the places in which these traditions intersect and duly demonstrates that ASU, with its Southeast Asian connections, is most certainly a part of those intersections.


The display also has a very practical function as well, to act in support of Professor Thomas Hudak’s concurrent exhibit in the ASU Museum of Anthropology, entitled “LOOM-inous Creations: Textile Traditions from Southeast Asia.” The library exhibit serves as bibliographic reference and introduction to ASU library holdings on the subject. And in this regard, special mention should be made of the valuable contribution of ASU alumnus and PSEAS adjunct faculty member William Sage, whose gift of ethnographic materials from Laos to ASU has deeply informed several parts of this exhibit and stands as an extraordinary contribution to the study of ethnic minority groups in Laos. Finally, the exhibit benefits visually from the generosity of Professor Hudak, Jacqueline Butler Diaz, and Anom Rahayu Kusumasari, who have provided the beautiful textiles that decorate the cases.
