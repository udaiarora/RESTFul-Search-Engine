Massachusetts teacher signing bonus program fails to produce expected benefits

TEMPE, Ariz.- Research to be published April 22 in the Education Policy Analysis Archives, a peer-reviewed scholarly journal, highlights the shortcomings of Massachusetts' rapid teacher certification program. The rapid teacher certification program attempts to alleviate teacher shortages in the state by offering financial bonuses and a fast-track teacher certification program.

The Massachusetts Signing Bonus Program for New Teachers offers $20,000 bonuses to those who join the Massachusetts Institute for New Teachers (MINT) and was created "to encourage high achieving candidates to enter the profession who would otherwise not consider a career in teaching."

Researcher R. Clarke Fowler of Salem State College found that the Massachusetts rapid teacher certification program fails to increase either the supply of teachers or their quality - two of the nation's most important educational issues. Fowler's research has important national policy implications due to the U.S. Department of Education's advocacy of rapid teacher certification programs.

Recruitment Failures

According to Fowler, in an attempt to attract "the best and the brightest," MINT maintains that it scoured the country for teaching talent. He argues MINT's efforts to recruit teacher trainees from states other than New England, New York, and Pennsylvania proved costly and ineffective, wasting $921,250 on teachers no longer in the program. Ironically, twice as many teachers joined the MINT program from states where no recruiters were sent. What's worse, 76% of MINT graduates said they would have entered MINT without a bonus.

High Attrition Rate

Fowler reports that in spite of contradictory claims by MINT, the Bonus Program suffered first-year teacher attrition at nearly twice the national rate (17% vs. 9% in 2001). High attrition rates, Fowler argues, not only entail a loss of teachers, but also a loss of training and bonus money.

Program Ineffectiveness

Fowler argues that the combination of high attrition rates, inadequate recruiting, and the squandering of financial resources make the MINT and Bonus programs inefficient and ineffective. According to Fowler, Massachusetts' fast-track program looks more like an expensive quick-fix than a model for solving the nation's teacher shortage problem.

Caution to Policy Makers

Fowler urges federal and state policy makers to resist the temptation to mount expensive and ineffective programs like the Massachusetts' Bonus Program. He cautions officials that experimental programs should demonstrate the rigor, transparency, objectivity that characterize proper experimentation and that the participants' characteristics, prior educational experience, eventual teaching placements and the retention rates in such programs be consistently evaluated.

Education Policy Analysis Archives (EPAA) is edited by Dr. Gene Glass. EPAA is a peer-reviewed scholarly journal published entirely without cost to readers since 1993. More than 2,000 persons visit the EPAA website each weekday. Articles are produced in English, Spanish, or Portuguese under the supervision of an editorial board representing scholars from seven nations.

The journal is available on the Internet at: http://epaa.asu.edu.

The Education Policy Studies Laboratory (EPSL) at Arizona State University offers high quality analyses of national education policy issues and provides an analytical resource for educators, journalists, and citizens. It includes the Commercialism in Education Research Unit (CERU), the Education Policy Analysis Archives (EPAA), the Education Policy Reports Project (EPRP), the Education Policy Research Unit (EPRU), and the Language Policy Research Unit (LPRU). The EPSL is directed by ASU Professor Alex Molnar.
