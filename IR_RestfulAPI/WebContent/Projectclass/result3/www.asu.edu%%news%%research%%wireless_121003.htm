ASU rides the wireless wave

Wireless is the wave of the future and ASU is on the edge of it.

Last July, Virtual Private Network (VPN)/SecureRemote became available for wireless users on ASU Main and East campuses.

"On an average day, it's estimated that 500 to 700 connections are made to the wireless network at ASU," says John Babb, assistant vice provost for Information Technology Instruction, Research & Infrastructure Support.

Wireless at ASU is an extension of the wired network that provides convenience and mobility and is a new technology that's still developing, according to Babb.
What does wireless mean to the average user?

It allows access to ASU servers without being hardwired, meaning a user can check e-mail from a laptop computer or a personal data assistant (PDA) while visiting an outdoor coffee shop on the campus.

"Students in the Residence Halls have access to wireless in some of the day rooms, where they can work collaboratively using their own computers, and staff will bring their laptops or PDAs to meetings to take notes or access information during the meeting," says Babb. "Even professors are using the technology when teaching classes outdoors."

Stumbling blocks for the development of all wireless networks include security, registration and authentication, according to Babb.

"Having the VPN/SecureRemote is a huge step forward, because providing security and privacy on wireless systems has always been difficult, and the VPN technology allows ASU to provide both," says Babb.

While the wireless network is expected to grow, it will be some time before campuswide wireless coverage is possible at Main and East.

At present, ASU has islands of connectivity at both campuses. As the network develops, the number of islands will grow and eventually merge. Currently, the wireless network on the Tempe campus is divided into five zones. The connection zone, sometimes called a hot spot, is a limited area of coverage.

"It may be necessary to 'release and renew' when moving from one zone to another or you may have to reboot the computer," says Babb.

It's expected that in the near future the release/renew process will be automatic, thus providing seamless roaming between zones.

For computers and PDAs to work in a zone, they need to be able to support the technology used by the local area network to connect. Wireless cards are included in new laptops and PDAs, and adapter cards can be purchased for older devices. Computers also have to be registered and configured for the ASU network before having wireless access.

Babb recommends that those interested in installing a wireless system consult IT's Datacomm group. Using their assistance will help departments avoid creating security and interference problems on the university's network.

The wireless world, at least on the Main Campus, has the potential to go beyond geographic boundaries.

Last week, representatives from ASU and the City of Tempe met to discuss expanding wireless service to include Mill Avenue.

"We also are looking at including the community around us, at least in Tempe, in ASU's wireless world," says Babb.

For more information about connecting to ASU's network, visit the ASU Wireless Web page at (www.asu.edu/wireless) or contact (comm-q@asu.edu) with questions.
