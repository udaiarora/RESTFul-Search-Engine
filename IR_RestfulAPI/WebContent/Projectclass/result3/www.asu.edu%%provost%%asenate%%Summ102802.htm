3.C  University Provost?s Report (Milton Glick).

 

Let me be relatively brief.  With regard to what happened at the University of Arizona?I know all of you are very concerned for your colleagues at UA.  One of the questions that might be on your mind is, are we doing everything we can do to avoid these acts of violence from entering our campus?  The answer is that you can never do all that you can do.  I think that we do have a lot of the right things in place, but I will ask you if there are any steps we should be taking that we have not taken, recognizing that part of our problem is that we want to allow freedom of expression--and how you stop that freedom at a point before a tragedy occurs is a very difficult issue.  A few years ago, the President of the Arizona Senate worked hard to get a law passed that would have allowed people to carry guns on our campus?It took all the effort of the University lobbyist to kill that bill and this is now a weapons free campus.  The very fact that we had major state leaders arguing that we ought to allow our universities to be places that are armed seems to say a lot about the society in which we live and what we need to do to protect the civilized atmosphere of the campus.  That leads me into a second issue, which is that as things evolve in Washington and in the world, we will probably see more and more of a variety of protests on all campuses, including ours.  We will as a campus have to come together to decide how one does protect both freedom of speech and the rights of all the individuals on the campus.  It is our intent to use a model we have on this campus, the Campus Environment Team, which I think has been extraordinarily effective in difficult times in the past.  We want to reinvigorate them and bring them on line early as to what kind of steps we should take, when various protests may arise. There were some anti-war protests happening around the country this past weekend. They were not here, but they will be here if things escalate, and I think we need to make sure that we make this a campus both safe for people, but also safe for ideas.  I will be looking to you to help in that task.

 

The last few weeks we have been looking intently at three things: 1) How do we deal with this year?s budget? 2) Because this year?s budget is already into the fiscal year we have limited options before us--so how do we make the decisions now, to prepare us for next year?s budget? and 3) how do we in some collegial fashion talk about what the essential programs are for ASU, and how do we distribute them among our several campuses?  On the first item, we did receive an official letter from the Governor last week, saying that she was asking us to operate with only 95% of our 2003 appropriation.  I do know how you react to something, but it usually depends on what you had been prepared to see.  Normally we would say that having 5% of our budget removed would be a horrific experience, especially since we are well into the fiscal year.  This year since there was so much talk about 10%, since most of other state agencies, except K-12, and since we know the state?s overall budget, 5% seemed somehow like something to celebrate.  Nonetheless, to remove 5% of our appropriation at this point in the year, or at any point, is going to be tough and not painless, and it will be a serious issue.  We are grateful that as of now the reduction is being held at 5%.  As I have said before, we will do this by protecting first academic programs and student access, student progress, second, strategic research areas and third (the  least protected), will be the administrative functions of the university.  Once again, we are hitting the administrative side of the university harder in order to protect the academic side.  I am assuming that everyone in this room agrees with us that this should be our priority, however, that means when our services go down, we will not be able to do more with less, at some point.  We have also told the administrative units, or will be telling them, when we inform them of their budget reductions that they are not allowed to pass on those reductions to you (to internal users).  As an example, if we reduce a service unit, they can?t just charge the academic units to make it up.  If they can generate additional revenues outside the university that is fair game--but not inside the university.  Each of these reductions make it more difficult for us to meet our agenda.  Nonetheless, given the state of the state, we think that if we can get away with where we are, we have been treated fairly, not in terms of our needs and in terms of our legitimate aspirations for the state, but in terms of the choices the lawmakers right now have available to us.  It is likely that the Governor will call a special session, right after the election to pass these reductions into law.  As of now, she has not done so.  There is pressure building for her to do so, and she has said she will not do it if the legislature is not ready to embrace this request.  The same letter said that she is very concerned about the state?s ability to put aside enough money to allow the state to move into self-insurance next September or October.  You have heard about that from Doug Johnson, who has worked very hard on this issue.  I believe that the state, and therefore the university, would be best served if the governor would get the legislature to delay a year on self-insurance.  If they do that, we intend to use that time to put together models on what will serve universities citizens best in terms of health insurance.  We have a lot of people within this university assigned and working on that already. 

 

I noticed that later on the agenda there is comment from one of your committees on child care.  You should know that even in these tight financial times we have allocated an additional $50,000 for the improvement of child care.  One of our campus units will be taking over the operation of Open Horizons, which will guarantee our people a number of additional slots. 

 

The Inauguration Ceremony is on Friday, November 8.  It is our intention within ten days following the Inauguration to have a town hall, so that faculty, staff and students having heard or read of Dr. Crow?s plans will have a chance to come and talk to the President about those plans.  At that time, questions about how we are managing the budget, questions about strategic priorities and questions about the overall agenda would be welcome and we would be looking forward to an interactive dialogue.  We will let you know when there is a time and place for the town hall arranged. 

 

In addition, it is clear that the tighter the budget becomes, the more little inconveniences become intolerable for people.  In that in mind, I would like to ask you to let my office know of any barriers you see within the institution that make it more difficult for us to achieve our full potential.  I am thinking particularly about administrative policy barriers.  You are welcome to name other ones too, but I do know that the higher you aspire and the tighter the budget becomes, the more those little barriers will get in the way.  Please, if you have a problem, if you see a policy or practice that ought to be changed, let me know precisely what it is?but do not draw the big picture of saying simply, HR gets in my way because that may well be true but it doesn?t help very much to generalize.  If you have specifics, we will try to deal with them.  Please provide us with particular suggestions, and preferably if you have a problem, you may also have a solution in mind and that is even better.  Share with us those ideas that you think would help you do your job better.  That concludes my report and I would be happy to answer questions.

 

Senator Johnson: The Joint Legislative Budget Committee report this week indicated that revenue collections for university tuition was exceeding that in the appropriations budget, by something like $18 million.  How does that affect the budget revertment or our ability to manage?

 

Provost Glick: The combination of the rate increase and the number of students has generated for the three universities more tuition revenue than we had projected.  That is used to cover classes largely, and we were pleased that the JLBC understood that it was in fact being used to cover the cost of additional students, and that they would not try to take it away and take it to solve the state?s problems.  As we put together our budget, those of you that are closely aware of the budget will know that, except for some money for enrollment growth, we did not distribute money to the colleges and to the vice presidents, which is new money.  The reason being that we did not anticipate this large a revertment, but we did anticipate some revertment.  Because of that, when we pass on these reductions to the units we will be passing on less than 5% in some cases.  We did hold that money in order to allow us to deal with the likely budget reduction; we are passing on only half of the budget reduction.  We tried to prepare.  One of the problems that we have is that the major growth has been at East and West.  The majority of that money is not on the main campus, and yet both East and West did not get any enrollment growth money from the legislature because they had in fact in a percentage view 20-35% growth on their campuses, and no enrollment growth money.  Therefore, a major use of our tuition increase has to be to help those campuses make sure they can meet their fundamental responsibilities.  If we did not have that surplus tuition, I would be sitting here in much greater angst then I already am. 

 

3.D  President-Elect?s Report (Antonio Garcia).

 

A lot of you may have heard of the Translational Geonomics Research Institute, so I will report to you on that today.  The Translational Genomics Research Institute is a big thing to pronounce, so they have shortened it to TGen (pronounced tee-gen).  I have had a long conversation with Mike Berens, who is a VP for Research for TGen, and he shared some information with me that I will pass along to you.  TGen and the relationship of TGen to the universities is something that the Board of Regents is working on as one of the items on their agenda for the fall.  According to Dr. Berens, TGen is an entity that is a startup company of 350 employees, when it comes up and running.  It has been in operation now for about 15 weeks, so it has just been given birth to basically.  Of those 350 people, the break down, at least as they envision it now, will be about 80 administrative support people and what they call principal investigators or main researchers.  Of that 350, there will be about 25 principal investigators and each PI will have 10 research staff associated with their group or their lab.  The reason that I am breaking this down for you step by step, is that there has been a lot of discussion about TGen and how the relationship of TGen is going to work, how they will be affiliated or pointed at the universities and at the same time be a member of TGen.  From my conversation with Dr. Berens, he explained that we are really talking about 25 PIs of this enterprise.  Of that number, he felt only about half of them will want to seek an affiliation with the universities.  Rather than talking about 350 people somehow being affiliated with the universities, there will be about a dozen people who will want to be affiliated with the universities.  In fact, there has already been one hired in this regard, someone who is affiliated with TGen and also with ASU.  As this situation progresses, I think that all of us will be interested in how TGen develops, and I will be pursuing an interview with Dr. Berens more fully, in either the next Academic Senate Newsletter, or the following Newsletter after that. Dr. Berens is very excited about affiliating with the universities, and especially ASU, and is looking toward having ASU faculty get involved.

 

3.E  ASASU President?s Report (Michael Leingang).

 

Last week we held a constitutional election to approve the new constitution, which we worked on diligently last year.  It passed by 83% voting for it.  Three percent of the students voted, which is actually very good considering that not many people even knew there was a new constitution to be voted on.  What does that mean for us?  It means there will be three separate entities within the student government: a graduate student government, an undergraduate student government and a programming board that will bring activities to campus.  It is good in a sense, because it allows the graduate students the ability to focus on their own issues, which in the past went through ASASU, and they will be able to focus on their own issues now.  We have a tuition town hall coming up in November.  It will be patterned after the Arizona Town Hall?students will be sitting around and addressing questions such as: What they want to see with the expected tuition increase? What do they want to see for their money? How are we going to address accessibility, financial aid and make sure that every one can still attend ASU? 

      

We will be working with Institutional Advancement and we will be holding a legislative dinner in January.  It will be sponsored by ASA, but spearheaded by our government relations department and it should be pretty good.  We will fight the battles for the University administration, because it seems as though the legislature tends to like the students more than the administration (Laughter here). 

      

On the IT issue, I have discussed this matter with Bill Lewis a couple of times.  We discussed a possible IT fee for printing and extended hours of operation, and I am happy to announce that we are no longer investigating an IT fee.  They will be reverting to the former hours at the Computer Commons; it will be 24 hours Monday through Thursday.  It will be a good thing for the students. 

      

I am happy to announce that there is a vacancy in the Executive Vice President?s position and I am accepting applications.  Resumes will be accepted and then reviewed by a board that I have appointed.  Interviews will be conducted and we are trying to get people from outside of ASASU into government, people who are not disenchanted with what has happened.  Along with this vacancy, there are others still to fill, because other senators have decided to resign as well.  Hopefully we will get some senators in there that are concerned about student?s issues.

3.G  West Academic Senate Report (Fran Bernat).

 

The Changing Directions process has begun at the West Campus.  We are doing a lot of meeting among different groups.  We are trying to determine what the current academic agenda for West Campus is, and should be.  There are four questions that various task force groups have been addressing over the past month: 1) What is the current academic identity? 2) What are we good at?  3) What are our unique aspects? 4) What do we aspire to do or to be?  The Academic Senate will meet on Wednesday to review a lot of the different discussions that have been happening across the campus and units.  Classified staff council will also meet to discuss these questions, advisors from student affairs, student groups, CET at the West Camus, and community input is going to occur within the next week.  There will be representatives from the community advisory boards discussing the ASU West Changing Directions initiative and there will be focus groups with the deans and various other institutional planners.  Stage I will be completed by October 30th.  There will be synthesis done by another task force, to be completed by November 7 and the task force will write a report that will be circulated back to the faculty, classified staff, students, community groups and others.  Stage II will be completed by November 20th.  Stage III is that the West task force will review input that comes back from the second round; we will complete a draft report on that by December 15th.  Stage IV is that the Provost and President will receive this by mid-December, and they will provide feedback to the West Task Force by January 15.   I am happy to inform you that we are well on our way.   Stage V is the report that comes back from the President?s office, which will be reviewed by the Task Force and it will be circulated back to the faculty, staff, students, for some review, but the final report will be completed by February 15.  People on campus think that this is an aggressive agenda--the departments and faculty members have had input into the discussion?and we believe that the input is going to be heard.  We do not have any idea of the final result and that is part of our discussion.  We have an idea?there are always concerns that faculty have in changing processes, but I believe the faculty feel that they at least have input into what their directions will finally be.
