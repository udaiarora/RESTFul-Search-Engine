April 13, 2005

ASU’s Sawhney develops model to measure global economy

Construction modeling and statistics research being conducted in the Ira. A. Fulton School of Engineering could help developing countries around the world build and maintain economic data, a vital task for participation in a global economy.

A new measurement model for comparing construction sector costs internationally has been developed by Del E. Webb School of Construction associate professor Anil Sawhney and professor Kenneth Walsh from San Diego State University. It has been adopted by the International Comparison Program (ICP) Global Office, housed in the World Bank, as the ICP prepares to launch a new round of surveys in more than 110 countries.

ICP price surveys are conducted globally every five years to generate internationally comparable data in the form of Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) estimates for sectors such as government services, construction, rental housing and capital goods. PPP estimates are used by national policy makers, economists, academicians, multilateral corporations and international organizations to determine the relative purchasing power of national currencies.

According to Yonas Biru, senior economist and deputy global manager of the ICP program at the World Bank, the ICP is probably the most difficult international statistical activity to manage – and one of the most complex to implement.

“It involves collecting very detailed comparable price (cost) and expenditure data according to agreed standards on a coordinated basis in all participating countries,” Biru says.

Most sectors of the economy use a basket of goods and services for comparing commodities such as clothing and footwear, says Sawhney, who specializes in construction econometrics.

“In construction sector comparisons, however, arriving at such a basket is inherently difficult,” Sawhney says. “For this very reason, economists have called the construction sector a ‘comparison resistant sector.’ ”

Previously, the ICP performed construction sector comparisons using a basket of standard construction projects developed in 1970 by Eurostat, the EU statistical office. The standard approach involved building a global list of construction projects, whose cost was collected by all participating countries. The objective was to get the most representative, in terms of widespread availability, and most comparable projects in project components that would provide an average cost of the project under consideration.

Sawhney and Walsh successfully argued that the standard projects used in the basket were not representative of construction in most countries, and so were not an appropriate basis for comparison. To address these issues, Sawhney and Walsh developed the basket of construction components (BOCC) approach, where economists price a set of construction components in ICP countries.

“The new approach simplifies the data collection process, reduces the cost associated with global construction surveys, while improving comparability,” Biru says. “The BOCC approach takes us a long way in resolving one of the most vexing problems of the ICP.”

In 2004, Sawhney and Walsh conducted field missions in seven African countries, plus five countries in Asia and the Middle East , to test their approach within a regional context.

“Countries such as Rwanda and Cameroon that are in the process of strengthening their national statistical institutes showed great enthusiasm at the prospect of utilizing ICP efforts in their country’s construction index work,” Sawhney says.
