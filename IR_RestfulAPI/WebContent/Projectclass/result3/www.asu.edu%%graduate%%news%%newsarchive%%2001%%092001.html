ASU STUDENTS WIN PRESTIGIOUS FORD FOUNDATION FELLOWSHIPS FOR MINORITIES

Benah Parker and Verma Lynne Miera, graduate students at Arizona State University, are 2001 recipients of the prestigious Ford Foundation Pre-doctoral Fellowship for Minorities. The Ford Fellowship is a three-year award with an annual stipend of $15,500; tuition, fees and some travel costs for conferences are also covered. Twenty ASU graduate students have won this award in the past 18 years.

Sixty pre-doctoral students received the fellowship in this year's national competition administered by the National Research Council (NRC) of the National Academies on behalf of the Ford Foundation. Winners are individuals who have demonstrated superior scholarship and show great promise for future achievement as scholars, researchers and teachers in institutions of higher education.

Parker is a Ph.D. student in the Department of Psychology, with a research interest in social psychology. "My current research efforts are aimed at exploring how gender and ethnicity interact to help shape an individual's self-concept," she said.

Working with Regents' Professor Nancy Felipe Russo, Parker recently completed her second year in the program. She defended her master's thesis in August and plans to spend the next year preparing for the comprehensive examination and submitting manuscripts for publication to various research journals. Parker recently presented research at the American Psychological Association national convention in San Francisco.

A native of the Houston suburb of Friendswood, Texas, Parker attended the University of Texas, Austin for her undergraduate studies. Prior to receiving the Ford Fellowship this year, she received a Graduate College Support Program Fellowship (1999), Graduate Academic Scholarship (1999), and the University Graduate Scholar award, the Ford Foundation Pre-doctoral Fellowship Honorable Mention (2000) and several awards while attending UT-Austin.

"I hope to continue a life of academia after completing my Ph.D.," Parker said. "At this point in my career, my goal is to work at a large research-oriented university and continue teaching and conducting research. However, I am also interested in public policy-making, so there may be some less-than-strictly-academic work in my future as well."

Miera is working toward a doctorate in biology. Mentored by the chair in biology, Prof. James Collins, she studies disease in amphibian populations. This is Miera's second year in the program, and she hopes to finish up in four years. Miera is also a University Graduate Scholar.

A Tucson native, Miera earned a bachelor's degree in ecology and evolutionary biology at the University of Arizona. Prior to starting graduate studies at ASU, she worked with the Arizona Game & Fish Department's frog program for three years.

"My goal is an academic career where I will continue to study the ecology of amphibians," Miera said. "I am also interested in the interaction between policy, sociology and science, which may lead me to some involvement in government and policy making."

The Ford Fellowship will allow Miera the time to concentrate on her individual research. "The Ford Fellowship will also help provide some funding for my research supplies and field work," Miera said. "This support gives me the freedom and independence to explore areas of interest to me."

The two ASU students who won this award in 2000 — William Graves (anthropology) and Vicky Lomay (counseling psychology) — are now in their second year as Ford Fellows.

Three sociology students received honorable mention in this year's competition for the Ford Foundation Dissertation Fellowships for Minorities: Yvonne Luna, Thomas Macias, and Edwardo Portillos.
