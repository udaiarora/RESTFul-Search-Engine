January 27, 2006

Dynamic center offers innovation, economic engine

The new ASU Scottsdale Innovation Center is gearing up to be an important new economic generator and civic amenity in Scottsdale.

The dynamic center will be a home for a diverse set of tenants: ASU units, large multinational corporations, small and medium-sized technology companies, and arts and culture organizations, among others. These tenants will build a culture of innovation to advance the regional economy.

Construction on the 1.2 million square foot project begins in March with occupancy scheduled for early 2007.

On a larger scale, the center will be a global focal point for cross-disciplinary collaboration, technological innovation and entrepreneurial ventures. Physically, it will embody creative architecture in an urban environment with 1.2 million square feet of high-tech commercial office space, displays of ASU’s most innovative technologies, multimedia-enriched pedestrian areas, retail space, restaurants and community outreach programming.

“This center is not a traditional research park, nor a business park, nor a university campus; it is a hybrid of all three,” says ASU President Michael Crow. “This center will become home to activities and organizations that stimulate new forms of commerce, research, technology, art, education and economic development.”

The Innovation Center, which embraces ASU’s design imperatives in creating a New American University, represents the ideas of excellence, impact and inclusion. It will be able to influence the community through partnerships with local schools, community colleges and arts programs, and it will serve as a hub for international technology collaboration. The hope is that by gathering a variety of innovative and creative activities, a new entrepreneurial culture will grow that advances the greater Phoenix metropolitan area economically, socially and culturally.

The center aims to promote an inclusive, innovative ecology where cultural, social and educational ventures thrive alongside businesses. ASU is the driving force behind the center’s focus on interactivity – creating an environment where large global enterprises, individual creators, entrepreneurs, ASU researchers and community members can connect and exchange ideas and skills.

The university will locate its entrepreneurial training and technology transfer units at the center, in addition to some of its community outreach programs, globally focused initiatives, and technology education and research programs.

The Innovation Center also is designed to be a place where creative individuals and organizations can achieve global excellence through innovation and collaboration. Advantages to the center’s location include enhanced local and global visibility; opportunities for connections with new public and private partners; and additional opportunities to commercialize university research.

The center’s global orientation offers a venue for the Valley’s numerous internationally focused business, community and cultural organizations to gain visibility, conduct festivals and performances, and play host to clients, meetings and conferences.

The genesis of the center came from the “Which Way Scottsdale?” report released by ASU’s Morrison Institute for Public Policy in 2003, which noted that the city was “behind the curve in hooking into” cutting-edge, technology-based industries. The report suggested the Los Arcos Mall location, at the southeast corner of Scottsdale and McDowell roads, as a potential site to spur such activity.

With the previous failure of a number of plans to rebuild at the site, ASU, the ASU Foundation and Scottsdale officials reached an agreement in May 2004 to buy and develop the site. Scottsdale acquired the 42-acre site and is leasing 37 acres to the ASU Foundation, while also being responsible for more than $40 million in infrastructure upgrades at the site.

To further strengthen the project, ASU’s Office of Economic Affairs, within the Office of the Vice President for Research and Economic Affairs, has worked to identify global models that correspond to the aims of the center. ASU representatives, including Associate Vice President for Economic Affairs Rob Melnick and Assistant Vice President for Economic Affairs Julia Rosen, traveled extensively to study the best practices of science parks and innovation centers around the world. Global experts on research park practices and operations also were consulted to develop the mission for the site.

“We believe SkySong is unique in providing a regional portal to the global economy, as well as a one-stop connection between global businesses and a university’s intellectual capital and sources of innovation,” says ASU Foundation President Ira Jackson. “It’s an exciting model, a transformational development, and a real game-changer for Scottsdale, the Valley and for ASU.”

Higgins Development Partners and its local partner, The Plaza Cos., were selected in February 2005 to develop the project. The development team includes the noted architectural firm of Pei Cobb Freed and Partners.

The initial site plan for Phase I, approved by the Scottsdale City Council, will include 300,000 square feet of retail, office and open space. Tenants are expected to move in during the first quarter of 2007.

The first two non-ASU tenants for the facility are eFunds and Wildfire Broadband Communications.

eFunds provides payment processing solutions to businesses and anticipates the move to the center will provide the opportunity to promote and accelerate its innovation processes as part of its solution strategy. Wildfire Broadband, a provider of wireless solutions to the private and public sector, will provide wireless services at the center in addition to being a tenant.

The university is also in discussion with the Scottsdale Unified School District and the Maricopa County Community College District regarding participation and expansion of education-related programs at SkySong.
