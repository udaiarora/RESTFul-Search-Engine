September 9, 2005

Glass, Swadener earn national recognition for education efforts

Gene Glass and Beth Swadener, professors with ASU’s College of Education, were honored recently by the American Educational Research Association with two of the organization’s most prestigious awards:

• Glass, Regents’ Professor of educational leadership and policy studies, was presented the 2005 Distinguished Contributions to Research in Education Award for his extraordinary leadership in the field spanning more than 40 years.

• Swadener, professor of curriculum and instruction, is the recipient of AERA’s 2005 Book of the Year Award, presented by the narrative special interest group for her work titled “Decolonizing Research in Cross-Cultural Context: Critical Personal Narratives.”

The Oxford English Dictionary will forever link Glass with the term “meta-analysis,” in recognition of the innovative statistical tool he and colleague Mary Lee Smith, ASU Regents’ Professor of educational leadership and policy studies, developed for evaluating research results across disciplines.

The technique is used today as the standard tool for the integration of empirical research in social sciences and medicine.

Glass also is well-known for his breakthrough work in creating the peer-reviewed, online journal Education Policy Analysis Archives in 1993 as one of the very first free-to-read journals. Through his commitment to scholarship, the journal has developed into a leading publication in education research, cited in the New Yorker, read by teachers and parents, and regularly turned to by scholars from 80 countries around the world.

After putting out the journal in three languages – with the assistance of associate editors Gustavo Fischman, assistant professor of curriculum and instruction at ASU, and Pablo Gentili – over the last dozen years, Glass recently stepped down as editor.

“His commitment to creating new avenues of communication among research and the public, including the open-access, peer-reviewed journal Education Policy Analysis Archives, is legendary,” says AERA awards chair Annemarie S. Palincsar. “Whether applied to the study of psychotherapy, the efficacy of special education or the issue of class size, his work has advanced the field’s capacity to integrate and learn from its collective research.”

“Many other scholars have done more in their lifetime and deserve the award more,” Glass said to colleagues when he accepted the award at AERA’s annual meeting, held in April in Montreal. “One never really truly knows why one has been singled out.”

In “Decolonizing Research in Cross-Cultural Contexts: Critical Personal Narratives,” – a book Swadener co-edited with Kegendo Mutua, an associate professor at the University of Alabama – international scholars share their experiences with the challenges inherent in representing indigenous cultures and decolonizing cross-cultural research.

“Work that explores decolonialism is absolutely needed,” says Gaile Cannella, ASU professor of curriculum and instruction and a nationally known expert on the subject explored in Swadener’s award-winning book. “The strengths of this book include coverage of general postcolonial issues; the multiple and traveling positions, identities, and subjectivities that are experienced by postcolonial scholars; and the possibilities for reconceptualizing research as a movement toward decolonialism.”

“The work was recognized as making use of a relatively new genre in educational research: critical narrative,” Swadener says. “We were surprised and delighted for this recognition. It builds upon work that Kagenda and I have been doing for many years and includes essays that are now being cited in other literature.”

Before joining ASU in 2001 as a professor and graduate coordinator of the College of Education’s Early Childhood Education program, Swadener was a professor of teaching, leadership and curriculum studies in early childhood education at Kent State University.
