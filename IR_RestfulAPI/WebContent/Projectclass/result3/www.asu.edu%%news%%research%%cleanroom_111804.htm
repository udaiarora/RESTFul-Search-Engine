Report: Cleanroom space an untapped resource

An overlooked resource in developing metropolitan Phoenix ’s biosciences sector is the large supply of available cleanroom space in the region, according to a report from the Office of Economic Affairs, a unit with ASU’s Office of the Vice President for Research and Economic Affairs.

The contraction of the semiconductor industry locally has left a significant amount of cleanroom space that can be converted into wet labs for biotech users. According to the report, “Positioning Arizona as a Bioscience Leader: Leveraging Greater Phoenix Assets to Help Meet Bioscience Facility Demand,” the Phoenix metro area has 1.4 million square feet, if not more, of vacant, or otherwise available, cleanroom space and associated support space.

“The report uncovers a hidden asset that Phoenix has to develop – its bioscience industry,” says Keith Aspinall, coauthor of the report and analyst with ASU’s Office of Economic Affairs. “The inventory of cleanroom space illustrates the amount of space that is available. While the properties themselves may change, the principle of the report will remain that there is a viable way for Phoenix to increase its supply of lab space for biotech users.”

A key element of the report is that the conversion of cleanrooms to wet labs is significantly cheaper than building new lab facilities, and it takes less time to do so.

New lab facilities can take up to three years from design to occupancy and cost more than $350 per square foot, while conversion can be done in months and cost under $200 per square foot. The report also identifies the issues and challenges associated with converting cleanrooms into wet labs and includes a comprehensive inventory of cleanrooms that are suitable for conversion in greater Phoenix.

“Cleanrooms can be an excellent target for conversion,” says Allan Chasey, director of ASU’s Construction, Research and Education for Advanced Technology Environments, “assuming there is a good match between the facility and the potential user. Cleanrooms and wet labs have similar structural and engineering systems, which can make up nearly half the cost of constructing new labs. By converting the facilities, those systems can remain in place, and be modified to suit the biotech user.”

“The report is a valuable addition to efforts to grow the local biotech industry,” says Saundra Johnson, associate director of Public Programs for the Flinn Foundation.

The report – “Positioning Arizona as a Bioscience Leader” – is a product of ASU’s Office of Economic Affairs, which works to connect the university and the community to stimulate and add value to Arizona ’s economy. The report is available online at (http://researchnet.asu.edu/BioscienceReport.pdf.)
