Americans' stress level high, says ASU Professor

At this moment in time, Americans are faced with an unprecedented level of uncertainty. Between dwindling financial resources, corporate downsizing, threats of terror and war-not to mention normal daily challenges-feeling stressed out is a normal response to exposure of these acute and chronic stressors according to Margi Waller, an associate professor in the School of Social Work, at Arizona State University in Tempe.

Waller says it is important to recognize that most Americans are probably experiencing the impact of these stressors, even if they don't feel distressed at the moment.

"Ambiguity is one of human beings' least favorite things and Americans' are particularly vulnerable because we have so little practice dealing with this level of ambiguity in our lives and particularly, about our safety. We are dealing not only with this fear, but also with the real anxiety about the current level of uncertainty in our lives, " Waller said.

Chronic stress is like an illness that requires management as opposed to acute stress that calls for mobilization, she says.

Chronic stress may cause sleeplessness, memory problems, worry and anxiety, sexual problems, hair loss, chronic tiredness, and a change in eating habits and appetite. In some cases, suffers may feel overwhelmed" or "out of control", and may be more mistake or accident-prone. Chronic stress is also associated to an increase in work-related absenteeism.

Given the right amount of stress, everyone is susceptible to chronic stress serious enough that it requires professional help. Knowing how to identify the symptomatic road signs is critical to maintaining ones physical and mental health says Waller.

Coping with stressors is not a one-size-fits-all process. She says coping styles are idiosyncratic and each individual should employ techniques that work for them. However, while everyone's coping style is unique, there are some basic practices that seem to work well for most people, Waller says.

Generativity. Order your personal priorities, giving the most energy to the things and people that give your life meaning and purpose. Most of us have a finite amount of energy, so prioritizing is important to ensure that you are not needlessly over-extending yourself, she said.

Attend to the Basics. Basics include body maintenance, including sleep, healthy diet and exercise. Waller also says in times of chronic stress it is equally important to schedule some down time. "Many people respond to stress by trying to go into high gear and produce more, but this is not generally a healthy response. If anything, we should commit to and produce less while reminding ourselves that down time is necessary maintenance to prevent additional health problems relating to failure to take care of ourselves."

Nurturing. Treat yourself to special things. Laugh, dance and sing. Take a walk or a long relaxing bath, putter in your garden, or go to a movie or spend the weekend fishing. "As any child will tell you play is a restores body, mind and spirit," she said.

Social Support. Thirty years of research has confirmed that social support strengthens individuals, families and communities, says Waller. "People with social support cope better than who are socially isolated." Increase the time spent with people you feel good around, and let trusted people know how you feel and talk about what is bothering you. Likewise, minimize the time spent with people you feel stressed around and limit your exposure to hostile and fear engendering messages.

Distract yourself with purposeful activity. Rather than shutting down in front of the television set, Waller suggests it is healthier to engage in planning a trip (even if you can't take it right away) or helping someone in some small way. Engaging in an activity that makes you feel good inside can work wonders, she said.
