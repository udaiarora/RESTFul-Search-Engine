Parking and Transit Services

Parking and Transit Task Force Report

The Parking and Transit Task Force Report presented the following report to President Crow and university administration.

The Purpose

  • Examine parking and transportation needs of the ASU Tempe campus and develop a sustainable plan for our future parking and transit needs.
  • Review and analyze existing documents and reports:
    • The ASU Master Plan adopted by the Arizona Board of Regents, Spring 2005;
    • The 2005 Walker Transportation Survey;
    • 2001 ASU Bicycle Study;
    • Municipal Light Rail plans;
    • City of Tempe transportation and development plans;
    • Information and research from peer institutions,
    • ASU parking principles and polices; and
    • Other information that may be identified by the task force or ASU University Business Services.

Methodology

  • Review of ASU Master Plan
  • Review of Transportation Plans:
    • City of Tempe
    • Valley Metro
    • Light Rail
  • Review of Various Studies:
    • Regional
    • Pac-10 and ABOR peer benchmarks
  • Results from Task Force survey’s:
    • Commuter Students
    • Resident Students
    • Faculty and Staff
  • Focus Groups
    • Academic Senate
    • Campus Events
    • Classified Staff Council
    • Disability Resources
    • Parking Advisory Committee
    • Residential Housing/Student Affairs
    • Service Professionals
    • Undergraduate Student Government and Graduate & Professional Student Association
    • Input from transportation consultant Mark Soronson (S.R. Beard & Associates) and City of Tempe officials

Task Force Key Observations and Findings

Parking

  • The current parking inventory of 19,000+ spaces is sufficient for current demand. However, many who park in Lot 59 consider it unsatisfactory.
  • ASU Master Plan calls for the reduction of 5,000 parking spaces over the next five years.
  • There are two events that could present a major challenge:
    • Sale of Rio Salado property ( Lot 59N) would result in the loss of 2,900 parking spaces used daily and for events.
    • Sale of Holiday Inn (by Apache and Rural) could include the loss of Lot 72, which is the only parking available for University Services Building employees.
  • ASU community input was not solicited in Master Planning process, nor was there sufficient communication between the planners, Light Rail or City of Tempe Transit planners. The result being that the planning is suboptimal.
  • On an average weekday, 30,000-35,000 vehicles visit the Tempe campus.
  • On an average weekday, 19,000 bicycles access the Tempe campus.
  • ASU hosts an average of 1,600 events requiring PTS support each year. Parking for these events is inadequate.
  • Visitor parking on the Tempe campus is inadequate.
  • Each university college needs to have some parking spaces allotted to them for their control.
  • There is a growing need for employees and students to have parking privileges on multiple campuses. This could reduce the available inventory.
  • Parking that is inconvenient can have a negative impact on faculty recruitment and employee productivity.
  • Currently, each campus manages its own parking program.

Data from the task force survey

  • 64% of employees and 49% of students want parking that is convenient over parking that is plentiful or inexpensive, but not convenient.
  • 82% of employees and 76% of students use a car as the primary means of transportation to the Tempe campus.
  • 56% of employees and 53% of students do not have a secondary means of transportation.
  • 53% of residential students us a car for work related travel.
  • 41% of residential students work off campus.

Transportation

  • The implementation of the ASU free bus pass program (U-Pass) was successful. ASU students and employees tallied 120,000 Valley Metro boarding’s during the month of October 2005.
  • Valley Metro buses are inconvenient for bike riders (only 2 bike holders per bus).
  • The City of Tempe is taking steps to calm traffic within the city.
    • Apache Boulevard going from 6 lanes to 4 lanes.
    • Proposal passed to not widen any more streets.
  • Traffic in Tempe will increase with the rise in downtown housing and additional student housing along University Drive.
  • FLASH services are not adequate to meet current demand.
  • Demand for intercampus transportation is expected to increase as the university re-locates programs to other campuses.
  • Opening of Light Rail in December 2008 should provide attractive transportation options.
  • There are two park and ride lots planned to support the Light Rail in Tempe. Planning calls for 3,200 parking spaces open to the public, but planning did not incorporate ASU demand.
  • Light Rail will cross Rural Road and University Drive every six minutes. City of Tempe has not projected impact on traffic flow.

General and Financial

  • Safety and security concerns must be addressed at ASU including parking lots and structures.
  • ASU current parking rates are significantly under market.
  • Current parking rates cannot support PTS programs and operations as the parking inventory is reduced and demand for services are increased.
  • There is an opportunity for demand driving parking rates.
  • Parking and Transit Services does not benefit financially from event parking.
  • Long term solutions must include enhanced transit programs and remote parking sites.
  • Campus signage is inadequate, including parking lot signage.

Critical Issues

The Parking and Transit Task Force found consistent themes throughout the data collection process that were narrowed down to the following critical issues:

Customer Service: Findings of the task force identified customer service as the foundation to providing outstanding parking and transportation services and programs. PTS supports 1,600 events per year. ASU must be sensitive to serving all internal and external communities.

Options/Alternatives: There is a need to shape policies, programs, and projects in a way that enables members of the ASU community to have options in parking and transportation that are responsive to individual financial and scheduling needs.

Safety and Security: There is a recognition that ASU must address needed enhancements to campus-wide safety, including walk/bike ways, parking lots and structures, shuttle service, escort service, etc.

Planning/Policy: Parking and transportation are institutional issues that must be addressed as part of the university’s planning process as well as the City of Tempe and surrounding communities.

Fiscal Responsibility: ASU must maintain the financial integrity of this operational unit. ASU must agree on support that will come from PTS for other campus needs and whether areas of this department should be supported from other funding sources.

Urbanization: With ASU’s growth over the past several years and with the increasing demographic concentration of the Tempe population, the university has an obligation to collaborate with key partners such as the City of Tempe, Valley Metro, etc. ASU’s parking and transportation programs must reflect the changing community.
