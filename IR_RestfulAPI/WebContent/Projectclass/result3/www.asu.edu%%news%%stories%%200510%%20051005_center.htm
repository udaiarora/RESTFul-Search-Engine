October 5 , 2005

Center targets sustainable engineering education

A new Center for Sustainable Engineering is being launched to help understand and address some of the important environmental and social implications of engineering in the modern world. The center, funded by the National Science Foundation and the Environmental Protection Agency at more than $2 million, brings together researchers from Arizona State University, Carnegie Mellon University and the University of Texas-Austin.

The overall goal of the center is to help future engineers design better products and systems in a world where resources may be increasingly limited and where globalization raises new environmental and social considerations.

“This center will be a focal point for integrating social and environmental dimensions into traditional engineering education,” said Brad Allenby, a professor of civil and environmental engineering and the Lincoln professor of engineering ethics at Arizona State University. “In the past, engineers have been able to pretty much focus on technical issues. But today we live in a very different world, one that requires us to be smarter about the social and environmental implications of our designs and our technologies. We need to continue to provide a high quality of life with less impact on our natural resources.”

“We want this new center to help the nation’s 1,500 engineering programs realize that being ‘green’ may not be easy, but that it is vitally important,” said Cliff Davidson, a professor of civil and environmental engineering at Carnegie Mellon University, Pittsburgh. Davidson is principal investigator on the project.

Traditional energy sources, like fossil fuels, are a prime example of using a resource without too much consideration to its consequences. It is fairly well known that there are finite amounts of traditional energy sources in the world, and that using them has a number of environmental and social impacts. These may have been acceptable in the past, but at current levels of use a number of concerns, such a global climate change, arise.

Additionally, short-term variations in supply due to limited technological options leave many nations at the whim of fluctuating (and mostly rising) prices of oil, impacting any products that are made from it. When a calamity strikes, like hurricane Katrina which shut down several refineries, then the U.S. as a whole sees spikes in energy costs that can temporarily damage, or even cripple, its economy.

“Energy systems are a classic example where we need to rethink many technologies -- together with their environmental and social dimensions -- in a systems perspective,”noted Allenby, who is working with John Crittenden, an ASU professor of civil and environmental engineering on the center. “The problem is not so much that we’ll run out of fossil fuels, but that the impact of producing and using them will become unacceptable. An important contribution of sustainable engineering to this situation is to provide technological options, so that society has choices on how best to meet its energy needs.”

Some of the programs of the Center for Sustainable Engineering will include holding workshops to improve engineering faculty teaching, creating a website with peer-reviewed education materials about sustainable engineering, and conducting a nationwide survey of sustainable engineering programs and courses to benchmark the status of education in this emerging discipline.

“The technologies that engineers develop today will shape the societies and environmental quality of the future,” Allenby said. “Engineers have always set high ethical standards for themselves, and accepting the new challenge of sustainable engineering by establishing this center is not only critical for our future as a society, but it is being done in the best engineering tradition.”
