September 13, 2006

Chinese educators get taste of ASU campus life

Sichuan University and ASU have a lot in common. Both are large in size, situated in major metropolitan areas, and offer a wide range of courses in many disciplines to graduate and undergraduate students.

Also, the city of Chengdu, the ?panda capital of the world,? where Sichuan University is located, and Phoenix, home to ASU's newest campus, have been sister cities since 1986. Thus, it seemed logical that Sichuan and ASU establish an official relationship.

As the first exchange, Sichuan University sent 20 professors from its College of Foreign Languages to study American culture and English teaching methodology in a program conducted by ASU's American English and Culture Program (AECP). AECP is a part of ASU's University College School of Extended Education.

Sichuan University, which offers bachelor's, master's and doctoral degrees, has a faculty of about 250, says Chris Bragg, assistant director of Special Programs for AECP.

?Sichuan University has mentioned the possibility of eventually sending all 250 to ASU for professional development programs that include America culture studies and teacher training methodology,? Bragg says.

The Chinese faculty members were at ASU for five weeks this summer, studying such topics as American idioms, American culture, cooperative learning, using films and software to teach a foreign language, online technology and other subjects.

According to Yanhong Wang, the Chinese group's leader, Sichuan University will be celebrating its 110th anniversary this year, and it has about 42,000 students.

All students have to take English courses for two years, and every class has more than 50 students in it.

Wang says studying English is crucial for Chinese students because ?there are more job opportunities if you learn English.?

The professors from China had ample opportunity during their stay in Tempe to learn about American culture. Their schedule included time for ?field-based learning? ? trips to the Heard Museum, Desert Botanical Garden, Rawhide, and the Phoenix Zoo; horseback riding; a movie in downtown Tempe; dinner at Rustler's Rooste; an Arizona Diamondbacks baseball game; and a ?home-stay? weekend with American families.

One of the most popular outings was to see ?Beauty and the Beast? at the Broadway Palm Dinner Theatre.

Before seeing this play, the group members participated in a dramatic reading and retelling of the story, which helped bring the play to life when they saw it.

AECP has conducted a number of teacher training programs for groups from Korea and Japan, but this was the first program they have offered for a Chinese group.

?Working with the Sichuan University English teachers was a delightful experience,? says Mark Rentz, director of AECP. ?They were eager to learn new and innovative teaching techniques and strategies for the classroom.

?The program was a big success and a lot of fun. I can't wait to welcome another group of Sichuan University English teachers to ASU!?
