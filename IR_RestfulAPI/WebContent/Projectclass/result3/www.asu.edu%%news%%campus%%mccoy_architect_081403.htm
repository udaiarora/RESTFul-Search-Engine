August 14, 2003

McCoy accepts role as university architect

Ron McCoy, professor and director of the School of Architecture, has added yet another title to his door: University Architect.

He won't be designing all the new campus buildings, but will try to manifest in physical form the "New American University," as envisioned by President Michael Crow.

"The president has big plans for the university in all its settings," McCoy says. "He has written a lot about what he wants ASU to be, and implicit in that is a different kind of physical setting for the university. We're trying to figure out what that means."

Universities usually grow much like cities, through incremental decisions, McCoy says.
"I will try to bring some coherence to those decisions so they will reinforce and express the ambitions of the university," he said. "The mental picture of ASU should be different in the future. The University Architect will not design buildings, but will try to communicate and give a strategic coherence to the university."

Though builders usually follow architectural drawings, there are no existing plans for the physical manifestation of the New American University, McCoy says.

"We don't have examples of that next-generation of architecture, so this university could become unique," he says.

In a "large way," McCoy says, he will be responsible for seeing that the university "is designed for the community of people who populate it."

Often, when expansion is planned the first questions are the "most innocent," such as what to build, McCoy said. In the past, the answer would have been classrooms, academic buildings or perhaps laboratories, McCoy said. But in the New American University, the plan might call for a building that mixes retail, housing and academic uses, for example.

McCoy has served as interim dean of the College of Architecture and Environmental Design for the past year, but in his role as professor he will once again be more involved with students, who, most surely, will be part of the university design process.

"They will participate in all sorts of ways," McCoy says, "from serving on committees to offering input on housing."

Without a visionary leader such as President Crow, ASU would not be able to imagine a new type of infrastructure for the university of tomorrow, McCoy says.

"It happens when, in the Office of President, there is someone who understands the importance of the quality of physical place, in all its aspects. And, supporting that leader, there must be an entire organization of people trying to make that happen. But you must have a leader who understands."
