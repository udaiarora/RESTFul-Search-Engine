In 1996, Dr. Zah was recruited by ASU to help address the education concerns of the growing Native American student population and their respective communities.

A major area of concern was the low retention rates of Native American college students, which also had a rippling effect for Native American high school and junior high students. With this challenge at hand Dr. Zah set out to find the solutions (see FACTS).

Dr. Zah continues to work with state, federal and tribal governments, along with national and local organizations to continue to improve the education of all Native people.

MISSION

• To foster open communication between Arizona State University and American Indian Tribes. 

• Ensure that all those who attend ASU receive appropriate student support services.

• Work to recruit, retain, and increase the graduation rates of Native American students.

• Strengthen the University relationship with students, parents, Tribal leadership, and University officials

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

BIOGRAPHIES

Dr. Peterson Zah
Advisor to the President on American Indian Affairs

Dr. Peterson Zah is Diné from the Navajo Nation. He attended Phoenix Indian School and graduated in 1958. He is also an Alumna of Arizona State University graduating with a bachelor’s degree in education and has earned honorary doctoral degrees from Arizona State University, Colorado College, and College of Santa Fe.

Dr. Zah has taught at Window Rock High School and worked for the Navajo Nation as a construction project estimator. From 1967-1982, he was co-founder and later became the Executive Director of DNA People’s Legal Service.

From 1982 to 1987, Zah served as Chairman of the Navajo Tribal Council. In 1990, under a new tribal government, Dr. Zah was elected again—this time as President of the Navajo Nation and served a four-year term. This makes Dr. Peterson Zah the last Tribal Chairman and the first elected President of the Navajo Nation leading the movement to restructure and modernize the government system from a council to a nation. Throughout his career he has made education his first priority.

In 1998 he became Director of the Western Regional Office of the Save the Children Federation and established an office in Albuquerque, New Mexico.

Since 1995, Dr. Zah has been serving Arizona State University as the Special Advisor to the President on American Indian Affairs. He meets with students, presents guest lectures, and represents ASU in external relations with non-profits organizations, local communities, federal, state, and tribal governments.

Dr. Zah is considered one of the 100 most important Native Americans in the last century, a key leader in Indian Country and education.


Jaynie Parrish
Coordinator of Special Projects and Special Assistant

Jaynie Parrish is Diné, originally from the Window Rock and Kayenta communities on the Navajo Nation. She is a a graduate of Arizona State University with a Bachelors of Interdisciplinary Studies Degree and received a Certificate in American Indian Justice Studies.

Ms. Parrish has worked as an educator and advocate on behalf of Indigenous communities and various non-profit organizations throughout the United States. She has served as Manager of Strategic Relations, Program Coordinator, and Youth Advocate, all in the service to strengthen local communities by way of education, shaping pending legislation, promoting community service, advancing the arts, and developing community infrastructure.

With this commitment she has returned to her ASU roots and looks forward to developing ASU’s relationship with Tribal communities.

Ms. Parrish works closely with Dr. Zah in areas of recruitment & retention, student inquiries, development, coordination of events, and other initiatives developed by Arizona State University.
