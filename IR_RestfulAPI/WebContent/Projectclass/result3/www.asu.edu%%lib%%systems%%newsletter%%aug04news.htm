LIST's Newsletter
August, 2004

After our brief hiatus in July, “We’re LISTening” has lots to report. Read on to find out about improvements to the public workstations and printing system, progress on the library café, new databases, and another option for spam relief.


Public Printing System Update

As some of you may have noticed, Copy Services has installed new printers at both Hayden and Noble in order to speed up our public printing process. During this installation, LIST noted that there was some confusion and frustration as to where print jobs can be released. In order to relieve this, all the print queues have been tied together. This means that no matter which public workstation a patron prints from, they have the ability to release the job at the Pharos station of their choice. The result is less confusion, and more places to print from if a certain Pharos station is congested. LIST will be updating the printing information sheets to include this information.

Workstation Improvements

· The Google toolbar has been added to all the public workstations by request.
· The games filter is up and running for the upper concourse this week, and will be launched globally next week. The filter employs a proxy server to screen out URLs that have no legitimate research use, such as games, online chat, etc. If you see a patron accessing content that should be filtered, please get the workstation number and notify Jeremy (5-1902) right away so that he can access the machine remotely to see what site they are visiting and add it to the filter list.

Charlie’s Café Biblioteca Coming Soon; Will Have Wireless

Construction is expected to be completed on the library coffee shop/café by September 23rd, and Charlie hopes to be open for business by the following week. Hayden’s patrons and staff can look forward to enjoying wireless internet access along with Charlie’s selection of coffees, pastries, and light lunches.

Q. "I’ve tried Spam Inspector; I’ve tried the Outlook rules wizard. Is there anything else that can help me deal with spam?"

A. While no method of spam detection is perfect, there is something else you can try: spam filtering using IT's EMMA service. IT provides an FAQ page - http://www.asu.edu/mailbox/faq/sf.htm - that explains how the filtering service works. To access this service, log on using your ASURITE at http://my.asu.edu, then select EMMA, then Spam Filtering.

It's important to note that this filtering service blacklists everything by default – in other words, until you add addresses to your “white list,” ALL of your email will go into the spam quarantine on EMMA. Fortunately, you can easily set it to receive email from anyone at ASU by adding “@asu.edu” to your white list. You can also manually add non-ASU email addresses to your white list, but it may be easier to simply let the non-ASU emails gather in the quarantine, then check the box next to the ones you want and click “deliver selected” to send them to your Outlook mailbox. Whenever you choose to deliver an email from your spam quarantine, future emails from that sender will automatically be delivered to your Outlook mailbox rather than to the spam folder.

You need to check the spam quarantine often – at least daily – at first, to find and deliver legitimate emails that get directed there. But after a few weeks you’ll find that legitimate messages rarely show up in the quarantine, and you can check the folder less and less often. LIST staff who’ve tried the EMMA spam filter find that when used as directed, it’s somewhat more effective and less time-consuming in the long run than the other options mentioned above.

Your spam filter can be even more effective if you first check the Electronic Post Office (“EPO Update” in the links on the left side of the EMMA screen) to see all your email addresses and aliases. A Google search on each of your addresses/aliases will reveal if any of them is listed online – for example, in the archives of a listserv. If you find that one or more of your aliases does appear online, this is a likely source of spam. Setting your spam filtering options on that address will catch the bulk of your unwanted email and direct it to the spam quarantine, where it’s easy to delete en masse.

For complete details about setting up this service, please request Jeremy Kurtz’s handy illustrated guide, available either from Jeremy or from Heather Knowles via Outlook.
