National Science Foundation Honors ASU Scholars

The National Science Foundation has honored 33 graduate students at Arizona State University for the 2004–05 academic year. Eight of those students are new recipients of the foundation’s prestigious Graduate Research Fellowships.

“These are some of the most highly sought after fellowships,” said Paul Johnson, associate vice president for research at ASU. “Because of the financial support that they provide, they allow a student to play a larger role in setting the direction of their research. They also offer students continued recognition in the future for having been selected as a recipient. I would like to congratulate all of the recipients and those that received honorable mentions in this year’s competition.”

The fellowships offer three years of support for advanced study to approximately 900 outstanding graduate students in the math, physical, biological, engineering, and behavioral and social sciences fields. Recipients receive an annual stipend of $30,000 and education allowance of $10,500 paid to their institution in lieu of tuition and fees.

The 2004–05 National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellows are:

Lubna Munir Ahmad who is working toward a Ph.D. in biomedical engineering under the guidance of Professor Eric Guilbeau. She invented and has applied for a patent for a breath biosensor that can be used to monitor physiological parameters of interest for conditions such as diabetes and obesity. She said her research mentors Professors Guilbeau and Bruce Towe have been tremendous sources of guidance and direction.

Mayra Y. Bámaca, a Ph.D. student in Family and Human Development, who is working with Professor Adriana Umaña–Taylor. She was awarded the fellowship in 2001, but deferred acceptance until this academic year. Her research interests include adolescent development among ethnically diverse populations and the influence of contextual factors, parent–adolescent relationships and their influence on adolescent development, and resiliency among Latino adolescents and their families.

Visar Shahin Berisha, a Ph.D. student in electrical engineering, who is conducting research on enhancing signal quality in cellular applications. More specifically, he studies algorithms that aim to reduce the information rate in digital communication systems and methods for increasing the efficiency of such algorithms. Berisha’s academic advisor is Professor Andreas Spanias.

Jonathan Roy Davis, a Ph.D. student in the School of Life Sciences, who is interested in the ways in which an organism’s physiologic needs influence its behavioral ecology. Specifically, Davis’ research focuses on water storage in the urinary bladder of reptiles and amphibians. He is using a desert–adapted lizard, the Gila monster, as a model to address questions related to the novelty of this presumably beneficial morphologic adaptation. His academic advisor is Professor Dale DeNardo.

Jennifer Nisha Desai, currently a master’s student on track to earn a Ph.D. in electrical engineering, who is in her second year of research under the guidance of Professor Sayfe Kiaei. Desai is researching methods for improving the efficiency and linearity of transmitters and power amplifiers in wireless handsets. All of the designed circuits are created using the newest technology provided by industry. The goal is to find new transmitter architectures that allow for reduced power consumption in radio frequency handheld devices while also reducing size and cost of the chips by making the power management system entirely on–chip.

Brigitte Wieshofer Hogan, a Ph.D. student in biology, who recently returned from a three–month stint in China where she began collecting preliminary data for her dissertation on the ecology of the plateau pika, a small mammal, on the Qinghai–Tibetan plateau in western China. Hogan’s research focuses on how the pika population density affects plant species diversity and whether pikas compete with local yak and Tibetan sheep for grazing forage. She hopes her research will lead to better rangeland management for Tibetan pastoralists and eliminate the extensive poisoning of pikas, which results in ecological damage. Her advisor is Professor Andrew Smith.

Teresa Ann Murray, who is working toward a Ph.D. in bioengineering with an emphasis in neural engineering. She is working with Professor Neal Woodbury in the Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry and Ron Lukas of Barrow Neurological Institute in Phoenix on a project to select oligonucleotides that will tag specific neuron receptors to study brain function.

Michael Teague O’Mara, who is working toward a Ph.D. in anthropology with an emphasis in physical anthropology and primate behavioral ecology. His most recent project has focused on the spatial distribution of positional behavior and activity patterns in Costa Rican spider monkeys and the relationship of these behaviors to the physical structure of the forest environment. This research aims at evaluating disturbance levels in a forest via satellite imagery, and how the changes in physical structure impact conservation of the primate community. Teague’s advisor is Professor Leanne T. Nash.


Two former ASU undergraduates, bioengineering student Merrill Jay Birdno and economics student Christopher Aspen Gorry, also received fellowships but went on to other universities for their graduate studies.

In addition, seventeen ASU students received Honorable Mentions in the competition this year. They are: Jamie Melichar Hodgkins, Emily Kathryn Landis, Thierra Kennec Nalley, and Lydia Virginia Pyne, Anthropology; Aaron W. ASU also has eight continuing NSF Fellows:Jessica Thompson, Anthropology; Robin Roth, Chemical Engineering;Karen Chancellor, Computer Science;Nicholas Schmerr, Geological Sciences;Cinnamon Hayes and James Heffernan, Life Sciences;Noah Goldstein and Vladas Griskevicius, Psychology.
