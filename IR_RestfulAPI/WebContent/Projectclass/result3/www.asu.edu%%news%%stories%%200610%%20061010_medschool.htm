October 10, 2006

State leaders celebrate ASU-UA collaboration in new college of medicine

There’s a new kid on the block out to change the face of medicine in Arizona. The University of Arizona College of Medicine Phoenix program in collaboration with Arizona State University celebrated its new home on the Phoenix Biomedical Campus with a gala opening Oct. 10.

Founding faculty members, including several from ASU who have joint appointments, showed off their new digs to neighbors from the Translational Genomics Research Institute (TGen) and the International Genomics Consortium, as well as hundreds of dignitaries from throughout the state.

UA President Robert Shelton (left) and ASU Michael Crow clasp hands in celebration around former UA President Peter Likins. The trio were among the dignitaries on hand for the celebration at the Phoenix Biomedical Campus.

Clinical partners, university leaders, business leaders, city and state government officials, members of the Arizona Board of Regents, researchers and physicians were joined by Arizona Gov. Janet Napolitano and Phoenix Mayor Phil Gordon to mark this milestone, which is part of a collective plan to improve health care options for Arizona citizens by training more doctors and collaborating on biomedical research.

Located in three renovated historic Phoenix Union High School buildings just east of ASU’s new downtown Phoenix campus and a few blocks from ASU’s College of Nursing & Healthcare Innovation, the College of Medicine’s four-year, personalized medicine curriculum will be taught to the first cohort of 24 first-year medical students next summer. Plans are to eventually increase that number to 150 students.

ASU President Michael Crow cites the commitment that the two universities have to work together to expand medical education and research in Arizona.

“This collaborative endeavor will maximize the strengths of ASU and UA and their strategic clinical and research partners,” Crow says. “Twelve of our ASU faculty members will be serving with joint appointments on the faculty of the College of Medicine. This collaboration will provide ASU with an important opportunity to expand its bioscience, nursing, bioinformatics and related research and educational programs.”

Locating ASU’s new Department of Biomedical Informatics on the Phoenix Biomedical Campus greatly enhances an already distinctive arrangement. Biomedical informatics is a critical element for the new College of Medicine curriculum, one that will emphasize personalized medicine. Biomedical informatics melds large quantities of information in medical research, clinical practice and patient care with the power of computing, communications and informatics. It is becoming the standard for evidence-based medicine.

ASU’s biomedical informatics strength was featured on one of the tour stops during the opening celebration. Also on display were research from the Biodesign Institute at ASU, the College of Nursing & Healthcare Innovation, and two units in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences: the School of Life Sciences and the Center for Metabolic Biology.

Faculty from these ASU colleges and research facilities are among the founding faculty at the College of Medicine’s Phoenix program. They will teach in a variety of areas, including embryology, developmental biology, molecular bases of medicine and physiology, as well as biomedical informatics.

Another important role they have been playing is in the development of the curriculum for the Phoenix program.

Additional ASU faculty members are expected to be added over the next several months from units such as psychology, communication, history, philosophy and life sciences.

At the Oct. 10 event, Crow, Regent Gary Stuart and former UA President Peter Likins were recognized for their collaboration in this watershed achievement. Crow and other dignitaries also were recognized for outstanding public service, receiving founders’ awards.

David A. Young, ASU vice president and dean of the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, was one of two recipients of a MVP (most valuable player) award for his leadership in helping shape the curriculum. The academic work group members also were singled out for recognition at the ceremonies. Receiving this award from ASU were Young, Sethuraman Panchanathan, director of the School of Computing and Informatics, and Kathleen Matt, ASU assistant vice president for research and director of the Office of Clinical Partnership.
