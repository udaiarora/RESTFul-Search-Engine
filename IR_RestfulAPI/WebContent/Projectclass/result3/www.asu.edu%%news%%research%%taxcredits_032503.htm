Reports challenge value of education tax credits and wisdom of expanding program to include corporations

An Arizona program that gives taxpayers a dollar-for-dollar income tax credit for donations to finance tuition grants for private schools is "fundamentally flawed," and seems certain to hurt the majority of poor children attending public schools, a new analysis finds. A companion report concludes that expanding the tax credits to corporations would compound the injustice. The reports, "Education Tax Credits: No Net Benefit to Arizona's Impoverished Students" and "Peer Review of Education Scholarships: Expanding Opportunities for Students, Saving Taxpayers Money" prepared for the Education Policy Studies Laboratory at Arizona State University were authored by Kevin Welner, a lawyer and professor of education policy at the University of Colorado - Boulder.

In " Education Tax Credits: No Net Benefit to Arizona's Impoverished Students" Welner concludes, "As a means of providing equity and improving achievement for impoverished students currently enrolled in the state's public education systems, Arizona's educational tax credit laws are fundamentally flawed."

Welner examined A.R.S. § 43-1089, which grants a tax credit of up to $625 for families who donate funds for private school grants, and A.R.S. § 43-1089.01, which provides a similar tax credit of up to $250 for families who donate money to cover the costs of certain public school extra-curricular activities.

The 1997 law was enacted with the stated goal of helping poor families who wished to transfer their children to private from public schools. Welner observes, however, that the Arizona program largely benefits well-off families whose children already are enrolled in private schools.

A provision barring taxpayers from taking credits for donations earmarked for their own dependents is undermined by a loophole that allows "quid pro quo" donations in which a pair of taxpayers might make donations earmarked for each other's dependents, Welner notes.

Welner also finds:

- The tax credits, contrary to supporters' claims, provide little incentive for families to switch from public to private schools. As such, they primarily benefit wealthier rather than poorer families, they don't save the state much money on the cost of operating public schools, and they deprive the state of revenue.

- If the credits do prompt construction of private schools in poor communities where schools are overcrowded, those schools are likely to shut out the poorest and most difficult students. At the same time, those schools will lack resources to match the quality of wealthy suburban private schools.

- Tax credits, by promoting the departure of high-scoring students from public schools for private ones, will undermine public schools' attempts to comply with the federal No Child Left Behind act, which requires schools to consistently demonstrate increases in test scores.

- Tax credits deepen divisions by social class, income, and ethnicity.

The report offers ways in which the law might be made somewhat more fair. It warns, however, that any such changes cannot overcome the law's inherent flaws. Welner argues that, "While possible changes in the tax credit programs may make them marginally more equitable, reforms cannot rescue them from their inherent inability to accomplish the stated goal of their architects: improving the quality of education for impoverished students."

Expanding the program to include corporations would compound the law's flaws. In an analysis of the Goldwater Institute proposal to open up education tax credits to corporations, Welner found little empirical support for the proposition that this is a sensible way to channel the financial contributions of the corporate community. Welner concludes, "While there is real value to policies that encourage corporate generosity, little is gained from a policy that merely gives corporations the power to redirect tax money that is otherwise targeted for public purposes."

The Education Policy Research Unit (EPRU) conducts original research, provides independent analyses of research and policy documents, and facilitates educational innovation. EPRU facilitates the work of leading academic experts in a variety of disciplines to help inform the public debate about education policy issues.

Visit the EPRU website at http://educationanalysis.org/

The Education Policy Studies Laboratory (EPSL) at Arizona State University offers high quality analyses of national education policy issues and provides an analytical resource for educators, journalists, and citizens. It includes the Commercialism in Education Research Unit (CERU), the Education Policy Analysis Archives (EPAA), the Education Policy Reports Project (EPRP), the Education Policy Research Unit (EPRU), and the Language Policy Research Unit (LPRU).

The EPSL is directed by ASU Professor Alex Molnar.
