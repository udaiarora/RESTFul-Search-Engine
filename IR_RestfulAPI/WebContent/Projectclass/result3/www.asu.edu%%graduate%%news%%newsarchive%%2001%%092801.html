ASU FACULTY EMERITI ASSOCIATION FUNDS AWARDS FOR PREPARING FUTURE FACULTY STUDENTS

The Faculty Emeriti Association (FEA) at Arizona State University has given its first FEA Awards, which benefit graduate students in the Preparing Future Faculty (PFF) program. The two award recipients, Jere Boudell and Sean Nevin, were honored at the September Emeriti luncheon, and will be honored again Oct. 4 during the PFF fall reception at the University Club, on the Main Campus in Tempe.

The FEA is an organization of retired ASU faculty members and academic professionally-ranked librarians. Members live throughout the U.S. and in several nations. An Emeriti Fellowship Fund was begun in 1994 in the ASU Foundation. The FEA is now able to fund graduate fellowships from the endowment's interest. Dr. Marvin Fisher, president of the association, said, "These fellowships are the first concerted effort by the faculty emeriti of this university to fund a program of direct benefit to both ASU and graduate students in doctoral or similar terminal degree programs. PFF fills a need that many in the teaching profession have long recognized for many years, and gives ASU graduate students a real advantage when applying for positions in the professiorate. The FEA is proud to support a program that assists graduate students in preparing for a rewarding career in the academy."

PFF is a national program specifically designed to prepare doctoral students for careers in the professoriate. The program offers new approaches to faculty development by integrating teaching, research, scholarship, creative activities and service through situational and experiential training in a variety of higher education settings. The program encourages new thinking and planning in faculty preparation, creates new support programs and identifies strategies to improve the quality of teaching and learning.

This year, 92 doctoral or Master's in Fine Arts students are participating in PFF. During the annual PFF reception Oct. 4, deans from the Graduate College will introduce new PFF students, recognize their mentors and introduce the program's plans for the upcoming year.

Boudell conducts research in riparian plant ecology with her mentor, Associate Professor Juliet Stromberg, and Professor Emeritus Duncan Patten, in the Department of Plant Biology. Her research focuses on the role that seeds located on or in the soil play in maintaining and restoring biodiversity in the rare riparian ecosystems of the Southwest.

Nevin is working on a master's in fine arts in the English Department and teaches composition and creative writing at ASU. He is also the poetry editor for Hayden's Ferry Review. Nevin's poetry has appeared in numerous publications. He has received several awards, including the 1999 Dorothy M. Hood Prize for Poetry, and was a finalist in Arizona's 18th annual statewide poetry contest this year. Nevin's mentors are Regents' Professors of English Alberto Rios and Norman Dubie.
