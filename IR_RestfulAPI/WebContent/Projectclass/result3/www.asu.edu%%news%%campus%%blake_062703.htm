June 27, 2003

ASU mourns 'Family Man,' 'Mr. Maroon & Gold'

June 19 should have been a day of glorious celebration.

House Bill 2529, the bill securing $440 million in state funds for research infrastructure at the three universities, finally made it through one final vote and was on its way to Gov. Janet Napolitano's desk for signature.

Unfortunately, June 19 was a day of tremendous sadness as news circulated that Blake Anderson, ASU lobbyist and one of the bill's greatest champions, had died hours before HB2529 made it past its last hurdle. Anderson died of complications from lupus, following a brief hospitalization.

Anderson worked tirelessly alongside ASU President Michael Crow to convince members of the legislature that the bill was not only good for ASU, but also good for the future of the entire state of Arizona.

In an unprecedented tribute to a lobbyist, the flag was flown at half-mast at the state capitol the day Anderson died.

Crow, in an emotional note to colleagues, said, "Blake was a man who served his family and his university with all of his heart and all of his energy.

He contributed to our success and development with his careful thinking, great wisdom and friendly spirit. He was a man of honor."

At Anderson's memorial service, held June 21, there was standing room only at St. Benedict's Catholic Church, which holds 500 people. Dignitaries included former Gov. Jane Hull; Rep. Gary Pierce, R-Mesa; ASU President Crow and former President Lattie Coor; Rep. Bob Robson, R-Chandler, who introduced HB2529 in the Legislature; Allan Price, who was vice president for Institutional Advancement and Anderson's boss at ASU before leaving in 2001 to take a position at the University of Oregon; Jaime Molera, former state schools chief; and numerous others.

During the service, Anderson's three children, daughters Lindsey, 13, Emily, 10, and Julie, 7, read a poem as a tribute to their dad, who, above all, was considered a family man by all who knew him. He enjoyed sharing his love of ASU with his girls, who particularly enjoyed attending Sun Devil baseball games.

Others also paid tribute to Anderson at the service, including his college roommate Cliff Ochser, Crow, Robson and Price.

In his tribute, Price characterized Anderson as a mensch, which, in the Hebrew language of Yiddish, is the highest honor you can pay someone. It stands for someone who is dignified, righteous and honest.

"All my life, I had never come across anyone who could truly be characterized as a mensch, until I met Blake," Price said. "I did everything possible to put Blake in difficult situations, with difficult people, to try and prove what I had always thought - that no one could possibly live up to being a mensch - but he proved me wrong. Blake, you're a mensch."

Anderson was employed at ASU since 1994. He received his B.S. in Political Science from ASU in 1986. In addition to his three daughters, Anderson also leaves his wife Debbie, who he married in 1987, his parents, Dr. Robert and Mae Anderson, and two brothers, Craig and Eric.

An account has been established at Wells Fargo Bank to ensure the future education of Anderson's children. Contributions can be dropped off or mailed to any Wells Fargo Bank in care of Deborah Anderson, Account No. 9667549142.
Contributions may also be sent to the Lupus Foundation for Research.
