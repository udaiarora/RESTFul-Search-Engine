July 18, 2005

NIH grant spurs trial study of aid for stroke victims

Kinetic Muscles Inc. (KMI) has announced that it has received a $1.2 million Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) Phase II grant from the National Institutes of Health (NIH). The grant will fund a three-year, 70-patient clinical trial to study the use of the KMI Hand Mentor in the homes of stroke patients.

This proprietary medical device is based on active repetitive motion principles that help stroke survivors regain hand function and independence. KMI devices facilitate stroke therapies based on new ideas that have shown that the adult brain has the ability to rewire and repair itself (called neuroplasticity).

KMI credits ASU Technopolis and a grant from the Arizona Federal and State Technology (AZFAST) Partnership Program – a program of the Arizona Department of Commerce – for providing the assistance needed to prepare the award-winning NIH grant proposal for SBIR funds.

SBIR grants support business and product strategies with funding from the federal government as an alternative to or in coordination with other forms of business financing.

Stroke is the leading cause of adult disability in the United States. There are about 590,000 stroke survivors each year, and 4.8 million people with residual disability as a result of their stroke. KMI devices can improve the quality of life for both groups of patients.

“An essential ingredient to improving the function of stroke survivors is lots of hard work by the patient,” says Edward Koeneman, co-founder of KMI. “This NIH award will help us quantify the positive results our customers are seeing.”

KMI is seeking candidates for its new clinical trial, and interested participants residing in greater Phoenix can contact KMI at (480) 557-0448 or visit the Web site (www.kineticmuscles.com) for more information.

KMI, located in Tempe, was named the “Start-Up Company of the Year” at the Governor’s Celebration of Innovation, and “Medical Device Company of the Year” by the Arizona Bioindustry Association. Over the past two years, KMI technology has been the basis for grants and a contract from NIH totaling more than $3.5 million.

ASU Technopolis has assisted KMI through its SBIR/STTR suite of entrepreneurial programs. ASU Technopolis is the high-impact initiative of ASU President Michael Crow designed to move research in technology and the life sciences into the private sector and international economic arena.

Through a combination of targeted entrepreneurial education, coaching, and networking, ASU Technopolis is producing results that contribute to business expansion, job growth and greater prosperity for the state. For more information, visit the Web site (www.asutechnopolis.org).

Leland, with the Office of Economic Affairs, can be reached at (480) 965-4525 or (karen.leland@asu.edu).
