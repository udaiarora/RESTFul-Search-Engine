March 8, 2006

'Valor' recounts stories of WWII Latino soldiers

Arizona State University and the University of Texas at Austin have partnered to create "Voices of Valor," a theatrical production that holds its world premiere on March 11 at 7 p.m. in the Grady Gammage Auditorium. The play recounts how American Latinos lived, fought and died during the global struggle for democracy, despite a legacy of discrimination in the U.S.

Inspired by the "U.S. Latinos and Latinas World War II Oral History Project" created by Maggie Rivas-Rodriguez at UT Austin, "Voices of Valor" recalls in vivid detail how the war served as a catalyst for the Hispanic struggle for civil rights and social recognition in the United States.

The "U.S. Latino & Latina World War II Oral History Project" gathered more than 500 interviews from American Latinos who served in WW II.

The stories of these men and women have gone virtually untold in plays, film or scholarly writing. UT's Oral History project sought to capture the stories of these men and women and archive them to pay tribute to their sacrifices. An estimated 500,000 American Latinos served in World War II.

ASU graduate and Arizona playwright and journalist James Garcia wanted to make these remarkable stories more accessible to the public, so he created the play, "Voices of Valor," which follows the lives of six fictional, composite characters - four men and two women - whose memories of life before, during and after the war are vividly played out on stage. Voices of Valor is the story of how American Latinos moved from the ranks of an invisible minority to a place where they finally saw themselves as full-fledged Americans.

For American Latinos, World War II marked not only the defeat of fascism, but nation's validation of their "American "citizenship" - though the overwhelming majority of Latino soldiers were U.S. born. In the years following the war, these men and women made astounding civil rights advancements for all Latinos in: school desegregation, voting rights, and civil rights. Powerful organizations grew out of this era, including the G.I. Forum and the Mexican American Legal Defense and Educational Fund.

As one of the characters in the play recalls in telling the story of his first day at boot camp: "I'd been called a lot of things in my life, a wetback, a spic...but that was the first time in my life I'd ever been called an American."

Since 1999, Rivas-Rodriguez has interviewed more than 500 Latino/a WW II veterans throughout the country. Rivas-Rodriguez's father was stationed in the Aleutian Islands during the war, and her mother worked at Kelly Field in San Antonio.

Rivas-Rodriguez and Garcia were colleagues as journalists covering the news in Texas and Mexico for several years. Rivas-Rodriguez, a reporter for the Dallas Morning News, and Garcia, working for the Austin American Statesman, often crossed professional paths. In 2004, the two began discussing the possibility of a play.  In 1994, Garcia pitched the idea to ASU Public Events, which then took the project to UT's Performing Arts Center.

James Garcia is currently a lecturer at ASU where he teaches journalism, screenwriting and ethnic studies.  He is also a Latino affairs correspondent for the National Public Radio affiliate, KJZZ 91.5 FM, in Phoenix. Rivas-Rodriguez teaches journalism at the University of Texas at Austin and taught a class dedicated to the WWII Project for six semesters.  Many of the interviews were conducted as part of that class.

A photographic exhibit featuring photographs of Arizona men and women interviewed for the WWII project will be featured in the lobby of Grady Gammage Auditorium on the night of the performance.

Beginning at 5:30 p.m. on the night of the Phoenix performance there will be free food, drinks, live music and other pre-show activities in and around ASU Gammage to celebrate the kick off of "Arte Es Amor," an annual celebration of Latino arts and culture presented throughout Tempe and ASU. "A Voices Of Valor Symposium, The Latino Experience, WWII," will be held Saturday, March 11, 2006 - 10 a.m. - 1 p.m. It's free and open to the public and will be held in the ASU Gammage Lobby.

The symposium is sponsored by the Department of Chicana and Chicano Studies at ASU Tempe in cooperation with ASU Gammage. The Symposium discusses the themes of James Garcia's new play which recounts the story of how American Latinos lived, fought and died in the global struggle for democracy, despite a legacy of discrimination in the U.S.

The symposium will include Pete Dimas, history professor, Phoenix College; Edward Escobar, professor of Chicana/o Studies, ASU; Benjamin Olguin, history professor, University of Houston; Christine Marin, associate archivist of the Chicano Collection, ASU; Maggie Rivas, associate professor of journalism, founder of the Voices of Valor Project, University of Texas, Austin; Arturo Rosales, history professor, ASU; Carlos Vélez-Ibañez, professor of Chicana/o Studies and Anthropology, ASU- Moderator.

"Voices of Valor" is presented by ASU and UT Austin with support from Bank One, SRP, the National Endowment for the Arts, the Arizona Commission on the Arts, Colores Actors-Writers Workshop and ArtesAmerica.

For more information on " Voices of Valor" visit asugammage.com <http://www.asugammage.com/ > . For more information on the "U.S. Latino & Latina World War II Oral History Project," visit utexas.edu/projects/latinoarchives <http://www.utexas.edu/projects/latinoarchives > .
