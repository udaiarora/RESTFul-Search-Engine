May 27, 2005

Decision Theater puts perspectives in future tense

Researchers at the Decision Theater at ASU are taking input from facts and figures on paper and adding the visualization of two dimensions – space and time – to help decision makers see the results of their actions.

The result: Officials now have a powerful tool to map out possible futures for a variety of scenarios here in the Valley – and beyond.

“This is a facility that is about the decision-making it will assist,” says Jonathan Fink, ASU’s vice president for research. “The focus is on how you take a facility like this and perfect the decision-making process.”

The Decision Theater is a world-class facility for science-based, informed analysis to help local, state, national and international decision makers visualize the future.

The premiere for the Decision Theater took place May 23, where government and civic leaders got an up-close look at what the facility has to offer. Dignitaries invited to the opening included Gov. Janet Napolitano, ASU President Michael Crow and ASU alum Ira A. Fulton, who donated $3 million to the Decision Theater.

“The Decision Theater will help us make better decisions to grow our state, and predict how those decisions affect quality of life,” Napolitano says. “There are so many areas of exploration. In addition to the ones you’ve seen in the demonstration, there are applications for health care, homeland security and many other areas. It really expresses the vision of the New American University and the new Arizona.”

“Decision Theater is a tool to reach across disciplines,” says R.F. “Rick” Shangraw Jr., the executive director of the Decision Theater. “It has applications that can be used in such areas as space exploration and water resource management. Politicians and other decision makers can see the results of their actions in ways they haven’t seen it before.”

Discipline sciences that are advancing their research through collaborations with the Decision Theater include geology, geophysics, environmental fluid mechanics, anthropology, biosciences, urban planning, remote sensing and network design, high-performance computing and cyberinfrastructure.

The projects are presented via a 270-degree, three-dimensional view, which is displayed on seven flat-panel screens.

Part of the initial presentation included an overview of the numerous water wells, canal systems and water treatment plants in the Phoenix metropolitan area, followed by a three-dimensional comparison of underground water tables from 1983 and 2002.

Another “wow factor” moment occurred during a presentation of “heat island” differences in the Valley based on data collected in 1973 and 1998. In that 25-year time span, nighttime heating has gone up between 5 degrees and 10 degrees, Shangraw says.

ASU researchers were then able to extrapolate the data collected and show what the Valley heat island might look like in the year 2040, based on the effects of urban sprawl.

“You will hear people say you cannot predict the future,” Crow says. “They are almost right. With the Decision Theater, you can begin to conceptualize what could happen, what might happen. It’s a new tool, a new kind of laboratory that allows citizens of all backgrounds to help invent the future.”
