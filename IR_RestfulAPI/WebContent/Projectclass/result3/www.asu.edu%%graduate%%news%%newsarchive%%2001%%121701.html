DOCUMENT IMAGING SYSTEM BOOSTS ASU'S ABILITY TO RECRUIT TOP GRADUATE STUDENTS, ACHIEVE RESEARCH MISSION

An upgrade to the electronic application system will enable ASU to streamline its graduate admission process and compete more effectively for top caliber graduate students, who will be trained to make vital contributions to the state's workforce. The new capability enhances the quality of service to students and enables the university to compete more aggressively for the very best graduate students, thereby aiding ASU in achieving its research mission.

Document imaging is a new system that makes the graduate application process more electronic from start to finish. This process allows the Graduate College admissions staff to combine electronic applications with scanned copies of transcripts and other paper items that are mailed in. The result is a completely electronic file that is placed online for academic units to review in order to make admit/deny recommendations. Directors of graduate academic programs can review applications to their respective departments from anywhere, with a browser.

Graduate College systems analyst Roger Lurie said, "Academic units and admissions staff now have instantaneous access to the data received from applicants. The system gives more control to academic departments for screening applicants to their programs."

Admissions personnel scan and upload copies of paper materials onto an Internet screen. By using a search function, processors match scanned information with matching Web applications. Paper documents are stored briefly, then shredded. Each year, more than 18,000 individuals apply for graduate studies at ASU. Approximately 70 percent of them apply through an Internet application system. Students who don't apply online manually fill out a form that is electronically scanned and uploaded into the electronic system. Document imaging is currently in a pilot phase and will become fully operational in 2002.

Larry Penley, dean of the College of Business, said, "Document imaging is a wonderful addition to the way we treat graduate student applicants. It enhances the quality of service to students and enables the staff to provide better services."

Judy Heilala, director of graduate programs in the College of Business, said that document imaging improves file management. "With the Graduate College collecting everything and forwarding it to us electronically, this will prevent things from getting lost," said Heilala, who recently began reviewing the first MBA applications online. "It won't take as long to put applications together, and it speeds up the application process."

After purchasing 10 new PCs, two scanners and two servers to make document imaging possible, the Graduate College Information Technology team worked with Binary Office, Inc., to customize the system and implement it to fit ASU's needs.
