October 8, 2004

Signs of real estate 'bubble' on the horizon

Data from Arizona 's explosive residential real estate market support the growing consensus that the Greater Phoenix area has entered a period of "irrational exuberance," according to an analysis in the current issue of the Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast.

The number of building permits has skyrocketed, reported Elliott Pollack, a contributing editor of the Arizona Blue Chip. "In 2002, Greater Phoenix permits increased 7.6 percent over the previous year. In 2003, permits issued grew by 22.6 percent to 47,720 units.

"There is no sustainability to the numbers we have seen so far this year," Pollack said. "A large part of the increased demand, as far as we can calculate, is because of the investor market. Anecdotal information from homebuilders and brokers suggests that upward of 10 to 20 percent of the new and resale market now is being sold to investors. These investors are buying new homes in anticipation of either 'flipping' them at higher prices upon delivery or renting them for a few years in order to get 'massive' appreciation.

Pollack cited a recent survey indicating 28 percent of home buyers thought that prices were going to increase by 20 percent a year for the next 10 years. "This is an almost impossible outcome. Thus, unrealistically optimistic expectations, almost the definition of a bubble, seem to be in place in many housing markets. "

The potential ramifications and impact of this looming bubble will be taken up in the next issue of the Arizona Blue Chip.

Elsewhere in the Arizona Blue Chip, Editor Tracy Clark surveyed the consensus panel on its long-range economic forecast. Arizona 's expected slowing of population growth will have a widespread economic impact over the next 20 or so years. "The slower growth is the result of fewer people in the general population who will be within the prime moving age span of 18 to 29," Clark said.

The Bank One Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast includes the consensus forecast of economists from 19 groups and institutions. It is published by the Bank One Economic Outlook Cente , an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute in the W. P. Carey School of Business at Arizona State University. For information, call (480) 965-5543.
