Beyond Borders - Programs build cultural links locally, internationally

Whether it's here in the communities of the Valley or the newsrooms in Europe, the College of Public Programs is working on projects involving individuals and organizations as diverse as the programs it offers.

Asian Pacific American Studies
Faculty in the Asian Pacific American Studies Program are doing two oral history projects that focus on two separate Asian communities in Arizona and in Pennsylvania.

"Islands in the Desert - An Oral History of the Pacific Islander Community in Phoenix" is a project led by Professor John Rosa to document a sampling of Pacific Islander experiences in the Phoenix metropolitan area for future generations.

Oral history interviews will serve as the groundwork for the Pacific Islander community oral history project that will eventually be maintained by the Asian Pacific American Studies program and stored in the Arizona Collection of ASU's Hayden Library.

"In general, the interviews from this summer and those to be collected in the fall are a sampling of Pacific Islander experiences in the Phoenix metropolitan area," says Rosa. "Native Hawaiians, Samoans, Tongans and Micronesians are the largest Pacific Islander groups in the state of Arizona, but the project will also highlight some Maori (Native New Zealander) experiences as well."

The oral histories will be on display at the 2004 Aloha Festival in mid-March in Phoenix.

Professor Melinda de Jesus is exploring aspects of racial identity and cultural maintenance as represented in oral histories of Filipino-Americans living in a small steel town in Southeastern Pennsylvania.

Through edited life histories collected from 1999 to 2001, this research traces the themes of identity, belonging, family, community and spirituality.

"Overall, I believe my study will contribute greatly to the understanding of the distinct, local cultures that comprise the Filipino American experience today," says de Jesus.

Hugh Downs School of Human Communication
A little closer to home, Professor Sara Amira de la Garza, in the Hugh Downs School of Human Communication, is making a link with psychology faculty at Escuela Libre de Priscologia, A.C. in Chihuahua, Mexico.

De la Garza received funding from the Institute of International Education, Inc., and the U.S. State Department for a project that will create an online digital journal for the dissemination of scholarly work between faculty from both universities. The online aspects of the ethnographic journal will permit visual and audio technology to be part of the ethnographic record.

Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication
In the Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication, two different projects look at the media's influence locally and abroad.

Professor Carol Schwalbe is examining how news photographs of the U.S. civil rights abuses published overseas during the early years of the Cold War (1947 - 65) affected American policy makers, causing them concern about our image abroad.

"Scholars have written about the negative repercussions of racial discrimination on U.S. foreign relations, but this research has been based mainly on non-visual documents," she says. "My study provides a unique opportunity to study primary sources that show connections between the power of still photography and U.S. foreign policy."

Not only are faculty studying the impact of media on foreign policy, they are showing ASU students firsthand the differences between the American media and media in other nations. "News Bridges: Transatlantic Television News, British Broadcasters at ITN" is the first international program offered by the Cronkite School.

Professor William Silcock received funding from the Association of Schools of Journalism and Mass Communication to coordinate a lecture seminar for ASU students in London and other parts of Europe this summer. Topics included international reporting, global newsroom producing skills, ethical decision making and global newsroom management.

A group of 24 students, along with Silcock and Professor Joe Foote, spent three weeks in England, Ireland and France in intensive visits to media operations in all areas of mass communication.

"Our students experienced and learned a great deal in a short amount of time," says Silcock. "They were really focused on two issues - the differences between American journalism and English journalism and the differences in media culture between the two systems."
