October 10, 2003

ASU's bond rating improves, on right track for financial health

The two major national debt rating agencies, Moody's and Standard & Poor's (S & P), have determined ASU is on the right track for financial health.

On Oct. 7, Moody's issued a report raising the university's revenue bond rating from A1 to Aa3 for new bond issues. The agency also raised the ratings on already issued bond series.

Another major bond rating firm S&P, is expected to issue a similar report improving ASU's Certificates of Participation Lease-Purchase financing rating from A+ to AA-.
Gerald Snyder, associate vice president for finance and university treasurer, says the improved ratings are more than symbolic.

"It's a positive, independent assessment of our credit worthiness and our institutional direction," he says. "An increase in ratings like this is a strong endorsement of the path the university is on. Bottom line, it reduces the rate we pay for bond-financed construction projects at our various campuses. Even more importantly, it makes debt issued by ASU more attractive to a wider variety of investors, making sales of new bond issues more effective."

Snyder says in most cases, it will reduce the interest rates paid on bonds by seven basis points or 7/100 of a percent.

"On the face of it, that may not seem like much on an annual basis, but over the course of the 30-year lifespan of most bond issues, it can mean savings of up to $1.5 million dollars for the university on $100 million in debt," he explains.

Snyder says several factors played into the new rating:

. The very positive campus visitations the rating agencies had last spring;
. The recent sizable fund-raising contributions;
. A noticeable enrollment increase this fall, despite a tuition increase;
. The state appropriations for research infrastructure facilities that begin in fiscal 2008; and
. The good efforts of ASU's bond financial adviser to present the many positive attributes of ASU.

In the report issued to investors around the world, Moody's cited several factors that contributed to the improved credit ratings.

"We believe that ASU is well-positioned to maintain continued growth in enrollment, ultimately translating into increased student revenues to offset the university's rising debt burden," the report said. "ASU is developing into a multi-campus system serving the dynamic Phoenix metropolitan area, including the Main campus in Tempe, ASU East, ASU West, ASU Downtown, and extended (virtual) education. Very healthy projected demographic growth among traditional age students in Arizona should contribute to continued student demand, even as ASU further enhances its research capabilities.

"Total enrollment across the university's four campuses is in excess of 52,000 full-time equivalent students for fall 2003, reflecting a nearly 5 percent increase from last year's enrollment and a nearly 20 percent growth rate over the past five years. Most notably, this year's increase comes on the heels of a $1,000, or 40 percent increase, in tuition. Despite this significant one-year jump, tuition remains highly affordable compared to other states, with the university targeting tuition to remain within the lower one-third of all other states. To maintain affordability, the university has set aside a portion of the tuition increase for financial aid.

"In addition to growing enrollment, the university's research activity has continued to increase, with research expenditures up over 20 percent in FY2003 from FY2002 levels," the report continued. "A significant portion of this increase reflected the second year of receipt of Proposition 301 funds. Proposition 301 is a relatively new sales tax with a portion of proceeds dedicated for higher education. The current issue will continue the university's investment in its research enterprise, as will the legislature's recent approval of $185 million of research infrastructure funding for the university. The university will be issuing debt in its own name for this research investment, although the legislature has agreed to appropriate $14 million annually for 24 years beginning in FY2008 for debt service."
