May 9, 2003

University researchers' discovery helps expedite DNA testing process

A team of ASU researchers has developed a new technique that cuts the time it takes to complete a variety of DNA tests from almost a day to less than an hour. The new low cost process revolutionizes the testing and enables use in outpatient settings such as a doctor's office.

The process, cavitation microstreaming used with microarray biochip testing devices, was developed by a team led by project managers Robin Liu and Ralf Lenigk of the Center for Applied NanoBioScience with the Arizona Biodesign Institute. The findings were featured on the cover of "Analytical Chemistry," April 15. The work for the study was completed while Liu and Lenigk were a part of the Life Sciences group at Motorola, which has since transferred to ASU to form the center.

Current DNA testing can take up to a day, due to the speed in which sample material diffuses over an array of analytical probes. Cavitation microstreaming drastically speeds up the process, using trapped air bubbles and low frequency sound waves to mix the genetic material and deliver it to the probes. The new process completes a task in six seconds that used to take more than eight hours.

"The advantage is that it's very simple," says Liu. "Other micromixers use a lot of equipment or you have to redesign the entire test. With our method, basically you have an array of air bubbles, apply sound to it and the bubbles vibrate. This results in rapid mixing."

The use of microarray biochips is rapidly growing as a way to quickly and inexpensively test large quantities of genomic information for the presence of genetically based diseases and conditions, including AIDS, Alzheimer's and some forms of cancer.

The biochip device, smaller than the size of a standard business card and essentially sandwiched between two glass slides, is covered with a dense checkerboard of 100,000 tiny spots. Each spot, half the width of a human hair, holds millions of DNA probes that grab portions of a genetic sample for analysis.

With current tests, a DNA sample is placed on the chip and it must slowly dissipate over the probes by diffusion. Since the chip chamber is so shallow, there is little room for the liquid to flow, causing it to have about the same fluidic behavior as honey. It can take more than eight hours for the material to diffuse across the probes and 20 hours for the probes to be able to extract the genetic material.
Liu says the beauty of the team's new process is its simplicity.

The team created a new housing for the biochips that includes specific locations where air bubbles will be trapped and added a piezoelectric disk, a material used in pagers. With the addition of an alternating current with about 10 volts of electricity, the micromixer turns on.

"This technology will revolutionize how DNA testing is done by speeding it up," says Lenigk. "It used to take up to 20 hours to complete a test. Now we are able to get some results in 30 minutes."

Motorola, which manufactures the microarray biochips, is planning to implement the process into its products. The various DNA tests cost between $20 and $500 and the new process adds only about 10 cents to the cost.
