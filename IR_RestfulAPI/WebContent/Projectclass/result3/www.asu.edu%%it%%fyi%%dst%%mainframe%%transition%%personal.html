PERSONAL TRANSITION PLAN

I. Why plan your transition

Any move to a new environment can seem overwhelming without a roadmap to guide you. It is important to identify where you will be computing and how you will get there. The goal of creating this plan is to clarify what steps are needed and reduce your anxiety about the move. It also offers a means to chart your progress.

II. What to consider in planning your personal transition plan

 

Inventory your computing needs.

You need to determine what platform(s) to which you will transition your computing work. The choice of platform(s) is dependent on what software you are using and the type of computing you do.

Create a list of the software applications that you use. Appendix IV is a software cross reference which lists where various applications can be found. Choosing the system to transition to

You may find that your software can be found on more than one platform. The choice usually will be between PC/Mac sites, personal workstations (PC, Mac, or UNIX workstations) and IT central servers (MVS and UNIX). The following considerations can be used to choose a platform.

One consideration is easy. If you do not have the funding to either purchase a personal workstation or, if necessary, upgrade your current machine, then either the PC/Mac sites or IT central servers will be the choice.

Do you use large datasets? Do you have large memory requirements? The IT central servers will be the best choice if either (1) your datasets will not fit on the hard drive associated with a PC/Mac in a site or your personal workstation or (ii) your programs will not fit in the available local memory of a PC/Mac in a site or your personal workstation.

Do you have long running jobs or a large number of small jobs? If so, you may need a system with greater performance capabilities than a PC/Mac.

Keep in mind that if you choose to acquire your own system to transition your work to, you will be responsible for the administration of the machine. This can be a formidable task f or the inexperienced user, especially for a UNIX workstation.

III. How to create your own personal transition plan document

Now that you have considered your options, and selected the platform(s) you will transition to, it is time to prepare your own personal plan, formalizing the major steps required for your transition. This section outlines the steps needed for most transitions.

These are some of the essential steps required to move to a new computer system regardless of the level of computer experience. The following is a generic list of steps you may want to consider and include in your personal transition plan. You may find some of the steps are more involved than others. You may have additional requirements that have not been listed.

 

Locating where to find (topic specific) help

A crucial step, yet one which is often ignored, is establishing a list computing assistance resources. Knowing who to contact or where to go for specialized help could same you valuable time and minimize your frustration caused by system problems.

Locating existing information sources

Another important step is identifying what information resources exist for your specific needs. The previous step answers the question "who do you contact if your computer hangs up?" compared to this step which answers questions like "how do I get my application to do XYZ?".

 

Acquiring system access

Acquiring system access may be as simple as requesting a computer account on one or more of the IT central servers, or may be as complex as purchasing a system of your own and setting it up to run your specific applications. For example, if you select to purchase your own computer system it is very likely you will need to have an ethernet connection installed in order for your machine to communicate with other machines.

 

Logging in and out

On some systems logging on/off may be as simple as turning a machine on/off. Others have specific "commands" for logging off the system. To logoff UNIX systems, you must type "exit"; for MVS systems, you must type "logoff". It is important to know the specific requirements for getting on and off of a new system. Terminal and windowing environments can also add to the complexity of making a new computer system usable.

 

Transferring files

Your files exist on one of the legacy systems, you will need to transfer them to the platform that you will continue your work on. The ftp command is the way to transfer these files but these files need to be in a transferable format. See the appendices for system dependent file types and, if necessary, how to convert these files to a transferable format.

 

Editing files

To make full use of a new computer system, you will have to use an editor. You will need to know how to edit an existing file and how to create new files.

 

Manipulating files

In addition to editing files, you will need to become familiar with the file naming conventions and filesystem structure on the new system. You will need to know how to copy, rename and delete files.

 

Executing programs and applications

The software that you will be using on the new platform will probably be invoked differently than what you are used to doing on the legacy system. Documentation and consulting concerning how to execute your applications on the new system are available.

 

Printing and plotting

How will you obtain your hardcopy results in the new environment? You will need to explore your options on how to obtain hardcopy output.

Once you have identified the steps necessary to complete your transition, you can begin creating your personal transition plan.

 

Mapping Progress

On a separate piece of paper, create a list of the steps required to complete the transition. List each step (task) in a concise descriptive manner and estimate the number of weeks it will take to complete the step. When all the steps are identified, number the steps in the sequence in which they will be carried out, and then include a starting date for each step.

You now have a map of your transition. For every week of your transition you can easily find what step or steps you are involved in and for every step you will know at what week of your transition you will be doing this task.

We hope this guide will assist you by making your transition a smooth one.
