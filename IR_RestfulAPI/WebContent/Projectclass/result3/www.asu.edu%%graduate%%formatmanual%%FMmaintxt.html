Main Text Requirements and Guidelines


Font and Size
It is recommended that you use a font such as Times New Roman, Arial, Helvetica, or Century Gothic, with 10, 11, or 12 pt. font size, but ultimately this is your choice. Whichever font and font size you choose, you must use it consistently throughout your document, including headings and page numbers. Table and figure captions should utilize the selected font and font size, even if the table/figure has been reduced.

Pagination of the Main Text
The first page of each chapter/section, as with the first page of the entire document, is counted but is not numbered. As such, the first page of text is counted as page 1, but numbering begins with ?2? on the second page. The document is numbered consecutively with Arabic numerals in the top right corner. The numerals appear on the first line after the 1-inch top margin and are followed by a double-spaced line (See Figure 10.). (Master of Fine Arts in Creative Writing students, see Exceptions.) This format applies to the first page of the notes and references and the cover page of each appendix.

Note: You may use a header for creating page numbers if your software provides this feature, but be sure to adjust your page numbers to the same font and size to match the rest of your text, and ensure that headers are set 1 inch from the top edge of the page.





Headings and Subheadings
The format for headings and subheadings should be determined by your style guide; please review it carefully. Some book-length style guides, such as APA, provide an elaborate, well-defined structure, while others, such as Chicago, provide scant suggestions, and still others, such as MLA, provide no direction for formatting headings and subheadings. Students following journals as style guides in most cases are also not provided with such directions, since such formatting can be typeset by the publisher after an article has been accepted. If you are using a style guide that does not provide instructions, you and your advisor will need to decide on an appropriate, logical sequence of headings.

As a general guideline, it is recommended that headings and subheadings appear in the same font and size as the rest of the text, even if a style guide makes such alterations. However, capitalization, bold, italics, and indentation may be applied in a logical and consistent sequence to designate sections of text. Once you have chosen a sequence, be sure to follow it consistently throughout your document.

A reminder: another student?s document cannot be used as a style guide when formatting headings and subheadings or any other portion of your thesis, dissertation, or equivalent.

Paragraphs and Indentation
All paragraphs of your text should be indented as indicated by your style guide or journal. Indent consistently throughout your document. It is recommended that you do not add any extra space between paragraphs (unless doing so is a standard practice as indicated by your style guide). Indentation of block quotations and reference entries should conform to your style guide or journal.




Other Text Considerations
Right Justification
Justifying the right margin is not recommended because it can cause a number of problems, especially in the references. Many programs that justify your text at the right margin add spaces between words to stretch out a line, leaving awkward gaps in the text.

Widows and Orphans
“Widow” lines occur when the last line of a paragraph appears alone at the top of a page. “Orphan” lines occur when the first line of a paragraph appears alone at the bottom of a page. To fix a “widow” or an “orphan,” move a line from the previous page to accompany it or move the line onto the next page. You may allow more than 1 inch at the bottom of a page in order to avoid “widow” and “orphan” lines. Use good judgment in correcting such problems, but avoid placing text within the 1-inch bottom margin. Most word-processing programs, including Microsoft® Word and Corel WordPerfect®, have a feature to prevent or fix this error.

In-text Citations
You must identify all sources for other?s concepts, ideas, quotations, or paraphrased text that you have used in your document. Ensure that the author name, publication date, or page number (depending on your style and the type of source) are correct. Follow your style guide for the treatment of in-text citations in terms of appearance, content, punctuation, and placement.

Notes
If you wish to use notes, you may (1) place them at the bottom of the page (i.e., footnotes), (2) group them at the end of each chapter (i.e., endnotes or chapter notes), or (3) group them with the back matter (at the end of the document), before your references (i.e., notes). All notes should be indicated in the text with corresponding superscript numbers, so that they are distinct from the text. Follow your style guide or journal for the numbering and format of notes. You may single-space within each note, double-spacing between them. To format a document with endnote or chapter note organization, begin the notes section on a new page, which is counted but not numbered, as with the first pages of each chapter. Number the subsequent pages.

Tables and Figures
Refer to your style guide for the formatting of tables (including borders), table titles, and figure captions, including placement, typeface, alignment, and wording. You may need to reduce the size of a table or figure, either xerographically or by using a smaller size type within the table or figure, to meet the margin requirements. However, table titles and figure captions should remain the same size as the regular text, even if the actual tables or figures are reduced and/or the text within the table or figure cannot be changed. Photographs and Graphics
Each photograph or graphic, like all figures, must be given a number and a caption and must meet margin requirements, reducing the image if necessary. A “LIST OF PHOTOGRAPHS” or “LIST OF ILLUSTRATIONS” should also be included in the preliminary pages to assist readers in finding the images.

Should you wish to include photographs or illustrations, you have two options: (1) scanning them or (2) reproducing them xerographically (provided that the resolution is clear enough).

Black and white photographs (or ink-jet-printed or photocopied versions of them) are preferred, but if you must use color photographs, laser printing is required in order to maintain lasting color in the archival copy of your thesis/dissertation. Also, please be advised that color laser printing can be costly.
