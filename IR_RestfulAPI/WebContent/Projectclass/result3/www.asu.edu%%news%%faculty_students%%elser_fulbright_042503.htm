April 25, 2003

Fulbright grant takes Elser to Norway

ASU Biology Professor James Elser has been selected for a Fulbright grant that will take him to the University of Oslo to engage in field work in the Norwegian arctic archipelago of Svalbard.

Elser will work as part of an international group being organized by Norwegian researcher Dag Hessen with funding from Norway's Center for Advanced Studies.

"Our goal is to analyze data and further advance theory relevant to ecological stoichiometry as applied to understanding of aquatic ecosystems, especially lakes," said Elser. "Ecological stoichiometry is the study of the balance of energy and multiple chemical elements such as carbon nitrogen and phosphorus, in ecological systems. As a result of our work, we will be better able to achieve a fuller basic understanding of how ecosystems work."

Between August 2003 and July 2004, about 15 scientists from the United States, Asia and Europe will be visiting Norway to work on the project.

Some, like Elser, will be in long-term residence in Oslo, while others will visit for shorter periods.

"During these in-person sessions and also via the Internet, the participants will dig into massive data sets compiled by Dag Hessen as well as those contributed by project participants," said Elser. "This is a critical time in the development of ecological stoichiometry as a new paradigm in ecology. If we can be successful in moving even further ahead in understanding ecosystems using ecological stoichiometry as a guidepost, then ecological stoichiometry will be solidified as a central theory in ecology and its international adoption will be accelerated."

While in Norway, Elser also will teach the world's first course on Ecological Stoichiometry using his recent book on this topic as the course text. "Ecological Stoichiometry" is expected to be reviewed by both "Nature" and "Science" magazines in coming months.

"The course will be aimed at graduate students and advanced undergraduates preparing for graduate studies, and we will use my new book as a text," Elser said.

The Fulbright Scholar Program is recognized as the U.S. government's flagship program in international educational exchange. The program sends 800 scholars and professionals each year to more than 140 countries, where they lecture or conduct research in a wide variety of academic and professional fields. It is sponsored by the U.S. Department of State. Out of the 800 grants, fewer than 200 are awarded for research projects.

The Fulbright Program was established in 1946 under legislation introduced by former Senator J. William Fulbright of Arkansas.
