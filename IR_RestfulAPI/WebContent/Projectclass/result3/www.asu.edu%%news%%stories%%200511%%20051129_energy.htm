November 29, 2005

Facilities Management keeping energy use under control

During a time when energy costs are on the rise, ASU’s facilities management is controlling the university’s energy use and staying within budget.

The department is transitioning the university’s thermostats from 76 degrees to 72 degrees to provide an appropriate comfort level for the winter season while conserving energy. According to the Heating, Cooling and Air Conditioning Society of America, these temperatures are within the range of standard industry comfort factor zones.

“ASU realizes greater savings by operating warmer in the summer and cooler in the winter,” says Dean Hooks, director of facilities management. “By setting the temperature at a standard level, university-wide, we can stay within our annual $2 million energy budget.”

To ensure temperature levels stay consistent and within the comfort zone, Hooks says technicians conduct random tests of air temperatures on campus with a sling psychrometer, which takes a more accurate reading than any other method, including an infrared device.

“Our technicians will test air temperatures with two specialized thermometers and chart the readings to determine the area’s humidity level and comfort zone,” Hooks says. “This method is the most accurate way to obtain air temperature readings.”

Additionally, Hooks says most thermostats on campus have tamper-proof covers to prevent frequent manual temperature adjustments.

About 70 percent of campus buildings are heated and cooled through a central plant, which is located in the center of the campus near the Memorial Union. This plant is operated by a building automation system, which manages and controls thermostats electronically to ensure they remain set at the designated temperature. This system analyzes temperatures at all times; if a unit is malfunctioning, the system alerts facilities management of the unit’s location and a technician troubleshoots the problem.

Older campus buildings and the University Services Building do not operate from the central plant, but instead have direct extension air conditioning units, much like those found in single-family homes. Facilities management technicians adjust and calibrate these individual units twice a year to maintain functionality and comfort levels.

According to Hooks, May and November are the most difficult times of the year to keep temperatures at a consistent level. Since the outside air temperature changes drastically from morning to afternoon, the department switches from heating to cooling with as little change as possible. To help with energy efficiency, the department takes additional measures, Hooks says.

“We consider additional methods to save energy – for example, sealing older windows to prevent cool air leakage or upgrading buildings with better insulation,” Hooks says.

Facilities management encourages students, staff and faculty to be aware of the temperature perimeters to help the university with energy efficiency. Individuals that are near a thermometer should check to make sure it is set at the standard 72 degrees. If it is not set at the right temperature, or if an air conditioning unit is malfunctioning, contact the facilities service center at (480) 965-3633.

For suggestions, questions or comments on energy conservation at ASU, please call (480) 965-4521.
