FACULTY BIOGRAPHIES

 

Mark Cruse received his Ph.D. from New York University and is a specialist in Medieval French literature. His research interests include manuscripts, visual culture, theater, and kingship. He has translated Blue: The History of a Color ( Princeton, 2001), and co-translated The Memoirs of Catherine the Great (Random House, 2005).
E-mail: mark.cruse@asu.edu (2006)

Sylvain Gallais is Professor of International Economics in W.P. Carey COB and of French Civilization in the Department of Languages and Literatures. Gallais has a PhD. in Economics from Parix IX-Tours Universities (1978) and a PhD. in Political Science from the Institut d'Études Politiques-FNSP de Paris (1982). His specializations are in International Economics and the EU, and in Democracy and Citizens Participation. Twice he was awarded the Palmes Académiques by the French government. He was teaching mostly in Tours F-Rabelais University and twice in Bucknell University, PA (French Writing, French Civilization, French 20th Century Thought). Gallais is a founding member of research teams in Political Science and Information & Communication. He publishes in the two fields of his specialization. His most recent books and articles include: France Encounters Globalization (2002) and Approches transdisciplinaires de la délibération (2005).
E-mail: sylvain.gallais@asu.edu Spring 2006

   Aleksandra Gruzinska is Assistant Professor of French in the Department of Languages and Literatures. She has a Ph. D. in French from Pennsylvania State University. Her dissertation is entitled “Women’s Mental Landscapes in the Novels of Octave Mirbeau.” She has published scholarly articles on subjects of interest to women. She has directed theses and served on committees related to French Women including George Sand and Madame de Charrière. She is the co-translator with Murray D. Sirkis of Antoine Pecquet’s Diverse Thoughts on Man, translated from the original French Pensées diverses sur l’homme (1738). She is the editor and collaborator of Essays on E.M. Cioran. Rasinari 1911 – Paris 1995.
E-mail: gruzinska @asu.edu

  Anthony Lacy Gully received his MA from the University of California at Berkeley, and his Ph.D. from Stanford University. He is associate professor of Art History in the Herberger College of Fine Arts, Arizona State University, and a senior member of the art history faculty. He is the author of French Clocks in the Age of Napoléon (1999) and co-author of The Art of Seeing: John Ruskin and the Victorian Eye (1993). He served as editor of Studies in Iconography from 1982 to1993. He now serves on the Editorial Staff of College Art Journal, Albion, Scotia. He has published articles and exhibition catalogues on William Blake, Francisco Goya, Thomas Rowlandson and the Pre-Raphaelites.
E-mail: anthony.gully@asu.edu

Suzanne Hendrickson is Senior Lecturer and Supervisor of Lower Division French in the Department of Languages and Literatures. She received her PhD from Washington University in Saint Louis, for which her dissertation was a genetic study of Samuel Beckett’s play Fin de partie. In addition to her specialization in twentieth century French literature, she is pursuing a teaching and research interest in Francophone culture and literature in North America. She teaches courses in French language, literature and civilization, as well as Franco-Canadian civilization and French presence in North America. She has been a member of thesis committees on topics of pedagogy and Quebecois literature/civilization.. Her approaches to teaching involve textual analysis and studying literary works as reflections of the society which produced them.
Suzanne.Hendrickson@asu.edu

  William L. Hendrickson is a specialist in Medieval French and Occitan Language and Literature. He teaches Medieval French Literature, French Film, and Franco-Canadian Civilization, along with many other French language classes. In the literature and civilization courses he stresses the contributions of women writers: the poems of the Trobairitz or female Troubadours, the Lais of Marie de France, the multiple works of Christine de Pizan, for the Middle Ages, and narrative works of Gabrielle Roy, Anne Hébert, Antonine Maillet, and Suzanne Jacob in the civilization course; for the French Film course, the films of directors Agnès Varda and Diane Kurys are prominent. He has been a member of thesis committees on various Trobairitz and has directed theses on the works of Marie de France, Abélard and Hé1oise, Christine de Pizan, and Gabrielle Roy. He participated in the National Endowment for the Humanities Summer Seminar on the Medieval Lyric and is a member of the Medieval Academy. The French Government has distinguished his contribution to the French language, literature and culture by bestowing on him the Palmes Académiques.
E-mail: bill.hendrickson@asu.edu

  Anne Hibner Koblitz received her Ph.D. in 1983 from Boston University in Russian Intellectual History and History of Science. Her dissertation, which later became her first book, was a biography of the Russian mathematician, feminist, and social activist Sofia Kovalevskaia (published as A Convergence of Lives. Sofia Kovalevskaia: Scientist, Writer, Revolutionary (Rutgers UP, 1993). Her second book is Science, Women and Revolution in Russia (Harwood Academic Publishers, 2000, Second Edition). She has also written on gender and science theory and cross-cultural comparisons of women in science, technology, and medicine, and has done consulting work for the United Nations on cross-cultural comparisons of women's status in the sciences.
E-mail: koblitz@asu.edu

  Deborah Losse is Professor of French and Chair of the Department of Languages and Literatures. She has published extensively on the works of Rabelais, the storytellers of the French Renaissance, on Marguerite de Navarre, and on Montaigne. She is the author of two books, Sampling the Book: The Prologues of the French Conteurs and Rhetoric at Play: Rabelais and Satiricial Eulogy. She has also written on the intertextual relationship between Montaigne and his adoptive daughter, Marie Le Jars de Gournay.
E-mail: dlosse@asu.edu

  Kristin Valentine is Professor of Communication and Women’s Studies. Her research area within Communication focuses on Performance Studies. She was academically educated with a B.S. at Univ of Wisconsin; M.A. Univ of Washington; and Ph.D. Univ of Utah. She publishes in Communication Journals and Folklore Journals. Her ethnographic fieldwork is conducted in Spain and New Zealand. She has directed Interpreters Theatre productions including Beowulf ; and Highly Classified: Narratives of ASU’s Classified Staff.
