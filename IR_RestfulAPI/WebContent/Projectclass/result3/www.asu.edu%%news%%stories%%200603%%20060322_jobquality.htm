March 22, 2006

Arizona job quality near national average

Job quality in Arizona in 2004 was 2.0 percent below the national average, but ranked 23rd among all states, according to the W. P. Carey School of Business at Arizona State University. The industrial mix portion of job quality was 1.6 percent less than the national average, ranking 23rd, and the occupational mix portion was 0.5 percent below average, also ranking 23rd.

The state's job mix being tilted toward low-paying industries and occupations accounts for 2 percentage points of the nearly 7 percent shortfall in Arizona's average wage from the national average," said Tom R. Rex, associate director of the Center for Business Research.

The sub par industrial mix in Arizona in 2004 primarily resulted from its above-average shares of employment in the low-paying administrative support sector and very low-paying accommodation and food services sector, and from its below-average employment share in the high-paying professional, scientific and technical services sector. The inferior occupational mix largely derived from the higher-than-average proportion of workers in the very low-paying food preparation and serving group.

In comparison to the 2.5 percent decrease in national job quality between 2001 and 2004, job quality in Arizona fell 0.7 percent more, ranking 44th. Relative to the national average, the industrial mix slipped 0.3 percent, ranking 42nd, and the occupational mix declined 0.4 percent, ranking 30th.

"Historically, the state usually falls further behind the nation during periods of economic weakness, as experienced in 2002 into 2003," said Rex. "However, job quality also fell by more than the national average in 2004."

Arizona's poor performance on the change in industrial mix between 2001 and 2004 particularly was due to a loss of jobs in the high-paying semiconductor manufacturing industry. Even in 2004, a disproportionate amount of the decrease in industrial job quality relative to the U.S. average originated in this industry. Despite its recent problems, semiconductor manufacturing remained a large positive force on the state's 2004 job mix.

The decrease in occupational mix relative to the national average can be traced to small impacts from several occupational groups, with the largest from the computer and mathematical group.

Among six neighboring states, job quality in Arizona in 2004 was fourth highest overall, on industrial mix and on occupational mix. Overall, the job mix in Arizona was better than in New Mexico and Nevada, but worse than in Colorado, California and Utah. Arizona also ranked fourth on the 2001-to-2004 changes in overall job quality, industrial mix and occupational mix. Overall, Arizona performed better than Colorado and New Mexico.

The report Job Quality in 2004 and the Change between 2001 and 2004 is available online at wpcarey.asu.edu/seid/Reports.cfm.
