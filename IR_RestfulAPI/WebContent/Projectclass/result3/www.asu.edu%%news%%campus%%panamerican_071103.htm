July 11, 2003

ASU's global engagement agenda moves forward

ASU is expanding its role as an institution of higher learning by building academic and professional collaborations beyond the borders.

As a means of setting the New Gold Standard for ASU, President Michael Crow has emphasized the importance of global engagement as a critical part for the advancement of ASU, metropolitan Phoenix and the state.

ASU's global engagement efforts are moving forward with the establishment of the Pan American Initiative, a series of programs to promote teaching and research in all aspects of Latino culture and society. Its reach extends south from the borderlands to beyond, where ASU will engage in productive social, economic and cultural dialogue with Mexico and the rest of Latin America, and north toward Canada as well.
Newly named Director of the Pan-American Initiative and Special Adviser to the President Jorge de los Santos will help lead this effort to enhance ASU's academic landscape and international profile.

"Because of the geographical proximity, Mexico and Latin America can enhance teaching and scholarship relevant to the region," de los Santos says. "The interdisciplinary approach covers society and culture, but also technological advancements, scientific understanding, poverty education and sustainable development. Similar beneficial collaborations will be sought with Canada."

The first of many programs through the Office of Pan American Initiatives (OPI) was initiated June 27 between ASU and Insitituto Tecnológico y de Estudios Superiores de Monterrey (Monterrey Tech).

Eugenio Garcia, dean of Engineering & Architecture of Monterrey Tech and Peter Crouch, dean of the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering at ASU signed an agreement that establishes a faculty exchange program between both of the institutions.

Through the program, selected faculty members are expected to spend approximately one week at their host institution in an effort to exchange ideas (academic and professional), in areas that include biotechnology, environmental science and research. This exchange program is setting a foundation for continued exploration of future professional and academic collaborations between ASU and other institutions.

"I'm very excited about this partnership," Garcia said. "We go into this partnership with the heart and sincere interest of 'El Tech' (Monterrey Tech) and ASU - in the pursuit of success. This is extending ASU beyond the borders and it's allowing us to enhance our abilities to help Mexico through this exchange of knowledge."

Crouch says this program is a win-win situation for both institutions.

"ASU can bring its knowledge of and experience in highly sophisticated educational systems to improving Latin America," Crouch said. "In addition what we will gain from Monterrey Tech is not only a global exchange of minds, but the sharing of ideas that will help both of our institutions move forward toward enhancing their programs."

Once appropriate channels are in place, the OPI will take concrete steps toward fostering collaboration between institutions of higher education, governments at all levels, private sector and civil society entities on issues relating to higher education. It will build stronger regional partnerships for development and implementation of programs.
