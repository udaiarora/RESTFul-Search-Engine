September 3, 2003

'Stretch' rewrites ending to struggling students' futures

In the early 1990s, faculty members in ASU's department of English were facing a frustrating reality - more than a third of students taking ENG 101 and ENG 102 were failing. Students coming to the university with low test scores and academic deficiencies were especially at risk.

ASU English professors wondered how they could help under-performing students become capable writers and ultimately successful graduates.

The answer came in 1994 with the Stretch program, which has been so successful it is now used as a model for several universities throughout the nation. Last spring, President Michael Crow recognized the program by giving those involved an ASU President's Award for Innovation.

The idea for the program came from John Ramage, associate professor of English, and David Schwalm, vice provost for Academic Programs and Services at ASU East. In 1991 they began a pilot program, which was the basis for the Stretch program. The concept was simple, but highly effective. What struggling writing students need to succeed is more time to practice writing.

The name of the Stretch program is fitting. ENG 101 is stretched over two semesters instead of one. Students take Writing Across the Curriculum (WAC) 101 their first semester and then take ENG 101 the next semester. Stretch students usually take both classes with the same instructor and same students.

Karen Dwyer, a senior lecturer with the Department of English, serves as the Stretch program coordinator. She says all students have the potential to be skilled writers, but some need a bit more attention and encouragement.

"When students get more teacher time, more time working on assignments and more time making friends in their Stretch classes, they are likely to flourish," Dwyer says. "They find that they have a support system because the program is designed to help them succeed."

And there is no doubt the students are succeeding. Stretch students consistently pass ENG 101 and ENG 102 at a higher rate than their counterparts not in the program - those students who initially scored higher on achievement tests.
Dwyer says teachers in the Stretch program are very committed to their students.

"Many of our teachers have repeatedly asked to teach Stretch classes even though they could have other teaching assignments. They like getting to know their students over the whole school year, and they can see that they are making a real difference in the classroom," Dwyer says.

ASU Executive Vice President and Provost Milt Glick says the Stretch program is an impressive example of what can be achieved when creative individuals step back from an issue and give it a fresh look.

"They were able to design a new approach that has a much higher success rate, both in the course and in the follow-up courses, and provides excellent learning in the area of composition, a critical skill for success across the board," Glick says.

For information about the Stretch program, go to (www.asu.edu/clas/english/ composition/cbw/stretch.htm).
