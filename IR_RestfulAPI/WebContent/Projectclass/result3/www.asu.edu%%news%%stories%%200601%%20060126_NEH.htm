January 26, 2006

Professors earn prestigious NEH Fellowships

Three ASU professors have been awarded prestigious yearlong fellowships from the National Endowment for the Humanities. The $40,000 grants will allow them to pursue special research projects during 2006 that will add to scholarly knowledge or general understanding of the humanities.

They are among 76 university faculty members to receive the awards, which are extremely competitive. An additional 90 went to college teachers, independent scholars and researchers.

The ASU awardees are:

. Robert Bjork, a professor of English and director of the Arizona Center for Medieval and Renaissance Studies.

. Frédéric Canovas, an associate professor of French.

. Diane Wolfthal, a professor of art history.

Bjork's grant will help him complete a book titled "The Emergence of a Discipline: the Scandinavians and Anglo-Saxon Literary Studies," which he began several years ago. He discovered that many of the contributions to the study of Anglo-Saxon literature - some 300 books and articles over 200 years - had not been translated from Danish, Swedish, Norwegian, Icelandic, Finnish and Scandinavian Neo-Latin.

"I am analyzing their background and content, translating a selection of the most important of these texts in to English, and including a complete bibliography of these and related materials," Bjork says. "The book will be a history of the emergence of a discipline and a study in the history of ideas and culture."

Bjork plans to visit the Royal Libraries in Stockholm and Copenhagen, and also the British Library in London. He also will give the Fell-Benedikz Lecture at Nottingham University and the keynote address at the Viking Society for Northern Research at the University of London.

Canovas is working on a book on the relationship between the work of French writer André Gide and the art of post-impressionist painter Maurice Denis. The two had a personal and professional relationship, collaborating on a book and maintaining a rich correspondence, as well as personal journals. Their interpretation of classical art followed opposite directions because of differing religious and moral beliefs.

"Ninety percent of the documents I will refer to in my book are original manuscripts that have never been published before, as Gide's and Denis' families authorized me to consult their father's and grandfather's papers," Canovas says. "I believe my study can redefine the existing notions about both artists and develop new ways to look at their works."

He will conduct research at the Musée Maurice Denis in Saint-Germain-en-Laye near Paris.

Wolfthal plans to complete several chapters of a monograph she is writing on Hugo van der Goes, one of the greatest Early Netherlandish painters.

She began working on his historiography last summer during a Mellon Visiting Senior Fellowship in Washington, D.C. She also will write about his Italian patronage and his relationship to the Devotio Moderna, a reform movement.

"No comprehensive study of this artist has ever been written in English," Wolfthal says, "yet Hugo not only produced works of incredible beauty that have become canonical within the discipline of art history, but also deeply influenced the development of Netherlandish and Italian art."

She plans to conduct research at the Metropolitan Museum, where she has a C. Coleman and Pamela Coleman Memorial Fund Art History Fellowship.

Wolfthal also will continue her study at the libraries of NYU, Columbia, the Institute of Fine Arts, and the New York Public Library.
