November 3, 2005

Watson: Alito confirmation struggle of 'good' vs. 'just'

The lines are clearly drawn in the coming fight over the nomination of Samuel Alito to the Supreme Court. During the process, the public will have unprecedented opportunity to become informed and express their opinions about the new nominee, said George Watson, a political science professor in ASU’s Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication.

Those opinions will be heard, added Watson, an expert on the Supreme Court nomination process who has written numerous pieces on the process. Watson also has been a consultant on a video produced by the Congressional Research Service for use in educating senators and their staff on the nomination and confirmation process.

In stark contrast to the ill-fated nomination of Harriett Miers, President Bush’s choice of Alito is a nomination that promises to be a monumental struggle welcomed by the right and feared by the left. Since publishing a Blueprint for Judicial Reform in 1981, political conservatives have plotted a course to transform the judiciary into a force for the political “good,” at least from their perspective, Watson said.

“They see the Alito nomination as their best opportunity to solidify the Supreme Court as an ideologically conservative institution and overturn certain past decisions of a more liberal era,” Watson said. “Beleaguered liberals in the meantime, forced to reconstruct conservative Justice Sandra Day O’Connor as an icon of moderation, view the nomination of Alito as a mortal blow to the Supreme Court as the only remaining bastion of justice.”

“The result will be a titanic struggle of the ‘good’ versus the ‘just,’ the likes of which we have never seen,” Watson added.

But this battle will not be “Bork revisited,” Watson said. The nomination of Robert Bork in 1987 collapsed under a blitzkrieg of liberal opposition, leaving conservatives stunned at the superior political organization and strategy that doomed their hero. This time around, the conservatives have more money and have created organizations whose sole purpose is to confirm the judicial nominees of President Bush.

For those opposed to Alito, the filibuster remains an option.

“Any attempt by supporters of the nomination to head off a filibuster by claiming that nominees deserve an up or down vote in the Senate is simply a political ploy, as evident from its notable absence during the Harriett Miers nomination,” Watson said. The filibuster remains a rarely used but significant protection of a substantial minority (41 percent or more) against a bare majority (50 to 60 percent).

As the nomination moves into the public sphere, American citizens will have ample opportunities to be active in the confirmation process.

“Public opinion will do much to influence positions taken by senators to support or oppose the nomination,” Watson promised. He makes several suggestions on what to do to participate in the process.

•  Be wary of partisan jargon. Descriptions like “strict constructionist, outside the mainstream” and “won’t legislate from the bench” are propagandist uses of language that obscure more than they inform.

• Inform yourself about the arguments for and against the nominee. Get beyond the 30-second commercials and partisan jargon to specific analyses of Alito’s lengthy career. There is plenty on record to consider. Look for opinions in specific cases to see how Alito thinks on different constitutional issues.

• Pay attention to the Senate Judiciary Committee hearings to see how Alito handles the tough questioning of U.S. senators. Observe what groups and opinion leaders favor and oppose the nominee. They serve as effective cues for like-minded individuals.

• Make up your own mind about whether he should be confirmed. Lend support to organized groups consistent with your views on the matter. Communicate your feelings to your senators.
