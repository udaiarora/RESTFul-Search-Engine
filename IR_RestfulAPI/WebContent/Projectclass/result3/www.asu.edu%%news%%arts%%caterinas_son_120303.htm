December 3, 2003

Lyric Opera Theatre premieres 'Caterina's Son'

"The DaVinci Code" is currently a bestselling novel, and a new composer is hoping that his musical about Leonardo DaVinci will enjoy the same popularity, with a little help from ASU's Katherine K. Herberger College of Fine Arts. With a Ph.D. from Stanford University and years as a successful engineer behind him, Tak Tarbo has combined his love of Broadway musicals and fascination with Leonardo DaVinci to create "Caterina's Son," premiering Nov. 21, as part of the Herberger Mainstage Lyric Opera Theatre season at ASU.

The musical focuses on DaVinci as a gifted artist who also was the illegitimate son of a peasant woman who gave him up for a better life. "Caterina's Son" is ultimately a story of redemption that spans DaVinci's life from 1452 to 1519 and is set in Renaissance Italy. DaVinci was reunited with his 65-year-old mother when he was 41 and in Milan working on "The Last Supper."

Tarbo hand-picked the Lyric Opera Theatre program to perform the musical and J. Robert Wills, dean of the Herberger College of Fine Arts, will direct it. Lyric Opera Theatre director William Reber is musical director for "Caterina's Son," marking the first collaboration between Reber and Wills since they worked together at the University of Texas at Austin nearly 20 years ago. Both were fascinated with the unique subject matter of the musical.

"DaVinci is known around the world as the universal man and Caterina is his largely forgotten mother," says Wills. "It's a wonderful story to be told and that's what intrigued me from the beginning." Over the past year, Wills has worked closely with Tarbo to develop and refine the script, even traveling with him to DaVinci's birthplace in Tuscany to do research for the production.

"No trace of Caterina's family has been found in the archives of the old churches in Tuscany, indicating that she belonged to a neglected class," says Tarbo, who feels that the soul and core of DaVinci's genius may be attributed to his mother. "I am interested in Caterina and her position in the life of Leonardo."

Some experts think the model for DaVinci's "Mona Lisa" is his birth mother Caterina.
"I decided to address this unexplored aspect of Leonardo's life and propose a new dimension to the universal intrigue of the "Mona Lisa," which means also telling the story of Leonardo and Caterina," says Tarbo.

It is unusual for a university music program to premiere an original musical, but the Lyric Opera Theatre program, which recently received eight AriZoni theatre awards for last season's production of "A Chorus Line," is ready for the challenge.

"Caterina's" ensemble cast of 17 will portray nearly 60 characters. Many of the students helped shape the musical during rehearsals and in a Broadway Scenes class that met three times a week from January through May 2003. The experience has been rewarding for both students and faculty.

"Caterina's Son" evokes a cinematic feel with large-screen video projections of Da Vinci's work and images of Tuscany. Some of the cast will wear vintage costumes used in the movie "Becket" with Richard Burton. Esther Turner, coordinator for the School of Music's costume shop, borrowed the costumes from the local Renaissance Singers performance group.

"These costumes are like works of art," Esther says. "We are helping restore them to their original condition."

More than 70 costumes will be used in the production; 10 of them are from "Becket."

Show times for "Caterina's Son" are 7:30 p.m., Nov. 21, 22 and Dec. 3, 5 and 6; and 2 p.m. on Nov. 23. Tickets are $12 for seniors, faculty and staff, and $5 for students. For information, call (480) 965-6447 or visit (http://herbergercollege.asu.edu/calendar/mainstage.html).

Tanguay, with the Herberger College of Fine Arts, can be reached at (480) 965-7144 or (tanguay@asu.edu).
