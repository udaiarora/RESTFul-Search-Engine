University presidents decry state of science education

Many attendees at the Science Coalition dinner in New York bemoaned the nation’s K-12 science education as inadequate. But a suggestion that a debate over evolutionary theory has led to science’s declining presence in the classroom was roundly dismissed. ASU president Michael Crow said the issue hasn’t appeared “at any time, at any level” during his three-year tenure.

Newsday, April 6.

Scientific creativity may skirt ethical boundaries

Scientists are mixing animal and human genes, hoping to test new drugs more safely and accurately, harvest organs for transplant and find new cures for human diseases. Some find the idea morally repugnant. “Scientists are under a lot of pressure to be creative,” says bioethicist Jason Robert, “to come up with solutions that will help lessen the lag between basic research and cures. But it’s not clear that building chimeras is going to solve the problem”

Christian Science Monitor, March 17.

Inconsistent test scores cause concerns over validity

Nearly all of the Houston schools suspected of possible cheating on the state’s standardized achievement tests last year produced sharply weaker exam results this year. While it is not proof of wrongdoing, “you wonder about the validity of scores when they jump around like that,” says education professor Thomas Haladyna. Teacher turnover rates and changing student populations could cause score changes, but not in every suspected school.

Dallas Morning News, March 19.

Full disclosure addresses conflict of interest issues

A police officer who made a presentation touting the benefits of Tasers to the Chandler, Ariz. city council should have revealed that he received stock options from the company. Just because no laws were broken doesn’t mean he acted ethically, says Marianne Jennings, business ethics professor. “The council might have made the same decision anyway, but they deserved to know.”

Indianapolis Star and Baltimore Sun, March 20.

Media critic draws criticism

Neurologist William Polk Cheshire, affiliated with a Christian think tank, questioned the diagnosis of Terri Schiavo and also criticized the media for skewing the issue of stem-cell research. His conclusions drew criticism from his peers. “Presumably what Cheshire wants is not really analysis of words but that we all recognize his particular view of truth,” says biologist Jane Maienschein. “This is highly problematic.”

Palm Beach Post, March 24.

Phoenix housing market appears to have staying power

With mortgage rates heading higher, some economists are forecasting that home sales and ever-escalating prices are likely to drop. But not everyone is predicting ballooning prices will burst. In Phoenix, the market has been so hot, it’s going to take a while for it to cool down, says business researcher Jay Butler.

Newsday and Houston Chronicle, March 25.

Social norms may trump danger concerns

Eight men who arrived at a condominium for a ski trip smelled propane gas, but nobody acted with any sense of urgency, so they ignored it. The lodge exploded a few hours later, killing three. Stephanie deLusé, interdisciplinary studies lecturer, says this is common behavior because social rules of belonging and acceptance trump looking different. “People tend to look at how everyone else is acting when the danger is not clearly defined.”

Rocky Mountain News, March 25.

Deserts drawing bedroom communities

The desert in northwestern Arizona could become America’s next great bedroom community, as Las Vegas development spills over its borders. Builders are quietly buying up land. “Thirty years ago, if you had said there will be this huge growth out here, people would have laughed their heads off,” says policy researcher Rob Melnick. “We’re poking into all kinds of areas that you wouldn’t have believed.”

Washington Post, March 26.

Teams focusing on fan-friendly experiences

Baseball owners know that they have to field a good team if they want to maintain attendance, but winning isn’t everything. Teams are paying more attention to what their customers want. “The experience is really important today,” says sports business expert James Kahler. “You are not just selling tickets to a baseball game. The customer service you have to offer is no different than what Disney or another them park has.”

San Diego Union-Tribune, March 27.

Expert sees laws harming cultural education

Many educators worry that an unintended consequence of the No Child Left Behind law may be that Native American students will lose instruction in their cultural traditions, history and language. “We feel an effective education is one that’s defined primarily by the goals of the community,” says Denis Viri, researcher in the Center for Indian Education. “In my opinion, No Child Left Behind is just another one of those roadblocks.”

Christian Science Monitor, March 29.

Finance officers, journalists face communication disconnect

Business reporters rarely understand corporate finance, and finance officers have to be terrific communicators because they are expected to explain it. W. P. Carey School dean Robert Mittelstaedt says he has chided journalists for not making an effort to understand compensation practices. Stock options are very different from salary, for instance, but they are “reported as income, because that’s what the formula says.”

CFO Magazine, March 2005.
