July 28, 2004

ASU professor wins one of 5 Wilson grants in U.S.

Michael Coyle, a doctoral student at Arizona State University, is one of five recipients of a 2004 Woodrow Wilson Practicum Grant.

Coyle, a student in the School of Justice and Social Inquiry, received the award for his research on the social costs of the recent massive incarceration movement in the United States, and on the various gender, class, race and ethnicity inequities entailed in imprisonment.

"With this grant I intend to achieve two things: First, I will contribute to the work of the Arizona Coalition for Effective Government (AzCEG), a human-rights-for-prisoners community organization that helps prisoners help themselves as they re-enter their communities," Coyle said. "Second, I will use this grant to broaden my research agenda and strengthen the capacity of AzCEG to function as a center for criminal justice and prison policy analysis."

Coyle, who grew up in Greece, said his passion in life is human rights for prisoners. "This theme impacts every aspect of anything I do - teaching, research, activism, etc."

His research involves two basic themes - first, what he calls "the language of justice," and second, human rights issues in the U.S. criminal justice system. He also is researching post-correctional homelessness.

"The language of justice" is a series of investigations into the linguistic categories defining justice in everyday life, Coyle said. "This research examines the usage of the words we use to talk about justice in our society via close examination of such words in media, cultural artifacts and speech."

Coyle has studied the usage of such terms as "innocent victim," which implies that being a victim carries responsibility for being a victim, he said.

Coyle received his bachelor's degree in religion with honors at ASU in 1993, and his master's degree in philosophical anthropology at Harvard University.

He has written or co-authored a number of articles, including "The Social Cost of America's Race to Incarcerate," "Latinos and the Drug War: Alternatives to Incarceration," and "Race and Class Penalties in Crack Cocaine Sentencing."

For more information on Coyle's research, contact him at Michael.coyle@asu.edu

The grant was presented by the Woodrow Wilson National Fellowship Foundation.
