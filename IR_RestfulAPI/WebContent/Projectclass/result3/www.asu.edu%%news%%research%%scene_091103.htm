September 11, 2003

SCENE links high school students with ASU scientists

A collaborative science education program is helping Arizona high school students get a taste of college-level research and science - with spectacular results.

The Research Experiences for High School Students program, offered by the Southwest Center for Education and the Natural Environment (SCENE), targets students considering a science major in college and gives them a firsthand look at the world of scientific research. SCENE, a non-profit organization affiliated with ASU's Center for Environmental Studies, links students with ASU scientists and research projects for six months of each academic year.

Through the program, students are exposed to the scientific process, learn how to collect and analyze data using sophisticated scientific equipment and are active participants in cutting-edge research projects.

"The program involves students in the whole process of scientific discovery, not in just one tiny aspect of it," says SCENE director Kathryn Kyle. "It provides the opportunity for high level scientific achievement."

Now in its fifth year, the program culminates with a research poster presentation by the students. The students are encouraged to enter their research in the Central Arizona Science and Engineering Fair (CARSEF) and other science competitions, where they typically excel. Program participants have won first, second and third place prizes in numerous categories at CARSEF, as well as the grand prize for each of the last three years. Last year, a student in the program also took first place at both the regional and national levels in the Junior Science and Humanities Symposium competition.

Several students have won Regent's Scholarships to ASU, and most have won cash and scholarship prizes at one or more science competitions. In addition, the program itself awards a small scholarship to each participant who completes the program successfully.

In 2000, CARSEF grand prize winners, Chrissy Hobson and Nachelle Brown, worked in a bioengineering lab studying balance. After working with the researchers in the lab, the students saw a need for a portable device that could be used to diagnose balance problems and provide balance training to correct them.

In their second year, the two designed and built such a platform, using their winnings from CARSEF the previous year to finance the project. The first platform did not function as expected, so they redesigned it with better, more expensive parts, for which they secured grant funding.

In her third year in the program, Hobson tested the successfully redesigned platform with senior citizens, comparing balance between test subjects who had no balance training and those who had Tai Chi. Brown and Hobson now attend ASU, and are considering patenting the portable balance platform.

"The students in this program are incredibly bright," says Kyle. "When they describe their research it's mind-boggling - graduate level research done by 15-year-olds."

Kyle says the key to the program is its collaborative nature - a partnership among SCENE, many departments at ASU, SRP and other program sponsors, and Valley high school teachers and students. The program was piloted under a grant from the Arizona Community Foundation in 1997. In subsequent years, SRP has been the principal sponsor, with additional support from the City of Tempe for students attending Tempe high schools.

SCENE, a nonprofit educational organization, is itself built as a collaborative partnership between ASU's Center for Environmental Studies and members of the private sector.

Applications for the 2003 - 04 year are due Sept. 15 and can be found online at (http://aspin.asu.edu/scene). For information, call Kyle at (480) 965-4179 or e-mail (kkyle@asu.edu).
