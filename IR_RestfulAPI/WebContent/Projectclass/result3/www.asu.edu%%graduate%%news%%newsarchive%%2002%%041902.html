Dennis Durband

April 19, 2002


PFUND OVERCOMES ADVERSITY TO SUCCEED IN PREPARING FUTURE FACULTY, DOCTORAL STUDIES

Born with a severe form of lazy eye in both eyes, Michele Pfund had eye surgery when she was only a few months old. She underwent several more surgeries and worked through intensive therapy through her second-grade year. At an early age, Pfund learned a strong work ethic from her parents and doctors as they encouraged her through each stage of therapy. By the age of four, Pfund was reading the newspaper.

Today, Pfund is doing much more than reading newspapers. This summer, she will complete work on a Ph.D. in industrial engineering. Pfund is one of the many impressive Preparing Future Faculty (PFF) fellows who will display information about their teaching projects at the annual PFF Capstone Fair on Thursday, April 25, from 2–4 p.m. at the Memorial Union, Arizona Room.

Preparing Future Faculty provides innovative training and mentoring for doctoral students preparing for faculty careers in higher education. ASU is nationally recognized as a pioneer and leader in this program. PFF requires at minimum a one-year commitment from participating doctoral students. After the first-year exploratory phase, students successfully completing the first year apply for the second-year participatory phase. Students learn about faculty roles, inclusive teaching strategies, job search techniques and engage in teaching, research and service projects with faculty mentors and students at different types of institutions.

Pfund kept raising the bar of achievement in her life. She earned a B.S. in chemical engineering at Case Western Reserve University and a master's degree in industrial engineering at Purdue University. She worked for more than six years as a manufacturing and development engineer in medical device manufacturing and disease detection. Well published, she serves as editor of ORMS Tomorrow, the operations research professional society student journal. Pfund's research has been funded by the National Science Foundation and the U.S. Air Force. A super achiever, Pfund is convinced that her early-life trials made her stronger.

"My father thinks that by teaching me to work so hard to overcome my eye problems, it influenced me at an early age and affected how I approached my school work, sports practices, music lessons and the like," said Pfund, who has also competed in international piano competitions. "So, while I am a straight A student and the work comes naturally to me, I do know that part of it is due to my strong work ethic. I will admit that I do have to work really hard to succeed in classes I don't particularly enjoy. That's my biggest advice to students: choose a field that you love and you will no longer have to worry about grades."

In her second year of PFF, Pfund worked with the Southwest Center for Education and the Natural Environment (SCENE), an organization that promotes learning about the environment through scientific discovery. Through SCENE, she worked with three Phoenix-area high school students to develop a project for the Central Arizona Regional Science and Engineering Fair, held annually in Tempe. The students — Hong Han, Yen Yeh and Anson Wong — placed first in the computer science competition. Yeh and Wong attend Westview High School in Avondale, and Han attends Dobson High School in Mesa.

Pfund's Capstone Fair exhibit will focus on her research, teaching of the supply chain management class, coursework she developed for the Maricopa Advanced Technology Education Center and the Intel Institute and service activities with SCENE. She won the John W. Teets Outstanding Teaching Award for her work with the supply chain management course — a course outside her own academic area.

Like others involved in PFF, Pfund sees great potential for a trickle down theory in which PFF affects positive change among doctoral students, who eventually become faculty members passing what they've learned on to their own students.
"What is really important for the student is to learn the scientific method of problem solving and critical thinking," Pfund said. "This is my guiding principle. I strive now to provide learning opportunities through discovery. So instead of giving a student an answer directly, I now try to ask the right questions or create the right scenarios to let the students discover the answers themselves. The benefits are much greater for both myself and the students."

Pfund is also mentoring undergraduate and graduate college students. "Through PFF, I have learned so many skills," she said. "We had a seminar on cooperative and multimedia learning in the first year that I quickly integrated into my supply chain management course. This seminar greatly improved my teaching skills and helped me reach my students more effectively. In addition, we had sessions on how to integrate research with teaching. These again trickled down into our research lab as I began mentoring both master's and undergraduate students within the Modeling and Analysis of Semiconductor Manufacturing lab. I learned how to ask effective questions so that students found the answers rather than being told the answers. PFF helped me to make this transition. My doctoral education has been greatly enriched, and through PFF I have enriched my interactions with high school students, undergraduate students and master's students."

John Fowler and Matt Carlyle serve as faculty mentors for Pfund. "They are incredible and have ensured that I am more of an apprentice than a doctoral student," Pfund said. "They have had me teach in their classes, mentor their master's students and aid them in the management of their research projects. This combination has ensured that I am prepared for the academic job."
