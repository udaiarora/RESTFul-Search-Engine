January 19, 2004

Staff, students come unbound by sailing

While on a hiking trip in 1984, 18-year-old Jack Oppenhuizen fell 60 feet and severely injured his spinal cord. He was left a paraplegic and in need of a wheelchair. Far from devastated, he learned to adapt and found solace in sailing.

"I had been very athletic before then, and I had to make some drastic changes," Oppenhuizen says. "But I found an enormous amount of joy in sailing."

Through the years, and after extensive therapy, Oppenhuizen made amazing progress and is now able to walk again. In or out of a wheelchair, he still finds sailing to be a truly liberating experience.

Oppenhuizen, currently an ASU graduate student, oversees Voyages Unbound, an innovative community-based program that sponsors several local endeavors, including ASU's Sailing Club. The group operates eight sailboats at Tempe Town Lake, each outfitted with adapted technology, including a joystick-type device allowing disabled participants to operate the boat's controls.

Oppenhuizen says the club is open to all ASU students, alumni and staff and encourages those interested in trying something new to give sailing a try.

"Sailing is a powerful experience," Oppenhuizen says. "Through Voyages Unbound, the club is meant to be a fully integrated program between those with and without disabilities working together on the same team."

ASU alumnus Jake Geller is a member of the club and describes sailing as exciting and empowering.

"I like the freedom and independence of sailing," Geller says. "It also takes me out of my wheelchair and allows me a chance to interact with others on the
same level."

Geller, who was diagnosed with Duchenne muscular dystrophy at the age of 3, has been using a wheelchair for the past 14 years. Geller says he highly recommends sailing - for both disabled and able-bodied adventure seekers.

"We are not just a sports club," Geller says. "We also develop leadership skills. My involvement in the club has allowed me to grow as a person."

ASU Alumnus Loren Worthington recently went sailing with the club for the first time, and says he found the experience to be very relaxing.

Worthington has been in a wheelchair for the past 18 years due to a sports-related accident, and says Sailing Club members did a great job of introducing him to sailing.

"Jake and his volunteers do a good job," Worthington says. "They were friendly and safety-conscious at the same time. We all had fun. I use a manual wheelchair to push around Phoenix. It is nice to get in the boat and let the sails do all the work."

Geller says the club will continue sailing through the spring, but unlike most parts of the country, summer sailing in Arizona is far from ideal.

"The sailing season here is basically reversed from most areas," he says. "It is just too hot and the wind dies down during the summer."

For more information about Voyages Unbound and ASU Sailing Club activities, call Oppenhuizen at (602) 570-5300 or go to (www.asu.edu/clubs/sailing).

Zajac, with Media Relations & Public Information, can be reached at (480) 965-8145 or (izajac@asu.edu).
