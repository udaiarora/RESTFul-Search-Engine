Korean, Chinese, Japanese and American films in series

Arizona State University's Center for Asian Studies will begin its fall film series with "Together," Sept. 1, and conclude it with "Lost in Translation" Oct. 6.

All films will be shown at 7:30 p.m. in Room 101 of the Nursing Building on the ASU Tempe Campus. The series is free.

"Together," scheduled for Sept. 1 (Wednesday), is a 2002 drama, directed by Chen Kaige. It's in Mandarin with English subtitles, 116 min. Haruki Kaku, professor, Hiroshima Shudo University, will introduce the film.

In "Together," the realities of modern Beijing serve as backdrop to a tale of family loyalty. A 13-year-old violin prodigy and his illiterate and destitute father travel to the capital in search of a violin teacher.

"Chihwaseon" will be screened on Wednesday, Sept. 15. A 2002, drama, directed by Kwon-taek Im, the film is in Korean with English subtitles, 117 min. Introduced by Nayoung Aimee Kwon of ASU's Department of Languages and Literatures will introduce the film.

In a time of political and social unrest in 19th century Korea, the self-taught painter Jang Seung-up explores his natural talent amidst the repressive world around him. The film's Kwon-taek Im took a prize for direction at the 2002 Cannes Film Festival.

"Record of a Tenement Gentleman" is set for Wednesday, Sept. 22. This is a 1947 drama, directed by Yasujiro Ozu, in Japanese with English subtitles, 72 min. Sybil Thornton of ASU's History Department will introduce it.

A comic and bittersweet tale about a gruff, stubborn widow who reluctantly takes in an abandoned boy, the film is a charming portrait of small town life with a surprise ending.

"Earth" will be shown on Wednesday, Sept. 29. This 1998, drama, directed by Deepa Mehta, is in English, Hindi, Parsee, Punjabi & Urdu, and it's 110 min. Anne Feldhaus of ASU's Department of Religious Studies will give the introduction.

This second film in director Mehta's trilogy of the elements fire, earth and water is a moving account of the partition of India in 1947. Based on Bapsi Sidhwa's novel Cracking India, the calamity is portrayed through the eyes of an 8-year-old girl from Lahore.

"Lost in Translation," on Wednesday, Oct. 6, concludes the series. This 2003 drama/comedy, directed by Sofia Coppola, is in English and Japanese, 102 min. Miko Foard of ASU's Department of Languages & Literatures will introduce the film.

Two Americans from disparate backgrounds cope with jet lag and dislocation in modern-day Tokyo. Their unusual friendship provides a welcome escape from boredom and loneliness and they quickly team up to venture into the urban Japanese landscape.

Free parking is available after 7 p.m. in Parking Structure 3, located at 10th street and Myrtle Avenue.
