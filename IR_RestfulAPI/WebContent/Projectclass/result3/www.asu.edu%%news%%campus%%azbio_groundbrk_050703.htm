May 7, 2003

Ceremony points to institute's impact; speakers laud AzBio's promise

It was a day of ceremony and celebration as leaders from ASU and around the state gathered to mark the official groundbreaking of the Arizona Biodesign Institute April 29. The event honored both the start of the project and the future promise it holds for ASU and the state.

"In spite of the state's severe budget shortfall, we are here today building our future - not wringing our hands over our problems, but planning for tomorrow and positioning ASU and the state of Arizona to be leaders in an exciting field," said Gail Howard, the governor's policy adviser for economic development."As Governor Napolitano has said, for too long Arizona has been a big state with small ideas. Today we are a big state with a big idea, an idea that we can be a world leader in bioscience. Today we are laying the groundwork toward that success, and how exciting it is to be here and be a part of it."

The ceremony drew more than 150 participants from around campus, including several state legislators and other state dignitaries. The event was hosted by Jonathan Fink, ASU's vice president for Research & Economic Affairs, and included speakers Frank AtLee, AzBio advisory board chairman, Charles Arntzen, AzBio's founding director, and Howard, on behalf of Gov. Janet Napolitano, who had a previous engagement out of town. ASU President Michael Crow also spoke during the event, delivering his vision for the institute and announcing the selection of George Poste to head AzBio.

The new building, slated to open in fall 2004, is the first phase of a four-building biodesign corridor, slated as the eastern gateway to campus. The $69 million, 170,000 square-foot building will include flexible modular lab space to allow for rapid changes in the facility's use. The complex will house researchers and students working at the intersections of biology, chemistry, physics, math and engineering, developing pharmaceuticals and medical devices for both the developed and developing worlds.

"Even though ASU is one of youngest of the world's research universities, it has made a name for itself by being flexible, innovative and interdisciplinary," Fink said. "This complex reflects those same qualities that distinguish ASU."

AtLee, chairman of the board of the Monsanto Company, a leading plant biotechnology and genomics company, said AzBio and the new facility would greatly enhance the university's ability to partner with industry.

"Research partnerships with industry are directly focused on commercial applications that will drive new research opportunities for AzBio and are a win-win for ASU and Arizona," he said. "This new facility will demonstrate to potential industry partners that we have human and facility resources to deliver on our research commitments."
Charles Arntzen, the founding director of the institute, said the new facility will help answer the need for new high-tech research space, felt by faculty members across campus.

"As we plan for the four-building campus on this site, I can foresee an incredible spurt of new scientific activity as the space crunch is resolved, and we are only constrained by our imaginations," he said.

He said even though the work is just beginning on the facility, AzBio is already helping generate success for the state in terms of partnerships with industry and other researchers. He said the institute is working closely with Jeffrey Trent of the Translational Genomics Institute to pursue new federal grants and recruit top scientists in genomics.

"I'm sure this is just the tip of the iceberg as AzBio continues to grow and our collaborations in the community expand and mature."
