The Libraries' External Environment:
Opportunities and Challenges

Early in the strategic planning process, thirty-six members of the Strategic Planning Council conducted an intensive scan of the Libraries' external environment to determine the factors that are having a significant impact on the library and its ability to provide high quality services to the ASU community. The factors identified can be grouped under three major headings:

  • developments in electronic information technologies
  • diminishing fiscal resources
  • changes in the user community

Electronic Information Technologies: Opportunities and Threats


The burgeoning of electronic information technologies continues to present exciting opportunities and challenges to the ASU Libraries.

The key word in the arena of technology is more. More information resources available in electronic form. More demand for electronic information on the part of virtually all library users. More library users regularly accessing electronic information, including the library, from their own homes and offices. More interest in all information formats, partly stimulated by the astonishing growth of electronic resources. More opportunities to solve problems and streamline operations through technology.

The attractive promise of technology, however, comes with a new set of challenges. Many in the Libraries' service community have unrealistic expectations of technology: that it will solve all of their information problems; that it will replace the need to ask real live library staff for assistance; that it will obviate the need for more traditional (e.g., print) resources. In reality, traditional resources continue to be an essential means by which the Libraries provide services, and many library users will continue to need assistance in navigating the world of information.

Moreover, potentially difficult problems appear on the horizon. As costs for electronic information increase, the gap between the technological haves and have-nots in the user population is likely to grow. Current legal barriers to access (e.g., copyright, site licenses) may become even more difficult to surmount. Competition from other providers of electronic information (e.g., on the Internet) may degrade the quality of information services available to users as the value added by library professionals is removed. Finally, the ASU Libraries are not on the cutting edge of technology: to get there will cost, and even replacing an aging infrastructure will have its price tag.

Resources: Opportunities and Challenges


Resources available to the library for its operations and services remain static. In fact, given the chronic steep increases in the cost of virtually all library materials, including electronic resources, the Libraries' buying power is on a steady decline. Problems attributable to diminishing resources include low salaries, which cause problems in recruitment and retention; the growing obsolescence of buildings and equipment; the continued physical deterioration of library collections; and, of course, the declining ability to acquire materials.

This chronic situation has challenged the Libraries to find new solutions: doing more with less through strategies such as process improvement; investigating alternative funding sources; pursuing alternative service strategies, such as more substitution of access for ownership.

The User Community: Opportunities and Challenges


The Libraries' user community is rapidly becoming more and more diverse, more demanding of services. On any given day, the Libraries serve the needs of a broad range of users -- from the distinguished veteran scholar to the community member needing remedial help. The development of the East Campus promises to add to this diversity and to the challenge of being the only comprehensive academic research library in the vast Phoenix metropolitan area. The campus' emphasis on the undergraduate experience and on its Research I status provide welcome guidance in establishing priorities for library services. Finally, establishing partnerships in the user community --with businesses, with other educational institutions -- holds significant promise.
