April 23, 2003

ASU chosen for minority scholarship program

ASU is one of only 13 colleges in the United States chosen to participate in a new scholarship program to recruit, enroll and graduate minority engineering students. The university has been named a NACME Scholar Institution by the National Action Council for Minorities in Engineering, Inc., the nation's largest private source of scholarships for African American, American Indian and Latino engineering students.

More than 120 schools applied for the new program, according to Mary Anderson-Rowland, associate dean of the College of Engineering & Applied Sciences (CEAS).
ASU will receive 10 scholarships of $2,500 each for incoming minority engineering students who have both academic qualifications and financial need. More than 15 percent of minority engineering graduates since 1974 have received NACME scholarship support and are now leaders in government, industry and academia.

"In the last 11 years we have more than doubled our enrollment of undergraduate engineering majors from underrepresented minority groups, from 315 in 1991 to 771 this past fall," Anderson-Rowland says. "These enrollment numbers demonstrate our commitment. Receiving this scholarship grant is very big, as these scholarships usually have gone to minority institutions.

"We have very strong recruiting and support programs for underrepresented students, and a critical base of minority students to support them when they come here."

The engineering college has a number of summer programs, including a two-week residential Minority Summer Bridge program for admitted freshmen and a two-week Summer Transportation Institute, a residential camp for high school juniors and seniors also targeted at underrepresented minority students.

Freshman retention from the 2001 Minority Summer Bridge program was 93 percent at ASU, and 86 percent of the total remained in engineering, Anderson-Rowland says.
More than 700 young minority students from over 30 schools participate in annual design competitions through MESA (Mathematics Engineering Science Achievement). During the academic year hundreds of high school students come to campus for a day of tours and meetings with engineering students and faculty. Other summer programs are open to all students.

Support continues once students enroll, through the Freshman Year Experience program as well as a two-hour academic success class geared for minority engineering freshmen. The CEAS Center for Engineering Diversity & Retention offers engineering students nearly 100 events each semester, including workshops and speakers from industry.

ASU is one of the top 10 producers of American Indian engineering baccalaureate degrees in the country.

"Engineers create and design solutions to problems, to help people," says Anderson-Rowland. "The more diverse an engineering team, with people who think differently and have different experiences, the better solutions there will be. Engineering is a very creative profession and one of the best backgrounds you can have for being a contributing citizen."

The 13 schools chosen to participate represent geographic diversity as well as diversity in student population. They include the University of California San Diego, the University of Washington, University of Central Florida, Georgia Institute of Technology and the University of Illinois Chicago.
