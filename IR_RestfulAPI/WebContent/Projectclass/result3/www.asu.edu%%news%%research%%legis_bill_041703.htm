April 17, 2003

Arizona legislature introduces bill to stimulate economy through $400 million in university research infrastructure construction

Despite budget woes, a group of Arizona legislators are leading a bipartisan effort to stimulate the state economy by funding more than $400 million in high tech research infrastructure construction at Arizona State University (ASU) and the two other state universities: the University of Arizona and Northern Arizona University.

An 11th hour bill introduced in the Arizona House of Representatives April 16 by Speaker of the House Jake Flake, and Speaker Pro Tempore Bob Robson, and co-sponsored by more than half of the legislature, proposes to build these new research facilities during the next three years.

ASU President Michael Crow, who led the infrastructure initiative, said the project, if funded, would result in a significant return on investment for ASU and, ultimately, the state of Arizona.

The announcement came at a press conference at the Capitol where legislators and university presidents presented the plan.

For ASU, the bill will provide $185 million to build Phase 2, 3 and 4 of the Arizona BioDesign Institute, the School of Life Sciences building, and research facilities to house burgeoning biotech fields such as nanotechnology, materials science, information technology and bioinformatics. The university also plans to create a research center and integrated learning environment at ASU West, and an ASU East applied biological sciences research complex.

Crow believes these scientific fields will be the catalysts for economic expansion and opportunity.
"Arizona has to get in the game, and the most effective route for creating new and high value economic opportunity for its citizens is through increasing the research infrastructure and innovative capacity of its universities," said Crow. "This bipartisan effort is the right move at the right time to stimulate economic development throughout the state," Crow said.

Under the plan, ASU and UA would each receive $14.5 million and NAU would receive $5.9 million for lease purchase financing on the facilities beginning in 2004. No money from the state's general fund would be used to finance the projects until 2008. The proposed financing mechanism allows the universities' projects to be exempt from construction sales tax to bridge payments on lease purchase financing.

In addition to substantial long-term economic benefits, Robson said there are immediate economic benefits too. It is estimated that construction spending will produce 6,000 new jobs and create $630 million in total immediate economic impact. Additionally, the projects will generate about $28 million in state, county and city revenues.

"This bill promises to supercharge the economy and the state education system as well," Robson said.
House Speaker Jake Flake said economic stimulus generated from the construction of the buildings would help Arizona grow itself out of its current slump.

"Just because you are in hard times, is a farmer not going to plant corn?" asked Flake. "We can do it and we must, for the future of Arizona."

The bill has received wide legislative and gubernatorial support, as well as being embraced by the industry and business community and local city officials. The National Association of Industrial and Office Properties (NAIOP) provided substantial support for the measure, including hiring a lobbyist on behalf of the universities. NAIOP chapter President Craig Coppola said Arizona's two major universities currently are perceived as second-tier research institutions because they lack needed facilities. If they want to be on the first tier and attract more grants, especially from the National Science Foundation and the National Institutes of Health, Arizona's universities need more lab space, Coppola said.

District 17 Rep. Mark Thompson of Tempe said the bill could change the landscape of the Arizona economy by supercharging it with research performed by students under the direction of professors.

"Arizona's current economy is based on technology and tourism. Both sectors have been hit economically hard recently. When we looked at this plan it made sense to us to fuel a new sector that can add to the state's job base. We have all the essential ingredients right now to make this work except the infrastructure. This bill will provide the buildings. This is an important first step in enabling the universities to move closer to an entrepreneurial model," said Thompson.
