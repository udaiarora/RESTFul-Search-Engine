Doctoral Students Receive Awards from the Division of Graduate Studies

E. Billionniere R. Gholkar P. Harms L. Myles J. Parker I. Rodriguez M. Soto J. Tang

Doctoral studies may be one of the most challenging endeavors a person undertakes in their lifetime. Completing a dissertation can often take several years and requires extensive research that it is both time-consuming and costly. For ten ASU doctoral students, the research process became a bit more manageable after receiving fellowships from Division of Graduate Studies (DGS) that enable them to focus exclusively on their studies and research for an entire year.

Five outstanding students received the DGS Doctoral Enrichment Fellowship, a $15,000 award given to Ph.D. candidates who contribute to the diversity of ASU's student population. Another five doctoral students were awarded the DGS Dissertation Fellowship, a $17,000 award designed to support them in the final stages of their post-candidacy doctoral work. The students are the inaugural recipients of these two fellowships, which DGS introduced last year to recruit and retain top Ph.D. students and increase diversity.

Patricia Harms, a DGS Doctoral Enrichment Fellow, began her dissertation on the history of Guatemalan women in 1999. Harms said the fellowship has helped her tremendously because it has allowed her to focus exclusively on her project. "The fellowship has allowed me to spend time with my family while maintaining a strict writing schedule," said Harms. "It is truly a gift that I needed in this last year of my graduate career."

"This group represents the best and brightest of our doctoral students and these awards are a tribute to their hard work," said Maria T. Allison, Vice Provost and Dean of Graduate Studies.

The 2005–06 Doctoral Enrichment fellows are selected for their strong record of achievement and contribution to the diversity of ASU:
  • Elodie Vanessa Billionniere, a Ph.D. candidate in computer science with an emphasis in database systems, is interested in the advancement of minorities in the sciences and hopes to contribute to knowledge discovery and data mining research.

  • Rucheeta Gholkar is a Ph.D. candidate in educational leadership and policy studies. A former middle school teacher, her research interests include relationships between culture, class, schools, and equity.

  • Melinda Gonzales-Backen is a Ph.D. candidate in family and human development. Her research focuses on the development of ethnic identity among bi-ethnic adolescents.

  • Isel Rodriguez, a Ph.D. candidate in theatre and performance of the Americas, decided to pursue a doctoral degree to help preserve and prolong the art of theatre of her country, Puerto Rico, as a means of social change.

  • Mariela Soto is a Ph.D. candidate in geography. Her research involves mapping and modeling vegetation of the Southern Levant, with a focus on the reconstruction of pre-historical landscapes, in order to understand human impact on the land through time.

The 2005–06 DGS Dissertation fellows are selected for their scholarly promise and outstanding dissertation research:
  • Patricia Harms is a Ph.D. candidate in history and her dissertation focuses on the history of Guatemalan women from 1871 until 1954.

  • Lynette Myles, a Ph.D. candidate in English, will defend her dissertation, "Beyond Borders: Black Women, Space, and Female Subjectivity in African American Women's Narratives of Enslavement," in spring 2006.

  • John Parker is a Ph.D. candidate in sociology and his dissertation focuses on interdisciplinary research efforts involving the sociology of work and science. While at ASU, he's been involved in the National Science Foundation-sponsored Integrative Graduate Education and Research Training (IGERT) program in Urban Ecology.

  • Gregson Schachner is a Ph.D. candidate in anthropology whose dissertation, "Community Formation, Migration, and Social Transformation in Ancestral Puebloan Society," examines how small-scale societies cope with regional level social changes.

  • Jian Tang is a Ph.D. candidate in computer science and engineering. His dissertation, "Power-Efficient and Survivable Design for Multi-hop Wireless Networks," studies cross-layer design problems in wireless mesh and sensor networks for power-efficiency enhancement, survivability support and throughput improvement.
