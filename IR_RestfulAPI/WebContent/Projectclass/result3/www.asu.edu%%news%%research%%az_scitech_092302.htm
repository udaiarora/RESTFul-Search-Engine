Study examines state's economic competitiveness in science and technology

Every day brings new evidence of an extraordinary 50-state race for high-wage jobs and prominence in the knowledge economy. A new study from Morrison Institute for Public Policy at Arizona State University shows how Arizona is doing in this intense competition compared to four chief rivals: California, Colorado, Utah and Washington.

Morrison Institute's 38-page report, titled Economic Development Via Science and Technology: How Can Arizona Improve Its Standing?, answers some of the questions business and political leaders and economic development practitioners have about Arizona's economic strengths and weaknesses and what the future might hold for the state.

Case studies for California, Colorado, Utah, Washington and Arizona focus on current state trends in economic development, tax and fiscal affairs, education and high tech business development programs. "A team of long-time observers of the Arizona economy analyzed how these leading states in science and technology got where they are today," says Rob Melnick, ASU's associate vice president for economic affairs and director of Morrison Institute. "Our findings shed light on Arizona's current readiness to prosper from economic development based on technological innovation."

This information comes at the right time, according to Morrison Institute board chair Cathy McKee, senior vice president and director, Strategic Business Services, General Dynamics Decision Systems. "To move ahead economically, we must start with a baseline of where Arizona stands in comparison to the recognized leaders in science and technology in the western region. This report provides new knowledge that will be useful immediately to economic development practitioners, policy makers and high technology employers."

As the report shows, the differences and similarities among California, Colorado, Utah and Washington are readily apparent. All four show the effects of sustained governmental funding, state and local human and capital infrastructure commitments, private investment, catalytic events and strong leadership. For Arizona, these lessons from leading states suggest the need for collective action and for state and regional policy makers to play important roles in developing the relationships needed to compete for a future that benefits from science and technology. Economic Development Via Science and Technology: How Can Arizona Improve Its Standing? concludes with five strategies for a high tech future in Arizona:

. Lasting, enthusiastic leadership that recognizes the value of science and technology
. The right message and strategy to convey the urgency of this matter
. Investment in the creation and sustenance of first-tier research institutions
. More and better mechanisms to improve the transfer of ideas into the market place
. A belief that the state can be a leader in science and technology

From the research, it seems clear that the states that are most likely to prosper will involve leadership from all sectors and make smart investments from many sources. Our results show that working together on economic development and science and technology will have to be a high priority for Arizona to compete in today's economic race," concluded Rob Melnick.

For a copy of Economic Development via Science and Technology: How Can Arizona Improve Its Standing? visit (www.morrisoninstitute.org) or call (480) 965-4525.
