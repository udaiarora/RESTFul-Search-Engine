PHOTOS - Monsoon Research

Arizona State University's Global Institute of Sustainability is leading research in monsoon storm development over the Santa Catalina mountains, north of Tucson, AZ. With the help of the University of Arizona and the University of Wyoming, researchers will gather data which will allow them to build accurate representations of the very beginnings of the monsoon storms. At the Windy Point Vista on the Coronado National Forest, north of Tucson and about half way up Mt. Lemmon, ASU graduate student Bryan Paris monitors data being sent from weather balloons and the University of Wyoming airplane flying overhead. The researchers use satellite links (the dish on top of the truck he is sitting in) to look at data and communicate with each other via internet chat.

At the Windy Point Vista on the Coronado National Forest, north of Tucson and about half way up Mt. Lemmon, ASU graduate student Bryan Paris prepares a weather balloon for launch. Equipped with recording instruments and radio transmitters, weather data is sent to the researchers as the balloon rises.

Professor Joe Zehnder (left) of Arizona State University and John Nangles of the University of Arizona, evaluate some of the data being gathered on monsoon storm development in the projects command center in the Physics and Atmospheric Sciences Building at the University of Arizona in Tucson.

  

The University of Wyoming is supplying the heavily instrumented Beech King Air airplane, funded by the National Science Foundation, for the project. The plane is piloted by Tom Drew (left) and the flight is derected by Rick Damiani. Photo by Larry Oolman, Senior Research Scientist with the University of Wyoming.

From left, Brad Busby, ASU Grad Student in Climatology, Brian Pettegrew, from University of Missouri-working for NCAE (National Center for Atmospheric Research), and Bryan Paris, ASU Grad Student in Climatology, look at the data being sent back from a recently launched weather balloon and from the University of Wyoming airplane. The researchers are able to sell the data in real time and communicate via Internet chat. They were launching balloons from Windy Point Vista on the Coronado National Forest, north of Tucson and about half way up Mt. Lemmon.
