December 13, 2004

ASU’s Bauer Wins Prestigious Prize for Surface Physics

Arizona State University Distinguished Research Professor of Physics Ernst Bauer has been awarded the prestigious Davisson-Germer Prize in Atomic or Surface Physics for 2005 by the American Physical Society.

The $5,000 dollar prize is given annually by the society, the professional organization of American physics, “to recognize and encourage outstanding work in atomic physics or surface physics.” The prize was established in 1965 by AT&T Bell Laboratories (now Bell Labs, Lucent Technologies). It is awarded in even numbered years for outstanding work in atomic physics and odd numbered years for outstanding work in surface physics.

According to APS, Bauer received the award in recognition of his “contributions to the science of thin-film nucleation and growth, and for the invention of Low Energy Electron Microscope.”

Bauer is internationally known for inventing the Low Energy Electron Microscope (LEEM) in 1962. In the late eighties/early nineties he extended the LEEM technique in two directions by developing two new surface microscopy methods: Spin-Polarized Low Energy Electron Microscopy (SPLEEM) and Spectroscopic Photo Emission and Low Energy Electron Microscopy (SPELEEM).

The LEEM records images of surface atoms by illuminating them with very low energy electrons -- between 0 and 100eV (as opposed to 200 to 400 kV for High Resolution Electron Microscopes), hence the term "low-energy." A special feature of the LEEM is the capability to alternatively image the low-energy electron diffraction pattern of a surface or use some of the diffracted electrons to form an image of the surface with a lateral resolution as low as 10 nanometers and with atomic depth resolution.

Unlike other surface imaging techniques which usually require some form of scanning, the LEEM uses a static electron beam which lends itself naturally to real-time imaging with an on-line video system. The LEEM is therefore ideally suitable for observation of dynamic processes on the surface, such as phase transitions, epitaxial growth, chemisorption, desorption, and sublimation.

Ernst Bauer joined the ASU Department of Physics and Astronomy as a full time faculty member in 1996 with the title of Distinguished Research Professor. He received his MS (1953) and PhD (1955), both in physics, from Munich University, Germany. In 1958 he moved to the Michelson Laboratory in China Lake, California, where he became Head of the Crystal Physics Branch and U.S. citizen. In 1969 he accepted the position of Professor and Director of the Physics Institute at the Technical University Clausthal in Germany.

Bauer is the author of about 400 publications, including 80 reviews and a book on electron diffraction. He is the recipient of the Gaede Prize of the German Vacuum Society (1988), of the M.W. Welch Award of the American Vacuum Society (1992) and of the Niedersachsenpreis Prize for Science (1994). He was elected a Member of the Goettingen Academy of Science in 1989, Fellow of the American Physical Society in 1991 and Fellow of the American Vacuum Society in 1994.

For more information on the award, see <http://www.aps.org/praw/davisson/index.cfm> . For more information on Bauer, see <http://phyastweb.la.asu.edu/directory/facinfo.asp?LstName=Bauer&FrstName=Ernst> and <http://phy.asu.edu/homepages/bauer/biography.htm> .
