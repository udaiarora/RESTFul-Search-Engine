January 6, 2003

Center to study role of religion in conflict

Violent global clashes, differing visions of stem cell research and disagreements over school prayer are among the issues that will be examined at a new Center for the Study of Religion & Conflict being created at ASU.

Among the first of its kind in the nation, the center will enhance research and education on the role of religion in public controversies around the world. It will focus on the fundamental issues driving public disagreements and explore the complex roles of diverse religious traditions in contemporary conflicts.

"The role of religion in conflict has become especially prominent with recent world events," says ASU President Michael Crow. "Religious-based conflict exists in areas as diverse as foreign policy, international law, teaching and learning in our schools, science and technology research and application, news coverage and political ideology.

"The growing recognition of religion's enormous role in conflict and public affairs around the world clearly indicates the urgent need for the creation of the center."

The center will create a variety of seminars, conferences, lectures and workshops that bring together scholars from all over the world with ASU faculty and students and members of the local community. It will also support a fellowship program and will help develop new courses and new units in existing courses. Initial funding will come from a seed grant from the ASU Office of Research & Economic Affairs. Plans are to have the center operating by January.

"We plan to bring together the voices of professors from many different disciplines," says Linell Cady, ASU religious studies professor who will direct the center. "We hope to establish exchange programs for faculty from other parts of the world, so we can explore the different relationships between the religious and secular worlds. Our agenda will be a global one, as ASU has a very strong religious studies faculty with expertise in many religions and cultures."

The academic study of religion is an American and western European phenomenon and can be a force for an open discussion and a greater understanding of its influence in various societies, Cady says.

"Our failure to recognize how religion shapes identities and values in other parts of the world has contributed to our lack of understanding of the dynamics of other societies, and our relationship with them," says Cady. "This is an issue of enormous importance."
