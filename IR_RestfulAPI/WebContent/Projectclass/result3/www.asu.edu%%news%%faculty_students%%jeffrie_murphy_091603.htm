September 16, 2003

Professor's distinctive voice discusses wrongdoing, forgiveness

When it comes to popular views on both vindictiveness and forgiving, Jeffrie Murphy, ASU Regents Professor of law, philosophy and religious studies, considers himself a skeptic.

Forgiveness is not always a prescription for moral and mental health; vindictiveness is not always irrational and immoral. Murphy's philosophy allows nuances of both.
It is in this context that he has written "Getting Even - Forgiveness and Its Limits," a book that takes a sensitive, unconventional look at the popular topic. He will talk about his latest book and sign copies at 7 p.m., Sept. 24, at Changing Hands bookstore, 6428 S. McClintock Dr., Tempe.

In the book, he proposes that vindictive passions actually deserve a more legitimate place in our emotional, social, legal and religious lives than we currently recognize, and that forgiveness should be more selectively granted.

He grounds his views on careful analysis of the nature of forgiveness, an understanding of the psychology of anger and resentment and an appreciation of the ethical issues of self-respect and self-defense.

"Jeffrie Murphy has written a wonderful and sensitive book on an almost forbidden topic, the topic of revenge," says Robert Solomon, Quincy Lee Centennial Professor and Distinguished Teaching Professor at the University of Texas at Austin. "But it is also a book about forgiveness, and it is striking a judicious balance between these two that makes Murphy's book such a challenge and a success."

Forgiveness is a very "in" topic in bookstores, Murphy points out, to the extent that many titles are almost messianic in their celebration of forgiveness as a cure for a variety of psychological, moral and spiritual ills. A movement of "forgiveness boosterism" has grown up and become extreme.

"Although I am in no sense an enemy of forgiveness - indeed I celebrate it in many contexts - my writings on forgiveness have received some attention because I am one of the few voices out there with anything negative to say about it," says Murphy.

"Hasty and uncritical forgiveness, prior to repentance and atonement on the part of the wrongdoer, can sometimes be - not a virtue - but rather the vice of servility. The person who forgives in this way may be failing to show a proper respect for himself, a proper respect for the moral and legal order that makes civilized life possible, and even a proper respect for the wrongdoer as a responsible agent."

Murphy explores the dangers of hasty forgiveness in morality, law, psychotherapy and religion, closing with a defense of forgiveness that is partially religious in nature and arguing that such forgiveness should always be cautious and only selectively granted.

The book was published by Oxford University Press, which is sponsoring the book-signing event.

The ASU College of Law is developing a concentration in law and philosophy, and Murphy will contribute to the program along with Dean Patricia White, Law Professors Patrick Brennan, James Nickel, Mary Sigler and James Weinstein, as well as Michael White of the Philosophy Department.
