ASU undergraduate McCarthy claims top humanities honor

Humanities students sometimes take a back seat to those in the sciences in prominent national scholarships.

But Erin McCarthy, an ASU senior in English literature, has won the Andrew W. Mellon Fellowship in Humanistic Studies, a major award given to exceptionally promising students in the humanities.

The fellowship covers full graduate tuition and fees for the first year of doctoral study and also a stipend of $17,500.

McCarthy, who is from Glendale, is the only Arizona university student chosen out of about 90 awardees. She will attend Ohio State University in the fall.

“Mellon Fellowships are among the most prestigious and hotly contested national fellowships for humanists, and the lion’s share of them go to students from very prestigious schools,” says Curtis Perry, associate professor of English who directed McCarthy’s senior thesis. “It is impressive company for Erin to find herself in, and it bodes well for her academic future.”

Perry is one of several faculty members who noted McCarthy’s passion for learning. Since entering ASU on a President’s Scholarship, she consistently has shown an inquisitive nature and a degree of commitment rarely seen in an undergraduate.

“Erin is able to see quickly through critical debates to the heart of complex and controversial subjects,” Perry says. “The thing I admire about her, aside from her talent, is her disposition toward knowledge. Instead of just taking notes on what the professor says, she poses questions for herself and then takes steps to arrive at her own answers.”

Mark Lussier, associate professor of English, says McCarthy stood out in his literature classes for her insightful and probing analysis, even as a sophomore. She sought him out during office hours to discuss the writings more deeply, and eagerly read complex works that weren’t required.

“Erin is one of the best-prepared undergraduates I have had the pleasure to teach across the last decade at ASU,” Lussier says. “What impressed me most was her understanding of the demands made on those seeking to teach at the university level. She has now read three papers at graduate student conferences, and her commitment to complete her doctorate has only intensified.”

McCarthy acquired a love of teaching when she helped develop the Writing Center program at the Barrett Honors College and began tutoring other honors students, an activity she has continued for three years.

She also has lived in a freshman residence hall to tutor students in writing, psychology, economics, French, Spanish and math as part of the Freshman Year Experience program.

She is president of the Associated Undergraduate Scholars of English and will graduate summa cum laude in May.
