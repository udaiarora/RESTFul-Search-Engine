Designing the Future for Multidisciplinary Graduate Education


Arizona State University is at a very important juncture in its academic history. Now led by President Michael Crow, ASU is moving boldly and expeditiously to build a framework for the New American University framed with design imperatives that focus on a range of transformational principles including responsibility for active engagement in societal change, an environment rich in knowledge entrepreneurship, Pasteur?s principle of use-inspired research, intellectual fusion, and transnational and transcultural engagement (www.asu.edu/president/newamericanuniversity). Within these design imperatives are major interdisciplinary and transdisciplinary initiatives and structures that will have long-lasting effects on ASU?s intellectual community including the ways faculty conduct research, the ways ASU academic units build curricula, and the ways in which we interface, as scholars and educators, with our local, regional, national and global communities. It is within this dynamic of transformational movement that the concept of an Interdisciplinary Graduate Academy was developed.

The Interdisciplinary Graduate Academy (IGA) is the center for advanced interdisciplinary and transdisciplinary intellectual, professional, and community-based activities for graduate students. Students will not only be trained to be entrepreneurial, but they will also actively be a part of the process of attracting private and commercial funding. The IGA will be the center to facilitate multidisciplinary research activities that require complex partnerships and intellectual fusion between researchers in disparate fields such as the social and life sciences, ecology and the environment, bio-, electrical- and materials-, and computer engineering, mathematics, etc. To facilitate these efforts the IGA will serve as a home for a series of Interdisciplinary Graduate Education Programs (iGEP?s) and degrees that are in the planning process. The IGA will provide a home for students to interact at a whole new level unprecedented in current graduate education.

Preparing Future Faculty (PFF) and Preparing Future Professionals (PFP)
Preparing Future Faculty (PFF) is a professional development program for doctoral, MFA, and MSD students who are preparing for academic careers at institutions of higher education. It is designed to prepare graduate students for the multiple roles they will face as faculty members, and to allow them to understand how these roles and expectations can vary in different campus settings. The Division of Graduate Studies launched the PFP program to assist doctoral students interested in pursuing nonacademic professions. The goal of this program is to familiarize doctoral students with various nonacademic career tracks and help them to develop skills to successfully pursue a wide range of career opportunities.

Responsible Conduct of Research (RCR)
The mission of the RCR programs is to raise the depth of understanding about research ethics at ASU and to integrate and internalize responsible conduct in research as the goal of this cooperative effort between the Arizona State University's Division of Graduate Studies, the Bioethics Program, the Office for Research and Sponsored Projects Administration (ORSPA), the Consortium for Science Policy Outcomes, and the Lincoln Center for Applied Ethics.

Graduate Partners in Science Education (GPSE) (sols.asu.edu/sols_news/21_news_06.php)
Graduate Partners in Science Education created by graduate students Nathan (Nate) Morehouse and Jon Davis in the School of Life Sciences is changing the face of science education in urban public schools. Davis and Morehouse?s brainchild aims to bolster science and technology education for students from under-represented and economically challenged groups in downtown Phoenix. This year, their program partnered eight graduate student mentors from School of Life Sciences with teachers, Karen Griff and Keith Brazier, and 45 seventh- and eighth-grade students from Phoenix Preparatory Academy. The IGA will help facilitate this activity and to broaden its scope to include non-science disciplines.

Leadership Curriculum
Interdisciplinary research requires participants to have leadership skills that allow them to communicate with others from diverse and multiple disciplines. This is a skillset that can be learned. The object of this curriculum is to guide students through the unique challenges they will face in building interdisciplinary teams and to equip them with the skills and tools needed to be successful.

Intellectual Entrepreneurship in Graduate Education
Entrepreneurship is about developing new ideas and making them work. This does not necessarily mean that it must lead to commercialism. Thus graduate education and the activities of graduate students are inherently entrepreneurial. The IGA will host an occasional lecture series that focuses on what it means to be an entrepreneur in a university setting.

Sustainability
Issues that are related to sustainability can touch a very wide range of disciplines, thus such initiatives typically need to be trans/inter-disciplinary and comprehensive in nature. Therefore, DGS can play a critical role in coordinating sustainability efforts of units and faculty by being an information broker for graduate level initiatives, by helping develop graduate academic programs that cut across multiple units and multiple degrees, and by helping to create a culture of sustainability through professional development opportunities for graduate students.
