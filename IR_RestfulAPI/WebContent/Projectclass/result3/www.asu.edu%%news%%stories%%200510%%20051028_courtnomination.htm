October 28, 2005

Watson: Mier's withdrawn nomination puts president in tough spot

EDITOR'S NOTE: President George Bush nominated Samuel Alito, who has served on the 3rd U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals since 1990, to replace Sandra Day O'Connor on the Supreme Court Oct. 31.

The withdrawn nomination of Harriet Miers to the Supreme Court has set up a politically thorny situation for President George W. Bush, according to George Watson, an ASU professor of political science in ASU’s Walter Cronkite School of Journalism.

The withdrawal of Miers to replace Justice Sandra Day O’Connor is a reminder of just how potentially contentious the current political setting is, Watson says.

"President Bush appears to have underestimated the passion of the conservatives for replacing O’Connor with someone distinctly more conservative, especially on those social issues of abortion, religion and equal protection on which O’Connor was often the fifth vote for a liberal majority,” Watson says. “He also overestimated the esteem in which he is held by various opinion leaders on the right, who were singularly unimpressed with the nominee and with assurances by the president that Miers would be the kind of Supreme Court justice they seek.”

Watson, an expert on the Supreme Court nomination process, has written numerous pieces on the process and has been a consultant on a video produced by the Congressional Research Service for use in educating senators and their staff on the nomination and confirmation process.

Watson says that, if anything, the pressure on the president has intensified.

"The success by the right in securing the withdrawal will likely increase their expectations that the new nominee will have impeccable credentials and a performance record with which conservatives are comfortable,” he says. “In the meantime, Democrats have already fired warnings shots suggesting they are prepared to oppose a nominee who would roll back what they perceive as gains in civil liberties and rights over the years.”

For Bush, it’s back to the drawing board – and a likely revisiting of the list of potential nominees with which he and his staff are quite familiar.

"It should mean a new nomination will come soon, although the Bush administration would be wise to vet potential nominees through key leaders on both the right and left if it wishes to avoid controversy,” Watson says.
