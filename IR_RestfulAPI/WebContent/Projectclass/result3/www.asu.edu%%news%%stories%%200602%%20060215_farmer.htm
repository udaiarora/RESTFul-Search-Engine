February 15, 2006

National Academy of Engineering taps Farmer for membership

Another of ASU’s Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering faculty members has been elected to the prestigious National Academy of Engineering.

Richard Farmer becomes the 11th among the school’s teachers and researchers to earn NAE membership, considered one of the highest distinctions in engineering.

It honors those who have made outstanding contributions to engineering research and education, advancing traditional engineering fields and pioneering new technology.

Farmer is the third member of the Fulton School’s Department of Electrical Engineering to be elected to the academy. His colleagues say the recognition is long overdue.

“It’s a testament to his lifetime of achievements in both the educational and industrial components of the electric power field,” says Stephen Phillips, professor and department chair.

Farmer was a principal engineer with the Arizona Public Service Co. (APS), the state’s largest electric utility, for almost 30 years. In that time, he led projects that developed technology to improve the capacities, efficiency and reliability of electrical power generation and transmission systems.

The techniques have become industry standards in the field of high-voltage energy systems, says Vijay Vittal, an Ira A. Fulton Chair professor in electrical engineering.

Vittal lauds Farmer as “the world’s foremost authority” on some of the methods and technologies used by power utilities to detect, analyze and remedy system problems.

The NAE cited the far-reaching impact of Farmer’s accomplishments in power systems engineering as reason why he merited membership.

Farmer has been bringing that expertise into ASU classrooms and research laboratories as a part-time faculty member since 1966, two years after earning his master’s degree at the university.

“He brings a unique perspective to the students” because he uses problems faced by the power industry – and the solutions devised to remedy them – as teaching tools, says Gerald Heydt, a Regents’ Professor in electric engineering.

He also has been valuable as a mentor to students and resource for fellow faculty members.

Farmer’s extensive industry contacts – from major executives in the power industry to experts in various facets of electrical engineering – have aided many of the department’s educational and research efforts, Heydt says.

Acknowledgment of his contributions by colleagues at ASU and engineers with whom he worked at APS is as much or more satisfying than gaining NAE membership, Farmer says.

“It’s a big thing, but it’s just frosting on the cake,” he says. “Awards are nice, but it’s the joy of a rewarding career that has the greatest meaning for me.”

But Farmer’s election to the NAE is especially meaningful to the Department of Electrical Engineering.

“It contributes significantly to the recognition, reputation and visibility of all of our programs,” Phillips says.
