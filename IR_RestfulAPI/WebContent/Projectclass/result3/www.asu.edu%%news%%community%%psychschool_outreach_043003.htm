April 30, 2003

ASU school psychology program launches new outreach venture with local schools

The College of Education's School Psychology Program at ASU has launched an outreach venture in collaboration with local school districts to promote the advancement of school psychology as a science and a professional discipline.

Conceptualized and developed primarily through the efforts of David Wodrich, associate professor of psychology in education, the School Psychology Sharing Program (SPSP) provides school-based psychologists with access to recent research findings, university-based psychologists with enhanced access to practice issues and students with multiple role models. It is a forum in which to investigate role definitions and practice methods, and a setting where experts from various disciplines can speak.

"The challenge for school psychology training programs, such as ASU's, is to train true scientist-practitioners. This program strives to bring science and practice face-to-face," Wodrich said.

Participants in the SPSP education programs include all interested members of the community. Program material, however, is specifically targeted to psychologists practicing in school districts in metropolitan Phoenix; school psychology trainers at ASU; school psychology students at ASU; and school psychology interns and post-doctoral students receiving training in metropolitan Phoenix.

"As academics we have an obligation to share information with the community. As trainers of school psychologists we must assure that our students learn about issues that matter to practicing school psychologists and educators," Wodrich said. "The program was designed to offer something to the university, local educational and psychological, and student communities."

"The School Psychology Sharing Program is an effort that is representative of my goals and those of ASU President Michael Crow," says College of Education Dean Eugene Garcia, "to ensure that the College of Education makes a difference in the lives of the citizens of Greater Phoenix and the state of Arizona through social engagement and community embeddedness of its people and programs."

The initial program consists of semi-monthly, free, 90-minute professional issues presentations by members of one of the targeted school psychology constituent groups. "Programs emphasize interaction, informality, and mutual learning; their style is similar to the 'grand rounds' concept used in medical education," said Mary Stafford, associate professor of psychology in education with the College of Education. "This program is the first step in a long-term plan to create closer links between ASU's School Psychology Training Program and local, school-based professionals."

ASU and the Tempe Elementary District are sponsoring the initial series of presentations jointly. Presentations are open to all members of the school psychology community: practitioners, students and trainers. These free programs consist of a 90-minute presentation, discussion and time for socializing.

The first presentation, which focused on the uses of curriculum-based assessment for school psychologists, was conducted on March 27 by Jennifer Ostrom, a school psychologist with the Tempe Elementary School District. The series continued on April 10 with a presentation titled "Preschool evaluations: Issues of identification related to low incidence or health-related disorders." A third presentation scheduled for May 1 will explore the ethical and practice issues in the psychoeducational assessment of linguistically diverse students.
