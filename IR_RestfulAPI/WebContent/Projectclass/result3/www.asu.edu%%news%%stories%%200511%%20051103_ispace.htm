November 3, 2005

InnovationSpace partners with Flexible Display Center

InnovationSpace and the Industrial Design department are teaming up with the Flexible Design Center/MacroTechnology Works (FDC/MTW) at ASU to investigate potential applications of display technologies in the development of products to assist emergency medical service (EMS) teams.

“The goal of the project is to create new products that help save people’s lives, while finding suitable applications for technologies being developed at ASU,” says Prasad Boradkar, co-director of InnovationSpace and an assistant professor of industrial design.

Student teams are working with scientists and engineers from FDC/MTW and other centers on campus to explore how emerging technologies can improve the task performance of emergency medical technicians.

Local leaders in EMS education and practice also have joined the project, providing students with firsthand knowledge of the difficult tasks required of emergency medical technicians and the resources – from terminology to equipment – typically used to perform them.

“Through the students’ collective efforts to understand the challenges facing EMS, they are shaping its future,” says Orlando Alcordo Jr., a paramedic educator with PMT Ambulance who has been working closely with the student teams.

Students are enthusiastic about the experience and grateful for the opportunity to conduct research that reveals possibilities for products.

“I never thought going to a 911 call center or interviewing the public information officer at the Tempe Police Department would be so fun,” says Spencer Dodge, a senior majoring in industrial design. “We have interviewed everyone from nurses to emergency room technicians, fire fighters, paramedics – the list goes on. We have gained a lot of insight into how they do their jobs, and what methods and practices can be improved.”

In the past two years, socially responsible, collaborative projects have become the foundation of InnovationSpace and the Industrial Design program. Last year, students worked with ASU’s Center for Cognitive Ubiquitous Computing to further develop the iCARE-Reader, a device that makes printed text accessible to people with visual impairments.

InnovationSpace is a transdisciplinary program at ASU that supports and accelerates the transfer of new product design concepts that create value for business while also contributing to a socially responsible and sustainable future. For more information about InnovationSpace, visit the Web site ( www.innovationspace.org).
