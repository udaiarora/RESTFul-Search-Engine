Re:            ASU EMPLOYEE HEALTH INSURANCE REPORT

 
Executive Summary       In 1999, the Arizona State University Academic Senate appointed an Ad Hoc
Committee on Health Insurance to review health insurance benefits available to
university employees. That committee surveyed faculty, academic professionals, service
professionals, and classified staff asking questions about the plan selected, the reasons for
selection, preferences for benefits, and identification of additional benefits for which the
employee would be willing to pay more.

    The Ad Hoc Committee identified two goals. First, the committee wanted to raise
university awareness of the issues surrounding the adequacy and availability of employee
health insurance benefits. Second, the committee hoped to identify a variety of
reasonably priced health care plans from among which employees could choose in order
to meet different major needs.

    ASU surveyed benefits-eligible employees twice in 1999 with regard to health
insurance benefits. Employees were divided into the following categories: Faculty,
Classified Staff, Academic Professional, Administrative, and Service Professional. At the
Time, ASU employees had the option of participating in the following plans: CIGNA
HMO, United (Health Partners) DA HMO, United (Health Partners) PCP HMO,
Intergroup HMO, Intergroup Indemnity Plus, Intergroup Medical Savings Account, and
PacifiCare HMO. Employees were asked questions about their current coverage and
about factors that would influence future choices.

    Survey results indicate that both cost and coverage influenced the plans chosen.
The majority of employees surveyed indicated a willingness to pay more for greater
freedom of choice in specialists. Faculty expressed a willingness to pay more for greater
freedom of choice in primary care physician. Faculty, Academic Professionals and
Service Professionals were willing to pay more for greater freedom of choice in out-of-
area benefits. The survey also revealed that although employees generally report having
received adequate information about their plans, many did not understand the specifics of
their benefits. The 1999 surveys did not directly address amounts of co-pays or domestic
partner benefits. However, the latter was addressed in the recent University Work/Life
Task Force report.

    After extensive study regarding benefits, review of state and university history
with regard to health care packages and consideration of the survey results, the committee
reached three conclusions:

                    1.    ASU faculty and staff are not well informed about choosing plans or about
                           the processes for appealing decisions made under individual plans. The
                           ASU Benefits Office has an opportunity to play a more active role in
                           educating ASU employees about the availability of options and employee
                           rights under each plan and in facilitating employee appeals.

                    2.    Health insurance preferences of campus employees may differ in
                            important ways from preferences of state employees generally. For
                            example, university employees indicate they want greater choices of and
                            access to physicians and specialists. They may also need greater out-of-
                            area benefits to accommodate travel associated with research, professional
                            development and sabbatical leave.

                    3.     The ASU Benefits Office should become more pro-active in the selection
                            of providers at the state level. Ideally, ASU should actively contribute to
                            the development of the Request for Proposals and have a seat on the
                            selection committee that reviews proposals. The appointed faculty
                            representative should possess the appropriate expertise to represent
                            completely the university?s interests with respect to health insurance
                            benefits.

    Reviewing these results in the context of the ongoing discussions with the
Department of Administration regarding the current RFP, we recommend that the
university take these additional steps to address employee concerns:

1.  Enhance the role of the Academic Senate through the Personnel Committee to ensure
     the university plays a more active role in selecting benefits plans;
2.  Reorganize and empower the university?s Health Benefits Committee to play a more
     meaningful role in the process; and
3.  Co-ordinate and integrate health insurance issues into the university?s work-life agenda.
