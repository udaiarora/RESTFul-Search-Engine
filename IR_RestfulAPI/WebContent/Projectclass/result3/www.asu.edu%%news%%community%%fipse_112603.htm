November 26, 2003

Program helps students, teachers navigate world of writing

The ASU English Department and Sam Fees Middle School in Tempe are partnering to give students and teachers a leg up on the world of writing.

The effort, supported by grants from the Fund for the Improvement of Postsecondary Education (FIPSE) Writing Coalition and Twenty-First Century, is building a link between ASU and the school for teacher training and collaboration as well as student achievement and tutoring. The ASU/Fees partnership is one of several nationally supported through the FIPSE program, and is receiving $12,000 yearly to fund student tutors, a writing center, speakers, workshops and other projects aimed at improving writing.

At the heart of the effort is the Fees Fire Writers Club, a student driven writing center linking Fees students with tutors from ASU, supported by the grants. The English education majors taking part in a Methods of Teaching Composition class, prepare lesson plans and help the students revise and prepare their works for possible publication. The writing club is an extension of a program originally developed through a Motorola Great Communities Grant.

ASU Assistant Professor of English James Blasingame, coordinator of the effort, says the twice weekly club caters to the interests of the students and helps build a genuine interest in writing, outside of a formal classroom setting.

"It gives the Fees students a chance to get excited about writing," says Blasingame. "And the ASU students claim this is the best preparation for teaching writing they could get."

For the teachers at the middle school, the program's benefit comes in the form of support. The grant provides opportunities for teachers at the school to meet weekly with Blasingame and other professors at ASU for training, workshops and other programs. Through the partnership, four of the Fees teachers were able to go to a recent state convention, and one was able to attend a national workshop for the FIPSE Writing Coalition.

"It's really about sharing resources," says Blasingame. "For the teacher in the trenches it can be difficult to find time or funding for training or to get the latest information. This helps create a link where we can share information."

The final major component of the grant is to bring guest speakers to the school for both the teachers and the students. Since September guest speakers have included ASU English Professors Don and Alleen Nilson, who presented the school's teachers with the latest in research on teaching vocabulary to students; 2003 Arizona One Book for Kids author Jack Gantos, who shared strategies for getting kids to write; and Arizona Native American writer, storyteller and artist Michael Lacapa who worked with 12 classes on writing personal narratives and assorted writing skills.
