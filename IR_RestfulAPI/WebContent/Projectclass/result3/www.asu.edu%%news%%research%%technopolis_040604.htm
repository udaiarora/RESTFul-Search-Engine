April 6, 2004

Emerging technologies build lasting impact in Valley business

ASU Technopolis, an education and training program managed by the university's Office of Economic Affairs, is ASU's leading edge program for technology and life science entrepreneurs in the greater Phoenix area and is quickly becoming a proven resource for fostering university-business partnerships and commercializing research.

Since its very recent launch only four months ago, it has already trained 86 entrepreneurs, engaged and connected 78 experts, and has assisted its entrepreneurs with submissions of $1.6 million in Small Business Innovation Research/Small Business Technology Transfer Research (SBIR/STTR) proposals.

On March 30 at a special engagement for business leaders, ASU President Michael Crow discussed the economic impact of the Technopolis program and how it is enabling local entrepreneurs to bring emerging technologies to market.

"Technopolis is a catalyst - it is helping to create an environment in the region where entrepreneurs, discoverers and venture capitalists come together," said Crow. "Technopolis demonstrates ASU's commitment to expanding its impact on the regional and state economy."

Rob Melnick, ASU's associate vice president for economic affairs, explained how this work and activity will help the Valley and state become a world-class competitor in technology and life science industries.

"Technopolis serves a niche market for early stage companies whose principals need more than a one-hour or half-day seminar on technology-business issues, but who don't have the time or don't need to complete a full degree program," stated Melnick.

Several technologies created by entrepreneurs who have recently participated in ASU Technopolis programs were showcased as examples of early stage products that have enormous economic potential for business opportunities and as revenue generators.

"The societal and economic benefits of these technologies could be quite substantial," said Jonathan Fink, ASU's vice president for research and economic affairs. "We're proud that these local entrepreneurs have partnered with ASU by taking advantage of the training and coaching that Technopolis provides. Their technologies range from products that could greatly improve the lives of stroke survivors to state-of-the-art applications of science that can enhance individual and team sports performance," added Fink.

ASU's Office of the Vice President for Research and Economic Affairs initiated ASU Technopolis to help increase the breadth and diversity of greater Phoenix's knowledge economy. ASU Technopolis educates, coaches and connects technology and life science entrepreneurs by providing entrepreneurial education to create viable business plans; manage fast-growing companies; access public and private funding; and connect with other entrepreneurs, ASU programs and business and technical experts. For more information about ASU Technopolis and its upcoming program offerings, visit (www.asutechnopolis.org) or call (480) 727-6506.

Leland, with the Office of Economic Affairs, can be reached at (480) 965-0013 or (karen.leland@asu.edu).
