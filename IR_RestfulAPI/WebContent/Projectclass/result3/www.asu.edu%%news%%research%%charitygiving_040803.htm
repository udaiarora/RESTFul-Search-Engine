April 9, 2003

Study highlights strong charitable giving in state

Arizona residents show strong support for charities, particularly in volunteering their time, according to a new study by ASU's Center for Nonprofit Leadership and Management.

The representative sample study surveyed 1,000 households in the state during the summer of 2002 and identified the methods and motivations of residents in their giving and volunteering with Arizona's 11,504 charitable institutions. The ASU Center for Nonprofit Leadership Management, established at ASU in 1999, provides both research and resources while training students for careers in the nonprofit sector.

The study found that almost nine out of every 10 Arizona households (87.3 percent) made some form of charitable contribution in the past year. The figure is on pace with the national average of 89 percent.

When it came to giving time, Arizonans went well beyond the national average. The study shows that 55 percent of those surveyed volunteered at some point during the year. Nationally, the rate for volunteering sits at 44 percent. Volunteers in the state contributed an average of 194 hours, or 3.74 hours per week.

"This is really the first study of its kind," says Robert Ashcraft, director of the center. "For the nonprofit agencies, this information will go a long way to show where their support is coming from. For companies and cities that help support these groups it shows that the community does find value in supporting charities. For those who give, it shows that many of their neighbors are also interested in helping those around them."

The report also states that:
. Arizona residents gave to a wide range of charities, with 62.6 percent giving to religious organizations. Other frequent areas of giving were youth development and human services.
. The average total annual contribution was $1,572, or 3 percent of annual household income, very similar to the national averages.
. Nearly half of the respondents, 48 percents, said their level of volunteering had increased in the past three years, while 27 percent had reduced it, and 25 percent remained at the same level.
. Arizona residents rate the state's charitable organizations favorably, with a high level of trust and confidence. Of the respondents, 70 percent said charities were honest and trustworthy, while 85 percent said the groups "generally play a major role in making our communities better places to live."

The study also looked at the impact of Sept. 11, 2001, on charitable giving. On a whole, almost six out of every 10 Arizonans gave to a charity directly related to the tragedy. Of those who gave, 89 percent said they still planned to give as much or more to their regular charities in the coming year.

" Following September 11, Arizona nonprofits made a concerted effort to let people know there were still important needs here locally," says Stephanie Hiatt, research associate and editor of the study. "That message appears to have gotten through."
A complete copy of the study can be found on the Web at (www.asu.edu/copp/nonprofit).
