July 23, 2004

Western state job growth forecasts for 2003 were too optimistic

The latest issue of the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast contains its annual review of the state panelists' job growth forecasts for 2003 as compared with the latest revised figures from the Bureau of Labor Statistics for that year.

"For the third consecutive year, economic forecasters from New Mexico were most accurate in their projections of nonfarm employment growth," said Lee McPheters, contributing editor of the Western Blue Chip. The 2003 consensus forecast for New Mexico job growth prepared by the analysts in January anticipated an increase in employment of 1.3 percent. The actual growth rate, based on recently available data revisions, was 1.2 percent.

Nevada panelists were in second place, with a 0.3 percent difference between forecast (3.1 percent) and actual employment growth (3.4 percent). "The persistence of weak labor markets - except in Nevada - was a surprise to most analysts as the past year unfolded," noted McPheters.

The remaining top five Western state panelists' forecasts were:

•  Washington: 0.5 percent difference between forecast (0.7 percent) and actual job growth (0.2 percent);

•  Idaho: 1.0 percent difference between forecast (1.6 percent) and actual job growth (0.6 percent)

•  Arizona: 1.3 percent difference between forecast (2.4 percent) and actual job growth (1.1 percent).

The Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast publishes the consensus forecasts of panels of economists in 10 Western states. Panelists are drawn from leading firms, universities and state agencies. The publication also features historical data for each state.

The Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast is published 10 times a year by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute at Arizona State University's W. P. Carey School of Business. The cost is $110 for 10 print issues ($99 for e-mail subscription). For subscription information, call (800) 448-0432.
