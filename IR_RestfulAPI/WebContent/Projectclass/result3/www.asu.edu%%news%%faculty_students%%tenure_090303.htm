September 3, 2003

President says tenure candidates display scholarly excellence

The promotion and tenure process at ASU had an extra set of eyes this year, as President Michael Crow took an active part in evaluating applications and making decisions.

"Nothing is more crucial to ASU's success in becoming the 'New American University' than the quality, hard work and contributions of faculty in the areas of teaching, research and service," Crow says.

"The overwhelming majority of tenure cases were successful, and some candidates were absolutely outstanding," he says. "The majority of promotion cases were successful as well. Overall I came away with a very positive view of the candidates and of the current process."

Twenty-nine ASU faculty have been awarded tenure, and 48 have been promoted, which is about the same number as last year. They represent some of the finest teacher-scholars at the university, Crow says.

In recognition of the hard work required for promotion and tenure, Crow and Executive Vice President and Provost Milton Glick doubled the standard salary increase for all faculty who received promotions.

"Because I so firmly believe in the importance of promotion and tenure decisions as the foundation for ASU's continuing and future excellence, I believe it is vital that I accept the responsibility of making those final decisions," Crow says.

In reviewing tenure cases, Crow says he looks to see that the standards used are, at a minimum, comparable with the standards of peer institutions whose student bodies, size and institutional mission most closely resembles ASU. More importantly, tenured faculty must be effective teacher-scholars, engaged in research and/or creative activities that inform teaching and teaching in a manner that informs research and creation.

"I am fully committed to the premise that teaching is our prime directive, and scholarship is our pathway to better teaching," he says.

The process of promotion and tenure at ASU has room for improvement, Crow says. There was a wide variation in the quality of submissions, with some files missing information or containing inadequate documentation or other serious flaws.

In most cases President Crow's decisions were consistent with departmental, college and/or university-level recommendations. Most of the candidates who were denied tenure may reapply later, including a handful who received promotions without tenure.

"Tenure represents such an enormous, long-term institutional investment that I am not willing to make positive promotion and tenure decisions unless files are complete and persuasive," Crow says.

Nine academic professionals also received promotions or achieved continuing status, which is different from tenure but has similar employment security provisions.

For the 2003 - 04 academic year, the Provost's Office has revised procedures and guidelines for promotion and tenure and supplied more detailed instructions for personnel actions. The office will be conducting workshops for department chairs and faculty to assist in improving the quality of promotion and tenure packages.

In addition the Provost has appointed a Promotion and Tenure Task Force of faculty and administrators from all three campuses to examine policies and procedures and make recommendations for long-term changes. The faculty will be fully engaged in these discussions before any final recommendations are issued.

For information, visit the Academic Affairs Web site at (http://www.asu.edu/ provost/personnel).
