November 3, 2003

Solheim family gift funds scholar program, lab at East Campus

A financial gift from the John A. and Louise Solheim family will endow the Solheim Scholar program at ASU East and give the campus's Professional Golf Management (PGM) students a high-tech laboratory to improve their swing.

"This gift demonstrates the Solheim family's commitment to the game of golf and the education of tomorrow's leaders in the golf industry, and we are extremely grateful to the Solheims for this gesture," says Richard Grinage, director of the PGM program. "Scholarship money is always a major issue for any academic program, and being able to recognize our students as Solheim Scholars helps separate us from other programs."

The Solheim gift provides $100,000 for the Solheim Professional Golf Scholarship endowment. The endowment will provide funds to cover one scholarship a year for one student in the amount of $2,500. Solheim Scholars must be enrolled in ASU East's PGM program and maintain a 3.25 grade-point average.

The larger portion of the Solheim gift funds the Ping Swing Lab, named after the legendary golf equipment manufacturer founded by the Solheim family. The $200,000 indoor teaching laboratory with state-of-the-art technology allows students to do many of the things inside that they could do on a golf course.

"There will never be a weather issue in terms of golf and play," said Grinage. "It's not too hot, not too cold. It will be a plus for our program to have that kind of controlled environment."

The lab includes a projection screen with sensors that allows users to hit a simulated round of golf on some of the top courses in the world, including Pebble Beach, Oakwood or TPC Scottsdale. Students will hit a golf ball just as they do on the course. Sensors pick up the ball as it flies toward the screen, measuring its velocity, spin rate and height. When the ball hits the screen, the computer simulation takes over and a virtual ball flies down the course toward the green, "just as if you are standing, watching ball flight on a tee box," Grinage says.

"Most programs use indoor simulators where the student hits the ball into a net," Grinage says. "Also, potential employers who use this kind of technology will be attracted to ASU students because of their familiarity with the program."

ASU East's PGM program is among 14 baccalaureate golf programs in the United States accredited by the Professional Golfer's Association of America. The rigorous program prepares students for careers in the golf industry, whether as PGA teaching professionals or golf course managers, by fusing business courses with golf skills development. Students must have a handicap of 8 or better for admission to the program. To graduate with PGA Class A certification, students must complete two of three levels of PGA education in addition to their degree.
