May 13, 2004

Researcher helps guide sustainability efforts through U.N. project

ASU researcher Jay Golden has been selected to serve on a new task force working to address global sustainability issues and policies.

Golden, co-director of ASU's Sustainable Technologies Program, is serving for the next two years on a task force for the United Nations Environment Program's Life Cycle Initiative. The group will create a life cycle management handbook, a guide for developing life cycle management policies throughout the world.

The task force includes 13 researchers and industrial leaders from around the globe, with Golden the only member from a North American university.

"It's certainly an honor to have been selected," he says. "My contribution will be geared toward developing a life cycle management approach for governments and industry to address the impacts of urbanization - specifically issues of the engineered and built environment."

The life cycle management handbook will be a means to deliver transparent, practical and readily accessible decisions to a global audience to produce and consume products in a more sustainable manner. Appropriate science and engineering based approaches that address the social, economic and environmental aspects is a guiding principle for this work.

Golden, who joined ASU in July 2003 and whose expertise is in the area of sustainable technologies, says the concept of sustainability is becoming more visible as the world's population continues to explode. He says due to its relative youth and its growth, Phoenix is in a unique position as a model for rapidly urbanizing regions. ASU, with its growing reputation for research in this area, is also positioned to be a leader in the field.

" Phoenix is a great laboratory for urbanizing regions," says Golden. "Over the next 50 years, the world's population is expected to grow 50 percent, and global energy and materials use is expected to grow 300 percent. The explosive growth and rapid urbanization taking place in Phoenix as well as being an arid region facing the complex impacts of urban climate are similar to the sustainable imperatives and dynamics facing regions around the globe. By exploring our region's sustainability, we can work to maintain or improve our region's quality of life while engaging with regions and researchers from around the globe."

ASU's Sustainable Technology Program (STP) was established in 2003 by Golden and co-director John Crittenden as part of the Consortium for the Study of Rapidly Urbanizing Regions. The STP is a collaboration of ASU researchers who work with government, industry and community stakeholders to evaluate technologies to address the challenges of rapidly urbanizing communities.
