Searching By Subject


A "patent search" may also be referred tp as a "patentability search" or a "prior art search." A comprehensive patentability search includes checking the following resources:

  • U.S. granted and applied patents
  • Foreign issued and applied patents
  • Journal literature and ads
  • Internet

This page describes how to search the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office's databases for U.S. granted and applied patents; a patent search in these databases consists of determining the subject category, called the "class/subclass," and browsing the patents in that category to find inventions of interest. A comprehensive patentability search could take many hours - even days. Plan ahead!


Searching US patents by subject consists of the following steps:

  1. Searching for patents by keyword
  2. Identifying possible patent subject classifications via a keyword, similar patent, classification index, or classification manual search.
  3. Reading definitions of possible classifications to determine appropriate classes/subclasses
  4. Browsing patents in appropriate classes/subclasses
  5. Browsing patent application in the same classes/subclasses

See also:

  • Other Options for Patent Searching
  • Other Tutorials for Patent Searching

 

Step 1: Searching for Patents by Keyword

Go to the USPTO Patent Databases home page at: http://www.uspto.gov/patft/index.html and select "Quick Search" from the column on the left labeled "Issued Patents" This database contains all of the patents issued by the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office. Searches by keyword are limited to only those patents issued since 1976.



Enter in the Term 1 box a word or phrase that would be used to describe the invention and click on the gray search button . For our example we used "pacemaker"



A list of patents with the term "pacemaker" in the title or the text of the patent will be displayed. Click on each title to examine the full patent.

 

 

Go to Step 4: Browsing patents for more details about viewing the patents.

 

You have now identified patents from 1976 to the present that contain your keyword(s). Next you need to find the patents prior to 1976 and also the patents that are about your topic but do not use your keywords. This is done by determining in which subject class/subclass your invention would be placed and examing all the patents within that category or categories.

 

 

 

Step 2: Identifying Possible Subject Classifications

Four methods by which the appropriate classes/subclasses can be found are:

  1. Keyword
  2. Similar Patent
  3. Index to Classification System
  4. Manual of Classification

Not every method needs to be used; in some cases the class/subclass is easy to find. Others may need to try several methods, and even use the same method more than once (for example with different terminology) to determine the correct class/subclass. Many inventions are placed in more than one class/subclass; the searcher should explore every aspect or class that applies to the invention.

  1. Search for Classification by Method A: Keyword
    Go to the USPTO Patent Databases home page at: http://www.uspto.gov/patft/index.html and select "Quick Search" from the column on the left labeled "Issued Patents" This database contains all of the patents issued by the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office. Searches by keyword are limited to only those patents issued since 1976.



    Enter in the Term 1 box a word or phrase that would be used to describe the invention and click on the gray search button . For our example we used "pacemaker"



    A list of patents with the term "pacemaker" in the title or the text of the patent will be displayed. Click on a title that looks as if it might be similar to the invention and scroll the page down to find in which classifications the patent was placed .





    Go to the US Patent Classification home page at: http://www.uspto.gov/go/classification/
    and enter in the class/subclass number and click on SUBMIT button.





    Go to Step 3 in the search process to read the definition of the class/subclass


  2. Search for Classification by Method B: Similar Patent
    Go to the USPTO Patent Databases home page at: http://www.uspto.gov/patft/index.html and select "Patent Number Search" from the column on the left labeled "Issued Patents"



    Enter the number of the patent and click on the gray search button.


    Scroll the page down to view the classification.



    Go to the US Patent Classification home page at: http://www.uspto.gov/go/classification/
    and enter in the class/subclass number and click on SUBMIT button.





    3. Go to Step 3 in the search process to read the definition of the class/subclass


  3. Search for Classification by Method C: Index to the US Patent Classification System
    The Index to the US Patent Classification directs the user from common names to the legal/technical terminology used in the USPTO's subject classification system.

    Go to US Patent Classification home page at http://www.uspto.gov/go/classification/ and click on "Index to US Patent Classification (USPC) System" on the navigation bar near the top of the screen.



    Click on the appropriate letter of the alphabet - in this case, P for pacemaker.



    The index indicates that pacemakers are covered in Class 607, Subclass 9. The "+" indicates that more subclasses besides #9 are used for pacemakers. Click on the "9+" to go to the Manual of Classification.



    Go to Step 3 in the search process to read the definition of the class/subclass



  4. Search for Classification by Method D: Manual of Classification
    A search engine for the US Patent Classification system searches the Index to the Classification System, the Manual of Classification and the Classifications Definitions. Note that the Classification System uses legal and technical terms; iIf you are unfamiliar with this terminology, you may have better luck using the simplified Index to the Classification system which employs common names.

    Go to US Patent Classification home page at http://www.uspto.gov/go/classification/ and enter the word or phrase into the search box on the right and click on the SEARCH button.



    Click on the header to open up the entry.





    Go to Step 3 for more details on reading the Manual of Classification and the Classification Definitions

Step 3: Reading Definitions of Possible Classifications

The Manual of Classification shows the outline of each class; this is called the "class schedule". Dots and indentions are used to show the divisions within a class. The definitions of each class/subclass can be accessed via the manual.

We have been using "pacemaker" as an example throughtout this tutorial. Class 607, part of which is displayed below, is the section of surgical items using light, thermal or electrical applications. Subclass 9 is for electrical applications that regulate the heart rate, i.e. "pacemakers". Note that under subclass 9, subclasses 10-38 are subdivisions of this topic, listing more specific types of heart rate regulators. Subclass 9 is only used for those regulators that do not qualify for one of the more specific subclasses.

When viewing the Manual of Classification, note where the subclass falls within the entire class. Scan the entire class, paying attention to the dot indents, and looking for a more specific description.



 

To find an explanation of what is covered in a specific subclass, click on the subclass number . In this example, subclass 17 (Parameter control in response to sensed physiological load on the heart) will be examined in more detail. The definition tells how this subclass (17) fits into the class (607); in the example, subclass 17 is described as a division of subclass 9. A description of the invention is given and in some cases, such as this, a scope note is also included. Note that an alternate class/subclass is given for devices used for diagnostic purposes.

If the definition describes your invention, click on the to view a list of all patents within the class/subclass.



For more information about the US Patent Classification system see:
http://www.uspto.gov/go/classification/help.htm


Step 4: Browsing Patents in Appropriate Class/Subclasses

Look at all patents granted in the appropriate Class/Subclass(es); do NOT depend upon a title to give a complete description.




Recent patents (1976 to the present) have both the patent number and title listed. Do not depend on the title to sufficiently describe the patent. Older patents (1790 - 1975) only have patent number and classification numbers listed. To view patents, click on the patent number or title (if given).


Recent patents have both text and image versions of the patents. Text versions do not contain the drawings or pictures. Older patents have only the image version, no text version is available.

To retrieve the image version, click on the button at the top or bottom of the text page.

Note: To view image versions of patents, the web browser must have a TIFF-image viewer installed. Detailed information about patent images and tiff- image viewers is available at: http://www.uspto.gov/patft/help/images.htm




Step 5: Browsing Patent Applications in the Same Class/Subclasses

Go to the USPTO Patent Databases home page at: http://www.uspto.gov/patft/index.html and select "Quick Search" from the column on the right labeled "Published Applications." This database contains all of the patent applications pubished since March 15, 2001. Note that not all patent applications are published upon receipt; many do not appear in the database until 18 months after application.


To view Patent Applications by class/subclass:

  1. Enter the class/subclass number in the "Term 1" box;
  2. Change the "Field 1" box from "All Fields" to "Current U.S. Classification";
  3. On the Search Results page, click on either the application number or the application title to view the full text of the application;
  4. On the full text page, click on the "Image" button to view an image of the actual application.

Note: To view the images of the applications requires a TIFF-image viewer. Detailed information about patent images and tiff-image viewers is available at: http://www.uspto.gov/patft/help/images.htm

 

Other Options for Patent Subject Searching

  • Pattera
    http://www.paterra.com/
    Pattera has developed a search engine that will go from the U.S. Classification Number, search, and then display results from both the U.S. Patents Granted database and the U.S. Patent Applications database on the same screen. Start with:
    • Guide to U.S. Patent Classes
      http://cxp.paterra.com/

  • Google Patents
    http://www.google.com/patents
    Beta site released in December 2006. Only issued patents are in the database and at the time of release only the first half of 2006 was covered. Keyword searching is available back to 1790, however comprehensive results may not be obtainable because the optical character recognition program appears to have garbled some text.

    There appears to be a result limit of approximately 1200 and less than 600 hits per search can be viewed. Common keywords should be searched in small groups of years so that more comprehensive results can be achieved. There is a useful "search within patent" feature available.

    A tiff reader is not needed to view the full text of patent, however, the reproduction of the patent on the Google site is inferior to that available at the USPTO. To print full text from the Google site, right click on the page image; each page must be printed separately.
