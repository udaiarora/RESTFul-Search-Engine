The Center for Latin American Studies has 13 affiliates whose primary research focuses on education. For example, numerous other professors pursue interests in bilingual education, both as between English and Spanish in the U.S. and between Spanish and indigenous languages in Latin America. Issues of bilingualism and biliteracy occupy some part of the research interests of the education affiliates, as is the case with Dr. Eugene Garcia, whose work examines the psycholinguistics of bilingualism. Dr. Carlos Ovando, Associate Dean in the College of Education at ASU, takes a unique perspective on the effects of bilingual education, examining its political impact in multicultural schools. Meanwhile, Dr. Alfredo Artiles, Professor of Special Education, pursues research regarding the simultaneously individual and global concern of learning disabilities, as applied to ethnic minorities in Central America; Gustavo Fischman, Professor of Multicultural Education, focuses on cultural studies in education in Argentina, Brazil, and Mexico. Other research topics include cross-cultural cognitive psychology, mathematics education in Mexico, and sociocultural theories of learning. On the whole, the Center for Latin American Studies views the development of educational methods in Latin America as an invaluable opportunity to create closer ties with Latin America.

ASU exhibits an active role in educational projects in Latin America through programs such as the CASS Indigenous Teacher Professional Development Program and the Amazon Field School in Ecuador. While CASS brings in teachers from Latin America for a year of study at ASU, the Field School immerses U.S. trained teachers in the experience of the educational process in Latin America. Moreover, ASU sponsors student teaching programs from the College of Education in both Costa Rica and Guatemala. Teachers from both the U.S. and Latin America gain new cultural perspectives, and acquire unique educational skills and methods through such programs. Hence, the educational arm of the Center for Latin American Studies seeks to emphasize the need of K-12 educational development in Latin America.

With over 40 links to universities through Latin America, including schools in Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Costa Rica, Guatemala, Mexico, Peru, and the Dominican Republic, ASU students have unparalleled access to a first-hand experience of Latin America. Many other specialized connections exist, such as the partnership between ASU's renowned W.P. Carey School of Business and the Instituto Tecnológico Autónomo de México. However, ASU also opens its doors to Latin American students through historic programs such as CONACYT, which provides fellowships for 75 graduate students from Mexico each year. The Center for Latin America Studies at ASU is committed not only to a greater comprehension of Latin America through research, but also to the promotion of Latin American culture and society by providing new and diverse educational opportunities.

  • Andes and Amazon Field School Curriculum Development Fellowships for Teachers.
    All grade school teachers (K-12) and community college teachers with an interest in Latin America are eligible to receive one of five $1,000.00 teacher fellowships to participate in the second session of the Andes and the Amazon Summer Field School in Ecuador. Recipients of the fellowships immerse themselves in the Ecuadorian educational system in which they can acquire cultural knowledge and experience and further incorporate it into their classroom curriculum. These fellowships were made possible through a Title VI grant from the U.S. Department of Education. For application information, go here.
  • Student Teaching in Costa Rica.
    During a semester long program at ASU, students complete teaching requirements under the supervision of experienced mentors in Lincoln School located in Moravia, Costa Rica. Student teachers learn about the history and culture of Latin America, and acquire language skills in Spanish while being immersed in one of the most beautiful and diverse regions in the world. For more information, click here.
  • Student Teaching in Guatemala
    Since January 2006, ASU education students have an option of completing their student teaching requirements in Guatemala. During a semester long program, the student teachers apply their educational knowledge at the Colegio Americano del Sur (CAS), which serves preschool through high school students. This program provides an opportunity to learn new techniques when working with children of different cultural backgrounds. For more information, contact the College of Education, or the Office of Professional Field Experiences.

ASU offers the fellowship recipients more than 100 master's degrees and 48 doctoral degrees, with the option of specialization in a wide variety of areas.   • ASU/ITESM (Instituto Tecnológico y de Estudios Superiores de Monterrey) Partnership.
    The ASU-ITESM partnership has created a task force which focuses on research in new urban materials and technology. It allows for a potential institute under U.S.-Mexico Training, Internships, Exchanges and Scholarships (TIES) initiative. To read article, click here.
  • Community Learning Centers: ASU/ITESM
    ASU in conjunction with ITESM established a Centro Comunitario de Aprendizaje (Community Learning Center) at the ASU downtown campus. The CLL offer online courses to all members of the Latino community in Arizona. Courses include English as second language along with the opportunity of earning a high school diploma.
  • Southwest Center for Education Equity & Language Diversity (SCEED)
    SCEED's mission is to foster policy discourse, scholarship, and demonstration projects that support quality education for language minority children and youth. SCEED's long-term vision is to help develop a new pedagogy tailored to the needs of the multicultural region we serve, and one that is uniquely suited to the Southwestern borderlands.

  • Certificate in Latin American Studies.
    The Center for Latin American Studies offers a Certificate that develops a broad-based understanding of Latin America. Students may study history, society, politics, language, literature, environment, and culture while pursuing other degrees at ASU. Students take coursework in a variety of different disciplines and departments. The knowledge and understanding gained from this endeavor will benefit students both in their future academic work and careers in the public or private sector. For more information, click here.
  • ASU Teacher Training Program for Mexican Indigenous Educators: CASS Program.
    CASS focuses on the training of indigenous educators in a development training program in Multilingual/Multicultural Education. The program is delivered through a combination of university level course work, internships, seminars, focused workshops, and professional and community linkages. A one year certificate is issued to the educators upon completion of the academic program. For information about the program, click here.
