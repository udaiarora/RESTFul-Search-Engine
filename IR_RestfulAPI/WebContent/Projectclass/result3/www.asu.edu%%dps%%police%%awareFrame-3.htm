The Campuses

Arizona State University   (Main Campus)

              Arizona State University was founded in 1885 as a territorial normal school in Tempe .  The campus is located in Tempe , Arizona .  Arizona State University is an internationally recognized metropolitan Research 1 university serving approximately 57,115 students at 3 campuses in the Phoenix area (ASU in Tempe , ASU West in northwest Phoenix and ASU East in Mesa )

 The Department of Public Safety consists of 144 full time employees and is responsible for police services, parking, and special events on campus. Sixty-three members of the Police Services Division are sworn police officers who provide twenty-four hour service, every day of the year.

All ASU Police employees are committed to providing quality safety services for students, faculty, staff and visitors. Some Facts You Should Know

Sex Crimes

          Many people misunderstand sexual assault. One fallacy holds that sexual offenders are overcome by sexual desire and that victims "asked for it" by flirting or dressing seductively. These ideas assume that sexual assault is a crime of sex.  It is not. It is a crime of violence, a vicious hostile attack intended to hurt and to humiliate. Sex is the weapon.

Sexual Assault is a felony. Simply defined, it is oral sexual conduct or sexual intercourse, without the victim's consent, and with the use or threat of force. Legally, "sexual intercourse" refers to penetration by any part of a person's body ‑ or any object used by that person ‑ into the genital opening of another person.

Sexual assault can happen anywhere, anytime.  Most rapes are planned to the extent that the offender knew the rape would occur.  Most rapes occur between acquaintances.  "Acquaintance rape" can be misleading, because sexual assault can occur in a study situation or with someone known only in passing, outside of a dating situation. Regardless of the circumstances, any time two people have sex without both partners expressly consenting, it is rape, and it is a crime.

Sexual Abuse is defined as any sexual contact, not necessarily intercourse, with any person without that person?s consent. Consent is the defining boundary between conduct that is permissible and conduct that is criminal.

Arizona State University is committed to creating an atmosphere conducive to study and work, and to an environment that promotes the dignity and worth of all its members. Sexual assault and sexual abuse violate the law and the standards of conduct at the University. Sexual assault, acquaintance rape and sexual abuse are felonies in the state of Arizona.


On-Campus Reporting of Crimes

ASU provides emergency medical treatment, counseling, and relocation assistance to victims as part of a protocol to support victims of sexual assault. Not all crimes and in particular, not all incidents of sexual assault are reported to the police.  Incidents of non-forcible sexual offenses (incest, for example) are generally not reported to the ASU Police.

Students who request or receive assistance from Student Life, Residential Life, Counseling and Consultation, or Student Health are not required to report the crime to the police or to file a Code of Conduct complaint, but are encouraged to discuss available choices with those offices.

The University protects the confidentiality of parties and witnesses who report misconduct to the extent permitted by law and to the extent that continued protection does not interfere with the University's ability to investigate allegations of misconduct brought to its attention and to take corrective action.

Sanctions

Sexual assault is a criminal act that carries criminal and civil penalties under Federal and State law. When appropriate, formal complaints will lead to the initiation of administrative or disciplinary action. University sanctions, up to and including separation from the University, will be imposed upon those determined to have violated policy. The accuser and the accused are entitled to have others present during a campus disciplinary proceeding; and both shall be informed of the University?s final determination with respect to the alleged sex offense and any sanction that is imposed against the accused.

Unlike other self-defense programs, RAD teaches basic, practical techniques that require no special fitness level or equipment.  The objective of the program is to develop and enhance the options of self-defense, so they may become viable considerations to the woman who is attacked. 

Participants have the opportunity to test their newly learned defensive strategies during simulated attack scenarios.  Those who complete the course are entitled to review the class anywhere in the United States.  For more information call  Stewart Adams at (480) 965-1972.

Surviving a Sexual Assault

Anyone can become a victim of a sexual assault, regardless of gender, age, nationality, or physical appearance.  These are procedures all victims are advised to follow:


Report the assault. Individuals who have been sexually assaulted may choose to report it to ASU offices of Student Life, Residential Life, Counseling and Consultation, or Student Health in addition to, or instead of, reporting the incident to the Police. However, even if the victim does not want to prosecute the ASU Police are willing to assist in any way possible and will protect the confidentiality of the victim. Further, ASU Police personnel or the Office of Student Life will coordinate medical treatment, assist in changing class schedules, contact counseling service, and will notify family or friends.

Seek immediate medical treatment. Do not shower, bathe, or change clothing until after you have sought medical treatment because you might destroy evidence.  Evidence should be collected promptly even if the victim does not wish to file a police report; since there is a chance the victim may have a change of mind later. Further, medical examination and treatment is vital to determine if there are any injuries, internal or external, and to begin any treatment needed. This is also an important step for victims in regaining control of their lives.

HATE CRIMES

Unlike other crimes that target individuals, hate crimes and bias-motivated incidents can have a tremendous impact on an entire community.  When one person in the community is a target of hate-biased crime, the entire community often feels at risk.  Hate crimes can divide communities by raising levels of fear and suspicion which lowers openness and cooperation among its members.

WHAT IS A HATE CRIME?

A CRIMINAL OFFENSE, committed against a person or property which is MOTIVATED, in whole or in part, BY THE OFFENDERS BIAS, against a race, religion, ethnic/national origin group, sexual orientation, gender or disability.


Hate Crimes are NOT separate distinct crimes but any traditional criminal offense that is motivated by the offender?s racial, religious, ethnic, national origin, sexual orientation, gender or disability bias.

In determining whether an incident is to be reported as a hate/bias crime the following items should be considered by the reporting agency:

1.                  Totality of the circumstances.

2.                  Victim?s perception

3.                  Demographics of the area.

4.                  What the officer knows about the area.

5.                  Most importantly, motivation.

If determination is made that the incident is a hate crime the classification is available to the court to consider in sentencing.

Hate crimes can be expressed in several ways.  In some cases physical property is damaged and/or in other instances physical violence against persons occurs.  Violence Against Property examples include: breaking windows of religious institutions, spray-painting walls with offensive words and symbols, or defacing or burning property.

ARE HATE CRIME LAWS AGAINST THE CONSTITUTION?

Hate groups and individuals have the right to assemble.  They have the right to exist and to express their opinions.  They do not, however, have the right to break any laws.  The First Amendment of our Constitution protects most verbal or written expression, so racial slurs or derogatory speech is not enough to be considered a crime.  In Arizona, speech that threatens violence or physical harm to specific persons or groups may constitute a crime.


WHAT SHOULD I DO IF I WITNESS A HATE CRIME?

Hate crimes should never be written off as ?pranks? or ?bad behavior.? This type of behavior should not be tolerated in classrooms, residence halls, in offices, study areas or at social gatherings.  Hate crimes can and should be reported to the appropriate University departments and organizations so appropriate follow through can occur.

WHAT SHOULD I DO IF I AM A VICTIM OF A HATE CRIME?

Immediately report any hate crime incident.  The decision is yours, but reporting a hate crime is a key part of stopping hateful acts.  A person who commits a hate crime can not be held accountable for his or her actions if the crime or incident is not reported.  In addition, collecting accurate data on the number of hate crimes is one of the only ways for Police Services and community organizations to analyze the extent of the problem on campus and to collaboratively develop prevention efforts.

If you or someone you know is a victim of a hate crime you can rest assured that your report will be taken very seriously.  A thorough investigation will result, and administrative or criminal proceedings will follow.

Weapons, Drugs and Alcohol Policies at ASU

ASU Is a Weapons Free Zone

 

Possession, use, or storage of weapons in on-campus student housing is prohibited. Possession or storage of weapons on campus, on University property, or at a University-sponsored activity is also prohibited, except in very limited circumstances. To avoid creating undue concerns, the use or possession of non-operational or model weapons that have the appearance of actual weapons or firearms is subject to the approval of the ASU Police.  Participants or attendees at public or special events held on campus or at University facilities may be required to submit to security checks as a condition of entry at the discretion of ASU Police.  (A.R.S. 13-2911, DPS 201-05, ABOR 5-303.3)

 

Alcohol and Drug Policies

ASU takes seriously its responsibility for creating a community conducive to living, learning and working. In keeping with this responsibility is the establishment of clear rules and regulations regarding the use of controlled substances.

In 1994, ASU adopted a comprehensive policy regarding alcohol and other drugs. Copies of the policy are available from the Office of the Vice President for Student Affairs (965-7293). Detailed information regarding possession, use, and distribution of drugs and alcohol is set forth in ASU's Drug-Free Work Place Statement and Drug-Free Schools and Campuses Act Statement, which is printed in the class schedule. ASU maintains zero tolerance toward the possession, use, distribution and manufacture of illegal drugs.  Consumption of alcohol and alcohol advertising, sponsorship and promotions are also addressed in ASU's policy regarding Permits to Serve Alcohol Beverages on Campus.  (STA 106-03, DPS-PSM 202-03, RSK 304)

It is a violation of the law to possess and or consume alcohol anywhere if you are under the age of 21.  It is a violation of the law to consume alcohol in public even if you are 21.  State Law requires permission must be given for extended premises consumption.  It is a felony to posses any useable amount of illegal drug or drug paraphernalia.

Student Health and Wellness Center, Health Promotion section (965-1132) teaches a course on how to overcome Marijuana and Alcohol use.

Emergency Callboxes and Elevator Emergency Phones

All 9-1-1 calls on the main campus, including pay phones, feature automatic number and location identifiers, and are routed to the ASU Police Dispatch Center.  In addition, over 230 emergency callboxes are available to contact ASU police.  These phones can be identified most readily during the day by the yellow boxes and at night by the blue light mounted overhead.

Federal Bureau of Investigation Uniform Crime Reporting/National Incident‑Based Reporting System Crime Definitions

The following definitions are to be used for reporting the crimes listed in 34 CFR sec. 668.46 (previously 668.47) in accordance with the Federal Bureau of Investigation's Uniform Crime Reporting Program. The definitions for arson, murder, robbery, aggravated assault, burglary, motor vehicle theft, weapon law violations, drug abuse violations and liquor law violations are excerpted from the Uniform Crime Reporting Handbook. The definitions of forcible and nonforcible sex offenses are excerpted from the National Incident‑Based Reporting System Edition of the Uniform Crime Reporting Handbook.

 

Crime Definitions from the Uniform Crime Reporting Handbook

Arson

Any willful or malicious burning or attempt to burn, with or without intent to defraud, a dwelling house, public building, motor vehicle or aircraft, personal property of another, etc.

 

Criminal Homicide‑Manslaughter by Negligence

The killing of another person through gross negligence.

 

Criminal Homicide‑Murder and Nonnegligent Manslaughter

The willful (nonnegligent) killing of one human being by another.

 

Robbery

The taking or attempting to take anything of value from the care, custody, or control of a person or persons by force or threat of force or violence and/or by putting the victim in fear.

 


Aggravated Assault

An unlawful attack by one person upon another for the purpose of inflicting severe or aggravated bodily injury. This type of assault usually is accompanied by the use of a weapon or by means likely to produce death or great bodily harm. (It is not necessary that injury result from an aggravated assault when a gun, knife, or other weapon is used which could and probably would result in serious personal injury if the crime were successfully completed.)

Burglary

The unlawful entry of a structure to commit a felony or a theft. For reporting purposes this definition includes: unlawful entry with intent to commit a larceny or felony; breaking and entering with intent to commit a larceny; housebreaking; safecracking; and all attempts to commit any of the aforementioned.

 

 

 

 

Motor Vehicle Theft

The theft or attempted theft of a motor vehicle. (Classify as motor vehicle theft all cases where automobiles are taken by persons not having lawful access even though the vehicles are later abandoned including joyriding.)

 

Weapon Law Violations

The violation of laws or ordinances dealing with weapon offenses, regulatory in nature, such as: manufacture, sale, or possession of deadly weapons; carrying deadly weapons, concealed or openly; furnishing deadly weapons to minors; aliens possessing deadly weapons; and all attempts to commit any of the aforementioned.

 

Drug Abuse Violations

Violations of State and local laws relating to the unlawful possession, sale, use, growing, manufacturing, and making of narcotic drugs. The relevant substances include: opium or cocaine and their derivatives (morphine, heroin, codeine); marijuana; synthetic narcotics (demerol, methadones); and dangerous nonnarcotic drugs (barbituates, benzedrine).

 


Liquor Law Violations

The violation of laws or ordinances prohibiting: the manufacture, sale, transporting, furnishing, possessing of intoxicating liquor; maintaining unlawful drinking places; bootlegging; operating a still; furnishing liquor to a minor or intemperate person; using a vehicle for illegal transportation of liquor; drinking on a train or public conveyance; and all attempts to commit any of the aforementioned. (Drunkenness and driving under the influence are not included in this definition.)

 

Sex Offenses Definitions from the National Incident‑Based Reporting System Edition of the Uniform Crime Reporting Program

 

Sex Offenses‑Forcible

Any sexual act directed against another person, forcibly and/or against that person's will; or not forcibly or against the person's will where the victim is incapable of giving consent.

 

    A. Forcible Rape‑The carnal knowledge of a person, forcibly and/or against that person's will; or not forcibly or against the person's will where the victim is incapable of giving consent because of his/her temporary or permanent mental or physical incapacity (or because of his/her youth).

 

     B. Forcible Sodomy‑Oral or anal sexual intercourse with another person, forcibly and/or against that person's will; or not forcibly against the persons will where the victim is incapable of giving consent because of his/her youth or because of his/her temporary or permanent mental or physical incapacity.

 

     C. Sexual Assault With An Object‑The use of an object or instrument to unlawfully penetrate, however slightly, the genital or anal opening of the body of another person, forcibly and/or against that person's will;

      or not forcibly or against the person's will where the victim is incapable of giving consent because of his/her youth or because of his/her temporary or permanent mental or physical incapacity.

 


     D. Forcible Fondling‑The touching of the private body parts of another person for the purpose of sexual gratification, forcibly and/or against that person's will; or, not forcibly or against the person's will where the victim is incapable of giving consent because of his/her youth or because of his/her temporary or permanent mental incapacity.

 

Sex Offenses‑Nonforcible

Unlawful, nonforcible sexual intercourse.

 

     A. Incest‑Nonforcible sexual intercourse between persons who are related to each other within the degrees wherein marriage is prohibited by law.

 

     B. Statutory Rape‑Nonforcible sexual intercourse with a person who is under the statutory age of consent.

 

Source: Federal Register, April 29, 1994, Vol. 59, No. 82; Federal Register, November 1, 1999, Vol. 64, No. 210.

 


 

CRIME STATISTICS

These statistics have been compiled in accordance with definitions used in the uniform crime reporting system of the FBI, U. S. Department of Justice, as modified by the Hate Crime Statistics Act.

The data relate to all crimes reported as having occurred on any of the university?s  campuses, including its residential halls, or in any off-campus building or property owned by the University or on any public property within the campuses or immediately adjacent to the campuses, to the ASU Police by its own officers, the local law enforcement agencies having primary jurisdiction, and other university personnel who have significant responsibility for student and campus activities. Crimes, which have not been reported to the police, the Office of Student Life, ASU Counseling and Consultation and ASU Student Health, cannot be reflected in these tables.

For this reason, everyone is urged to report all crimes to the ASU Police or the law enforcement agency where the crime occurred.

Information regarding the definitions of crimes and of the campus property referenced in the statistics may be obtained from ASU Police or from the ASU Office of General Counsel, or at the URL: http://www.asu.edu/dps/. 

The statistics include all reports of campus crimes made to the ASU Police at the Main and West campuses.

The statistics for drug, liquor law and weapons violations relate to actual criminal arrests occurring on campus property to the full extent that these statistics are made available by local police authorities.

This report complies with the Crime Awareness and Campus Security Act and provides information regarding public safety at ASU.  Copies of referenced policies are available in many university offices, including the Departments of Public Safety at the ASU Main, West and East campuses and the ASU Libraries.  ASU currently has a variety of policies and procedures relating to campus security and it expressly reserves the right to modify them or to adopt additional policies or procedures at any time without notice.
