May 22, 2003

Students win honorable mention in competition

A five-member team of graduate students from the ASU College of Architecture & Environmental Design has won honorable mention in the 2003 Urban Land Institute Student Urban Design Competition.

The four finalists, Harvard University, Massachusetts Institute of Technology, University of Pennsylvania and University of Southern California, will do an additional project to determine the winner.

The competition attracted 45 submissions from 26 universities across the United States and Canada.

ASU's team included three masters-level students and one doctoral student from the College of Architecture & Environmental Design, and one graduate student from the Del E. Webb School of Construction. Team members were master of environmental planning students Mitu Singh and Himanshu Joshi, School of Planning & Landscape Architecture; Vivek Sharma, Del E. Webb School of Construction; Aalok Deshmukh, master of science program, School of Architecture; and Jesus J. Lara, doctoral program in environmental design.

The team's challenge: to create an innovative, creative urban design for a site in Washington, D.C., called South Capital District.

The large site presented complex challenges, and the students' solutions were to be "multifaceted, incorporating design, planning, market potential and feasibility and development strategies."

Background material for the competition was provided by the National Capital Planning Commision and District of Columbia Office of Planning.

Currently, the site is characterized by high poverty, with 50 percent of the population living below poverty level. Nearly half the households are headed by a single female parent, and 93 percent of the population is African American. The site also has high levels of air pollution and traffic, and many residents live in fear of cancer.

ASU's design team envisioned the "New South Capital District" as a potential "quality public realm," that is attractive and green outdoors, with streetscapes, noise buffers and high traffic lanes placed underground; adaptable to change through responsible sustainable interventions; has diversity, with variety and choice; and is easy to get to and to move through.

The ASU team's planning elements included protecting or restoring open space, determining appropriate landscape types and design using indigenous plants, reducing urban heat islands, advocating adaptive re-use of buildings and using locally manufactured and extracted materials.

Team adviser Hemalata C. Dandekar, director of the School of Planning and Landscape Architecture, and Lee Hanley, chairman and chief executive officer of Vestar, had high praise for the team.

"This achievement shows how creative these international students are, and how lucky we are to have this kind of human capital arriving at our doorstep," Dankekar said. "We are tapping into the intellectual wealth they represent."
