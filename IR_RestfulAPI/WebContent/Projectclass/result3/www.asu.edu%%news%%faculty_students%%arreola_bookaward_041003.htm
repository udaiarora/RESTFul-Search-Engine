April 10, 2003

Daniel Arreola wins national Jackson Prize for literary work

ASU geography professor Daniel D. Arreola was recently awarded the John Brinckerhoff Jackson Prize for his book "Tejano South Texas," published by the University of Texas Press, 2002.

The Jackson Prize for Popular Books in American Human Geography is awarded by the Association of American Geographers to the author of a popular book of scholarly and literary merit on the human geography of the United States, one that interprets geographical scholarship in language that nonprofessional readers can appreciate and enjoy. The prize is restricted to books written by geographers, with preference given to those by United States citizens or permanent residents. Special consideration is given to books that are carefully designed, appropriately illustrated, and physically appealing.

In his book, Arreola charts the many ways in which Texans of Mexican ancestry have established a cultural province in the Texas-Mexico borderland that is unlike any other Mexican American region. Arreola's approach combines historical perspective with contemporary assessment. Some issues discussed and illustrated include regional distinctiveness of Texas Mexican heritage, specific cultural celebrations and behaviors, and distinctive architectural aspects of town founding and layout in South Texas.

"For example, I did an analysis of Hispanic surnames by comparing those found in the telephone directory for San Antonio with surnames found in a directory for Los Angeles," Arreola says. "There are some dozen surnames common in San Antonio like Garza, Treviño, Villareal and Cantú that are rare in Los Angeles. The surnames in San Antonio are what I would consider Tejano surnames and thus identify Texans of Mexican ancestry. Furthermore, this ancestry is geographically connected to areas of northeastern Mexico, states like Nuevo León and Tamaulipas, where there are also many with surnames like Garza and Cantú. In comparison many Hispanic surnames in Los Angeles are geographically linked to states like Jalisco and Michoacán in western Mexico."

Moving into the present, Arreola examines many factors that make South Texas distinctive from other Mexican American regions - the physical spaces of ranchos, plazas, barrios and colonias; the cultural life of the small towns and the cities of San Antonio and Laredo; and the foods, public celebrations and political attitudes that characterize the region.

Arreola says this evidence suggests that the Mexican populations found today in regions in the United States have different place origins in the homeland of Mexico.

"As Mexican American populations evolved in separate regions of the United States so too did they create varied regional traditions that combine their homeland culture from Mexico with their present regional culture in the United States," he says.
Arreola says researching and writing this book required long commitment to a single project.

"This one evolved over a decade," he says.

"Dozens of visits to South Texas - some short, some extended, interviews with key informants, research in archives and repositories, and field observation and documentation are just some of the necessary steps in this type of project. This leads to a kind of obligation between a geographer and the place and region investigated. I believe this type of geography is necessary to tell the story of a people and a place, and the rewards can be considerable because one learns how people shape a place creating what some have termed regional personality. 'Tejano South Texas' is one such regional personality."
