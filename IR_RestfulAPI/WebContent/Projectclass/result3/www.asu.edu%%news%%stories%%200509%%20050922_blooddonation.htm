September 22, 2005

Freshman testifies to value of blood donations

If Kristin Bardin could give blood, she would.

But Bardin, an ASU freshman, has received so many transfusions (471) and battled such a serious illness that she is ineligible to be a donor.

She hopes that others will pick up the slack where she cannot.

When her friends tell her that they don’t want to give blood because they’re afraid that it will hurt, she tells them, “I’ve had so many needles in me. Get over it.”

Up until the age of 10, Bardin was an active, energetic young girl. But then she began tiring easily, and soon was so exhausted she could only go to school and sleep.

Her parents worried that she might have the flu, but tests showed a much more serious cause for Bardin’s tiredness: She had acute myelogenous leukemia.

This rapidly progressing disease occurs when the patient has too may immature cells in the blood and bone marrow.

After chemotherapy, with several blood and platelet transfusions to keep her strong, Bardin appeared to be on the road to recovery.

But complications set in. Five months after her last chemotherapy treatment, she suffered a relapse. She also endured sinus and ankle infections, and she had to have a growth removed from her arm.

Then, doctors began looking for a bone marrow donor for Bardin. In the National Marrow Donor Registry, they found 300 matches, including three people who matched on all six human leukocyte antigen tests.

In March 1999, Bardin received a bone marrow transplant, and she endured still more red blood cell and platelet transfusions.

“My family helped me a lot, and my friends gave me support,” Bardin says of her many transfusions.

Her treatment was successful, and she was able to return to school in Wickenburg. Now, instead of being exhausted, she found the time and energy to play varsity tennis, perform in the school musical, and serve on the student council.

She is a “Blood Hero” for United Blood Services and has spoken several times for community groups.

“I also headed up a donation drive at my school last year,” Bardin says.

Bardin is a freshman at ASU, planning to major in nursing and become a pediatric oncology nurse.

When she’s not studying, she’s busy doing what kids her age do: “I like hanging out with my friends,” she says.
