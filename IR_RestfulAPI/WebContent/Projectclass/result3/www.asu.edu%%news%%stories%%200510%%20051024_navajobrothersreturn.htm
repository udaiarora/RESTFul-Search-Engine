October 24 , 2005

Navajo brothers come home safely from Iraq

Two ASU students who were featured in Insight last month as soldiers serving in Iraq have returned safely to their home in Shiprock, N.M. Marine Lance Cpl. John Bailon, 22, and Cpl. Cheston Bailon, 21, two brothers who are members of the Navajo Nation, received a big welcome from their community.

Smiles, music and food flowed freely Oct. 8 inside a large festival tent as friends and family greeted them and fellow soldier Army Spc. Shawn Malueg, reported the Navajo Times. The brothers were excited about coming home but at the same time felt bad for the families of 48 Marines in their battalion who died.

Their unit saw heavy action, constantly fighting battles and taking ground.

“Syrian fighters would shoot at us with all they have, ” John was quoted as saying. “Along the Syrian border, they like to fight to the death and say, ‘God is great.’ We found torture chambers (in Karbala), and a car bomb factory with 32 cars near the Syrian border. We destroyed it.”

The tribe held a cleansing ceremony Oct. 6 for the brothers inside a teepee in front of their Shiprock home. People prayed and sang, to “cleanse our minds,” John says.

Both men plan to return to ASU in January. John, who is a pre-med major, plans to become a physician. He said he carried his Medical College Admissions Test book with him in Iraq but didn’t have time to study it. Cheston wants to become a lawyer.

John came to ASU in fall of 2001, enrolling as a freshman in the Native American Achievement Program. Cheston talked him into joining the Marine Corps Reserves following the 9/11 attacks. They continued their military training in the summers, and Cheston joined John at ASU last fall.

The two were active in NATIONS, a support and enrichment organization for Native American students. John had completed five semesters and Cheston one semester when they learned that their 4th Reconnaissance Battalion would be deploying for Iraq last January.

The Bailon brothers have a six-year reserve commitment to the Marines, making it possible they could be deployed again.
