December 5, 2005

Glick to step down as ASU's provost in July

Slated to become provost emeritus and University Professor

After serving for 15 years as ASU’s chief academic officer and helping two presidents lead the university’s climb among the ranks of national research universities, Milton Glick will step down as provost and executive vice president of the University in July.

At that time, he will assume a new role at ASU, that of University Professor, the first person ever to hold that title. He also will become provost emeritus and will remain a valuable resource for his successor, and for President Michael Crow.

"Milt has been a colleague, a mentor and a friend,” Crow says. “He has worked tirelessly for ASU, and for a decade and a half has been the academic backbone of the university. The entire ASU community is fortunate that Milt, in his new position, will continue to help advance the university he loves so much.”

A search committee will be impaneled immediately to embark on a national search for a new provost and executive vice president for ASU.

University Professor is the highest academic rank at ASU and is awarded in recognition for lifetime achievement. As University Professor, Glick will be able to teach in any of ASU’s schools. He will lecture across the university and participate in academic reviews in any subject. He also will serve as an academic mentor for university leadership and a general faculty mentor.

In addition, Glick will assume responsibility for a variety of special assignments, including advancing ASU’s case for admission to the Association of American Universities (AAU) and developing an academic leadership training program.

"The last 15 years have been a great ride,” Glick says. “I share with the university community great pride in the progress ASU has made. I have been fortunate indeed to work with and have the support of terrific colleagues who share my enthusiasm for ASU and its agenda.”

Some of the progress made during Glick’s tenure at ASU includes:

• An increase in undergraduate student success, as represented by improvement in freshman retention and graduation rates of 20 percent and 15 percent respectively.

• A tripling of international student recognition (such as Truman, Marshall, Rhodes and Fulbright scholarships).

• A tripling of the freshman class.

• A more than an eight-fold increase in National Merit Scholars (ranking ASU fourth among public universities).

• A doubling of the minority student population.

In addition, the university has tripled its sponsored research funding and achieved Research I status during that time.

Glick has published more than 100 articles on structural chemistry, trends in higher education and information technology. He has served as a trustee of EDUCOM and of the Higher Learning Commission.

In 2004, Glick was named a senior fellow for the EDUCAUSE Center for Applied Research (ECAR). Glick advises on ongoing ECAR research, in particular ECAR’s new research on the relationship between institutional process performance and information technology investment, leadership and culture. He frequently advises other universities on meeting the challenges and opportunities facing the nation’s universities.

Glick came to ASU in 1991 and served until 2002 as senior vice president and provost at ASU. In July 2002, Crow appointed him as executive vice president and provost of the university, with responsibility for all of the university’s campuses. His duties in that position include coordinating the building of all university programs, and the academic planning and budgeting activities associated with these programs.

Before coming to ASU, Glick had been provost at Iowa State University for three years, also serving the Iowa campus as interim president for eight months. Previously, Glick served as dean of the College of Arts and Science at the University of Missouri from 1983 – 1988. He was a member of the chemistry faculty at Wayne State University for 17 years, including five years as chair of the department.

Glick has promoted the careers of minorities and women in academia, receiving the 1988 Award for Exemplary Service in Enhancing the Status of Minorities at the University of Missouri. He encouraged the innovative and appropriate use of computers and programs to support learning and research.

Glick earned his bachelor’s degree from Augustana College and his doctorate from the University of Wisconsin-Madison.
