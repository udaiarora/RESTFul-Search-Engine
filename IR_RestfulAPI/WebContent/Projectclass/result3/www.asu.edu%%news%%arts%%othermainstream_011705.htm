‘The Other Mainstream’ raises social, political issues

A selection of 40 dynamic works that show an ongoing commitment to social and political issues, as well as artists of color, will be exhibited at the ASU Art Museum in “The Other Mainstream: Selections from the Collection of Mikki and Stanley Weithorn,” from Jan. 22 – April 23. A public reception will take place from 7 – 9 p.m., Jan. 21.

The exhibition includes works by contemporary, well-known and emerging artists such as Leon Golub, Vik Muniz, Kara Walker, Jon Haddock, Faith Ringgold, Robert Colescott, Enrique Chagoya and Sue Coe, as well as historic pieces that show the roots of socially conscious artwork in the early 20th century.

The project includes a 48-page color catalog with an essay by Marilyn Zeitlin, ASU Art Museum ’s director and chief curator, and an interview with the Weithorns by exhibition curator Heather Sealy Lineberry.

In 1988, the Weithorns began collecting contemporary art chosen by instinct and intellect, not art world trends. Their 300-piece collection is bold in its imagery and commentary on social issues and also reflects the couple’s individual career paths and shared values.

Mikki Weithorn worked for 40 years as a speech pathologist and taught at Adelphi University in New York. Her husband was the first lawyer in the United States to specialize in the field of charitable tax law, and he has devoted a significant amount of his time to social justice charities. They live surrounded by their collection in their two homes in Scottsdale and New York.

“Work by artists whose point of view is not derived from the mainstream may attract collectors who are themselves seeking an alternative to mainstream thought and action,” Zeitlin says. “The Weithorns, who are vigilant in their skepticism about received opinion and refuse to settle for a status quo that does not reflect their principles, clearly value the social critique in the work they collect.”

In Wanxin Zhang’s “Skateboarder” (2000), the ceramic figure appears to be a traditional Chinese scholar, but he zooms into the contemporary world on a skateboard. “In Barbie Visits the Tomb of the Unknown Indian,” artist Richard Glazer-Danay uses humor to show how many remember the dead not as participants, but as tourists. Kojo Griffin’s sculpture “Untitled” (elephant watching video, 2004) shows the Indian mythological god of knowledge, Ganesha, holding a remote control, surrounded by videotapes.

Additional artists featured in the exhibit include Robert Arneson, Belkis Ayón, John Bankston, Sandow Birk, Willie Birch, Brett Cook-Dizney, E. V. Day, Marita Dingus, Peter Drake, Marcel Dzama, William Gropper, Gottfried Helnwein, John King, Jacob Lawrence, Don Smith “Lelooska”, Francisco Larios Osuna, Kerry James Marshall, Beverly McIver, Takashi Murakami, Justin Novak, Tom Otterness, Sabastiao Salgado, Sol’ Sax, Joyce Scott, Travis Somerville and Joe Tilson.

Tanguay, with the Herberger College of Fine Arts, can be reached at (480) 965-7144 or (denise.tanguay@asu.edu).
