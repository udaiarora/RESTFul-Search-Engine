April 9, 2004

Three ASU students win top scholarships

Three ASU students have received prestigious Goldwater Scholarships, the nation's highest undergraduate award in science, mathematics and engineering. Jared Niska and Stephen Brink, bioengineering juniors, and William Hendricks, a junior in molecular biosciences and biotechnology, each will receive $7,500.

ASU students have won 28 Goldwater awards in the past 11 years, placing the university among the top schools in the country for the award. Faculty from schools across the country nominated 1,113 students this year, with 310 winners selected.

All three of this year's winners have been deeply involved in undergraduate research, working with senior ASU faculty and also developing their own projects. All are doing research that may have significant health care applications.

Niska, a 21-year-old from Gilbert, has been working on developing an implantable drug delivery device to treat glioblastoma multiforme, the most malignant form of primary brain tumor. He arranged a collaboration between the Translational Genomics Institute and the ASU Harrington Department of Bioengineering for his project, and last December he presented his research at the American Society for Cellular Biology. Niska plans to get both Ph.D. and M.D. degrees and to work in a brain tumor research center.

Brink, also 21, developed a fascination with neuroscience when he was on the academic decathlon team at his small high school in Williams, Ariz., where he grew up. He has been doing research at the ASU computational materials science lab. Brink has developed a project on magnetic nanoparticles in medical applications, and says he hopes to be among the scientists who will successfully integrate physics and biology in order to advance the frontiers of neuroscience.

Hendricks, 23, has been doing research on another type of brain tumor, meningioma, a kind that is difficult to accurately diagnose as benign or aggressive before surgery. Working with leading scientists at ASU and Barrow Neurological Institute, Hendricks has designed and is carrying out a series of experiments that may lead to improved diagnostic accuracy, more appropriate early treatments and improved prognosis for victims. Like Niska, he plans on getting an M.D./Ph.D. joint degree and continuing his cancer research.

The scholarship honoring U.S. Senator Barry Goldwater was established in 1986 with a federal endowment.
