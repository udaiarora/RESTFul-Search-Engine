May 16, 2003

First Lady recruits ASU students to teach in low-income schools

Surrounded by a crowd of 20 fifth-grade students from Maxine O. Bush Elementary School in a room at the ASU Carson Student-Athlete Center, First Lady Laura Bush was transported back to her days as a fifth-grade public school teacher in Texas.
But she may not have been prepared for their questions.

"Do you play basketball with the president?"

"Do you wash dishes at the White House?"

The gracious, unflappable wife of U.S. President George W. Bush recovered quickly and assured them that she and her husband do lots of things together, but basketball is not one of them, and no, someone else washes the dishes.

Her visit with the class on May 9 was part of a Teach For America (TFA) recruiting event, arranged by Phoenix fifth-grade teacher Beth Berselli, who met Mrs. Bush when Berselli was a Washington Post reporter, and Christine Wilkinson, senior vice president and secretary of the university who is on TFA's regional advisory board.
Her remarks to about a hundred ASU students who attended were aimed at opening their eyes to the benefits of teaching in an inner-city or rural school, where TFA teachers commit to teach for two years.

"There are few professions where you can see the impact of your work so vividly," she said. "And there are few careers that have such a lasting effect on an entire generation and an entire nation."

The children filled the risers on the stage, squirming only slightly as they waited for her speech. ASU President Michael Crow, waiting to introduce her, grinned widely at their anticipation and the hush that preceded her appearance.

More than two million new teachers will be needed in America's classrooms in the next decade, Bush said. Nationwide, two-thirds of fourth-graders in the poorest schools cannot read a simple children's book, and many struggle with the alphabet.

"Teaching is the absolute profession, the one that makes all others possible," said Bush. "Teachers help students develop the skills they need for a lifetime of learning. I wish all the ASU students here the very best as you go out into the world, and as you go out, I hope you'll think about teaching. Arizona's children are depending on you."

Berselli's students had been behind in their math and reading levels, but now are excelling at reading, fractions and science. She said teaching is the toughest thing she's ever done. She is one of 120 TFA teachers in the Phoenix area, and 9,000 throughout the country.

TFA works closely with ASU and the College of Education to recruit students, said Jason Williams, executive director. The organization is expanding the partnership to the W. P. Carey School of Business and the College of Law. Now in its 13th year, TFA trains its teachers to become lifelong advocates for children.

Berselli described taking her class on a field trip to ASU in January, where her students were amazed that there was a bowling alley and restaurants. They enjoyed a tour of the Mars Space Flight Facility and a presentation by the admissions staff. She said they were anxious to come back.

"We're holding a place for all of you," President Crow told the children.
