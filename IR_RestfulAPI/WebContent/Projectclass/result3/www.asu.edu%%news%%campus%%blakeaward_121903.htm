December 19, 2003

Groups salute late lobbyist Blake Anderson

The American Association of Community Colleges, the American Association of State Colleges and Universities, the Council for Advancement and Support of Education (CASE), and the National Association of State Universities and Land-Grant Colleges have named the late ASU lobbyist Blake Anderson as the recipient of the Marvin D. "Swede" Johnson Achievement Award for his dedication to state relations and advancing the cause of colleges and universities. Anderson passed away in June.

The Johnson award is only one of two national honors in higher-education state relations, a field that encompasses advocacy and outreach efforts on behalf of colleges and universities to governors, state legislators and other key policymakers.
Also awarded was the Edwin Crawford Award for Innovation to Matthew Hargrove from the University of California, Davis.

"These winners have profoundly improved the lives of all members of their campus communities through their persistent and all-too-often unacknowledged efforts," said CASE President Vance T. Peterson. "We are pleased to honor these distinguished professionals for the positive impact they have had on American higher education and for the outstanding example they have set for state-relations professionals everywhere."

Anderson served as the university's chief political adviser and lobbyist since 1994. Prior to joining ASU, Anderson worked for the Arizona House of Representatives and previously for Sen. John McCain. During his tenure at the university, Anderson assisted in the creation of the ASU legislative network, an advocacy mechanism in conjunction with the Alumni Association and other members of the campus community. He also was in charge of the organization and coordination of the annual Western States Governmental Relations Conference, which has been key in connecting government relations professionals from the region.

One of Anderson's greatest accomplishments was the passage of a bill to give $400 million to the three Arizona universities for constructing research facilities. Anderson was instrumental in getting this legislation passed, even as the state faced a $1 billion budget deficit. After Anderson's death, the members of the state legislature named the bill "Blake's Bill" and to a person they spoke of the effectiveness with which he had carried it through from start to finish.

"Blake was dedicated to ASU and worked hard on ASU's governmental affairs issues," says Steve Miller, deputy vice president in Public Affairs. "I am very proud of Blake's accomplishments and am honored to have worked with him."

Nominees for the Johnson Award are evaluated on three criteria - motivation/coordination/stewardship; support from policymakers and colleagues; and extraordinary contributions over a significant period of time.

The recipient of the Marvin D. "Swede" Johnson Award receives a $5,000 cash prize, sponsored by ACS, Inc., PeopleSoft and Unisys.

This article appeared in the December 12, 2003 issue of ASU Insight.
