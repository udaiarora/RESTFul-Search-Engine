June 30, 2004

HBLI provides youth with an 'Early Start to College'

Hispanic middle school students are getting an "Early Start to College" thanks to a program offered through ASU's Hispanic Border Leadership Institute (HBLI).

"Early Start," which ran June 13 - 15, is designed to educate and motivate eighth and ninth graders to obtain a higher education.

Leonard Valverde, executive director of HBLI, says the program addresses an issue that continues to concern Hispanics.

"Given the persistent high drop-out rate of Latino students and other students of color, and the low high school graduation rate, I wanted to put in place a program that increased the number of students who could go to college and, more importantly, their desire to go to college," says Valverde, a professor of Educational Leadership and Policy Studies at ASU.

The program, made possible through a $75,000 grant from the Lumina Foundation, consists of events and workshops providing an introduction to college/campus; financial planning workshop; college/campus recruitment night; community college visits; ASU summer mini-camp orientation; and an end-of-the-year celebration.

"'Early Start to College' is different from other programs of its type because it is based on a proactive approach, aimed at getting the average or low achiever to want to go to college and believe they can go if they start before high school," Valverde says. "It is an intervention strategy that starts before students are identified as potential dropouts."

This is the first time "Early Start," which was launched last September, has been offered through HBLI. "The program was designed to work with 200 students, but we had more than 500 students wanting to participate," Valverde says.

Participating schools in the 2004 Mini Summer Camp included: The Stardust House, Gilliland Middle School, P.H. Gonzales Middle School, Tolleson High School, Phoenix Prep Academy, Issac Middle School, Cesar Chavez High School, Teacher Prep High School and Santa Maria Middle School.

"Early Start" includes a parent component, which addresses concerns by parents who may not be familiar with the process for applying to a college or university. "Programs should be designed not only to educate prospective students, but also the families who support these students through their educational careers," Valverde says.
