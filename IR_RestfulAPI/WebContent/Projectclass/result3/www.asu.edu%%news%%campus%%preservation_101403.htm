Preservation key to ASU's history

Without the foresight of people over the years and the Department of Archives and Manuscripts, there would be no Arizona Territorial Football League Championship Cup (the oldest collegiate rivalry trophy in the nation, according to NCAA). Nor would there be the World War II correspondence and ASU records of Jim Creasman, the "Voice of ASU" who did play-by-play for football and other sports, and was one of the U.S. soldiers who liberated Auschwitz.

The Department of Archives and Manuscripts, housed in Hayden Library, has an extensive record of the university's and the state's past, as well as other historical documents and objects.

In celebration of the past as part of the present and future, Oct. 12 - 18 has been designated Arizona Archives Week.

"The purpose is to celebrate the value of Arizona's historical records and to recognize the people and programs responsible for maintaining our communities' historical record," says Rose Minetti, exhibit curator.

What does ASU have tucked away in its archival boxes and files?

The Department of Archives and Manuscripts houses seven separate research collections. Materials from these collections are on display at Luhrs Gallery on the fourth floor of Hayden Library.

Current exhibits at Luhrs Gallery are "Bricks and Mortarboard: The Construction Photographs of Four ASU Landmarks," which features construction photos of Sun Devil Stadium, Gammage Auditorium, Hayden Library and Nelson Fine Arts Center, as well as the architectural plans for Camp Tontozona and the Ross-Blakely Law Library; and "ASU Football: A Photo Essay."

At Labriola American Indian Center, on the second floor of Hayden Library, see "Publications by Alumni, Staff, Faculty and Students of ASU."

One need not make the trek to Hayden Library to catch a glimpse into the past, however.

The Archives Web site includes indices and exhibits such as "The New ASU Story," which has more than 400 digitized materials, and "The Mission to Arizona, 1916 - 1940: Father Augustine Schwarz, O.F.M," all at (www.asu.edu/lib/archives/).

The need to think about saving the past is more critical today than ever, says Robert Spindler, ASU archivist. "Just about anything electronic will not be saved unless people take specific actions to preserve them."
