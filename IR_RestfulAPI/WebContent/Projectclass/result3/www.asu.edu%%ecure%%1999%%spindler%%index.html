Speaker Presentation
Preservation and Access for Electronic College and University Records
October 7 – 8, 1999

Preserving Web-Based Records

Rob Spindler
University Archivist
Arizona State University Libraries

General Background Points:

  • Focus on preservation of Web-Based university policy manuals
  • ASU in the top five US universities with about 49,000 students on three major campuses
  • ASU is a public institution that must comply with Arizona public records laws
  • Arizona Department of Library, Archives and Public Records (DLAPR) has legal authority to approve all records retention schedules
  • ASU has eleven official university policy manuals that until 1998 were distributed as hardcopy notebooks with a roughly annual updating cycle
  • Academic and Administrative Documents (AAD) charged with manual publication and distribution
  • Individual manual content managers identified from the departments responsible for each manual

The Story:

  • December 1996 Provost approved exclusively converting the entire set of manuals to html encoded documents. Main motivations seemed to be cost savings, multi-user and remote accessibility, and real-time updating
  • AAD charged with developing a publishing and archiving plan
  • December 1996: 1st meeting w/AAD requested help creating an archiving plan for the policy manuals.
  • January 1997 issued draft report on electronic archiving with the following major points:

Academic and Administrative Documents and Information Technology:

  • Use non- proprietary HTML codes in compliance with the current W3 standard (currently HTML 2.0) Test compliance using the W3 verification service.
  • Establish firewalls to prevent tampering with university records.
  • Do not use compression algorithms in backup systems.
  • Insure that newly revised official versions of the entire web site are backed up immediately. Real time revisions made on the local AAD copy may be backed up on their regular weekly cycle.
  • Establish a program of media maintenance and refreshment for backup tapes based upon the reliable shelf life of the media, the frequency of policy updates and availability of the hardware needed to read the implemented media format.
  • Keep an extra set of backup tapes off campus in case of disaster.
  • Test migrations to new platforms with non-vital records before migrating vital records to avoid data loss or data corruption.
  • Each time the live policy manual is migrated to a new system the superceded versions should be migrated as well.
  • Draft and submit a migration plan to the Arizona Department of Library Archives and Public Records, Records Management Division for review and approval in accordance with public records laws.

Campus-Wide Considerations:

  • Establish an official campus standard for HTML browsers and require compliance with the current W3 standard for any campus world wide web application involving official university records.
  • Establish an official campus standard for backup media and insure that campus vendors support that media. Review the standard and revise as necessary.
  • Investigate rules of evidence and chain of custody requirements regarding electronic records in Arizona and Federal courts.
  • Review and/or implement university policies regarding tampering or distribution of falsified university records.
  • Establish a permanent campus-wide consultative or enforcement body to review technological applications for creation of university records, monitor developments in migration technologies, evidential or audit requirements and assure compliance with state records laws.
  • June 1997, ADLAPR memo issued to “University and Community College Registrars” suggesting that “use of electronic systems for retention of permanent public records would be a violation of permanent media requirements.”
  • ADLAPR staff advised that policy manuals were already scheduled as permanent public records and that permanent media requirements applied.

Campus-Wide Considerations:

  • Graduate College interested in ETD’s
  • concerns about unauthorized course descriptions presented by departments on the web
  • email retention questions coming to my office

Campus-Wide Considerations:

  • Revised policy has an “immediate and significant impact on university employees or students that it must be communicated…”
  • Absence of policies will result in legal liabilities
  • Changes mandated by US or AZ Legislature, Regents or legal opinion with an immediate implementation date
  • Updates would be drafted and marked up by the content managers and sent to AAD for posting on the official site
  • AAD would print the update at the time of posting and send copies to Archives
  • AAD would reformat the new posting to the standard presentation at the next full revision cycle.

Other issues we addressed in this process:

  • Linking to external documents not controlled by ASU, e.g. Regents Policies
  • Chain of custody procedures for dating printouts and logging delivery to Archives
  • Disclaimer language for web presentation that official copy resides at Archives

Lessons Learned (I think):

  • HTML encoded documents and websites in themselves are effective access tools, but not effective recordkeeping systems
  • Decentralized client-server computing and the popular ethos of an unregulated internet make it much more difficult to establish information policy and recordkeeping standards.
  • Capturing the record of an interactive website is a matter of snapshot timing as well as format and content scope.
  • In some instances it may be acceptable to identify the text of a web document as the record and the html presentation of that text as non-record artistry.
  • Contextual information or metadata about snapshot and backup procedures and how they were executed is important in establishing authenticity of the record.
  • We don't yet understand the costs of web preservation, particularly for “permanent” records, and cannot effectively compare it to the costs of hardcopy publication, distribution and retention.
  • Should appearance and functionality be deemed essential elements of the record, use of proprietary software and html codes increases the risk of record loss or corruption.
