April 30, 2003

ASU launches global effort to study cities and growth

Arizona State University is taking its leading research on urban areas and environments global with the formation of the Consortium for the Study of Rapidly Urbanizing Regions.

The effort will combine ASU's successful environmental science and engineering research activities in metropolitan Phoenix, the most rapidly growing urban area in the United States, with other related studies and expand the work to global dimensions.

"More than ever before, the world is becoming urbanized," said ASU Center for Environmental Studies Director Charles Redman, who will direct the new consortium. "The greatest single challenge to the world today is the issue of rapidly urbanizing regions - having them develop so that they don't become seeds of poverty and terrorism, but sustainable places. Our work in this area has been strong and an expanded research partnership could have major impact."

The consortium is one of a series of new "use-inspired" research initiatives being developed by ASU President Michael Crow to apply fundamental research to pressing community, national and global problems.

"The policy decisions that we make today will determine the quality of our environment 100 years in the future," said Crow. "To think clearly about sustainability, we must understand the relationships between all the variables involving human interaction with natural systems-past, present, and future."

"Phoenix is the ideal platform for this initiative in that it is among the most rapidly urbanizing regions in North America," noted ASU Vice President for Research and Economic Affairs Jonathan Fink. "ASU leads the way with a variety of local, federally funded research programs whose results and approaches can be leveraged into national and global initiatives."

"Because of long-standing rapid growth, Phoenix is somewhat of a laboratory for determining how science can help solve human and environmental problems." said Rob Melnick, director of the Morrison Institute for Public Policy. "The power of an ASU-led international consortium of universities and businesses will foster the creation of a network for export solutions developed in Arizona to other regions. This will bring acclaim to greater Phoenix and put it in a national and international spotlight."

Along with launching the consortium, ASU released the Greater Phoenix 2100 Atlas, a demonstration regional atlas with maps and scientific information that will be a resource for analyzing and solving a range of regional problems. The atlas is the first product of Greater Phoenix 2100, an initiative to develop and assemble regional information and projections to help decision makers understand the very long-term (100-year) implications of current urban policies and issues.

Much of ASU's research in urban areas and environments has been conducted under the direction of the Center for Environmental Studies (CES). CES is home to the Central Arizona-Phoenix Long-Term Ecological Research (CAP LTER) project, one of only two urban sites in the National Science Foundation's system of ecological research centers. Since the creation of CAP LTER in 1997, the center has leveraged funding to become an extensive socio-environmental research enterprise, receiving NSF support for a new interdisciplinary graduate research program in urban ecology and for a new nationwide study of Agricultural Landscapes in Transition.

Other ASU projects related to urban research include satellite-based remote sensing studies of global urban environments, extensive educational outreach programs, cutting edge environmental informatics projects and the Greater Phoenix 2100 initiative.

The Greater Phoenix 2100 Atlas shows the extent of the issues that GP 2100 and related projects have been involved in studying. "This atlas is really a demonstration project to show many of the different concepts we are looking at," said Ray Quay, Assistant Director in the City of Phoenix's Water Services Department and GP 2100's former director.

The atlas's data, drawn from research conducted at ASU and by many federal, state and regional agencies, focuses on a broad range of relevant topics, including transportation, water supply, air quality, pollen and allergies, changing demographics, Hispanic education, housing affordability, the high-tech new economy, open space preservation, and the urban heat island. Each topic area includes a number of maps illustrating aspects of the issue, with an accompanying discussion and a brief essay on the topic by a local leader. The atlas also includes overview chapters that summarize basic information and look at future research directions.

Along with the printed document, there will also be an electronic version of the atlas that will be linked to CES's databases, and eventually to many government and community-based databases, where additional extensive and up-to-date information will be available.

Building on the work of separate ASU projects such as GP 2100, CAP LTER, and urban remote sensing with NASA's ASTER instrument, the Consortium for the Study of Rapidly Urbanizing Regions will bring a new focus on rapid urban development issues and apply a wide range of resources and expertise.

"The consortium will be involved in developing global partnerships while being based at ASU," explained Redman. "It will be a place where urban things at all scales will be thought about and pursued. Beginning next year, it will sponsor conferences and other events to engage the community locally, nationally and internationally. The goal is to be a world leader in this critical field of social and scientific study."
