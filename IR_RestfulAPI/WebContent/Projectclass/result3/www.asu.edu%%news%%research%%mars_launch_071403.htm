July 14, 2003

Opportunity launch presents anticipation for ASU, NASA

On June 28, 40 minutes before the projected midnight launch of Opportunity, the second Mars Exploration Rover that NASA plans to land on Mars early next year, mission scientist Philip Christensen was in a kitchen at a rented condo at Cape Canaveral, Florida busily cutting and serving a counter full of pies to hungry fellow scientists.

No, eating pie at midnight isn't a space launch tradition, just a matter of finding a way to cope with the tense minutes, hours and days of waiting for the launch. Christensen and his ASU colleague, mission scientist Jim Rice, were among a hundred or so people floating in and out of the room, passing the time until the much-anticipated launch.

After a seemingly endless series of delays, the waiting paid off as Opportunity was launched at 11:18 p.m., (EDT) July 7, from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station, Fla. Flight controllers at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena, Calif., received a signal from the craft 83 minutes later that it was out of the Earth's orbit and was on course toward Mars.

The launch of Opportunity is the culmination of years of work for many of the scientists involved, and their plans for future Mars study absolutely depend on everything going right at many stages during the six-month trip. Whether research careers will be made or not in the coming years requires that many difficult and technically complex hurdles be overcome, and the launch is the first big one. After that comes the trick of landing on a planet 70 million miles away.

It's not just any launch. The rocket carrying Opportunity to Mars is a Delta II Heavy and this is the design's inaugural flight. Though the engineers are confident, nothing is certain in spaceflight, particularly when something is being done the first time. Before the craft was finally sent into space there have been problems - the launch was delayed for four days by cracks that have appeared in the rocket's skin and for an additional two days by a battery problem.

Christensen is in charge of the lander's Mini Thermal Emission Spectrometer (Mini-TES), a high-tech robotic geologist capable of identifying minerals at a distance. It's the culmination of a series of similar instruments he has developed, and may have the biggest science payoffs yet. The Opportunity lander, expected to reach the planet Jan. 24, is planned to explore a Mars site known as Meridiani Planum, where a previous Christensen instrument on the Mars Global Surveyor spacecraft identified big fields of specular hematite, a mineral that forms on Earth in the presence of water.

The Opportunity launch follows the June 10 launch of its twin rover, "Spirit." As of July 7, Spirit had traveled 48 million miles. It is slated to reach Mars in early January and explore a giant crater halfway around the planet from Opportunity's landing site.
