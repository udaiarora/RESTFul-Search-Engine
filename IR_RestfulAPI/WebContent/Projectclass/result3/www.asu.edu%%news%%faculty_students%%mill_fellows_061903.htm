June 19, 2003

Millennium Fellows add new knowledge to multiple disciplines

Recipients of the Millennium Interdisciplinary Dissertation Fellowship (MIDF) personify research that can best be approached through multiple disciplines. ASU doctoral students Gram Jones and Eric Haas, winners of the 2003 fellowship, will add new knowledge to multiple fields with the interdisciplinary dissertations they plan to produce in the coming year. The Graduate College presents MIDF awards annually to promote interdisciplinary dissertation research at ASU.

Jones majors in physical anthropology and will combine that discipline with biomechanics in her dissertation work. Haas is majoring in educational leadership and policy studies and will bridge education, policy studies and English in his dissertation project.

"The Millennium Interdisciplinary Dissertation Fellowship supports groundbreaking, innovative interdisciplinary research by ASU doctoral students," said Marjorie Zatz, associate dean of the Graduate College. "These students have proven ability to master the concepts and skills of multiple disciplines and to effectively bridge these fields. They are conducting highly original research and potentially quite significant research."

Millennium Fellows are engaged in research with colleagues from different disciplines and from at least two different academic units on campus. They have already defended their dissertation proposals and will devote the coming year to the writing of their dissertations.

In recent years, conservative think tanks have emerged as significant reference sources for the news media. Haas will examine the presentation of think tank information in news reports on education in a study that should aid education policy makers and practitioners, the public and researchers in a variety of disciplines. He plans to focus his dissertation on the question, "How do the news media portray discourse about education that originates in 'think tanks,' especially conservative think tanks?"

Haas is studying 1,800 samples from newspaper and magazine articles and radio and television news transcripts, from the time period of 2001 - 02.

Along with Professor Mary Marzke, Jones has applied to the National Science Foundation for a grant to fund a comparative study of the hand and wrist of two primate species: the tufted capuchin and the common squirrel monkey. The question addressed by her dissertation work is whether there are unique morphological features of the capuchin hand that reflect the distinctive manipulative behaviors of these monkeys.

Jones will collect quantitative musculoskeletal data on the hand and wrist of the capuchin and squirrel monkey by using biomechanical techniques. Her dissertation work will provide new comparative primate data for identifying features of the human hand that may reflect ancestral feeding behaviors and help to tease out unique skeletal features that may have evolved in humans in response to stresses associated with stone tool-making.

John Chance, chairman in the anthropology department, said, "Gram Jones is doing cutting-edge research on a significant scientific problem that will interest researchers in several fields."
