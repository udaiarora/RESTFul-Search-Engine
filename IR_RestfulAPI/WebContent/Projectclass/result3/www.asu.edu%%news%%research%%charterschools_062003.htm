June 20, 2003

Morrison Institute report gives state's charter schools high marks

In the first major study ever done on the quality of charter school authorizing, 15 states earned B- or better grades for their authorizers' work but just four received similar marks for the policy environment within which charter schools and authorizers function. Massachusetts and Texas led the pack while California, Pennsylvania and New Mexico brought up the rear.

Arizona charter authorizers received an average grade of B for their chartering practices, placing them ninth among the 24 jurisdictions rated in a new study titled "Charter School Authorizing: Are States Making the Grade?" by the Thomas B.

Fordham Institute and Morrison Institute for Public Policy at ASU. Marks ranged from B+ (for the thoroughness of their school contracts) to B- (for the quality of their application processes and for sponsors' own transparency and accountability), based on judgments by knowledgeable observers of the Grand Canyon State charter scene. Arizona received a B+ for the charter-friendliness of its "policy environment," placing it first among the 24 jurisdictions. Overall, Arizona was ranked 3rd in the nation. The full text of the report and supporting data are available at (www.morrisoninstitute.org).

"Arizona's charter school landscape is friendlier than in most states. And according to local experts, such as the charter school operators themselves, Arizona's authorizers have created good processes," says ASU's Morrison Institute for Public Policy researcher Rebecca Gau, co-author of the report.

"Charter School Authorizing: Are States Making the Grade?" examines 23 states and the District of Columbia to determine how supportive they are of charter schools, how good a job their authorizers are doing, and how policy makers could strengthen their states' charter programs.

Charter authorizers are the bodies - they may be local or state school boards, universities, mayors' offices, city councils or non-profit groups - that give birth to these independently-operated public schools, monitor their performance, hold them to account for results, and decide whether to renew their charters. More than 500 such entities are now responsible for America's 2,700 charter schools, which enroll almost 700,000 students. The new study gathered data from nearly 900 people who are knowledgeable about authorizer performance in the 24 jurisdictions where most such schools are located.

"Many studies have looked at whether charter schools are succeeding," said Dr. Louann Bierlein Palmer of Western Michigan University, lead author of the study, "but almost nobody has examined the entities that are responsible for such schools - the groups on the other side of the charter contract. Using 56 criteria, Louann Bierlein Palmer and co-author Gau reached these conclusions:

. Most major authorizers are doing an adequate job but mounting red tape is a concern, since autonomy is a key to the success of charter schools;
. Many states are not supportive of charter schools and authorizers;
. States with fewer authorizers, serving more schools each, do a better job;
. States getting higher grades have authorizers that assist schools as well as overseeing them, and serve as advocates for the charter movement; and,
. Quality authorizing costs money - and many authorizers don't have much.

"Arizona got off to a remarkable start with charter schools," said Fordham Institute president Chester E. Finn, Jr. "and the state and its authorizers have generally done a solid job. But when our analysts asked knowledgeable Arizonans for a 'holistic' grade for the overall charter situation in their state today, they recorded a troubling 'B-.' It appears that there's plenty of room for improvement if policymakers want Arizona to retain its reputation as a leader of this promising education reform strategy."
