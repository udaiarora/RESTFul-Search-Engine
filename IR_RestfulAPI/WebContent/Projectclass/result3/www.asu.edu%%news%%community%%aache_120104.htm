December 2, 2004

Conference focuses on reorganization of state universities

Hispanic citizens from the surrounding higher education community focused on issues relating to the proposed statewide reorganization of universities and the impact on Hispanics during an annual conference Nov. 12.

The plenary discussion was part of the annual conference hosted by the Arizona Association of Chicanos for Higher Education (AACHE).

ASU President Michael Crow was joined by John Haeger, president of Northern Arizona University, and Rufus Glasper, chancellor of the Maricopa County Community College District, who addressed concerns and opportunities facing Arizona’s reorganization of universities.

“This university is committed to diversity,” Crow said during the conference. “The single most important factor is the quality of the university, and that comes down to the resources that are available to these (minority) students. Our efforts are focused on ensuring all students receive a quality education at ASU.”

In addressing how restructuring will affect Arizona, each panelist spoke about the changing demographics and offered institutional and personal perspectives regarding present challenges and possible solutions.

AACHE is a statewide Chicana and Chicano nonprofit organization. Founded in 1983, the group was established to promote equitable educational and employment opportunities within Arizona colleges and universities. Those goals are being achieved through faculty recruitment and retention efforts, scholarship programs, and forums for discussion of Chicana and Chicano higher education issues.

Eddie Castañeda, an AACHE member who serves as the representative for ASU’s Chicano Faculty and Staff Association, says this conference is the best venue for concerned Latinos to express their views and learn about issues surrounding access to higher education.

“The restructuring of Arizona’s universities has caused concern for many Latinos in the state,” he said. “We want to be able to respond and be more proactive to the changes. To do that, we need to study what’s going on and provide input at the state level.”

The annual conference also included plenary sessions focusing on admissions requirement changes, issues surrounding changes at ASU’s West campus, and a presentation by the Northern Arizona Task Force on Higher Education.

Castañeda says all institutions should examine how the restructuring is going to affect Latinos – and all minority students, too.

“We do not want this restructuring to decrease the availability of higher education to Latinos,” he says. “We want to make sure it’s enhanced and that any changes don’t limit this access to Latinos.”

For more information about AACHE, visit the Web at (www.aache.org).
