Cost of parking at ASU Main is going up

Taking a bite out of the increase

This article appears in the December, 2002, Academic Senate Newsletter.

It is bad enough when a pay increase is largely wiped out by increased payments required for services, such as health insurance, as we witnessed last year. It is even more aggravating not to receive any pay increase at all, yet still be asked to pay more for these services. An announced increase in parking for the next academic year is not welcome news, all the more so perhaps because we are not used to seeing this particular cost increase. It may be little solace that parking at ASU remains a bargain compared to other universities and to other places in the Valley. Table 1 below compares ASU with its sister institution to the south and with the PAC-10 and ASU peers, respectively. The intervals displayed for the PAC-10 and peers represent the average of the lowest fees and average of the highest fees across these institutions, including ASU, there often being a range of options for different lots, as there is here.

It is more than a psychological comfort, however, to know that the increase will be considerably cushioned by the ability to pay for parking with pre-tax dollars, resulting in a tax savings by reducing taxable income. Keep in mind that a reduction in taxable income affects both federal and state tax, as well as Social Security/FICA taxes. Table 2 illustrates the difference between the gross increase in parking and the net increase, after taxes, using a hypothetical 33% combined tax bite. That likely covers most who have a family income of $40,000 or more.

For Access A decal holders, this means that a substantial increase of $111 over the next three years nets out to only about $32, roughly $10 more each year. For Access B and perimeter parkers, the news is even better. Both groups experience a net decrease in the first year through tax recovery. In the case of many Access B holders, for example, the increase from $100 to $125 next year will see a tax savings of about $41, meaning that their net parking cost has been reduced by about $19. By the end of the three -year increase, most Access B holders will be out no more money than they were this year.

The ability to use pre-tax dollars to pay for parking allows Parking Services to gain the considerable additional revenue to provide additional parking, to maintain important services and improve in other areas. There aren't many alternatives for an administrative unit that must pay its own way, a working assumption for them for many years. Increased parking fines provide nowhere near the revenue needed to provide new spaces. Moreover, ASU will lose over 3,000 parking spaces to new construction in this year and next. And while it might be environmentally preferable to have fewer spaces to encourage alternative travel means into campus, the fact is that for now we're stuck with the current limited methods of travel reduction and we need to have parking spaces.

Most of us are aware of many of the services that the parking fee covers in addition to a parking space. The motorist assistance program and the FLASH shuttle are two of the more prominent services. In addition, Parking Services covers the costs of the call boxes in parking areas, temporary parking for new faculty and car rentals, and visitor lots. With the new revenues, we should see upgraded lighting in parking lots and a new 'hands free' gate arm system, along with, of course, new parking structures.

While the pre-tax payment of parking is certainly welcome, its impact is nonetheless regressive, that is, those with lower income reap less benefit. Perhaps, the University should consider holding harmless individuals below a given income level, much like the effort to hold certain students harmless from increases in tuition. This applies more to staff than faculty, but it seems that in these hard times, we particularly need to pay attention to ways that we can buffer our staff against increases in service costs, especially with the expectation that we are likely to see an increase in health costs around the corner.
