January 22, 2003

Lecture series focuses on African-American culture, history

The Committee on African and African American Research (CAAAR) will welcome a group of distinguished academics this spring through the CAAAR Lecture Series

Brenda Brock serves as assistant dean for ASU's College of Law and will kick off the free lunchtime lecture series at noon, Jan. 29 in the Memorial Union (MU), room 080, with "Africa's muse, America's Creation: Considering Phillis Wheatley's Double Bind."

The CAAAR lecture series has existed since the early 1990s. CAAAR also sponsors a variety of conferences, seminars, and other activities and coordinates instructional and research activities related to African or African-American Studies.

Professor Thomas Davis says the series offerings reflect the current research and interests of the presenters.

"The CAAAR lecture series aims at stimulating and sharing among the ASU and general community research and thinking on issues concerning Africans and African Americans," Davis says. "Lecture focuses demonstrate the values and utilities of researching contemporary issues of public concern as well as substantive and thematic issues within the academy and individual disciplines and areas."

Brock says her lecture will focus on the life of Wheatly (1754 - 84) who was the first African American to publish a book in the United States.

"Phillis Wheatley stood in a unique position in her time," Brock says. "Born in West Africa, forcibly removed and enslaved as a child, and raised in the United States, Wheatley's life was divided between assumptions about African Americans in colonial society and her place as an American colonial poet."

Andrew Barnes, ASU history associate professor will lecture April 17 about research he has conducted on Christian missions in colonial Africa.

Barnes says CAAAR provides a valuable arena for learning and discussion.

"The goal of CAAAR is to provide a venue for faculty and graduate students researching topics on peoples of African descent," Barnes says. "The lecture series is the one opportunity available at ASU for scholars to share ideas relating to such research."

Other lectures offered include: "Founding Figure Forsaken: Elizabeth Hudson Smith, Race, Class and Gender" by ASU History Professor Matthew Whitaker, Feb. 19; "The Politics of Applying Islamic Law in Nigeria" by ASU Religious Studies assistant professor Muhammad S. Umar, March 6; CAAAR 2003 Distinguished Lecture "The Black Arts Movement in Islam," delivered by Ethelbert Miller, director of the African American Resource Center at Howard University, March 12.

All lectures will be held from noon to 1 p.m. in MU 080 with the exception of the Distinguished Lecture which will be announced in the future.

For information, call Barnes at (480) 965-6291 or go to (http://www.asu.edu/clas/history/caaar).
