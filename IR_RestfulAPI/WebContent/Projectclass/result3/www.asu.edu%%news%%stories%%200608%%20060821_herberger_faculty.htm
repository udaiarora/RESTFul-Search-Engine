August 21, 2006

New faculty members take center stage for Herberger College

The School of Theatre and Film in the Herberger College of Fine Arts at ASU casts four new faculty members this fall.

Two will help launch the school's new concentration in film and media production of the bachelor of arts degree in film:

? Michael Switzer, a Los Angeles-based director, has directed more than 100 hours of long-form television movies during his career.

? Crystal Griffith most recently taught film and video at Columbia College in Chicago . Her background includes direction of numerous documentaries and work on several independent feature films and videos.

?I attended a conference on ?Ethics of Entertainment Media' at ASU and was impressed with the quality of the students, their enthusiasm and curiosity,? says Switzer. ?When I learned they were starting a film school, of course I wanted to be part of it.?

Also joining the faculty are two theater directors. They are:

? William Partlan, who directed more than 40 theater productions at the Cricket Theatre in Minneapolis and served as its artistic director. While there, he directed, developed and premiered ?Triple Espresso.? Since its debut, the hit play has been showcased throughout the United States and Europe .

? Rachel Bowditch, a New York-based director and scholar who founded her own movement theater company, Vessel, in 1996.

?I bring a myriad of physical movement training into my work as a director,? Bowditch says. ?As a scholar, my strengths are experimental/avant-garde performance, ritual theory and theories of directing.?

Bowditch has called New York home for the past seven years, but she spent her formative years living in places like Ethiopia , Indonesia , Israel , Italy and Singapore . She believes that the School of Theatre and Film should take more advantage of outdoor theater performance at ASU.

Switzer also is stirred by the open spaces. In addition to his lengthy list of television direction credits, documentary pieces that depict Indian reservation life also are of interest to him. He hopes to draw more Native American filmmakers ? from within the university, as well as the community ? into to the school.

?Not only are their stories interesting and unique, but I am convinced that, if shared and committed to film, these stories can make an incredible impact,? he says. ?Film provides everyone an opportunity to express ideas in a different and richer way than with just words alone.?

All four bring fresh ideas to their new positions, and each has a wealth of personal and professional experiences to share with ASU students.

When asked how she feels the new faculty will make an impact, Linda Essig, director of the School of Theatre and Film, says that each new member has the chance to contribute to the ongoing growth and development of the school, as it fulfills its mission to move the arts of theater and film into the future.
