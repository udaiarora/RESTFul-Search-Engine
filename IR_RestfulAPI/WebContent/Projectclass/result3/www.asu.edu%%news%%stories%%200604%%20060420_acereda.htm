April 20, 2006

Professor joins prestigious Spanish language academy

ASU professor of Spanish Alberto Acereda has a new role - and a new responsibility.

In February, he was unanimously elected a member of the Academia Norteamericana de la Lengua Española (North American Academy of the Spanish Language), the U.S. branch of the Real Academia Española (Royal Spanish Academy).

With membership comes the responsibility of helping protect the Spanish language and promote its use and its cultural and literary manifestation around the world, including the United States.

Acereda is the first ASU professor to be elected to the academy - and, at age 41, he is the youngest member of this prestigious institution in the United States, which has 35 members in the entire United States.

The Royal Spanish Academy was founded in 1714, Acereda says. The U.S. academy, chartered in 1973, is its most recent branch.

The main goal of the Royal Spanish Academy and its branches is to "protect the Spanish language, its grammar and vocabulary," Acereda says. "This is an important task. We want to educate people how to speak and use Spanish the proper academic way. While respecting the obvious need for English in the United States, the academy wants to defend the Spanish language and its use in the United States, as well as in the entire world."

All members of the academy must participate on two of its committees, Acereda says.

"The committees deal with such issues as translation, lexicography, grammar, education and technology," he says. "I am interested in the committees on education, technology and the Spanish language in the United States."

He already has delved deeply into technological issues, working on a project that will be part of the world-class "Biblioteca Virtual Miguel de Cervantes" (http://www.cervantesvirtual.com), the largest digital library in the Spanish language in the world.

Acereda says the United States has more Spanish speakers in the world than every county except Mexico. The formal study of Spanish is thriving in the United States , he adds, and ASU is in the forefront with more than 250 undergraduate students majoring in Spanish and about 100 master's or doctoral students.

Acereda's honor will bring more visibility to ASU, he hopes, and help take the Spanish doctoral program, which includes classes in Spanish language, literature and culture, from being in the top 40 in the National Research Council rankings to the top 20.

Acereda was born in Spain and earned his bachelor's degree in Hispanic philology at the University of Barcelona. He came to the United States in 1988 and received his master's and doctoral degrees at the University of Georgia.

He joined the ASU faculty in 1998 and is an associate professor of Latin-American literature and coordinator of the Spanish, Portuguese and Romanian division in the Department of Languages and Literatures.

Acereda has written five scholarly books on Spanish and Latin-American literature, and six books on Latin-American modernist poetry. He also is the editor of three monographs on the Central American modernist poet Rubén Darío, an important Latin-American writer who, Acereda says, "changed the course of Spanish poetry."

Acereda also is a well-known contributor of book reviews and op-ed pieces to the prestigious Spanish newspaper ABC in Madrid and Libertad Digital, the largest online Spanish newspaper in the world.
