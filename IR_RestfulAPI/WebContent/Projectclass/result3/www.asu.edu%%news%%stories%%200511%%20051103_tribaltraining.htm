November 3, 2005

ASU launches training program for tribal leaders

Initiative aims to help newly elected officials from across America

ASU has established an innovative national training program for newly elected American Indian officials.

The program, which will be housed at the university’s Tempe campus, is being coordinated by Ivan Makil, former president of the Salt River Pima-Maricopa Indian Community, and is under the direction of ASU’s Office of Public Affairs. It parallels a training program the university runs with the League of Arizona Cities & Towns for newly elected non-tribal leaders.

The tribal leadership program is open to American Indian leaders throughout the country and will launch in the fall of next year.

“By establishing this initiative at ASU’s Office of Public Affairs, the university is formally recognizing tribes as governments,” Makil says. “This is a critical, but little understood, key public message.”

He continued that, as tribes have become more self-sufficient within the last 20 years, tribal structures have evolved significantly. In an effort to meet the growing needs of their people, tribal leaders must deal with many of the same issues that non-Indian elected officials face, he says.

The program will address the need for newly elected leaders to get “quickly up to speed” when they take office. According to Makil, the majority of newly elected tribal leaders have not had an opportunity to experience government in all its complexity.

“Tribal leaders usually are voted in because they are championing a specific issue,” he says. “But very few have had prior political experience, which can become problematic when they begin to work within the system. By presenting them with a discussion that defines the status of tribes and examines the relationship of tribes to state and federal governments, this new program will dramatically reduce the learning curve.”

The curriculum includes sessions on government structure, ethics, legislation, budgeting and management of natural resources, among other topics. Well-known presenters and panelists with governmental and leadership experience will add their expertise.

An advisory committee has been appointed to provide more resources. In addition to several ASU officials, members of the committee include:

• Sheila Morago, executive director of the Arizona Indian Gaming Association.

• John Lewis, executive director of the Inter-Tribal Council of Arizona.

• Derrick Watchman, president of J.P. Morgan Chase Bank.

“We are very excited to be hosting this timely and sensitive program,” says Sandra Ferniza, ASU executive director of community development. “Throughout the country, as development is redrawing the landscape, many once-remote reservation lands now border cities or suburbs. With closer physical proximity, comes the realization that shared problems that can only be solved by all the affected governments working together. This new ASU program for tribal leaders will help meet that need for strengthening government-to-government relationships.”

“This partnership with ASU not only will benefit tribes, but it also will have a positive impact on neighboring communities, states and the nation,” Makil says.
