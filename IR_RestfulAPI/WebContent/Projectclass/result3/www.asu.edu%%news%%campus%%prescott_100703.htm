October 7, 2003

Dr. Edward C. Prescott joins ASU's W. P. Carey School of Business; best-of-the-best pushes research frontiers in economics

Dr. Edward C. Prescott, whose work has shaped current thinking in macroeconomics and whose doctoral students are stars at universities nationwide, joined the economics faculty of the W. P. Carey School of Business at Arizona State University effective Oct. 3.

Prescott is one of a small circle of scholars who have altered the course of macroeconomic thinking in the past three decades. His research spans many areas, including business cycles, economic development, general equilibrium theory, and finance, and has addressed some of the most important long-standing questions in economics.

With the exception of one year spent at the University of Chicago, Prescott has been on the faculty at the University of Minnesota since 1980, most recently holding the titles of Regents Professor and McKnight Presidential Endowed Professor in Economics. He is also a senior advisor in the research department at the Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis, a position he will retain while on the faculty at the W. P. Carey School of Business. In 2002 he was awarded the Erwin Plein Nemmers Prize in Economics, a prestigious honor generally reserved for economists believed to be future contenders for the Nobel Prize.

He earned a Ph.D. in economics from Carnegie-Mellon University and a master of science in operations research at Case-Western Reserve University. His undergraduate degree in mathematics was awarded by Swarthmore College.

"We can remember the way we thought about the issues before Prescott's analysis, and the comparison with the way we think about them now gives a measure of the enormous effect each paper has had on our thinking," said Dr. Robert E. Lucas Jr., 1995 Nobel Laureate in Economics, who worked with Prescott on research projects in the early '70s. "Prescott's papers met with resistance, but in the end he has caused us to rearrange important parts of our vision of how the economy works, to start over in many respects."

Prescott is also revered for his teaching, especially at the graduate level. "His students don't learn to be acolytes of Ed Prescott," Lucas said. "They learn to be excellent researchers, to think for themselves."

ASU President Michael Crow has made economics a high priority in his efforts to raise the stature of the university. "The university recognizes that a concentration of economic thinkers is a vital component to a university that leads intellectually," said President Crow. "ASU is determined to build such an economics department in the W. P. Carey School, and has pledged significant new resources to make it happen."

Dr. Richard Rogerson, the Rondthaler Chair in Economics at the W. P. Carey School, was one of Prescott's students.

"Prescott is committed to graduate education," Rogerson said. "At some of the most famous universities the high profile professors have little time for students. Prescott is just the opposite. He spends lots of time with students, individually and in small groups, mentoring them and pushing them to think. That is the culture we are trying to build here."

Prescott's arrival comes shortly after the W. P. Carey School's $50 million naming endowment, the second largest single gift ever given to a U.S. business school. The appointment also coincides with the school's strategic initiative to build up doctoral studies, with the goal of elevating the program to peer status with the top 25 nationally.

"Hiring Edward Prescott is the W.P. Carey School's boldest move in its effort to attract world-class faculty. He epitomizes the type of faculty that are the foundation of a great university," said Dr. Philip R. Regier, interim dean. "The impact of his presence will be felt in the caliber of faculty who will strive to be associated with him, and the talent of doctoral candidates who will compete to study with him."

Department Chairman Arthur Blakemore said that the energy created by the W. P. Carey School naming, and the firm support of the university administration helped to attract Prescott to ASU. He called Prescott "the key building block."

"He is the ideal building block for us because of his superlative professional record. He has challenged the conventional thinking in the economics profession in the ways that only the elite do," Blakemore said. "We also wanted Ed Prescott for his absolute dedication to the profession of economics research and economics education. A large number of the top macroeconomists in the country have studied under Ed Prescott and have been inspired to great achievements in their profession by him. Now students at ASU will have this unique opportunity."

W. P. CAREY SCHOOL OF BUSINESS

The W. P. Carey School of Business at Arizona State University provides quality undergraduate, masters and doctoral programs that draw upon a dynamic learning environment to prepare students at all levels for the future. Internationally recognized faculty enrich the curriculum with their latest research findings, and deliver this knowledge in a technology-rich environment. Ranked 21st in the nation by U.S. News & World Report, the undergraduate program features Business Honors comprised of some of the best students in the country. The W. P. Carey MBA, ranked 17th in the nation among public programs, combines business fundamentals with marketplace issues and trends, giving graduates the knowledge and experience to excel in a global economy. W. P. Carey School of Business alumni are lifelong learners connected to the school and each other through an active alumni community and executive education opportunities.
