July 25, 2003

Public research investments paying dividends, report shows

New university-based research efforts in biodesign, nanotechnology, embedded systems and virtual manufacturing show that Arizona has stepped forward to compete in the knowledge economy, according to a newly released study by Morrison Institute for Public Policy, a unit of ASU.

The institute’s 44-page report, “Seeds of Prosperity: Public Investment in Science and Technology,” uses the research projects that ASU initiated with voter-approved Proposition 301 funds as a “lens” for understanding the value of science and technology research to Arizona’s economy. It also introduces a novel measurement framework (“CAT measures” – the connections, attention and talent the investments produce for the state) designed to assess the lasting economic value of such research for the state and region.

"This report is meant to stimulate thinking about public investment strategy and policy for economic growth,” said Rob Melnick, director of Morrison Institute. “We are developing the CAT measures to help state leaders get their arms around the many kinds of impacts from university science and technology research so they can better assess the state’s return on investment.”

The authors of the report note that technological innovation often leads to long-term economic development and competitive advantage for a region, and that most knowledge businesses rely on universities to conduct cutting-edge research that fuels innovation. Morrison Institute’s CAT measures provide a new model for assessing what have previously been viewed as “intangibles” – the important connections, attention and talent created by high-tech research that provide the fertile ground from which next generation technological innovation springs.

The report shows that the potential payoff for public investments in university research is huge. For example, “Seeds of Prosperity” finds:

• Biotech research – based primarily on molecular and genetic research – has already created a $39 billion industry, and recent genomics discoveries are expected to accelerate further growth;

• Nanotechnology – the science of manipulating matter at the atomic level – has been dubbed “the next big thing” in technological innovation, and has been forecast by the National Science Foundation to grow into a $1 trillion market by 2015; and

• The rapidly expanding information and communications technology industry is estimated to employ at least 10 million high-wage U.S. workers, and has accounted for nearly a third of recent U.S. economic growth.

The report cautions that simply investing in science and technology research is not enough to guarantee future economic growth. Nearly every other state in the country has also committed big investments toward high-tech science research in order to capitalize on the high-wage jobs and economic stimulus it promises. Long-term public investments have been running into the hundreds of millions, yet not every state can be a leader.

“The stakes are high,” says Melnick, “and the competition is hot. But few people even have any idea what these endeavors are all about.”
“Seeds of Prosperity” helps demystify some of today’s cutting-edge science and technology fields by explaining their economic context. It also describes the current consolidation phase of these projects in which multiple independent research efforts are integrated into large, interdisciplinary “mega-projects.”

“Proposition 301 funding is unquestionably enabling Arizona’s universities to significantly increase their competitiveness in scientific research and innovation,” said Dr. Jeffrey Trent, president and scientific director of Phoenix-based TGen, the Translational Genomics Research Institute. “The Morrison Institute’s report provides an important economic context for the universities as well as public institutions to evaluate how to best take advantage of this historic opportunity. The report also recognizes the importance of public-private partnerships in conducting science-technology research and the need to be accountable for the public’s investment.”

According to the report, ASU’s projects have nearly doubled the state’s investment by attracting $14 million in outside grants. To determine whether this investment will be a long-term economic development success, Morrison Institute analysts recommend that such research initiatives be regularly and systematically assessed not only on the products they develop and the external funding they generate, but also on the CAT measures.

“Collectively,” says Melnick, “these assessments will provide a unique way to measure economic impacts so that public investments can be held accountable.”

The Morrison Institute for Public Policy is an ASU resource for objective public policy research and analysis. A part of the School of Public Affairs in the College of Public Programs, Morrison Institute brings university scholarship and public policy development together for the benefit of Arizonans.

“Seeds of Prosperity: Public Investment in Science and Technology” is available online at (www.morrisoninstitute.org).
