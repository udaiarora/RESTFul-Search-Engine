NATIONAL SCIENCE FOUNDATION RECOGNIZES ARIZONA STATE UNIVERSITY SCHOLARS

Arizona State University announces that 24 present and former students have been honored by the National Science Foundation (NSF). Seven students won Graduate Fellowship Awards and 17 received Honorable Mentions.

ASU Graduate Dean Bianca L. Bernstein will determine the winners of future NSF Graduate Fellowships, among other awards, after recently accepting a two-year appointment as NSF director of graduate education. "My division at NSF administers this award program," said Dean Bernstein, who will begin her new duties in Arlington, VA in the near future. "My presence at NSF will be quite beneficial to ASU. I want to encourage ASU students to maintain their high level of excellence in research and in competing for the prestigious NSF Graduate Fellowships."

These awards represent the National Science Foundation's most prestigious recognition of students, said Gregory Raupp, associate vice president for research at ASU. "Our students will work with distinguished research faculty mentors and will engage in cutting-edge research in important topics ranging from human perception to new technologies for water purification. Our ASU graduates will pursue their studies at top research institutions across the country. We look forward to watching these student cohorts as they advance the boundaries of knowledge throughout their careers."

The National Science Foundation seeks to support students and their research in science, mathematics and engineering in the U.S. The NSF Graduate Fellowships offer recognition and three years of support for advanced study to approximately 900 outstanding graduate students in the mathematical, physical, biological, engineering and behavioral and social sciences, including the history of science and the philosophy of science, and to research-based Ph.D. degrees in science education. Awards carry a stipend of $27,500 per year and an annual cost-of-education allowance of $10,500, paid to Fellows' institution in lieu of tuition and fees.

ASU students, currently at the graduate and undergraduate levels, who received Fellowships are:
Vladas Griskevicius, a Ph.D. student in social psychology, is conducting research on persuasion and compliance. He studies how people perceive the world around them and what kinds of things affect their seemingly ordinary or unusual beliefs and decisions. Regents' Professor Robert Cialdini serves as academic advisor for Griskevicius.

Cinnamon Jill Hayes will begin a Ph.D. program at ASU in biology this fall. Her research interests focus on interactions between fungal plants living within host plants. She plans to examine whether infection reduces host plant susceptibility to natural enemies such as fungal diseases and native insect herbivores and seed predators. Professor Stan Faeth is Hayes' advisor.

Sophia Kenrick is an undergraduate student in ASU's chemical engineering program who plans to do research related to bioengineering and biomaterials at the University of California, Santa Barbara. "I am excited about doing research that has the potential to benefit so many different aspects of society, from manufacturing to medicine," she said. "This fellowship will allow me to take advantage of every possible opportunity I have in graduate school and to do good in the world."

Alison Polasik earned an undergraduate engineering degree in materials engineering at ASU and now pursues a graduate degree at Ohio State University. She worked with Professor James Adams on his textbook for an introductory, sophomore-level course. "She did an excellent job and impressed me with the depth of her understanding of materials engineering," Adams said. Polasik served as vice-president of the undergraduate materials engineering society. Her senior design project involved materials selection for equipment to sequester carbon dioxide in various minerals.

Justin Pucci, an undergraduate in aerospace engineering, conducts research in electric propulsion. "I have been fortunate to have several formative experiences at ASU that led me to receive this award and pursue this line of research," Pucci said. "First, in my freshman year, I worked with Dr. Robert Culbertson and Dr. Nicole Herbots at the Center for Solid State Science. Next, I was able to study acoustics research with Dr. Valana Wells, which led me to research in electric propulsion with Dr. Pavlos Mikellides. Quite possibly the most influential research experience I have had was my independent research regarding hybrid rocket engine combustion instability, which resulted in the publishing and presentation of my professional paper at the American Institute of Aeronautics and Astronautics (AIAA) Joint Propulsion Conference in 2002." Pucci has worked in the ASUSat student satellite program and is president of ASU's chapter of the AIAA. He is also a founding member and the project director of ICARUS, an innovative rocket program that will soon be making history in aerospace. He would like to study hall thruster plasma physics at MIT in graduate school.

Graduate student Robin Roth is working with Dr. James Beckman, one of the preeminent researchers in water purification and desalination. "My work with Dr. Beckman's group is very exciting," Roth said. "Water is becoming an increasingly important global issue, and we can make a difference to people who live in areas where sources of clean water are not available."

Nick Schmerr, a Ph.D. student in geological sciences, uses seismology to image and interpret regions of heterogeneity within the Earth's interior. These seismic observations help provide an understanding of what mineralogies and compositions are present within the deep Earth, and what types of processes take place in the interior of other planets. Schmerr's academic advisor is Dr. Ed Garnero.

ASU students receiving Honorable Mention in the competition for NSF graduate fellowships are: Jasmin Abreu, psychology; Sarah Baran, psychology; Joshua Billar, chemical engineering; Nicholas Brindley, mechanical engineering; Jeffrey Dailey, materials science; Jonathan Davis, physiology; Matthew Desmond, sociology; Susannah French, biology; Kathleen Faccia, anthropology; Matthew Fritz, psychology; Andrea Jani, ecology; Gwynne Johnston, physiology; Amy McAdam, geosciences; Katie McLaughlin, psychology; Brandon Moore, neuroscience; Adam Hartstone-Rose, anthropology; and Mason Thompson, archaeology.
