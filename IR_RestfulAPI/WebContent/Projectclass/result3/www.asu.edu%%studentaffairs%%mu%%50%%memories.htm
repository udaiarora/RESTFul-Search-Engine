Read Memories

The following memories about the Memorial Union have been submitted by ASU alumni and community members:

"I was involved in MU committees and then with the then brand-new MUAB throughout my four undergraduate years. Cecilia Scoular, that lovely lady who was the original director and was still there my first two years, called the MU "our home away from home"....we all kind of joked about that, but whether we were just passing through for a doughnut or listening to music in one of the lounges, or coming for a meeting or a movie....deep down, we knew she was right! Great memories with so many people -- Mrs. Scoular, Mrs. Thomas, Gay Holliday, Rick Eden, Roseanne Cartledge, Christine Wilkinson, and then all the students I met during those years because I was involved with the MU. I just want to recognize and express my gratitude to all of them on this wonderful anniversary."
- Layna Taylor, '75, '80

"I vividly remember Jan. 28, 1986 (is that the right year?) when I walked into the open TV room on the first floor and everyone was watching the space shuttle take off then explode. I remember wondering why I was so shaken when I didn't know the people but I cried for them and their families. Then I had to go back to work at the Business Advising counter and felt sad the rest of the day. It was surreal to watch someone lose their lives right before your eyes and then go on like everything was normal and nothing happened. That date (Jan. 28) is also my only brother's birthday. Not a great moment, but I've never forgotten it. It had an emotional impact upon me. I do remember studying in the quiet room so long that I would fall asleep on the comfy couches. Then a piano was put in there and I'd listen to the people playing instead of studying. Once, I wore a Venezuelan t-shirt to school and I was in the lower level MU at the copy counter, when a girl passed by and asked me where I got the t-shirt. I told her I had visited Venezuela and my husband was from there. We invited her to meet more of our Venezuelan friends and she ended up marrying the cousin of our good friends, the Gonzalez family. Rosa Gonzalez (her aunt-in-law) still works as ASU. She lives in Texas and is happily married with a baby boy."
- Kari Bowman, '86

"The MU was the place where my friend, Angie and I would meet in between classes. We would like to sit on the comfy couches and just people watch. The MU also allowed us to take naps during finals!!"
- Olga De La Torre, '92

"As an incoming freshman, I remember my very first visit to the Memorial Union - it was for Orientation in the Spring. I remember the building being really large and sitting in the Arizona Ballroom listening to a myriad of speakers. Little did I know that four years later, my home away from home would be the Memorial Union and the offices of ASASU. I remember very fondly planning Homecoming as an undergraduate and working with students and advisors who would become lifelong friends. Storing over 5,000 gold leis' for a Homecoming celebration or painting signage that would hang on the bridge over University Drive are the many things we all worked on inside the Memorial Union. The MU truly was our home and even as I come back and visit as an alumnus, the first place I go is always the Memorial Union!"
- Tom Studdert, '98

"When I first attended ASU in the early 1980s, I was an aeronautical engineering technology (AET) major, headed into the Navy after graduation to fly jets. I was a full-time student, working part-time at nights, and in flight training almost all other times in between -- except for about one hour per week when I could get away to relax. The place was the lower level of the Memorial Union, where for about 75 cents I'd play three games this stupid Star Wars-like video game called Gorf. I beat the "aliens" so many times that I wound up with more than three games, and had to leave the machine running for the next round of students, while I raced out to go fly or work. The MU, simply, was my escape and my sanctuary. It was also a place of refuge. When I returned in 2004 as an adult student, the MU was still there. The video games were still there, but not Gorf. That's OK. What stood out two decades later was how much this place seemed like "home." If I were 18 all over again, the MU would be everything to me -- the place to eat, socialize and study. Why would anyone need anything other than a ratty studio apartment to sleep in, when they have the MU for everything else?"
- Ben Winton, '82, '06

"I remember when you could drive past the Union when College Avenue was a thru street to University. I also remember when the ASU Bookstore was in the basement of the MU. I have enjoyed the Union as a student and a staff member. Happy Birthday!"
- Linda Van Scoy, '71

"My very first memory of the MU was when I was still a freshmen attending ASU east (now Polytechnic campus). I had to drive to the Tempe campus in order to get my tuition payment credited. I got lost driving around on Mill Avenue and finally just parked anywhere I could find a spot. I was told by one of the ASU staff to just walk until I found a building called the MU. It was a big brick building and there should be a lot of students in or around it. I finally found it and thought wow! how cool would it be to go to Tempe campus and be able to chill here everyday with my friends. That next year I did attend Tempe campus and hung out with my friends at the MU. We would usually be on the patio outside or in the fireplace lounge area. Burger King and Jamba Juice were the hot places to eat and Hoodlums was the place to get your new CD at! I had great times at the MU and never will forget my first visit to it! Happy Birthday MU! keep it up!"
-Jenifer Glavan-Lind, '05

"I worked at ASU from 1968-1989. They were wonderful years and I remember how much I enjoyed the MU -- especially the annual Holiday breakfast. It was the one time each year that I would see folks from across campus -- it was always a great 'reunion day.' My high school prom was held in the ballroom in 1959. That's how old I am! I remember playing on a bowling team back in the 'good ole days.' Until I read the site, I forgot that I had participated. Although I've been gone since 1989, ASU and the MU still occupies a place in my heart. Congratulations on 50 years of service to not only students, but faculty and staff alike.
- Barbara Hallack

"I remember the MU being the place to go and have a cup of coffee with friends. I really enjoyed the peace in quiet during the long afternoons between classes."
- Steven Goitia, '87

"The MU was an excellent escape to socialize, study in a great setting and create lasting memories."
- Dan Dangelo, '92, '02

"I remember when I first started going to ASU. My mom worked at Hayden Library, where I also worked as a student worker in between classes. I was 17 years old in the Fall of 1986. I used to meet her for lunch. I also used to meet my friends at the lower level of the MU to study - People used to be able to smoke down there and I would go home after studying all afternoon smelling like I was at a bar! - I don't and never smoked. There used to be a burger place down there, that later became a Burger King - The first fast food joint on campus! I also remember studying in the quiet room - much better than the basement...I also remember on ocassion getting an apple fritter and a chocolate milk for breakfast at the small cart/bakery in the hall that crosses the MU from North to South. I also remember the long lines that used to form on the second floor of the MU for early registration and eating lunch in the Pima room. It's hard to believe! That it was 20 years ago - I don't feel that old and the numbers sound so big! I had a lot of good times over the years and during my time at ASU and the MU was definitely a place where we hung out a lot. Those were the days....I also remember the small movie theater in the basement -- I think there's a gift shop (or there used to be) in its place now. I saw "9 and a Half Weeks" there!! There used to do improv/stand up comedy down there also."
- Mariselle Gonzalez, '91, '96

"My first job in Arizona was at the Recreation Center in the Memorial Union. While I was only a part timer down there, I have fond memories of the nice folks that I associated with down there including staff members Terry, Dee, Bruce G., Ralph V., and Cliff. The MU was truly at the heart of the ASU student body ready to serve. Happy Anniversary ole MU! Go Sun Devils!"
- David Major, '80

"My most vivid memory of the MU occured on November 22, 1963...I was walking by the front and there was a car parked (in those days you could still drive through campus) with its radio going and the passenger door was open with some students listening...I went over and heard the news that president kennedy had just been shot...I went inside the mu and they had a color TV in a lounge that was turned on to the shooting and many students had already gathered to watch...so while my memory of the MU is very vivid, it is not of a positive nature..."
- Steve Gottschalk

"I remember when the MU had cigarette vending machines. Boy that was a ways back."
- Gary Gradowski

"My only comment was regarding the Memorial Union 50th anniversary and a couple of memories: I walked to school (Mt. Carmel School, the brick building where the art annex is in front of the swimming pool) from home on Mill Ave. down College Avenue through the ASU campus and watched the construction of the MU every day. I was fascinated by the amount of brick-laying that went in to the project. I thought that I may want to someday be a brick-layer. Another memory was the annual MU Birthday parties probably in the early to mid sixties. A friend and I would go every year and next to Christmas was our favorite day of the year. We had some wild times!"
- Tom Voss

"I remember going to the MU just to relax and people watch, when you got tired by the fountain, while waiting in line in the early mornings for the fresh bagels that were so hot in the 1980's. Sorority rush time in the MU!"
- Kimberly Saban Anglim (Kimberly Ann Saban), '82

"The MU was the social hub of my college years. I value the range of cultural experiences I received through the concert and lecture series: the likes of Max Ernst, Agnes Moorehead, Les Brown and his band of renown, Merce Cunningham, and Jose Limon. It wasn't until after I later moved to NYC that I understand the depth of the talent that ASU brought to my front door. I also remember fondly my time as a Freshman Hostess, wearing white gloves as I handed out programs at these events. Another highlight of the MU was the "MU Birthday Party" era: I was involved in organizing 2 or 3 (San Francisco and an Elizabeth Theme - Footprints on the Sands of time or something like that). These were amazing and wonderful events - turning the building into a playland built around one theme each year. Lots of energy, team building, and an amazing learning experience were central to these events. I treasure those times and remember so very fondly the kindness and friendship of Cecilia Scoular and Trudy Thomas, who were committed to making the MU the best that it could be."
- Brenda Shears

"I spent many days in the lower level of the MU when the offices of MUAB (Memorial Union Activities Board) were housed there. A weekly ritual among the staff members of MUAB was to bowl (we were a makeshift league of sorts) and then retreat to the TV lounge to watch "Moonlighting". The MU continues to grow and change but still manages to maintain that "living room" feeling -- a home away from home and a great community space. Happy Birthday to the Memorial Union!"
- Melissa Werner, '89

"Back in the 80s when the Memorial Union Activities Board (MUAB) existed, I spent a LOT of time down in the offices across from the bowling alley. I was the VP of Membership my junior year and we hung out and worked down there from early in the morning until late, late at night. I have fond memories of napping on the couches, using the KROY machine to create posters and flyers, and participating in MUAB events. I made many great friends that year, most of whom I've lost touch with over the years. It was some of the most meaningful days of my college experience at ASU."
- Laurie McCarty Ralston

"I remember November 22, 1963, as if it were yesterday. I was having lunch in the ballroom of the MU and listening to a concert by the ASU Symphony Orchestra. At the end of the concert, the conductor informed the large student audience that Pres. Kennedy had been assassinated in Dallas, Texas. People streamed out of the ballroom crying or stunned in disbelief. One girl fell down the stairs and hurt herself (we later found out she had broken her leg). Everyone was in such a state of shock that for quite a while, no one stopped to help her. We all stood for the longest time in front of the Union as if in a daze, a very bad dream. It was surreal. It was as if we were afraid to leave the MU for fear the world was falling apart. It isn't a pleasant memory, but it is etched on my brain forever. I used to work in the catering department at the MU and waited tables in the faculty dining room. I spent lots of time there and made many wonderful friends. It was my home away from home. I am so glad to hear it is still there. I would love to come back someday."
- Carol Brown, '68

" Our wedding reception on July 19, 1975, was held in the MU ballroom with the assistance of Mrs. Trudy Thomas, who, along with Mrs. Scoular, was an MU "Mom" to us. All branches of student government were very much part of MU life. Mrs. Thomas knew us all and although I graduated in 1968, she remembered me when I came back in 1975 and she helped us arrange our reception, after a wedding in Danforth Chapel."
- Jacqueline Butler-Diaz

"The MU is the "capital" of Arizona State University; the "Grand Lodge"; the "urban core"; the MU was - a place to go to either after a class, or before a class, after finals or before, a place to "grab" something to eat, a snack, or a cup of coffee - a place to find comfort food, a place to see movies, find a ride or rider to California, a place to sit by a fireplace, a place to take a catnap - de-stress, to meet friends, to play in a backgammon tournament, or, my very special experience - to see a renowned speaker (as I saw William F. Buckley, Jr. speak - I read his book, watched his TV program, yet never met him in person as I did that night in 1986). Indeed, to me, the MU is the "gateway" to the rest of the great University. Thank you for all the memories."
- Peter Hovis

"The MU was the place to be. You could get food, postal stamps, music CD's, a date, plastic utensils for the dorm, or just simply catch some zzzz's downstairs while watching the big screen."
- Phillipe Angel Garcia

"Shortly after I transferred to ASU in the early 80's, I visited the MUAB office in the lower level of the MU to find out about involvement opportunities. I signed up for a non-credit wine appreciation class, which was held at the Howard Johnson's on Apache (now the Twin Palms). That's where I met my husband (we'll be married 20 years in July). Years later when I started working at ASU, my office was located in the MU lower level -- in the old MUAB area."
- Linda Sullivan, '84, '86, '95

"The MU was the heart of my life at ASU, and the experience I remember most fondly. A shout out to Gay Holliday and Rick Eden, who I haven't seen in 30 years--and would love to!"
- Joy Rothke, '74

"My first memory of the MU is when my mother started work as a cook in the Cafeteria (now Union Stage). We would come and visit her for lunch it was a big thing for a 9-year-old. Later on we had our high scchool prom in the Arizona Room and we also used the Starlight Terrace. We all thought how great it was to be on the university campus. Somehow the Memorial Union has always played a part in our family's life. It was even more special when my daughter had her wedding reception in the Arizona Room 13 years ago. Now I have the pleasure of reserving space for others."
- Sheila Garcia
