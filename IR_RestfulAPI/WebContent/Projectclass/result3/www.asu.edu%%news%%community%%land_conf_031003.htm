March 10, 2003

Conference considers issue of land use, conservation, design

Cities in the southwest face major questions daily on how to deal with drought, urban development and land conservation. To help better educate those decision makers and the public they serve, a collaboration of ASU groups is hosting an innovative conference March 22 at the ASU Downtown Center.

"The Day the Desert Stood Still: Lessons for People and Communities in Desert Regions," will bring together architects, designers, planners, ecologists and scientists to discuss land use, design and preservation throughout the Southwestern region. The all-day conference is part of an overall series of events designed to look at the topic, "Design with the Desert," which included a large public forum in fall 2002 at Gammage Auditorium and a similar conference in 2001.

"The rapid pace of development of Southwest cites brings about a whole set of problems and challenges for communities and people concerned with sustaining a healthy relationship between the natural landscape and the emerging urban center," says event organizer Richard Malloy, a horticultural specialist in the plant biology department. "This conference is bringing together professionals dedicated to make a difference in the region by presenting lessons learned from experiences from desert research, planning and community development that make sense for the region we live in."

The event will feature three concurrent sessions throughout the day. Participants will be able to pick and choose their areas of interest. Malloy says the various sessions build from the theme of the basic elements of soil, water and plants early in the day, then concentrate on the overall ecosystem. Later sessions will focus on people and the environment with the final topics addressing creativity in human design of cities, landscape and structures.

Malloy said the key to the conference is to present a collaborative educational environment in lieu of building the event around a single keynote speaker.

Charles Redman, director of ASU's Center for Environmental Studies (CES), one of the event's sponsors, says the conference will be an important opportunity for researchers to share ideas. But perhaps more importantly, it will give the community a chance to become involved in the process.

"We at the Center for Environmental Studies are very enthusiastic about the Design with the Desert conference because it will bring together ASU scientists with public officials and community members to discuss pressing issues," Redman says. "Much of the science we do may seem disconnected from the community, but it is through meetings like this we are able to share ideas with the community and learn their perspective as well."

The main sponsors of the event are the School of Planning & Landscape Architecture, the Herberger Center for Design Excellence, the applied biological sciences department at ASU East and the CES.

The conference is open to the public with no admission charge. Due to an overwhelming response to the event, pre-registration is being accepted. For information, call (480) 965-0451, e-mail (desertconference@asu.edu) or visit the Web at (http://ces.asu.edu/desert).

Campbell, with Media Relations & Public Information, can be reached at (480) 965-7209 or (garycamp@asu.edu).
