March 18, 2003

Outreach efforts pay off for students, employees

Many graduating high school seniors would like to attend college, but for various reasons many of them don't take the necessary steps to enroll or don't know what they need to do in order to begin their college career.

ASU's Undergraduate Admissions Office is making an effort to change that by providing prospective students the experience and encouragement to make their dream of attending college a reality.

Ken Hollin, assistant director of student recruitment in Undergraduate Admissions, says it's important for students and their parents to start preparing for college early.

"One of the most challenging things is to get students motivated for higher education at an early age," says Hollin, who has been working with ASU's student recruitment efforts for more than 20 years. "Students can't wait until the last two years of their high school careers to start preparing for higher education. They need to build a strong, solid foundation way before they reach their senior year."

The Undergraduate Admissions Office offers many programs designed to give prospective students an opportunity to learn more about higher education. These programs cover the necessary steps for attending ASU, including: application forms, transcripts and test scores, financial aid and scholarships, housing, immunization, registration, dining, parking and tuition payment.

In addition, one-on-one counseling also provides the opportunity to address concerns that students may have, including financial assistance.

"Our office has always worked with students who need financial assistance," says Stacey Kostell, associate director of undergraduate admissions. "Our staff is in the high schools regularly, so we have been able to prepare and educate the high school counselors that we work with, about the recent tuition increase and how a large portion of the increase will go toward assisting students with financial need."

Kostell says outreach programs are a strategic approach to the office's recruitment efforts, which have proven to be a success. In fact, during the 2001 - 02 academic year, early outreach programs brought 1,344 eighth grade students from across the state to ASU to participate in team building, tour the campus and learn how to prepare for college. Of the students participating, 853 were Hispanic; 66, Black; 62, Caucasian; 59, Native American; 8, Asian; and 296, unknown.

As a follow-up to early outreach programs, 36 high schools in the metropolitan Phoenix area are invited to participate in FOCUS programs, which offer students the opportunity to experience campus by listening to student panels, participating in financial aid and scholarship workshops, touring campus and sitting in on classes. During the 2001 - 02 academic year, FOCUS programs brought 1,811 students to campus - 715 were Hispanic; 346, Caucasian; 220, Black; 87, Asian; 57, Native American; and 386, unknown.

Amy Castro, a student recruitment/retention specialist for the Undergraduate Admissions Office at ASU, says the main objective for prospective students who visit the Undergraduate Admissions Office is to discover whether ASU meets their needs.

"The transition from high school to college can be a challenging time for everyone, especially first-generation college students," she says. "We are fortunate that our recruiters have excellent relationships with high schools and counselors throughout the state. It's a great feeling to know you've helped someone achieve their dream of pursuing higher education."

The Undergraduate Admissions Office also recruits National Hispanic Scholars. Each year recruiters contact these students and encourage them to apply to ASU and visit campus. National Hispanic Scholars receive a full tuition waiver plus a $1,000 cash award, renewable for three additional years. For the class of 2003 more than 140 of these top students have applied for admission.

Despite their office's dedication to recruit future ASU students, Kostell and Hollin say their success isn't always measured in numbers.

"What I find most rewarding is being able to help a student succeed in going to college," Kostell says, "whether that be assisting him or her through the admission process or helping them receive financial assistance."

"By far the most rewarding thing is to see smiling faces when students get admitted, or get scholarships or get that perfect schedule they were looking for," Hollin says. "When they - and their parents - tell you 'thanks,' that's why most of us are in this business."
