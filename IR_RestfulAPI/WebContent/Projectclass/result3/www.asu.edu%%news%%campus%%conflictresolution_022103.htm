February 21, 2003

ASU responds to global tension by providing conflict resolution

With the threat of war looming and the nation on heightened alert for terrorist attacks, ASU has been taking steps to make people feel safe, promote tolerance and provide healthy outlets for free speech.

While the ASU Department of Public Safety concentrates on the nuts and bolts of campus safety, a group of administrators and key campus coordinators has begun meeting regularly through the Provost's Office to discuss the campus environment and plan ways to foster communication in difficult times.

And as a direct response to the anxiety many on campus are feeling, the Intergroup Relations Center (IRC) has scheduled an innovative educational dialogue session, "Community Voices: Campus Lives - Global Tensions," 4 - 6 p.m., March 10 in the Memorial Union Ventana B room. It is open to students, staff and faculty.

The event will start with a "fishbowl" discussion in which designated people from different groups will share their perceptions and experiences related to the current global conflict. Participants will then break out into small, facilitated discussion groups, and later will be given information on involvement opportunities and campus free speech.

The Muslim Student Association and Hillel, the Jewish student organization, are among the sponsors. Others include the Asian/Asian Pacific American Students' Coalition, the Interfaith Council and a number of minority organizations, academic groups and Student Life offices.

"It's not a debate, or a search for solutions, but an attempt to understand each other's perspectives," says Kathy Wong, program coordinator. "In a time of crisis, we all react differently based on our experiences and backgrounds. As we continue to study, work and live together at ASU, having a deeper understanding of how we and those around us are impacted is important."

Instructors may encourage their students to attend as a way of applying their related course material to campus and global events, she says. Participants will sign attendance sheets and complete a brief online survey afterwards, and those responses can be forwarded to instructors.

The Campus Environment Team will respond to a U.S. declaration of war by offering forums so people can express their opinions and concerns in a constructive way, says Barbara Mawhinney, a member who also heads up the Equal Opportunity/Affirmative Action (EO/AA) office.

The provost's coordinating group is promoting these kinds of discussions, to encourage a productive response that will help students learn.

"We're a university, after all, and our goal is to promote critical thinking and learning," says Gail Hackett, vice provost. "Differences are something we must learn to embrace. But we would like any expression of opinions to take place responsibly and with respect toward others."

ASU has compiled a list of resources within the university to assist people who may be feeling uneasy or in need of services, found on the ASU home Web page, under "Security Update."

In an e-mail memo to the entire ASU community last week, President Michael Crow called attention to these resources and encouraged people to support one another. "I am extremely proud of the culture at ASU, which embodies acceptance, tolerance and a willingness to embrace and appreciate the diversity among us," he said.

Resources include Counseling & Consultation, which helps students individually or in groups. For faculty and staff, the Employee Assistance Office provides counseling, the Ombudsperson Committee can help resolve workplace grievances and the EO/AA investigates complaints of illegal discrimination.

Other avenues include Student Advocacy & Assistance, to help students who are in crisis navigate the system of university policies. Student Judicial Affairs oversees Code of Conduct violations.

Information and registration for IRC's new dialogue group are available online at (www.asu.edu/provost/intergroup) under the "Community Voices" link. Registration deadline is March 7.
