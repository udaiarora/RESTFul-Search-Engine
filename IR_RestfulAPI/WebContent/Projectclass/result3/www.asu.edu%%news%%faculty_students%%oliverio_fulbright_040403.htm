April 4, 2003

ASU professor to study terrorism on Fulbright Research Grant

ASU's Annamarie Oliverio will soon be headed to Italy to continue her study of international terrorism after receiving a grant from the Fulbright Scholars Program.

An adjunct faculty member with the School of Justice Studies, Oliverio is the author of "The State Of Terror," published in 1998 by SUNY Press.

"While this book provides a broad theoretical framework for exploring the relationship between terrorism, states and social control, the research Fulbright will provide the means for a more detailed theory, a systematic and intensive examination of cases, and a more comprehensive data set," she said.

"It is research like this that may help the United States and the rest of the world understand the sources of terrorism and design appropriate responses to it," said Anne Schnieder, dean of the College of Public Programs. "We are very pleased that she has now received this Fulbright award to continue her research."

Oliverio says her primary concerns have been the exploration of different ways in which nation-states define terrorism and the mechanisms devised for the ostensible resolution of terrorism.

"An underlying tenet guiding much of this work suggests that the means of resolving terrorist incidents vary from state to state based on factors such as the socio-economic and political structure of a particular nation-state," she says. "My major objective has been to examine these variations, attempting to discover the logic and coherence of the patterns of responses to terrorism by nation-states as well as the relationship between these state responses and other social institutions, including the distribution of power, and structures of domination."

Beginning in May, Oliverio will be located at the University of Lecce in Lecce, Italy. Lecce is located in southern Italy, in close proximity to Greece and Turkey.

"These are uncertain times, and once again, Italy, in particular its southeastern area, is caught in the political and geographic crossfire," she said. "Many of the best researchers on this topic are in Italy and all of my family lives around Calabria."

The Fulbright Scholar Program is recognized as the U.S. government's flagship program in international educational exchange. The program sends 800 scholars and professionals each year to more than 140 countries, where they lecture or conduct research in a wide variety of academic and professional fields. It is sponsored by the U.S. Department of State. Out of the 800 grants, fewer than 200 are awarded for research projects.

The Fulbright Program was established in 1946 under legislation introduced by former Senator J. William Fulbright of Arkansas. Approximately 250,000 "Fulbrighters," 94,000 from the United States and 155,600 from other countries, have participated in the Program since its inception more than 50 years ago.
