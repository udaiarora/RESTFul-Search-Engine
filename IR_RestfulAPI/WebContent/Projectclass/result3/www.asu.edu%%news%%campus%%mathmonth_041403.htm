April 14, 2003

Month's events add up to artistic, educational fun

Say the words "math" or "algebra," and many people blanch. There's no question that math has gotten a bad rap over the years.

But math is interesting, challenging and underpins "so much of modern society," said Andrew Bremner, chair of the mathematics and statistics department.

To illustrate the breadth of math, the department has joined universities across the nation to celebrate April as Math Awareness Month (MAM).
This year's theme is "Mathematics and Art."

"The connection between mathematics and art goes back thousands of years," said Steve Kaliszewski, coordinator of MAM activities. "The ancient Greeks and Romans used mathematics in sculptures and to aesthetically design buildings."

In the 18th and 19th centuries, scholars note, mathematics was extensively used in the design of Gothic cathedrals, rose windows, mosaics and tiling.

During April, several lectures will be presented including a panel discussion about current math research.

ASU creative writing graduate student Kelly Houle, who studied math, physics and astronomy at the University of Arizona, will discuss her anamorphic drawings and paintings during a colloquium at 4 p.m., April 24 in Bateman Physical Sciences Center (PS), room F-101, and her work will be on display that day in PS A-206.

So how do students get past their fears of algebra, geometry and calculus? Bremner says a lot depends on who their teachers are.

"Teachers must have a passion for the subject," Bremner said. "That's why we're trying to create a generation of good teachers."

Bremner says learning math is like learning a foreign language. Much as students must drill and drill to learn vocabulary words, grammar and syntax, and then spend hours practicing, math students must learn basic concepts and spend hours solving problems. But what good comes from knowing math?

"You can design beautiful bridges," Bremner said. "Or work in Internet security, or much more."

For a complete schedule of ASU's MAM events, go to (http://math.la.asu.edu/~mam).
