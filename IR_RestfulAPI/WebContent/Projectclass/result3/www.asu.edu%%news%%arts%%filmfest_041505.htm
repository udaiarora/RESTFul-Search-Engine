April 15, 2005

Reel entertainment: film festival returns for ninth year

Twenty-seven short films and videos ranging from animation and drama to comedy and documentaries will entertain adults and youth alike beginning at 8 p.m., April 23, at the ASU Art Museum’s Ninth Annual Short Film and Video Festival.

The festival is outdoors on the ASU Nelson Fine Arts Center plaza.

Attendees are encouraged to bring a blanket or lawn chair to enjoy the spring weather and a night of humorous thought-provoking films created by artists with different visions and levels of experience.

“We’re the festival for emerging filmmakers,” says organizer John Spiak. “People start here and go off and do great things.”

Attendance at the festival, which has developed a following among filmmakers and aficionados alike, has grown to about 1,200. The festival attracts entries from around the world; this year organizers received 365 entries from 28 states and 14 nations.

The jurors – Spiak and Bob Pece – chose 27 entries representing the United States, Canada and Germany. The works range from 60 seconds to 10 minutes in length. Two entries are from Arizona. “Kollectables,” by Alex Calleros of Chandler, is a “mockumentary” of the “Kute Kollectables Klub” of eccentric high school collectors. “Guitars and Idiot Sticks” is a short documentary by Tucson’s Logan Hall about musicians who have migrated to Tucson for the Old Time Fiddlers Festival.

Two videos already have received Juror’s Choice Awards. In “My Trip to Liberty City,” filmmaker Jim Munroe of Toronto inserts himself and his narration into the Grand Theft Auto III video game and changes his character as the game progresses. “Pass the Ammunition,” by Joseph Peragine of Atlanta , sets a cartoon chronology of Iraq war events to the sound of the popular World War II anthem “Pass the Ammunition.”

Other films in this year’s festival include “Cats and Pants,” a silly short by Jennifer Matotek of Oakville, Ontario, and “Le Vieil Homme et Les Poissons (The Old Man and the Fish),” by David Bokser of West Hollywood, about an old man who constructs a fish suit to fulfill his dream of becoming a fish.

“November 22, 1963” by Natalie Frigo of Brooklyn, N.Y , is an alteration of the Zapruder footage of the John F. Kennedy assassination, with the removal of JFK from each frame.

All films and videos will be projected in VHS format. Most of the entries in this year’s festival are family friendly, according to Spiak. The LeBlanc Audience Choice Award will be announced online at (http://asuartmuseum.asu.edu) following the festival.

Tanguay, with the Herberger College of Fine Arts, can be reached at (480) 965-7144 or (denise.tanguay@asu.edu).
