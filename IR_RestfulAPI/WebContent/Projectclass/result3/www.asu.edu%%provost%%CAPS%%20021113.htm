Committee on Academic Professional Status (CAPS)

Minutes of Meeting

November 13, 2002

Present: Patricia Feldman, Chair; Carol Behl, Sue Hunter, Stefan Myhajlenko, Barbara Trapido-Lurie, Victoria Trotta.

1.  The Minutes of the 10-02-2002 meeting were approved.

2. OLD BUSINESS 

Letter re: voting rights for academic associates to academic senate president George Watson.  The committee discussed the possible outcomes of the letter recently sent to George Watson. Gail Hackett indicated a willingness to help get this issue resolved.

3. Final Preparations for Spotlight Series presentation. The presentation is to begin at 11:00 a.m.

CAPS Spotlight Series speaker: Gaye Murphy, Assistant Director of University Budget Planning and Management addressed 32 AP?s on the timely topic of  self-insurance. Minutes of the address and questions are appended to these minutes.CAPS Chair Patty Feldman introduced the members of CAPS and Ms. Murphy.

The Arizona Legislature recently passed legislation mandating that the State of Arizona self-insure its employees no later than October 2003. Gaye Murphy is a member of four committees (State of Arizona and University) all of which are involved with implementing the State legislation.

Ms. Murphy educated the audience on the history of the legislation, what self-insurance actually is, and what this impending change means for University employees.

1. What do we have now? The State of Arizona is now ?fully insured?, which means that the State  contracts  with its health care provider(s) for a set monthly rate.  If the submitted claims during any given month   exceed or are less than that negotiated  rate, the provider is the one that absorbs the losses or receives the benefits.   

2. What is self-insurance?  Under a ?self-insurance? model, the State still contracts with health care providers for service for a set monthly rate, but the risk of loss or benefit in any given month is borne by the State itself. The State must also maintain a ?savings account or reserve? that would cover the claims in those months where claims exceed the monthly negotiated amount paid to the insurance provider. One major benefit of self-insurance is that it can target certain services, especially those in the highest demand.

3.  Why is this a good thing? There is some evidence that providers like doing business on this basis because they are not taking the downside financial risk of their contracted fee.  There is some indication that there actually might be more providers willing to bid on the State RFP if self-insurance is instituted, and therefore there may be more choices for employees. The State finds this arrangement attractive because they expect that the negotiated fees would be lower, since the contract price would not include the costs associated with taking the financial risks in the months that claims exceed the negotiated monthly rate.  This model is attractive to the State for other reasons, including having more control over claims data, and  reviewing information about how employees are being treated with their claims.  The State also believes that more claims data will help them develop wellness education and programs targeted to special needs and for   special geographical regions of the State.  In the long term, the State might save money, although this is not a sure thing.  Self-insurance is in place in many other States, many of which wouldn?t fund their State employee benefits any other way. Other States, such as Colorado, have had mixed, or negative results with this model.

4. What are the downside arguments of the self-insurance model?  Ms. Murphy?s Committee has identified a number of these:  Instead of one provider contract to develop, send out for RFP, gather, analyze and choose, under this new model there would be SIX contracts to develop and let.  There is simply not enough time to implement the legislative timeline with any thoughtfulness. Moreover, and this will come as no surprise to you, there have been no State funds set aside for the ?savings account/reserve?. Estimates of funding levels needed for a solvent self-insurance program also differ widely. The original idea was that $50M would be needed for this fund; consultants have stated that we need more on the order of $80M .  The committee is worried about locking the State into a system that might not meet its goal, e.g. to contain health care costs. The committee doesn?t like it because there is no provision for actually improving the quality of health benefits for Arizona State employees.

5. What else can you tell us about this process? Governor Jane Hull and the Department of Administration are in favor of deferring implementation of this mandate. The Universities support their position. To defer this deadline, another law would have to be passed. The status of this is unclear as of this time.

6.     Questions:

a.     What about the rumor that AHCSS will take over health care provision for State employees?  This would probably only be an option in the urban areas of the State. Even if it were possible,  AHCSS does not have capacity to take on this role  at present.

b.    Why are we part of the State system? Can?t the Universities be separate? The University Committee and the Tri-University Working Group(under the direction of  ABOR) is studying this question right now. A big fear is that if the Universities were to break off, would the State government withdraw their subsidy for employee health care?.

c.     Why aren?t there more health insurance options for employees?  The Committee has some evidence that if the State became self-insured, the providers might offer additional/flexible options for State employees in future negotiations.

d.    What?s the current status of the CIGNA contract? CIGNA is expected to announce premium increases for next year, maybe as high as 24%. It would then be the Legislature?s decision as to how much of that would be passed on to employees and how much they would subsidize. 

The meeting was adjourned at 12:00 p.m.

Respectfully submitted, Victoria Trotta, Secretary

Approved 12/04/02
