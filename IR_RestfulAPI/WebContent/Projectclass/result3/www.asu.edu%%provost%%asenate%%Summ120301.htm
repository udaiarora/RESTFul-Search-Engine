3.A  Senate President?s Report (Morton Munk).

 

First, I want update you on the presidential search.  The final draft of the leadership qualities that were written to guide the search committee in making recommendations regarding the selection of the next president of ASU was prepared with full consideration of the information that we received as a result of the 24 open forums that were held by the search committee.  The document was submitted to the Board of Regents at it?s meeting last week and that document has been approved, it is available on the Board of Regents? Website, which can be easily accessed through the ASU Home Page.  There is also a summary of the input received as a result of these 24 forums and you are encouraged to review that at your convenience.  At the present time, the major effort of the search committee is in reviewing and discussing candidates or applicants and prospects and the committee has spent a lot of time discussing those applicants and prospects.  There is still room for nominations, so I encourage any of you that still have some thoughts about promising individuals, please pass that information on to a member of the search committee.  Although certainly the list is not complete, I can tell you that already there is a rather rich pool of candidates.  If all goes well the committee hopes to begin interviewing those short listed members of the search in late February and completing our work in early March. 

 

One of the things that I would like to emphasize again is the importance of having each of you and your colleagues attend the open forums that will be held for each of the candidates that are invited to campus.  It is extremely important that we hear from you and your colleagues on the strengths and weaknesses of each of these short listed candidates.  Our voice should be pervasive across the campus and persuasive.  It is very important that the members of the Board of Regents who will make the ultimate decision hear from us.  I am going to ask the faculty advisory committee of the search committee to submit their evaluation of the candidates, again in terms of strengths and weaknesses I will ask the Executive Committee, the Consultative Committee, to submit an evaluation and I will ask chairs and directors.  It is critical that the Board of Regents hear from us in this regard.  We must make our voice heard.

 

As I indicated at the last meeting of the Senate, one of the issues Regent Jack Jewett has raised is the issue of intercollegiate athletics.  This is driven in part by the recent updated Knight Commission Report on Intercollegiate Athletics.  The title of that report is very revealing of the position of this commission.  You may recall that the title of the report is ?A Call to Action Reconnecting College Athletics and Higher Education.?  Regent Jewett has established a subcommittee to consider some of the issues related to intercollegiate athletics.  The committee consists of two other regents, Chris Herstam and Don Ulrich.  It includes the three presidents of the universities, the three athletic directors, the faculty representatives and the senate presidents of the three universities.  The subcommittee met prior to the Board of Regents meeting last Wednesday in Tucson.  The purpose of that meeting was to establish a set of issues that will be considered and to devise an initial action plan.  There were five primary issues to be addressed and I alluded to these last time we met.  There will be issues other than these five that will also be considered. 

Another issue that was discussed and probably will be considered, is the matter of the time demands that are made on student athletes with regard to the athletic program and the question is does that leave sufficient time for academics. 

 

The five primary issues are as follows:

 

1) Improve the measurement and reporting of graduation rates and academic progress for student athletes. 

 

You may recall that last month Jerry Kingston presented a report to the Senate regarding academic performance and graduation rates for Arizona State University.  He indicated that there are a number of problems regarding the way in which the information is currently reported, for example, there could be a student in good standing as a student athlete, and if that student decides to leave to go to another university or into professional sports that student is currently considered as a ?dropout.?  The question is should that be considered as a dropout and be reflected in graduation rates?

 

2) The emphasis will be on strengthening academics support services and academic opportunities for student athletes. 

 

The discussion was certainly broad based but it was also indicative that the need for student services and academic advisement is also great within our entire student body and that should not be overlooked, although that is certainly not the role of the subcommittee.

 

3) Establish guidelines for the reappointment of the Faculty Athletic Representatives.

 

Now as I understand it, this issue is driven by an inadequate performance of one faculty athletic representative at one of our sister institutions.  Jerry Kingston who was here last month has been the faculty athletic representative for ASU for the past 15 years.  He has done an outstanding job and I have some concern about changing the rules that may terminate an effective person like Jerry Kingston because of one isolated problem with one faculty athletic representatives at one of our universities; I don?t believe it is wise to change the rules just because of one isolated problem.  You have to deal with that problem individually.  I certainly would argue against a change in the rules that would terminate a very effective and knowledgeable faculty athletic representative that we have at ASU. 

 

4) Improve cost containment mechanisms and fiscal controls to avoid deficits that could tap into our general fund.

 

I would like to say that Gene Smith was at this meeting and I think he has done a very effective job in trying to minimize any deficits, and to live within the revenue that is collected by the athletic programs at ASU. 

 

5) Finally, the discussion will be on developing a coaching contract that supports both intercollegiate athletics and provides incentives for student-athlete academic success.

 

There was one example brought up at the subcommittee meeting, at one Pac10 university, the football coach receives a modest salary, that is modest by football coaches salaries, not by faculty salaries, then there is a large incentive bonus associated with the salary in the event that there is a winning season.  Some of us question the wisdom of an approach of this sorts, as that would tend to emphasize winning over academic success.  That is certainly something that would be discussed. 

 

Also I wanted to say that throughout the discussion by the subcommittee, it was recognized that any changes that suggested must be thought through very carefully and if changes are to be made, they are to be done incrementally, because to do anything unilaterally could compromise the competiveness of Arizona State University and UofA and NAU relative to other institutions.  I think there will be a great sensitivity in following through on some of these issues.

 

3.B  University Provost?s Report (Milton Glick).  

 

We have asked to see if the noise in this room can be addressed.  It is a great room but it is suffering due to the noise level of the air conditioning/heating mechanisms.  They are trying to fix that.

 

I want to express to you and to the faculty and staff through you, my appreciation that given the budget situation we are in, the events of September 11 and since, this could have been a semester with all kinds of student problems and class problems; that has not been the case.  It has been a very smooth semester and I believe that is because our faculty and staff have reached out to the students and done good for them, so I want to express my appreciation to all of you. 

 

On the budget, we do not know a d--n thing.  Most of you know that the Senate has now passed a budget out.    It is 4%, that is where we said our bottom line was.  It retains the 5% salary package; it is a one-year budget.  In all ways but one that is the best we could hope for.  The one way in which I worry about it is there intent is to rescind the second year and not take our budget back to where it was last year, but to this year, which means the 4% cut will be built into the base.  This is a serious problem for us.  The way we have handled the 4% cut, if it is a one-year budget and it goes no deeper and we can protect the salaries, we can continue to work at being better.  I worry if it becomes a two-year budget, or a three-year budget, or they delete the salary or go deeper that we will reverse many of the accomplishments of the last decade.  I think we can live with the Senate budget.  I don?t think that we can live with much below that.  The house has not passed a budget at all.  There are rumorous factions there?they are committed to a two-year budget; they are opposed to bonding, which I think the Senate is willing to accept.   It is all up in the air.  The good thing is ?He who writes the first draft usually wins!? therefore, the Senate being on the floor and voted upon, gives strong impetus to the Senate position.

 

 

As an update on the Tempe Center, I believe I told you last time that city council had voted against the proposed plan for Tempe Center, based on the absence of a grocery store and secondarily on the fact they wanted ninth street to go through, and that has never existed.  They also wanted the parking lot to go further into the five acres that we have reserved for academic purposes.  They voted last Thursday to reconsider and they have given the developer until January to come back with an alternative proposal.  I don?t think for the universities viewpoint the commitments we have set have to be there; that we will not allow the parking to extend into the south five acres; we will not allow a street to be cut through and that remains true.  This is now between the developer and the city and our position is firm. 

 

As an update on the Light Rail, you know the intent of the light rail was to come from downtown and to come up McAllister between University and Apache, then it would go on University in some direction.  About three months ago, some of our engineers and scientists raised the issue of electromagnetic radiation from the light rail as well as vibration.  We have had a consultant who specializes in these issues and they has concluded that we would need a 200 foot swab on either side of the light rail from any sensitive instrumentation.  We cannot afford to accept a 400 foot wide swab that would for the next 50 years would conclude any facilities that have sensitive instrumentation.  So, we have told the city here is the report; we are willing to work with you based on this report we do not believe that McAllister is an acceptable route for the light rail. 

 

In conclusion, many of you have been reading about the stadium and if you understand it all, then you understand more than I understand.  The first thing I don?t understand is how the vote got passed.  Our position continues to be that the Sun Devils wish to play in Sun Devil Stadium.  We are willing to work with the city if somehow there is property that we own that could make it possible for the city to build a separate stadium for the Cardinals, but to do that they would have to replace any space with contiguous space to the campus and there would have to be real benefit for the academic and athletic programs of the university.  My own opinion is, what would be best would be a major overhaul of Sun Devil stadium in which we would continue to own it and the Cardinals would play in it.  The Cardinals obviously consider that a non-starter but I think that is a problem for the Cardinals, but at any rate, we have not agreed to build a new stadium that our football team would play in.  Nor have we agreed to allow them to build on any land which has future potential to the academic or athletic programs.  You can read your newspaper to stay in touch to see how what we say we are not going to do actually plays out over the next few weeks.  I will be happy to answer questions now.

 

Senator Burstein:  The problem that I see with the stadium is if it actually got built next to Sun Devil Stadium?the pressure would be so enormous to play in that stadium that I don?t think we could avoid it. 

 

Provost Glick: I agree with you, David.  We have already told them that an adjacent stadium is not acceptable.  Candidly, I believe if it is within a couple of miles we will someday play in it but that is just my personal observation, that is not an institutional position.  It will be very hard to play in an oncovered stadium at 110 degrees when a mile from here there is a world class air conditioned stadium.  I do not disagree with David, and that is one of the reasons why we have been reticent to be there.  David?s analysis is true.  I still think the operating Sun Devil Stadium is what is in the State?s best interest, but I don?t think that is what the voters voted for.  If there is a world class professional stadium a mile away, some day we will play in it I believe. 

 

Senator Landers: It seems like we have heard a lot of issues here about what Tempe wants.  Are we on politically good terms with them or is there starting to be a difference between what they want and what the university would like to have?

 

Provost Glick: I don?t think there is friction, I think there are different priorities.  That is, if we look at Tempe Center, the city?s top two priorities are they want a grocery store there because the neighbors want it and we have said we would love to have a grocery store but we can?t subsidize it.  We have put in the plan where it could go?but it would have to be subsidized.  Secondly, they want to extend the commercial taxpaying enterprise as far south as possible, we really do not want to that because it does not meet our objectives.  The stadium clearly is wanted by Tempe.  I think our position is rather ecumenical on that, but not on our property but we have said that we would talk about a possible property swaps.  We have as I said, divergent priorities and I think what has happened is there are bigger issues on the table.  In the past, we were able to resolve most of these things because they were marginal decisions.  These are not marginal decisions, but I do agree with you, I find it interesting that the news always talks about the Mayor?s conflict of interest because he is an employee of ASU and the Mayor.  I can assure you that he has had not conflict of interest.  I would compliment him that he has never considered the fact that we pay his salary to drive his decisions. 

 

Senator Shama:  You have said that we don?t want the light rail and the swabs, etc. How much say so do we have on this matter?

 

Provost Glick: I believe we have the final veto.  I will tell you that two months ago when this first became  clear as an issue, we said to the city of Tempe it will be your responsibility to ameliorate, to make whatever accommodation are necessary and they agreed that it was their responsibility.  Unfortunately, when we said that--before we had the consultant in--I assumed there was a way to spend money and solve the problem.  Based on the consultant?s report, I do not believe that is true now.  You can spend money and reduce the problem and you might solve it for this year, but you do not know what the instrument will look like a decade from now.  Our position is that we believe there are alternative sites that do not cost substantially more, but we are not sure that is true.  I believe that we have the final say, it is our property and I regret by the way that this problem has come up, because even though I question whether the light rail at this point in this community?s development will really have an impact.  To the extent it does, having a light rail with a stop right in the middle of our campus would be a wonderful asset.  We think they could go to the edge of the campus and without substantially increasing the cost find an equally good solution that would not cause us to have to preserve so much of the campus as a ?free zone.?  I think it will require us to be strong.  We will feel pressures on this just as we will feel pressures on the stadium.  One has to decide what their priorities are.  In my view that should be that we have a landlocked campus, our core is 300 acres and we have got the acreage for the ICA facilities.  Our legacy should not be to reduce the usefulness of the core of our campus.

 

Senator Hajicek:  If the present developer of Tempe Center does get the plans done in January, does that open the table for ASU possibly?

 

Provost Glick: If it ends up that there is a decision that it is a no go?and by the way that is not our goal, if it works out that way we will go back to zero and begin the entire analysis and planning process again.

 

Senator Hajicek: Is it possible that the 2 five acre parcels could then be thought of as one?

 

Provost Glick: That is possible and we would go back to zero.  One of the problems you have is that a lot has happened in downtown Tempe since we started planning this, including the Brick Yard which has Borders and restaurants.  I think this makes it somewhat a less competitive piece of reality.  Yes, if at the end there is no resolution between the city of Tempe and the developer, we will go back to point zero and begin the whole planning process again, based on realities of 2001 rather than realities in 1995. 

 

Senator Hajicek: And this will end at the January meeting?    

 

Provost Glick: Yes.  I say that, but we have probably have had drop dead dates a half dozen times on this process and it keeps going on.  I would like to believe that a final decision up or down will be made in January but I thought that was true last September and then again in March.  These things have lives of their own.

 

Senator Hajicek: Do you know that date of the January meeting?

 

Provost Glick: Does anyone know the answer to that question, when the City Council meets.  I don?t know.  Call Steve Miller in Jack Pfister?s office or call Mernoy Harrison and he can tell you.  At any rate you have my assurance that if this thing cannot be resolved we will go back and start from scratch.  We may come up with the same plan but we will start out at zero again.  Again, this is not our goal to have this thing fall apart but if it falls apart we must start over. 

 

Thanks to all of you for the good that you do and have a good holiday season!

 

Senate President Munk:  Jeremy Helper is not with us today, George Watson could not be here, but I do want to say that with George?s enormous help the Senate Newsletter should be distributed by mail this week.  It is on the Academic Web page in Powerpoint and that location was emailed to each of you.  It deals mainly with the Presidential search so I think you will find a lot of information there.  Don Ulrich, the chair of the search committee and Lattie Coor also have articles in this newsletter.  I hope you will find it very informative.  Next we will turn to Doug Johnson, past president.

 

3.C  Past President?s Report (Doug Johnson).

 

As the Provost indicated, the legislative controversy over the budget still rages and that has colored a lot of what we have been doing since our last meeting.  I would like to first thank Craig Allen for representing the Senate at the Advocacy Training Session and we encourage each of you to attend a session like that in the future as we really need more spokesmen.  We encourage you to reach out to your legislators and to tell them ?No More Than 4? and not to use the state employee salary increases to solve these problems, let?s provide a long term sustainable financial support for the universities as a basis for creating excellence. 

 

The Board of Regents meeting in Tucson this last week was very much engrossed by the political controversy of the budget issues.  One of the things that was discussed at the Arizona Faculties Council meeting was the possibility of going beyond the type of political action that we have been able to take in the past, possibly exploring the forming of a Pac, other mechanisms that might provide us a little more louder voice in these discussions.  We are at the very early stages of doing that but if anyone has insight or interest in being in that type of thing, please let me know. 

 

I attended the Learner Centered Education workgroup meeting in Tucson on Friday morning.  One of the points that was discussed is a bit of good news that the Regents Innovation Fund, which was funded by proposition 301 dollars, will be going forward next month.  The idea is that one half million dollars is available to fund projects by individuals or teams from campuses, or university-wide projects, tri-university projects and particularly with other dollars being so scarce from other sources, that may be something that people want to think about.  Our RFPs will be out soon and we will post on that on our website.  The other type of discussion that occurred in that workgroup was related to our General Studies Program.  Some of you know that have been close to the General Studies Council this year, there have been some suggestions that we hold some open hearings on campus, give feedback from the business and community leaders as to how well our General Studies Program is proceeding.  This is always somewhat problematic because we are interested in good feedback but how do you go about getting outsiders to become informed about what general studies are and what we do, so they can make intelligent input.  I think Don Ulrich who was chairing that session made a positive breakthrough in beginning to think about the open hearing process in more political terms, that may be an opportunity to gather influencial people from the community, and help them think about how universities contribute to the success of Arizona in the future. I think that kind of idea will go forward, we will be asking faculty to be involved in some these sessions.  As soon as I know more, I will try to fill in with more details.

RESOLUTION OF THE ACADEMIC SENATE OF ARIZONA STATE UNIVERSITY, MAIN CAMPUS

 

?Whereas: The universities? share of state General Fund appropriations decreased from 16.1% in 1990-91 to 12.0% in 2000-2001; and,

 

Whereas: The universities received only a 48.0% increase (unadjusted for inflation) in the period 1991 to 2001, less than most other major state agencies, and less than half the 97.5% increase (unadjusted for inflation) in total state General Fund appropriations; and,

 

Whereas: For 2000-2001, the average state appropriation (inflation unadjusted) per headcount student for the ASU Main ABOR-designated peer institutions was $8,210 or 37% greater than the $5,999 for ASU Main; and,

 

Whereas: From 1991 to 2000, the gap in appropriations per headcount student between ASU Main and its ABOR-designated peers increased from $1,596 per FTE student to $2,211 per FTE student, a 395 increase; and,

 

Whereas: Excellent universities ensure a well-prepared workforce and serve as an engine of economic growth;

 

Be It Resolved by the Arizona State University Academic Senate: That the universities should suffer no more than a 4% budget cut in 2000-2002.?

 

?Moreover, Whereas: The failure to maintain competitive slaries for state employees has put services needed by the public at risk;

 

Be it resolved by the Arizona State University Academic Senate: That the 5% per annum salary incrase for state employees passed by the Legislature should be maintained.?

6.D Student Faculty Policy Committee (Craig Allen).

 

Student Faculty Policy Committee has Senate Resolution #2 to introduce to you today on recycling.

 

1)  Whereas, representatives of the ASU Academic Senate, including members of the Student Faculty Policy Committee, have held meetings and invited discussion on campus recycling,

 

2)  Whereas, ASU's campus-wide recycling program has not appreciably expanded since its formation in 1991,

 

3)  Whereas, recycling is important to the campus environment,

 

Be it resolved that the Academic Senate encourages leaders of the university to step up ASU's current recycling programs and to consider expanded recycling measures.

 

Let me apologize to the members of the committee who may be seeing the language of this for the first time.  We had a chance to get this before the Senate Executive Committee last week and we went ahead with it.  Essentially, just be Thanksgiving the committee spent a couple of days meeting with two student groups who had quite a bit of information to present to us on recycling.  A fairly sizeable ground swell of concern exists among these students about recycling.  I think for the most part the SFPC committee was persuaded by one aspect of their evidence that programs on recycling have not expanded appreciably since they began here ten years ago.  We have come up with a resolution that doesn?t greatly tie the hands of the leadership and divert attention from other things of importance, areas of budgetary concern, but nevertheless sends a pretty strong message to the leadership that this would be the will of the faculty. 

 

Senate President Munk:  Thanks Craig.  I attended that meeting and very impressed with how articulate and concerned the students were and they are going so far as to offer to build recycling containers, artistic containers that would be distributed on campus and they just wanted our support in their endeavor.  As Craig indicated, it is not something that would increase necessarily the cost to the university but they are a very dedicated group and certainly they make a strong case.
