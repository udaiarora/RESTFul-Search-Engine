December 17, 2003

Child development professor goes the extra mile for students

Office hours were long since over when a student tentatively knocked on Laura Hanish's door to ask for help with a term paper. Hanish, an associate professor of family and human development, dropped what she was doing and went over the entire draft with the student, making sure she understood the goal of the assignment.

That student nominated Hanish for the Featured Faculty award for December, for consistently going the extra mile for students. The award program was begun this fall by Co-Curricular Programs to recognize the contributions of faculty in enhancing student learning outside of class.

"Dr. Hanish goes out of her way on all levels," says Amy Long, ASU junior who is part of an undergraduate research team that gathers data for professors in the department. "She is an influential professor and mentor, taking the time to explain concepts and ensure understanding.

"She is the only professor who makes it to all of the undergraduate meetings. She answers all of our questions and constantly provides positive feedback. I feel like I have learned more from being a part of her research team than I could ever learn from a classroom."

At the weekly meetings, Hanish explains research concepts to the students that they haven't studied yet or that are on a graduate skill level, Long says. She shows her appreciation and tries to make sure they learn from each project.

Long says she once asked Hanish for a letter of recommendation at the last minute, apologizing for her poor planning. Though the professor was working from home that day, she later contacted the student to tell her there was a letter for her taped to her office door.

Hanish, who has been at ASU for seven years, teaches courses on child development, with emphases on childhood poverty, developmental problems and child-focused preventive interventions. She also teaches research methods.

"ASU students come from varied backgrounds and bring their unique experiences to the classroom," says Hanish. "I appreciate seeing my students learn and succeed. It's exciting when they graduate and pursue their own career goals."
