December 14, 2005

Federal grant targets campus wellness efforts

With an additional 1,000 students slated to live on campus next fall, ASU is reaching out to help its growing residential population of students live healthy lives. A new Living Well Network combines resources in counseling, health, recreation and wellness to provide comprehensive services and help students succeed.

The integrated network brings together four units, two of which have been renamed: Counseling and Consultation (C&C), Campus Recreation (formerly Recreational Sports), Campus Health Service (formerly Student Health) and Wellness and Health Promotion.

“The goal is to remove boundaries, ensure seamless service and develop strong, high-quality programs,” says Martha Christiansen, interim associate vice president for student affairs and director of C&C. “The current trend is toward comprehensive, holistic services to support students. We’re at the forefront of that trend.”

In recognition of ASU’s innovation, the Living Well Network has been awarded a three-year, $225,000 federal grant from the Substance Abuse and Mental health Services Administration to increase mental health services and suicide prevention programs. ASU is one of 22 universities across the nation to receive funding in the competitive process.

Increased mental health funding on college campuses has been identified as a national need by the American Psychological Association. With grant funds, the network will develop a comprehensive suicide prevention program targeted at first-year students in residence halls, with training programs, outreach materials and multidisciplinary intervention programs that include psychoeducation, nutrition, exercise and more.

Suicide is one of the leading causes of death in 15- to 24-year-olds, Christiansen says. C&C always has done suicide prevention training for the campus community, with special emphasis on the Residential Life community. In recent years, C&C officials have stepped up efforts to educate faculty and staff about warning signs in student behavior.

“ASU doesn’t have any greater problem than other universities, but we have a high resident population of 18- to 20-year-olds,” Christiansen says. “We want to increase awareness that they are a vulnerable population, and to identify students who are distressed at an early stage, before they get to a critical point.”

In the three-year grant, programs and materials will be developed in the first year. The second year will target students and residence hall staff in Manzanita Hall, expanding to McAllister Academic Village and the Student Recreation Complex the third year.

C&C provides on-call services after hours and offers individual appointments to about 2,000 students a year. Along with Wellness and Health Promotion, it also presents outreach programs to some 10,000 students a year. In the new network, both Campus Health and C&C have psychiatric staff.

Wellness and Health Promotion has expanded its outreach to offer weekly programs during lunch and dinner hours in residence halls and the Memorial Union.

In addition to student health services, Campus Health provides occupational health services for ASU researchers, public health monitoring of food and water safety, medical control for campus external defibrillators, initial worker’s compensation and consultation for serious infectious disease outbreaks.
