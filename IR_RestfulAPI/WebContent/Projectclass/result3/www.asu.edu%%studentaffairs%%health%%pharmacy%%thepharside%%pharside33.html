A.S.U. PHARMACY NEWSLETTER                            ISSUE THIRTY-THREE

Albuterol Inhalers: Going, going, GONE

      Some of you have noticed that albuterol inhalers have been slowly increasing in price lately. For over 20 million Americans with asthma and again that many with COPD it’s only going to get worse.
      Last year, the FDA gave its final ruling on albuterol metered-dose inhalers (MDIs) that use chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) as propellants. Essentially, they will all be banned after December 31, 2008.
      The ruling was in compliance with the Montreal Protocol on Substances that Deplete the Ozone Layer, which, in turn, grew out of the Vienna Convention for the Protection of the Ozone Layer which met throughout the 1990’s.
      Although the Montreal Protocol has been around awhile, the FDA has delayed the ban on albuterol inhalers, by exempting them as “essential use products.” There are now 3 non-CFC albuterol inhalers on the market with more to follow. These new MDIs use hydrofluoroalkane (HFA) as a propellant. Other manufacturers are developing inhalers that do not use propellants. You may be familiar with some of these, most notably, the Diskus ® system used in Serevent and Advair inhalers.
      The timeline presents many financial challenges for patients and payors. Prices on generic albuterol inhalers have already started to climb. Generic manufacturers are starting to exit this market and supplies are beginning to be limited, putting even more upward pressure on albuterol inhaler prices. Supplies of these inhalers manufactured before December 31, 2008 will be exhausted in early 2009. It looks like the first truly generic HFA inhaler will hit the market in 2010, so there will be no generic alternative throughout most of 2009 and into 2010.
      The generic HFA inhalers will certainly cost more than currently available CFC containing inhalers. Even OTC Primatene will be affected by these rulings.
      As patients with limited means are affected by this market turmoil, health officials expect to see an increase in asthma-related Emergency Department visits and hospitalizations. Most manufacturers of asthma products have pledged to increase sample distribution and ramp up patient assistance programs.
Asthma education will be more important than ever. Good control, with active patient participation, will be essential to patient care. One good side of this situation is that patents on some of the controller medications, such as inhaled corticosteroids and leukotriene modifiers will be expiring in the next few years.

Canadian Drugs? You Sure?

The FDA recently inspected close to 4,000 packages from “Canadian” internet pharmacies. Only 15% of the drugs were really from Canadian companies. The rest came from 27 different countries and 32 prescriptions contained counterfeit product.

“Operation Bait and Switch” checked arriving parcels at JFK, Miami International and Los Angeles International airports.

The Book Niche

Dr. Paul Offit’s new book, The Cutter Incident follows the rocky road to the development of the polio vaccine. Work began in the 1930’s and he tells of doctors injecting children with poorly inactivated virus, causing untold suffering and even deaths. In the early 50’s Jonas Salk found a way to render the virus harmless but capable of eliciting an immune response.

More problems arose, however, when pharmaceutical companies tried to adapt his methods to large scale production. In 1955, one company, Cutter Labs of Berkeley sent out thousands of doses of vaccine that were contaminated with live virus. Thousands of children were infected, nearly 200 injured and at least 10 died.

The lawsuit that followed was one of Melvin Belli’s first high profile cases. The case was notable from a jurisprudence standpoint in that the jury found Cutter had acted in good faith and had done nothing culpably wrong. The judge, however, instructed them to impose damages. And so began the concept of no-fault liability.

Managed Health (In My Humble Opinion – IMHO)

As a pharmacist, I have been a health care provider for over 30 years. I've seen the dawning of the computer age in medicine and the blossoming of the alphabet soup that is known as Managed Care. Much progress has been made, but have we lost something along the way? Is it time to shift our focus and take the next step forward?

Managed care has now pared nearly every speck of "fat" from the system. We now see steady increases in premiums and co-pays so that insurers can continue to generate profits. There is nothing wrong with profit, but we have relegated sound medical care to a secondary position on the ladder of health care goals. Along the way we have lost sight of the tremendous value of truly maintaining health rather than reacting to illness or disease. It is my contention that Health Maintenance Organizations (HMOs) are misnamed to the point of absurdity.

But we are a capitalist economy; monetary concerns, as a consequence, drive choices made in the delivery of health care.

There are plenty of organizations and people promoting healthy lifestyle choices, but there is a disconnect between those efforts and the goals of the insurers of our healthcare.

I propose that it is time to vigorously encourage the principles of preventive care and wellness within the arena of insured health care. Let's move from managed care to managed health. Let's encourage people to be healthy, to eliminate risky health behaviors and make it economically feasible. Many managed care plans that I am familiar with do not do enough to encourage long-term or lifetime changes that benefit a person's health. Many plans are forced to cover oral contraceptives, few cover smoking cessation counseling and products, few cover weight management, some cover disease management, but the support is weak, at best. Very little is done to reward health maintenance. The current design of health care premiums results in healthy patients subsidizing the unhealthy. Too often, those with healthy lifestyles are paying for the care of folks that have made conscious decisions that lead to illness and higher health care costs. We need to restructure the reimbursement system to reward healthy choices. The focus of insurers seems to be short-sighted and narrow, reactive rather than proactive.

We must find a way to encourage health care insurers to lose their shortsightedness and cover services that may not have immediate positive impact on an individual's health. The thinking by the insurers seems to be that they need not cover these proactive services since it is likely that someone else will insure the person benefiting from them by the time the results pay off. We should put mechanisms in place to change this perspective. We need flexible premiums that reward the healthy, creating a more equitable cost basis for the provision of health care. We need to give the power of medical care decisions back to physicians, to allow patients more control over their own health, to create a health care system that spends more capital on managing health.
