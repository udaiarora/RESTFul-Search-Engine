May 8, 2006

Alumnus earns engineering acclaim

Yazhou (Joel) Liu, who earned a doctorate from ASU's Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering in 2004, has been chosen as one of the "New Faces of Engineering" in the United States.

He is among 16 engineers in the early years of their careers given the honor for 2006. The winners were featured recently in USA Today.

The selections are made by the Engineers Week Foundation, a consortium of some the world's leading engineering and education societies - including the National Society of Professional Engineers - in addition to major corporations and government agencies.

Liu, an electrical engineer, is a senior power conversion system engineer for the THALES group, a Fortune 500 company, in Seattle, where he's playing a lead role in designing a power conversion system for the Boeing Dream Liner 787, a highly fuel-efficient aircraft.

He previously worked as a post-doctoral fellow at Florida State University on the conceptual design of the Navy all-electric ship power system.

In the past five years, Liu has published 19 technical papers in leading international peer-reviewed journals and conference proceedings. He has presented papers at 11 international conferences. His contributions to power-quality studies on utility systems and ship and aircraft power systems earned him a senior member position with the Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers in 2005, making him one of the organization's youngest senior members.

"This is an honor that's widely publicized nationally, and Yazhou deserves the recognition," said Dr. Gerald Heydt, an electrical engineering professor in the Fulton School who supervised Liu's doctoral work.

"He was a super student. I was amazed by his innovative ideas for the application of systems and signal theory in power engineering," Heydt said.

Liu credits his education at ASU for his achievements.

"Without the guidance of Dr. Heydt, I would not have been able to win this award. He taught me a lot about research in my field and working styles that are helpful in my everyday job," Liu said.

"He sent his students to many international conferences and helped us to work with outside researchers. We learned about newest research trends, and how to work together to produce better results," he said.

Engineers who are two to five years out of school and age 30 or younger are eligible for the "New Faces" honor, which recognizes those demonstrating outstanding ability and leadership in their disciplines.

Liu was nominated by the Chinese Institute of Engineers, which works to strengthen the role of Chinese American professionals in the United States.

Liu was an active member of a Chinese student and scholar association at ASU, and now is vice chair of the Society of Chinese American Aerospace Engineers.
