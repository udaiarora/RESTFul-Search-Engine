August 12, 2004

People key element for history program

For ASU's American Indian history program, history is not just about the past, it's about the people.

The nationally-known program has built its strength on four principles - native peoples must play a central role in researching and writing their own histories; students must employ oral histories, traditional teachings, and personal observation; history is not something that happened to Indian nations but is something they made; and Indian histories constitute continuing stories.

The principles are central to the program's research and instruction, which has attracted top scholars and graduate student recruits whose endeavors have garnered the program national attention. In the past decade alone, the program has graduated nearly 30 Ph.D.'s in the field, considerably more than any other university in the country.

These students in turn are teaching at colleges and universities across the country and offering new approaches to the subject to the next generation of students, says Regents' Professor of History Peter Iverson.

"Archival research remains important, but students must also spend extended time with indigenous individuals and in native communities, conduct oral interviews, and learn about traditional teachings," Iverson said.

In an effort to advance the field of American Indian history in this regard, graduate students in the program have taken the idea to an international stage. Doctoral students this spring collaborated with their peers in the United Kingdom to organize a conference linking American and English scholars to discuss the importance of using an inclusive approach in recording the history of native people.

The two-day conference in May, "The Next Millennium: New Directions in American Indian History," was hosted by the University of Cambridge, located an hour north of London. 

"When you study the history of native people, you should talk with native people, 'with' being the operative word," says Brian Collier, a doctoral candidate in 20th Century American Indian History who co-organized the conference. "ASU does a really good job of this, so some of us here thought, 'Why not take this on the road . to Cambridge.'"

Collier was able to do this with funding from ASU's Office of the President, the Division of Graduate Studies, and Department of History. Support also was provided by the Mellon Professional Fund and the University of Cambridge. Collier coordinated the transatlantic effort with Gabriella Treglia, a doctoral candidate in 20th Century Native American History at Cambridge.

"Often scholars conduct research in archives and never visit a native community, or talk with anyone from the community," Collier says. "So what we are advocating is that you start with the community and end with the community."
