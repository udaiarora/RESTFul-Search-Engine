February 10, 2003

Navajo Nation continues funding successful program

A program for Navajo students at ASU is helping them fulfill the hopes of their rural Native American community. Some are even surpassing their parents' dreams.

Thirty-three Navajo students in ASU's Native American Achievement Program (NAAP) have successfully graduated, with dozens more in the pipeline. One graduate is now studying at Harvard Law School. Another is a mechanical systems engineer, helping design satellite systems for space.

The innovative partnership with the Navajo Nation has led to an increase in the freshman persistence rate for Navajo students at ASU from about 48 percent to an average 72 percent, the program's primary goal.

Now the Navajo Nation has agreed to continue funding the NAAP at ASU for five more years at $491,500 per year, providing scholarships for Navajo freshmen and continuing sophomores who participate.

The program requires them to meet with professors and advisers, take study skills workshops and develop action plans to meet their goals. They also work with the Multicultural Student Center and the American Indian Institute.

"Getting them through the first year, and coming back to ASU for the second year, has been the hurdle," says Peterson Zah, adviser to the president on American Indian affairs. "We also provide them with considerable support in their sophomore year. Once they have made it through two years, they are generally committed and want to finish."

Native American freshmen coming to ASU face an uphill struggle, often marked by homesickness, culture shock, inadequate high school preparation and financial problems. For years they have been less likely to succeed than any minority group on campus.

Making the transition from a rural, primarily Native American high school to a large urban campus can be difficult, says Michael Begaye, program coordinator senior. Other barriers include a culture that emphasizes sharing over competition and the experience of being the first in their family to attend college. Almost a third declare an engineering major, an especially demanding freshman curriculum.

A total of 313 first-time Navajo freshmen have participated in NAAP during its first five years, and during that time their persistence rates have been higher than that of other Native American freshmen. A third of the first group of freshmen have already graduated, and the six-year graduation rate by May 2003 could approach 50 percent, almost double what it was in the past.

Zah, former chairman and president of the Navajo Nation, was instrumental in crafting the initial agreement and in securing continuing funds, a pact that was signed by the tribal council in the final hours of Dec. 31, 2002, before it expired. A transition to a new Navajo president in January had created a delay.

"The tribe is fully committed to Navajo youth and to giving as many of them as possible the opportunity to get a degree," says Zah. "The hope is not only to help them succeed, but to produce graduates who can go back to their communities and have a major impact.

"Indian tribes need help. They need a pool of resources to help them run tribal governments. They need teachers, principals, superintendents, doctors and lawyers to be role models, so young kids will look at these people and realize they can be leaders, too."
