April 27, 2006

Kosovo president takes part in ASU program

Fatmir Sejdiu attended classes focused on bolstering programs in public administration and business

An innovative ASU program focused on building academic programs at the University of Pristina in Kosovo had a greater impact than originally imagined.

Fatmir Sejdiu, a professor of public administration who participated in the semester-in-residence program at ASU in 2003, recently was elected president of Kosovo. He was among 17 Kosovar participants who worked at ASU to develop courses and teaching methods to bolster programs in public administration and business at the University of Pristina.

ASU's Russian and East European Studies Center in 2001 was awarded a two-year U.S. Department of State Bureau of Educational and Cultural Affairs (ECA) grant of more than $600,000 to administer the educational partnership. Faculty members from the School of Public Affairs and W. P. Carey School of Business also helped educate visiting scholars.

"Our goal is to prepare people for high level professional positions in government, so that they can really make a difference in society," says Robert Denhardt, director of ASU's School of Public Affairs. "Who would have guessed we would wind up helping to prepare the chief executive of another country? Having been to Kosovo and having friends there, I find the news tremendously satisfying and humbling. President Sejdiu has a difficult task, but he's a man of great intellect and great capabilities."

According to David Brokaw, assistant director of the Russian and East European Studies Center, the program was intended to encourage curricular reform by "combining hands-on classroom experience of American business management and public administration with small-group discussion of teaching methodology and the practical work environment."

During their visit, scholars attended classes in public administration and business management, met with a wide range of ASU faculty and students to expand their understanding of western theories of public administration, and developed practical teaching skills and methodologies. What they learned help inform development of the new public administration curriculum at the University of Pristina.

According to Denhardt, ASU and the Phoenix metropolitan area were ideal hosts for the program.

The School of Public Affairs is one of the most highly regarded public administration programs in the country, with the MPA program consistently ranked in the top 25 nationally by U.S. News & World Report.

"In addition, the city of Phoenix and many other local governments in this area are considered among the most innovative and "best run" governments anywhere," Denhardt says.

The visiting scholars attended classes in economics and public administration, met with local government officials and toured key destinations. They also produced their own course syllabi and developed academic Web sites.

"We wanted to give them a flavor of this area and its governance structure," says Barbara McCabe, an associate professor of public affairs and faculty liaison during the time Sejdiu was in residence. "We wanted the Kosovar scholars to experience how public, private and nonprofit interests can contribute to thriving metropolitan areas. ASU's urban focus and the experiences in metro Phoenix were especially important, because local government reform is a top priority for Kosovo."

McCabe adds that the ASU faculty also benefited from the program, and students in her classes "were privileged to hear a lecture on the history and governance of Kosovo from a legal scholar who is now Kosovo's president.

"As for myself, I had read about the region," McCabe says. "But it took spending time in Kosovo with Fatmir and his family and other friends from the university before I had any real understanding."

As a region that formerly was a part of the Yugoslav state, Kosovo has been an area of tension between the majority ethnic Albanian population and a minority population of ethnic Serbs. The United Nations has administered the region since NATO forces ousted Serb-led Yugoslav troops in 1999.

Last year, the international community finally agreed to begin negotiations on the resolution of a "final status" for Kosovo. Sejdiu will lead the Kosovar delegation at these critical international talks.

McCabe says she believe Fatmir is the right man for the job. She likens the former leader of the nonviolent opposition in Kosovo to Martin Luther King Jr., calling him "wise, brave and amazingly intelligent."
