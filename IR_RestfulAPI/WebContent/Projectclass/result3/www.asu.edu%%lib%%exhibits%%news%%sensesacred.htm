A Sense of the Sacred

Text, images, and symbols drawn from the
Archives and Special Collections of the Arizona State University Libraries

(originally posted 11/08/06)

Luhrs Gallery and Labriola National American Indian Data Center at Hayden Library, Tempe campus
October 30 to December 15, 2006


Danforth Chapel cross

What is Sacred? From the Latin word sacer, meaning untouchable, something sacred is set apart by solemn ceremonial use or deep philosophical understanding. It is not profane or common, but regarded with reverence and consecrated by divine sanction.

To many, life itself is sacred. The sources of life—the Earth, the Sun, and water—are sacred. As is the passing of life into eternity and the memory of life are also sacred.

The search for the sacred is an ancient cultural phenomenon. There is an inherent human need to have intimate contact with the divine to give greater meaning to human actions. The earliest people created art and spaces to contemplate sacred things or commit sacred acts. People of all cultures and faiths still actively seek the sacred through places, actions, and thought.
Perceiving the sacred gives us a sense of something beyond what we are and provides answers to the questions we ask:

Why are we here?
What is life and where does it come from?
What are the connections between living beings and the sacred?
What places, acts, or things unite us with the cosmos?

A Sense of the Scacred includes resources pertaining to sacred texts, historic Southwestern places of worship, cross cultural attitudes and beliefs. The materials on display are from the Hayden Library including images, artifacts, rare books and manuscripts from the Archives and Special Collections. Native american themes are specifically addressed through a related exhibit in the Labriola National American Indian Data Center.

Contribututors include: Robert Spindler, Dr. Christine Marin, Kathy Krzys, Marilyn Wurzburger,
Joyce Martin, Dr. Miguel Astor Aguilera and Regents Professor of English Alberto
Alvaro Rios.
