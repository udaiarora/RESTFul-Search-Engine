February 3, 2005

Fuel cell authority visits ASU for Eyring Lecture

Frank DiSalvo, co-leader of Cornell University’s Fuel Cell Institute, will speak on the potential for developing fuel cells, the technology behind hydrogen power, into an energy-efficient alternative to internal combustion engines and turbines in this spring’s Eyring Lecture in Chemistry and Biochemistry at Arizona State University.

DiSalvo’s public lecture, “Fuel Cell’s: An Important Part of our Energy Future?” will be held at 7:30 p.m. on Thursday, February 3, in the Bateman Physical Sciences Center H Wing, room 150. He will also give a more technical presentation in the same venue on “Fuel Cells:  Challenges and Opportunities” the following Monday, February 7, at  3:40 p.m. 

Though fuel cells were invented over 150 years ago and produce energy more efficiently than current technology, a myriad of problems has kept them from being widely used.  The problems mostly arise from the materials used to make fuel cells. These materials are too expensive and ineffective to make fuel cells a viable option for the production of energy. 

The Fuel Cell Institute at Cornell employs a multi-pronged approach to improving fuel cell technology and production. Using complex materials and ordered intermetallic compounds, DiSalvo and fellow researchers at the institute study the promise of novel electro-catalysts for electrodes as a solution for some of the problems associated with fuel cell production.

Frank DiSalvo is the John A. Newman Professor of Physical Science at Cornell University and the Director of the Cornell Center for Materials Research, in addition to his position as Co-leader of the Fuel Cell Institute at Cornell.  Previous honors include the American Physical Society International Prize for New Materials and election to the National Academy of Sciences.  He is also a Fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences.

DiSalvo’s research spans a variety of different material fields including chalcogenic compounds, oxides, nitrides, structurally low-dimensional phases, metal cluster phases and intermetallic compounds.  In addition to electro-catalysis, DiSalvo studies a wide variety of phenomena including thermoelectric cooling and superconductivity. 

The Eyring Lectures in Chemistry and Biochemistry bears the name of  LeRoy Eyring, Regents' Professor of Chemistry, whose extraordinary instructional and research accomplishments and professional leadership at Arizona State University helped to bring the Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry into international prominence.

For more information on the Eyring Lectures, contact the Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry at 480-965-3461.
