Again, your faculty leaders at NAU, ASU, and UofA (the Arizona Faculties Council) are working to defeat HB 2617.

3.B  University Provost?s Report (Milton Glick).

        At the legislature, they are discussing two opposing versions of the two-year biennial budget for the universities (the JLBC?s and the Governor?s proposals). In both proposals, there is a salary increase of 5% beginning on January 1, 2002, and then an additional 5% beginning on January 1, 2003. Because the revenue numbers are looking soft at this point, its is likely that the JLBC recommendations will be trimmed back somewhat. However, at this juncture, the JLBC is still recommending full funding for building renewal, while the Governor?s proposal recommends renewal funding at the 50% level. We are still fighting for some growth money for operations at East Campus. The legislature, if they determine the revenue forecast is too uncertain, could pass a one-year budget instead of a biennial budget. We have had a two-year budget for awhile now, but that might change. Anything that you can do as faculty members to influence this decision process at the legislature, I recommend that you try to do so.

        As Doug Johnson told you, the ASU Cares Project is a worthy cause, and one way to show that we do take the health of our communities seriously. I encourage you to participate.

        On Friday the Board of Regents will consider the universities plans for Proposition 301 money. The funding targets particular for each of the universities needs to be reaffirmed in this discussion. Two million dollars have been earmarked for the establishment of an Arizona Regents University (ARU). We want the ARU to become a mechanism for online learning, but not become a separate degree granting university. It looks like the Regents will agree with us. The idea of the A.R.U. is that the student has a home campus, but also may take any online course that the three campuses offer. They plan online advising for courses, articulation between the campuses, and even an online payment system. They want to develop a seamless admission process. They do not plan to offer degrees, have a separate faculty, or separate student services.

        In ten years, it is expected that there will be many education consortiums in the higher education market. The ARU is an experiment in this type of e-learning delivery. The want to be able to market courses at ASU, NAU, and UofA, for our students, any time and anywhere. We will continue to offer distance learning at ASU, and we will continue to emphasize the importance of the campus experience, and how that adds value to a student?s education. We wish to add value to society overall by increasing the cultural and social growth of all our students that come to ASU.

        Questions from the Senate:

We have a five-year classroom mediation plan that will depend upon the level of building renewal funding allocated this year by the legislature. We plan to use that money to fix the most pressing problems in the classrooms, and to update the mediation/technology in others.

The students will agree to raise tuition if 1) they have increased access to technology and 2) the classrooms are better maintained.

        Senator Yao: I want to say that since the mediated classrooms will be updated, may I suggest that to save time and effort, each instructor should have a Notebook computer so that they could come to class and connect to a projection system.

        Provost Glick: The Classroom Committee 2001 is looking at what is acceptable mediation in the classroom. At NAU, there is a proposal backed by the deans to have students come to the university with computers. Our Deans Council is discussing the same issue but they don?t agree yet. The cost of equipment and training to insure student access to technology is a big part of that decision.

        Senate President Johnson: There is a full cluster of issues being worked on by the Regents concerning the Arizona Regents University, related to distance learning. A great deal of technology is needed to enhance e-learning for our students in the classroom setting. The Power of Technology Series will have a Future Visions session, including ARU, on March 23rd. There are other problems to consider, such as articulation between campuses and the acceptance of credit for courses from other institutions.

        For example, there are issues in exam administration to insure that a person attending the class or taking an exam is in fact the person who is registered for the class. These are issues of academic integrity that the Senate Student-Faculty Policy Commttee and the University Affairs Committee will be discussing this spring. If you have interest or suggestions on any of these issues, please email them to me, and let me know if you want to participate in committee discussions.

3.G  Annual Report/Faculty Athletic Representative (Jerry Kingston).

        Normally this report is given in December to the Senate, but there were unavoidable delays this time.   (If you are a senator and were not able to be present at this meeting, a handout was distributed and may be obtained by contacting jerry.kingston@asu.edu .

        I want to begin by pointing out the statistics in the reported summary are 4-year average values, and only incoming freshmen on athletic scholarship were included. The data does not include those students who transferred into ASU.

        Overall we are improving every year. The numbers continue to look better. We feel this is in major part due to the ICA on recruiting efforts and on the Board of Regents higher entrance standards. The GPA for all sports, for men and women is higher (M 3.06 and W 3.47). The graduation rate has remained about the same (M 40% and W 65% which can be compared to 47% for all students), and should be improved. The Mean SAT Scores are higher for students upon entry (M 0989 and W 1022 and for all students 1089).

        There is still a problem with graduation rates evident in the sports of men?s baseball and football. One of the possible explanations is that students transfer due to a "lack of playing time," rather than finishing their degrees here. Professional baseball drafting opportunities in the junior year also takes a toll.

        The Second cycle of the NCAA accreditation review at ASU is underway, and there is progress to report. Last year, a group led by Milt Sommerfeld (including 25 faculty and administrators) completed their campus review for the NCAA accreditation process. Following the submission of their report, the University then received a response in December from the NCAA, and we were asked to provide additional information relating to gender equity issues. We now have one year to prepare our response.

        Our new athletic director, Gene Smith, is doing an excellent job and is very involved in discussions about the academic performance of his student athletes. He is bringing in all the coaches and meeting with them sport by sport. He is trying to design and add additional academic services that will strengthen academic performance and track student academic progress as they move toward degree completion.

        So I am pleased to report that there is some improvement in academic preparation, performance and graduation rates for ASU student athletes. This is encouraging.

3.H  Dean of ASU Libraries (Sherrie Schmidt), "Serial Reductions."

        Be ready for a bit of bad news. Serials will continue to rise in cost, and there is no increase in the funding for the purchase of publications from the legislature. In fact, over the last ten years, the state operating budget line item for libraries has been increased (8.3%) only once, by allocations received from the legislature. During this period, we have been able to increase searl spending by internal reallocation by the Provost Office. The bad news is that the cost of serials has gone up 200%. This past year, as many of you have noticed, we had to cancel over 120 subscriptions to journals and other serial publications. Much of this price increase in serials is due to the market power of a well-known foreign commercial publisher, who provides most serious science journals. There are several initiatives underway both at the university level and the national level, to combat this serious threat to the library serials. A gathering of library deans throughout the Pac10, who call their organinzation informally the Big 12 Plus, have already had a conference at ASU this year to discuss, among other things, guidelines for electronic publication. They developed a set of guidelines last year at their conference, dubbed the "Tempe Principles" because they held their meeting in Tempe. It may become necessary in the future to encourage our faculty to do more and more electronic publication, and to request that faculty refuse to publish, refuse to edit or review work online, for pirate-like for-profit publishers who require them to surrender their copyright privileges. As Senator Gooding of Economics so aptly put it to me, Sherrie, the supply side has power to limit supply, and there is inelastic demand! (much laughter here).

        The library is looking at many efficiency measures, to format and to add value to our purchased information resources. The volume of materials is also more complex in an information age.

        As a direct result of input received on our library survey last spring, we have relocated our reference area in our libraries. We are studying new ways to protect and archive resources online. As one senator suggested, we are in the process of studying Library budgetary needs, hoping to profit in the new economy, and working to build future collections. We need your cooperation in trying to stem the flow of rising costs for our most serious set of resouces, our published journals and subscriptions.

        Send any comments and or suggestions you may wish to make to Dean Schmidt directly   (sherrie.schmidt@asu.edu).
