April 26, 2005

Unique performance tells story of Hiroshima Maidens

Performance artist and puppeteer Dan Hurlin brings his latest theatrical endeavor, “Hiroshima Maiden,” to ASU’s Galvin Playhouse at 7 p.m., April 29 and 30. The work is an evening-length account based on the true story of the Hiroshima Maidens, a group of 25 women disfigured by the first atomic blast at Hiroshima in 1945.

The work, told through the eyes of a young boy growing up in Cold War America, centers on his experience with one of the women, who was part of the group invited to the United States in 1955 for reconstructive surgery.

Part of the proviso for the women getting the surgery was that they would make public and media appearances. The climax of this odyssey was their appearance on television’s “This is Your Life,” where they were faced with meeting the pilot of the Enola Gay, the plane that dropped the bomb on Hiroshima . In the piece, the boy’s innocence during the time of the bombing is juxtaposed with the horrors of the events that took place during the years of his childhood.

Performed in the style of Japanese Bunraku puppetry, “Hiroshima Maiden” uses nine puppets and set pieces manipulated by five puppeteers and four dancers.

The Bunraku style uses lifelike puppets, which are manipulated by performers, to narrate a story with an incredible range of intensity and emotion. Hurlin’s research extended to conversations with “puppet theorists” who claim that the reason a puppet translates emotion so intensely is because it serves as a blank slate for the audience to interpret. In that regard, Hurlin says, “The audience automatically fills in the emotional context of what they are seeing.”

A series of freestanding screens, which move in and out to reveal different settings, comprise the set. Hurlin created the design, using research he gathered from imagery of the works of Hiroshiga and Yoshitosi, Edo-era printmakers from the “atomic” style of art created in the United States in the 1950s. An original score by composer Robert Een borrows from Haiku poetry.

“Hiroshima Maiden” is a continuation of a series Hurlin began three years ago called “Everyday Uses of Sight” – an exploration of the different ways we see (or don’t see) the world around us and how we use our sense of sight for different purposes: refuge, flirting with danger, eroticism and more. This work continues this exploration, using Japanese and American theatrical traditions, in an autobiographical performance and as a means to examine the creator’s (and our cultural) need to look at and yet look away from our horrific realities.

As a daunting tale of despair, guilt courage and forgiveness, “Hiroshima Maiden” reconciles two different points of view of the war and its aftermath.

Hurlin and his creative and production team still feel the impact of the work today.

As the company prepares the piece for touring, he states, “ The piece hits us all at different moments; everyone involved in the work at some point will be wiping tears from their eyes.”

Ticket prices for the New Times Beyond Broadway engagement of “Hiroshima Maiden” are $10 for students and $20 for all others. Tickets are on sale at ASU’s Gammage Box Office and Ticketmaster. For more information, call ASU’s Gammage Box Office at (480) 965-3434, or visit the Web site (asugammage.com).
