Course Syllabi

ASB 494/591: Amazonian Ethnobotany
REL 494/591: Amazonian Ethnobotany
PLB 494/598: Amazonian Ethnobotany

Summary:

Since the emergence of human culture, people have used plants for food, shelter, medicine, clothing, hunting, and religious ceremonies. Ethnobotany is the interdisciplinary study of the relationship between cultures and the plants they depend upon. The Ethnobotanist uses a mixture of anthropological and botanical techniques to study how and why people use and conceptualize plants in their local environments.

The jungles and rain forests of South America contain an incredibly diverse number of many unknown, unique, and potentially useful plant species. Historically, a large number of medicines have been initially derived from plant material. Over the last couple decades, interests have shifted again towards plants as a significant source of new pharmaceuticals. Thus, scientists have realized that the study of the native cultures that inhabit these tropical regions can provide enormously valuable clues in the search for improved health. Industries also see it profitable to direct resources towards exploring tropical regions where plant medicine remains the predominant form of medical treatment. Such factors have drawn more attention towards ethnobotany.

This course will take a look at the central factors associated with people?s past and present use of plants, including issues that stem from an increased search for pharmaceutical clues in foreign lands. The course will focus on plants from the Upper Rio Napo valley, though some discussion of other cultures, including modern North America, will take place.

Major Course Educational Objectives:

  1. Over the course of the session, learn ten major plant families (and a representative species) with important ethnobotanical uses and their current relevance.
  2. Understand the current issues in ethnobotanical research and how you can be an important part of that effort.
  3. Know the ethnobotanical uses of some local plants.
  4. Become familiar with the ethnobotany of three different regions of the world, including major similarities and differences between the usages of plants in different cultures.
  5. Keep abreast of important sources of ethnobotanical information, such as the internet, journals, books, elders.
  6. Learn the basic pharmacognosy of hallucinogenic plants, and the cultures that make use of these plants.
  7. Understand some of the important conservation issues related to ethnobotany.

Writing, Research, and Project Requirements

  1. Every student will be required to research one plant or plant product, using reference materials, and, if relevant, local knowledge. The student should collect information about that plant, including its scientific name (family, genus, species), history of use, where it grows, what humans do with it (keep it clean, huh?), what you like about it, etc. The student will then condense the information into a 5-10 minute presentation and make a short outline of the talk with 3-5 good references.
  2. Class project and paper. By the conclusion of the class, students will be required to submit a written report about their class project. The possible topics for the project and the specifics of the writing assignment will be discussed on the first days of the class. Students will be encouraged to work in groups and be creative about designing a project based on the material presented in class.

Lecture topics

  1. Class introduction, discussion of class projects and requirements, sign-ups for class presentations, bitter manioc (cassava) preparation
  2. Historical overview. The science of ethnobotany in the context of modern research advancements and a discussion of some of the key players.
  3. Ethnobiological classification and its relevance to cross-cultural research.
  4. Nascent pharmacology. The science behind the origins of plant use in traditional societies.
  5. Ethnobotany resources, including books, journals, internet sites, etc.
  6. Ethnobotany of chocolate: botany, culture, phytochemistry, and health.
  7. Research in ethnobotany (including plant collection and identification). A "hands-on" exploration of the life of an ethnobotanist.
  8. Ethnobotany of food and beverages, including coffee, tea, fruits, vegetables, and tubers.
  9. Ethnobotany and endangered species. A brief tour of the state of the world and pressing conservation concerns.
  10. Ethnobotany of hallucinogenic plants. The culture, science, and phytochemistry of the major plants in this category, including a discussion of ayahuasca, a sacred plant for the Quechua peoples.
  11. Intellectual property rights. The importance of respect for traditional societies in the midst of scientific inquiry, and some intellectual property rights models.
  12. Herbal medicine and drug discovery. The role of the ethnobotanist, successes and failures, and the future for modern medicine.
  13. Quechua herbal medicine and shamanism.

Excerpt from K. Moeller's Field School Journal, Summer 2003:

We visited Jacinta's chacra (Quichua for garden) which is set in on the island and is surrounded by more densely planted areas. The piece of land we were on had small manioc plants that had recently been planted. In the chacra, different plants are planted with the primary crop of manioc (known in Quichua as lumu and in Spanish as yuca), which is the most significant crop for the Upper Napo Quichua. Other plants included plantain, papaya, cocoa, and peanuts.

When asked about her personal relationship to the manioc, Jacinta stated in Quichua, "I am the manioc's mother." The women of the chacra have an amazing, yet seemingly complex relationship with their plants and the physical area of the garden. In traditional Quichua culture of the Upper Napo, women are the only individuals to enter the chacra, along with their children.
