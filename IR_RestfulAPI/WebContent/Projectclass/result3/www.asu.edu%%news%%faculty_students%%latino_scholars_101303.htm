October 13, 2003

Latino scholars earn recognition from ASU, family, community

ASU has more than 1,200 Latino students who were awarded academic scholarships or fellowships for the 2003 - 04 academic year - a big accomplishment for the university and the Hispanic community. On Oct. 3, these students and their families were honored at the seventh annual Latino Scholars Recognition Reception, "Celebrating Our Success."

"We have so many talented and gifted Latino students," says Antonia Franco, executive coordinator of the ASU Multicultural Student Center and this year's event organizer. "Each year the reception serves as not only a recognition, but an example of how Latinos are continuing to strive in academics and higher education."

Sylvia Carrizoza, a senior majoring in early childhood education, was this year's student speaker, who spoke about her future goals and her experience with the Doran Community Scholars Program. Carrizoza, who graduated from Maryvale High School, has been an active participant with the program since she was a freshman. In addition to the many community service hours Carrizoza has dedicated, she has served as an instructor with the Wesley Community Center for the last few years tutoring neighborhood kids and helping with after school programs.

Juan Gonzalez, vice president of Student Affairs, congratulated students and their families on their success.

"All of you should be proud of your achievement," Gonzalez said to the students after asking them to stand and be acknowledged. "You are all winners and remember that your parents are also winners because when you succeed, they also succeed."

This year Ben Arredondo, councilman with the City of Tempe, and Ben Hartley, general manager of Sodexho's Campus Dining at ASU, were presented with plaques for their generosity and dedication to helping Latino students achieve their academic goals.

Arredondo, who is serving his third term as a councilmember, is chairman of the Council Community Partnerships Committee and is a member of the Council Neighborhood and Public Safety Committee and the Council Ad Hoc Human Relations Committee. Arredondo is a high school teacher and former member of the Tempe Elementary School District Governing Board. Among his many achievements, Arredondo received the ASU College of Education Distinguished Achievement Award in 1993.

Hartley, has been working with Sodexho for more than 22 years. Through his leadership, Sodexho has been a sponsor of the Hispanic Heritage Scholarship Program on the national and local level at ASU.

In addition to the support of community members like Arredondo and Hartley, Bob Soza, dean of Student Life at ASU, says the university's academic programs continue to contribute to the success of Latino students at ASU.

"ASU scholarship programs such as the Hispanic Community Scholarship Program, Hispanic Mother-Daughter Program, Upward Bound, Maroon and Gold Scholarship Program and others provide the financial assistance to recruit and support the many talented Hispanic students at ASU," Soza says. "The Multicultural Student Center and other academic and student support services programs, as well as the large number of talented Hispanic faculty and staff make ASU a great place for Hispanic students to obtain their degrees."
