May 5, 2003

Regents approve new admission requirements for state's universities

Establishing new 2003 - 04 university admission requirements, special class fees and new degree programs were key actions taken by the Arizona Board of Regents at its April 24 - 25 meeting in Tucson.

Board president Jack Jewett of Tucson said establishing new admission requirements is the last cornerstone needed for full implementation of the board's Changing Direction Initiative, a series of new policies designed to give Arizona's three universities more flexibility in defining their niche while increasing the likelihood for student success.

The board voted unanimously to support the new admissions policy, which assures admission for the top 25 percent of graduating Arizona high school seniors who have no curricular deficiencies. Formerly, the universities used grade-point averages and standardized test scores as criteria for assured admissions.

The new admissions policy also delegates authority to the universities to admit students eligible for what was formerly designed as "conditional admission." This allows universities to review admissions of some student applications on a case-by-case basis where academic deficiencies exist. This designation was changed to Delegated Admission by the action, which requires students have a class rank in at least the second quartile or a grade-point average of at least 2.5. Under this designation, students must also have no more than two curricular deficiencies, of which mathematics or science cannot both be included.

"It is important that the universities consider many factors for admitting students to our public institutions," said Board President-elect Chris Herstam of Phoenix. "While we are awaiting the U.S. Supreme Court's decision on the Michigan case, which I hope is upheld, I think this action is critical to ensuring a fair and reasonable opportunity for admissions for all students."

The board voted in favor of approving the university-recommended special class fees and deposits and special program fees for the 2003 - 04 academic year. Among those fees approved for ASU is a $500 B.S. in business fee to cover both fall and spring semesters and $125 fee for each of the two summer sessions. The intersession fee for the program is $70. The fees will apply only to incoming juniors, and will not affect current juniors and seniors already admitted to the program, said Larry Penley, dean of the W. P. Carey School of Business. The additional fees will be used to raise the quality of students' educational experience, he said.

Other ASU special program fees approved by the board include the Critical Languages Institute, CLI, $300; M.S.E. graduate professional certificate, $506; M.S.E. distance learning program, $506; M.S.E. accelerated weekend - evening program, $637; and the executive M.S.E. distance learning program, $786.

ASU received board approval to implement a new doctoral program in religious studies on Main Campus and an interdisciplinary minor in organizational studies at ASU West.
The new doctoral program will train graduate students for careers as scholars and teachers and provide supplementary training for graduate students in a range of related programs, including history, anthropology, political science, journalism, secondary educations and justice studies. The new minor in organizational studies will increase students' understanding of organizational behavior and the dynamics of social processes in the workplace.

The board is also considering a policy revision to expand the availability of tuition waivers to the children and spouses of Arizona military service personnel killed in the line of duty in response to a Presidential Executive Order, as well as to severely disabled combat survivors. Waivers are already provided for children and spouses of slain Arizona Peace Officers, fire fighters and emergency paramedics. The board is expected to vote on the measure in its June meeting.

Work on a new multi-tier, 1,500 vehicle, $15 million parking structure is expected to begin in August on current lot 59, with occupancy expected by August 2004.

Executive Vice President for Administration and Finance Mernoy Harrison said the new structure will become a signature gateway into the Main Campus from the north.
