September 17, 2003

Journalist, commentator and Middle East authority to discuss possibilities for peace

This fall, Arizona State University's Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict brings to the Valley Bruce Feiler, New York Times best-selling author and NPR commentator. Feiler will be delivering a lecture entitled, "Abraham: A Journey to the Heart of Three Faiths" as part of the Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict's "Religion and Conflict: Alternative Visions" lecture series.

The free public lecture is 7:30 pm on October 9, 2003 at the ASU College of Law Great Hall (Armstrong Hall, room 113). The talk will be followed by a book-signing. Reservations are required - for more information see the end of this release.

Feiler's visit will be the first in this series of public conversations with nationally and internationally recognized writers, scholars, and policy experts. The series will explore the sources of religious conflict and strategies for their resolution.

"In this lecture series, the Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict is bringing real insight about some of the biggest issues affecting the outside world home to Arizona," said ASU President Michael Crow. "Bruce Feiler, for example, is the kind of public intellectual whose thinking about religious conflict has enough impact to grab the cover of Time magazine. This is the kind of intellectual enrichment that a great university can bring to the community."

"We're excited to have Bruce Feiler inaugurate this year's lecture series," said Linell Cady, director of the Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict. "Anyone seeking greater understanding of the religious roots of violence within and between the monotheistic religions will find his work fascinating."

Feiler's lecture takes it name from the title of his sixth and most recent book, Abraham: A Journey to the Heart of Three Faiths (2002). The book, a runaway New York Times bestseller, tells the story of Feiler's own personal search for the shared ancestor of Jews, Christians, and Muslims. Abraham is "exquisitely written," according to the Boston Globe, "100 percent engaging." The book was featured on the cover of TIME Magazine, in which it was praised as "a revolution in thought." Abraham was listed as one of the best books of the year by Amazon.com, Borders, and Publishers Weekly.

Abraham's popularity certainly has something to do with its author's skills - Feiler is an incredibly talented storyteller who frequently writes for the New York Times, The Washington Post and Gourmet, where he is a contributing editor. He is also a contributor to National Public Radio. Feiler has been praised as one of the leading writers of his generation, and his work has won him the ASCAP/Deems Taylor Award for Excellence in Music Journalism, the International Association of Culinary Professionals Award for Excellence in Food Journalism and two James Beard Awards for writing about food and restaurants. Having traveled in more than 60 countries, Feiler has a great deal of world experience from which to write.

Feiler is the author of five other books. Walking the Bible: A Journey By Land Through the Five Books of Moses (2001) recounts Feiler's year in the desert, on the trail of the greatest stories ever told. On other topics, Feiler is the author of Learning to Bow: Inside the Heart of Japan (1991), Looking for Class: Days and Nights at Oxford and Cambridge (1993), Under the Big Top: A Season with the Circus (1995), and Dreaming Out Loud: Garth Brooks, Wynonna Judd, Wade Hayes and the Changing Face of Nashville (1998).

Feiler's success with Abraham may have been due to its message - one of interfaith understanding and hope, and of recognition of the great commonalities of the world's three great monotheistic religions. Abraham, in Feiler's vision, holds the key to reconciling the differences of Jews, Christians and Muslims in today's world.

The message is not confined to the book. Since Abraham's publication, Feiler has organized a nationwide network of grassroots "Abraham Salons" - open forums in which religious leaders and laypeople can participate in interfaith discussions. Feiler's "Abraham Initiative" has also made its way into universities, local non-profits, religious and community institutions. The Initiative holds the promise of a major breakthrough in building peaceful relations among the religions.

Of Feiler's lecturing style, Ball State University wrote, ". . . a joy . . . genuine and well spoken . . . one of the best speakers in the history of the program . . ." The Smithsonian Institution remarked that their audience was left "thrilled with the presentation." According to the Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict, Feiler's lecture will explore the Biblical landscape and the figure of Abraham as sources of division and hope. It will also recount Feiler's travels in the Middle East as he searched for the meaning of the figure of Abraham for Jews, Christians and Muslims.

Future lectures in the Alternative Visions series will include "Reading Lolita in Tehran: Women, Religion and Global Politics" with Azar Nafisi, best-selling author and international activist on behalf of women's rights and democracy, in February, followed by "When Religion Becomes Evil" with CNN commentator, government policy advisor and Wake Forest University Religious Studies Professor Charles Kimball in March.

Seating at the lecture is limited and reservations are required. Call 480-727-6736 to reserve seats. For more information on the lecture or on the ASU Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict, visit the website <http://www.asu.edu/csrc> or call 480-965-7187.
