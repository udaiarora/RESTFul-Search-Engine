September 23, 2004

Prop. 102 will aid university tech transfer

Proposition 102, which is on the Nov. 2 general election ballot, is an important measure for the entire state of Arizona because it will help generate high-paying jobs, says Scott Smith, ASU’s state relations director.

Prop. 102 will allow public universities to accept equity in companies in trade for technology created at the university. Smith said many states already let their universities hold equity stakes in companies they had a hand in starting.

Many times these companies are built around a specific technology that comes out of university laboratories. A prime example of this is Google and its relation to Stanford University. As Google goes public, Stanford stands to reap the benefits from the IPO.

“Prop 102 is an important measure both on campus and off,” Smith says. “The bottom line is it will mean more technology-driven jobs for Arizona.”

Proposition 102 allows Arizona to capitalize on the synergy of research that exists within its universities to create an economic hub of new companies – and the jobs that come with it – that otherwise wouldn’t exist.

Studies show that 84 percent of all new companies locate near where the research for their products is performed. With new, high-tech businesses come new, high-tech jobs – jobs that typically pay 50 percent more than the average private sector salary. As a result, Arizona ’s tax base will expand as its economy grows.

The Association of University Technology Managers estimates that, in 2001, technology transfer accounted for $1.07 billion in adjusted gross licensing revenue and the formation of 494 new start-up companies based on academic discoveries.

“By allowing these new companies to market new discoveries, we are creating jobs, we are moving innovations into the marketplace faster and we are strengthening our universities,” Smith says. “There is no cost or risk to taxpayers. An innovation can only be successful if it is allowed to go out to the marketplace.”

Smith says Proposition 102 is suppo rted by a bipartisan coalition, including Sen. John McCain, Gov. Janet Napolitano, Republican and Democratic leaders in the legislature, chambers of commerce and business leaders throughout the state.
