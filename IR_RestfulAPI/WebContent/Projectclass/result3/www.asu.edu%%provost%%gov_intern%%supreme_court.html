General Description
One temporary, full-time, paid intern positions are available at the Arizona Supreme Court, Administrative Office of the Courts, in Phoenix. The Arizona Constitution charges the Supreme Court with responsibility for providing administrative supervision over all the courts of the state. This is done through the Administrative Office, and includes adult and juvenile probation, juvenile detention, diversion programs, child support, child custody, adoption, domestic relations, restitution, substance abuse treatment, administrative and personnel matters; and issues affecting municipal, justice, superior and appellate courts. The intern will work in the Executive Division of the Administrative Office, assisting with judicial department legislation and other administrative responsibilities.

The term of the intern position is from January 2, 2007 to May 4, 2007. Normal working hours are 8 a.m. to 5 p.m.; however, legislative activity and other job duties will require extended hours and attendance at early morning or evening meetings. There are no holidays or semester breaks.

Duties
Required duties will include attendance at court and legislative committee meetings and hearings; assisting the Administrative Director and Legislative Officers in preparing, testifying, researching and tracking legislation with impact on the courts; acting as a liaison to the Legislature; and communicating with judges and administrative staff on legislative activity. The intern will be required to prepare written materials (e.g., fact sheets, legislative summaries and letters) and make oral presentations on legislation to court committees and staff. The intern may also lobby legislators on select proposals.

Qualifications
This position requires basic research, word processing, and strong communication and organizational skills. The intern will be expected to work independently. Preference will be given to applicants with experience and demonstrated interest or academic studies in public administration, the justice system, political process, or law. Both undergraduate and graduate students are encouraged to apply.

Application Procedure
To apply, complete the “Arizona Legislative and Government Internship Program” application. Applications MUST also be accompanied by a RESUME, TWO letters of recommendation, and an ADDITIONAL WRITTEN STATEMENT identifying current issues of interest to the student that effect Arizona courts and stating the student's reasons for and interest in applying for the Supreme Court internship.

Common questions asked about the Supreme Court Internship

Q: How many interns will be hired?
A: One. The intern, together with the Legislative Officers and the Administrative Director of Courts, will comprise the legislative team for the Arizona Judicial Department.

Q: Must I be a law school student to apply?
A: No! Prior interns have had educational backgrounds in justice studies, English, business, accounting, public administration, social work and political science. A demonstrated interest in public administration and/or the judicial system, through a student's course work, future career plans, or volunteer or work experience is preferred.

Q: Does the intern work with the Supreme Court justices?
A: The Supreme Court has two main responsibilities: hearing cases on appeal from the lower courts and statewide administration of the court system. The intern's work focuses exclusively on the administrative responsibility. In this capacity, the intern may be involved in preparing information for the Chief Justice relative to legislative proposals. Researching cases pending before the Court and writing legal briefs is not one of the intern's duties.

Q: Will the intern have contact with judges and court administrators?
A: Yes. Because the Arizona Supreme Court has administrative responsibility for the state court system, this internship exposes the students to all aspects of the judicial department. Part of the intern's responsibilities will be informing judges and court administrators about proposed legislation and obtaining feedback from the courts on introduced legislation.

Q: To what issues will the Supreme Court intern be exposed?
A: Criminal and civil law matters, death penalty issues, adult and juvenile probation, juvenile detention, diversion programs, child support, child custody, adoption, domestic relations, restitution, substance abuse treatment, administrative and personnel matters; and issues affecting municipal, justice, superior and appellate courts.

Q: How do prior Supreme Court interns describe the internship?
A: “...a great learning experience that could never be obtained inside a classroom.”
“...an invaluable educational experience involving a great deal of dedication, hard work and long hours.”
“...there is no better way to learn the political process and the behind-the-scenes action.”
“Exciting, intense, very educational, demanding, challenging, WELL WORTH IT!”

Q: What did prior Supreme Court interns do after their internship?
A: Many of the prior interns went on to law school and other graduate school programs. The schools they have attended for their graduate studies include UCLA, U of A, ASU, American University and Northwestern University. Nancy Swetnam a previous Legislative Officer, continues to work for the Supreme Court as a division director. Darren La Sorte, also a previous Legislative Officer, works for the National Rifle Association as a lobbyist. Marian Conrad works for Pima County lobbying particulate matter (air polution) issues.
