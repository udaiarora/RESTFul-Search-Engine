Scientific minds - East Campus welcomes future engineers to Chip Camp

Fifty of the state's juniors and seniors spent June 15 - 21 at ASU East learning about depth diffusion, transistors, gates and vacuum pressure. And pneumatics, chemical etching and optics, too.

That may seem like pretty deep stuff for a high schooler. But not if you ask Richard Newman, director of training operations at ASU East's Microelectronics Teaching Factory.

He sees the high school years as the ideal time to introduce the complex, scientific processes of semiconductor manufacturing to the engineers of tomorrow.

By adding in a little bit of fun, Newman hopes to hook the teenagers on a career in semiconductors, or at least to weed out the ones who need to realize now that this may not be the career path for them.

It's all part of Chip Camp, an annual summer program sponsored by ASU, the state's community colleges and Intel Corp. The camp introduces students to the science and technology behind computer chips and the career opportunities of the semiconductor manufacturing industry.

"We give kids some hands-on and formal, technical experience that they wouldn't get in their high schools," said Newman.

This includes donning a head-to-toe bunny suit and working in ASU East's 15,000-square-foot clean room. Students learn to properly dress themselves in the suits and work in the bulky attire. The bunny suits and clean room environment are essential to the manufacturing process because human hair, skin cells and dust will render a computer chip useless. That's one of the most important lessons for the students to learn, Newman said.

During a clean-room tour, camp participants enthusiastically awaited their turn to suit up. Giddy chatter was quickly replaced by dismay as they put on layers of garb. It quickly became apparent that clean-room work in a bunny suit was not fun and games. Putting the suit on was a challenge in itself. Learning to move about in the suit and operate machinery in the ultra clean environment would prove even more difficult.

"If they don't like working in a bunny suit, they probably aren't going to like working in this type of engineering environment, specifically a clean-room environment. Not everyone can work in a bunny suit all day," he said. "It's better that they find out now."

While there are lots of serious topics to be covered, the camp tries to integrate science and fun. Participants build robotic Lego cars that run on computer chips. For a pneumatics lesson, campers launch a rocket, and during a tour of Intel's Chandler operations, they take time out to build transistors using Play-Doh.

The annual camp concludes with a career awareness night and awards ceremony for the students, their parents and school counselors. ASU and the other academic sponsors present their academic offerings and help the students and their parents explore the best career options in the semiconductor arena.

"This is a great opportunity to provide information for parents that they'll deem valuable in helping their children choose an academic program," said Newman.
