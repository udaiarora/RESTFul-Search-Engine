October 5, 2005

Middle East expert discusses peace possibilities

Too often in the world, religion is the source of conflict – rather than peace. Marc Gopin, a leading expert on the role of religion in international diplomacy and conflict resolution, seeks to change that equation.

Gopin, who recently participated in a series of historic dialogues between Muslims and Jews, will give a free lecture at ASU at 7:30 p.m., Oct. 10.

His talk, titled “Religion, Terrorism and the Future of Peacemaking,” will take place at the ASU College of Law Great Hall (Armstrong Hall, room 113). The talk will be followed by a reception and book signing. Tickets are required to attend the event.

Gopin has trained thousands of people around the world to develop peacemaking strategies for religious and cultural conflicts. He has appeared numerous times on CNN and National Public Radio, and he also is the author of three important books on religion and conflict: “Between Eden and Armageddon: The Future of World Religions, Violence and Peacemaking,” “Holy War, Holy Peace: How Religion Can Bring Peace to the Middle East” and “Healing the Heart of Conflict.”

Gopin doesn’t just write books, however. His scholarship is strongly informed by direct personal action aimed at transforming the heart of conflict. In just the past year, he has been part of two historic dialogues between Muslims and Jews. In January, he traveled to Damascus, Syria, and took part in an unprecedented televised discussion on Jewish-Christian-Muslim relations in the Middle East.

Of the trip – and the public dialogue – Gopin says: “Those two hours of questioning were among the hardest of my life, in terms of dealing constructively with anger, and with a delicate balance of agreement, sympathy, solidarity, honest confrontation and positive vision.”

Gopin followed up his Syrian trip with another historical meeting in Washington, D.C., between King Abdullah II of Jordan and a group of more than 60 American rabbis.

“It was a transformative event,” Gopin says. “It was the first time in history that an Arab or Muslim monarch had so honored their religion and culture.”

Gopin’s work powerfully alludes to the relationship between one’s personal struggles and the larger political struggles facing the world.

“What I have discovered,” Gopin has written, “is that there is a fundamental similarity between the intractable feuds among rival nations that cause so much strife in the world and the destructive personal and family struggles that affect us so deeply as individuals. While the scale and stakes are obviously very different, the underlying process – the drama – is the same.”

The lecture is sponsored by the ASU Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict. For more information, or to reserve tickets, call (480) 727-6736 or go to the Web site ( www.asu.edu/csrc ).
