May 19, 2006

Paintings depict life in Arizona Japanese internment camps

Last fall, when Linda Whitaker was doing an inventory at the Arizona Historical Foundation (AHF) in Hayden Library, she chanced on a long-forgotten box tucked behind the stacks.

Inside were five stunning watercolors, flat in the box, with their frames loose and deteriorating. Some were held together by duct tape.

Whitaker discovered that the paintings were part of a collection on the World War II Japanese internment camps in Arizona given to the AHF by W. Wade Head, who was director of the Poston internment camp.

"We had no idea the paintings were so big - or so good," Whitaker says. "It took me months to track down the family to see who the artist was."

Those watercolors, now framed and on display in the AHF offices, sparked the first-ever collaboration between the AHF and ASU Library Archives and Special Collections, an exhibition May 22 - Aug. 11 in the Rotunda of Hayden Library titled "A Celebration of the Human Spirit: The Art & Artifacts of Arizona's World War II Relocation Camps."

A centerpiece of the exhibit will be the watercolors, which Whitaker discovered are the work of Tokutaro "Kakunen" Tsuruoka, an artist who was interned at Poston, a camp in Yuma County, 17 miles south of Parker.

Tsuruoka painted scenes from around the camp; it's difficult to guess that the artist was in an internment camp.

Since materials were scarce or nonexistent, Tsuruoka made do with what he had. One painting is on a piece of curtain; the others are on plain paper.

"What's amazing to me is how his artistry transcends time and place," Whitaker says. "It has the capacity to inspire. To be able to create beauty in an incredibly harsh environment, physically and emotionally, ought to be celebrated."

The exhibit, which documents the Japanese-American experience at the Gila River and Poston relocation camps, will include:

. A 1942 film of the construction of the Poston camp.

. Photos depicting camp life.

. Diaries, scrapbooks, letters and newspapers.

. Special objects on loan from the Japanese America in Arizona Oral History Project.

Many of the materials come from two major gifts to the AHF: one from W. Wade Head, who was director of Poston from 1942 - 1944, and one from Frances and Mary Montgomery, who taught at Butte High School, which was located at the Gila River Relocation Center from 1942 - 1945.

During the exhibition's run, the Japanese-American Citizens League will hold its national convention in Arizona for the first time in its 77-year history, with more than 500 members expected to attend the June 21 - 24 event.

Whitaker expects some of the JACL members to come to the exhibit and perhaps even see themselves, as children, in some of the photos from the camps, most of which are unidentified.

"I think this will be very emotional," she says.
