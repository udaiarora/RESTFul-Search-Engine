ECUADOR: The Amazon and the Andes Field School
Course Syllabi

ASB 494/591: Field Study of Andean/Amazonian Cultures I (Summer only),
REL 494/591: Field Study of Andean/Amazonian Cultures I (Summer & Winter),
ASB 494/591: Field Study of Andean/Amazonian Cultures II (Summer only), or
REL 494/591: Field Study of Andean/Amazonian Cultures II (Summer only)

This course examines various traditional field work methods. Students then practice these methods in carefully structured contexts under the supervision of the instructor. After practicing the activity, the group meets for critical discussion and self reflection. For example, after the group has learned basic Quichua kinship terminology, the instructor will hand out diagrams of Quichua kinship from previous ethnographies. He then divides the class into small groups and asks each group to work with the Quichua community people to map out their extended family. It turns out, of course, that actual kinship relationships are much more "messy" and not as easily diagramable as the culture's ideal of marriage patterns would lead one to believe. This leads students to employ creative thinking in regard to how to better study kinship relations. Community members participate in the critical reflection of this field work. On some days, the discussion will center around appropriate and inappropriate methods for studying Quichua cultural phenomena. For example, if the group has spent the day listening to a Quichua healer identify plant remedies in the forest, they may discuss not only Quichua theories of medicine but also issues of cultural and intellectual property rights.

Course goals:

  • To gain direct exposure to Runa culture in the areas of history, literature, dance, music, and the arts by observing Runa life and by listening to Runa interpretations of their lives.
  • To develop field work skills appropriate to the level of the course.
  • To become skilled at recognizing ethical issues that arise in the course of field work with indigenous communities.

Course assignments:

  • Read all required readings before leaving the U.S.
  • Keep a daily field journal to be turned in at the end of the course. Daily entries should be structured in the following way: The entry should begin with an observation that provoked thought or surprise such as, "Today I was surprised to observe 'x' when I might have expected 'y'." The entry should have a thesis sentence that proposes a possible explanation for the surprising observation. The rest of the entry should develop this thesis idea.
  • A written 10-20 page research project.

Grading: Research project for 50%; journal for 25%; participation for 25%. The participation portion of the grade is based on such factors as contribution to group discussions, being prepared for class by doing the reading, and having written the journal entries in a timely manner.
