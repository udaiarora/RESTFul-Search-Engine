February 2, 2005

Western tourism softens, immigration flow continues

In recent years, the number of overseas visitors to the Western states has declined while applications for citizenship remained steady even as immigration regulations were toughened in the face of political events, according to an analysis in the current issue of the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast.

“ Florida, New York and California are attracting the largest market share of overseas visitors,” according to Western Blue Chip Editor Dawn McLaren, “but the Western states of Nevada, Texas and Arizona also rank in the top 12 destinations.” She noted that all states have seen a marked decline in overseas visitors since the economic and political turmoil that beset the nation beginning in 2001.

Meanwhile, applications for naturalization have remained steady, according to the U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services (formerly the INS).

“In 2004, Western states took the top rankings for population growth, with Nevada in the number one spot with 4.1 percent growth,” McLaren said. “Just over 70 percent of that growth is attributed to international migration into the states, while 13.8 percent is due to domestic migration. The remainder is the net natural increase (births minus deaths).

“Arizona, ranking second in population growth, can attribute about half of that growth to international migration and about a fifth of it to domestic migration. Thus, while the flow of overseas tourist dollars into the United States has softened in recent years, the impact of the immigrant population remains strong.”

The WesternBlue Chip Economic Forecast publishes the consensus forecasts of panels of economists in 10 Western states. Panelists are drawn from leading firms, universities and state agencies. The publication also features historical data for each state.

The Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast is published 10 times a year by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center , an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute at Arizona State University ’s W. P. Carey School of Business. The cost is $110 for 10 print issues ($99 for e-mail subscription).
