March 31, 2003

ASU research scientist wins Priestley Medal for discoveries in plastics

For more than 60 years Edwin J. Vandenberg has researched plastics in order to customize them to different applications and manufacture them more efficiently and cheaply. The American Chemical Society will recognize Vandenberg's distinguished career when it presents him with the organization's highest honor, the Priestley Medal, March 25.

Vandenberg now works as a research scientist in the Harrington Department of Bioengineering at Arizona State University. But he began his career at Hercules, Inc., where he made his best-known discovery.

Today the plastic polypropylene is found in products from dishwasher-safe plastic food containers to indoor/outdoor carpeting. In the early 1950s, however, polypropylene was a soft, rubbery substance inappropriate for most applications. While working at Hercules, Vandenberg independently discovered a rigid, durable form of the plastic.

Polypropylene is composed of repeating units of propylene, which contains three carbon atoms and six hydrogen atoms. When propylene molecules react with each other to form polypropylene, two of each unit's carbon atoms are used in the backbone of the chain. The other carbon can hang off the top or bottom of the molecule.

If the carbon atoms are randomly up and down, the polypropylene is soft. Vandenberg developed a reaction method that forced all these carbon atoms to be on one side of the plastic. His discovery allowed Hercules to be the first, and then the largest, producer of polypropylene. Guilio Natta, a Nobel Laureate in chemistry, discovered the rigid form of polypropylene independently about the same time.

"It's not a trivial thing. You really need the right catalyst," Vandenberg said.

When Vandenberg retired from Hercules in 1982, he began a second career at Arizona State University, first as a visiting professor and then a research professor in the Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry. He now works as a research scientist in the Harrington Department of Bioengineering.

"I like working in the lab," Vandenberg said. "I want to continue the work I did in the chemistry department."

Since the late 1980s Vandenberg has collaborated with Vincent Pizziconi, professor of bioengineering, on plastics for biomedical applications. Much like his pioneering work with polypropylene, Vandenberg has developed a method to manipulate the creation of a biomedical material he invented so that small changes in the plastic's chemical structure adapt it to different applications or tissues in the body. Thus, based on the medical device for which the plastic will be used, Vandenberg can engineer the material's properties.

The new material also appears to be more biocompatible than other biomaterials now in use. The body's immune system will attack a medical implant, which can interfere with the device's function and necessitate more frequent replacement. Creating materials that provoke less of an immune response, or, better yet, no response, is a primary aim in the development of next generation implants.

"We need to train more young people to follow in the steps of this Arizona pioneer to come up with new polymer systems," Pizziconi said. "There's still a lot of work to be done to make truly useful biomaterials that can be used to design next generation medical devices to meet the clinical needs of the 21st century."

Vandenberg will receive the Priestley Medal March 25 during the American Chemical Society National Meeting in New Orleans. Previous recipients include co-discoverer of DNA Francis Crick, astronomer Carl Sagan, and evolutionary biologist Stephen Jay Gould.
