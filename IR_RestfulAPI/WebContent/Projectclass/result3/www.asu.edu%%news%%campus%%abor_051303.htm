ABOR moves universities forward with policy changes

The Arizona Board of Regents (ABOR) has made several substantive steps over the past year to increase quality and further the mission of the state's three research universities.

Under the leadership of President Jack Jewett of Tucson, the 13-member board has revised several policies aimed at giving each university the flexibility to seek its own distinctive identity and increase accessibility to higher education.

Jewett says the board's newly-implemented Changing Directions Initiative is designed with three goals in mind - make Arizona universities more inclusive, improve the state's student success rates and equip students with the skills necessary to meet the state's workforce needs.

"These goals can and will be accomplished if the universities are allowed greater flexibility to play a more centralized role in determining their institution's policy and outcomes," he said.

The board's first of three major policy revisions came in January when it eliminated all previous reference to program delivery within geographical boundaries. The move enables each university to now pursue addressing regional needs across the state.

Prior to the policy change, each Arizona university was guaranteed the right to deliver specific services within prearranged geographical boundaries without competition from its sister institutions. With the protected boundaries removed, university presidents are now responsible for coordinating program delivery as to avoid unnecessary duplication of services, one major component of the Changing Directions Initiative.

A second ABOR policy change allows universities to differentiate tuition by academic levels, degree programs and delivery methods - and for the first time - address financial aid within the context of setting tuition.

In doing so, Jewett says universities now have a mechanism to substantially increase tuition according to their needs while making higher education more affordable for financially disadvantaged families.

The third policy revision removes the 25 percent cap on nonresident enrollment to allow universities to register a mix of students that is more congruent with each campus. The new policy mandates the presidents ensure that qualified Arizona residents will be given full access to university programs and services.

The board approved a 2003 - 04 academic year tuition increase on March 6 that is tied to an enhanced financial aid package for resident undergraduates. The board says the increase in financial aid packages - worth more than $22 million system-wide - offers greater access to the public university system while protecting low-income families.

In approving the tuition increase, the board effectively moved the Arizona University System from the 50th position among the 50 states into the 34th position, well within the board's goal of maintaining a position ranked for affordability in the top of the lower one-third of senior public institutions.

Regent Gary Stewart of Phoenix said by approving the tuition increase, the board is effectively creating much needed funding to maintain quality institutions and increase access.

When the board unanimously approved in April to establish new admission requirements, Jewett said the last cornerstone was laid for full implementation of the board's Changing Direction Initiative. The new admissions policy becomes effective for the 2006 freshman class. It assures the top 25 percent of graduating Arizona high school seniors who have no curricular deficiencies will be accepted to the three universities. The board agreed that phasing in the policy would give adequate leeway for future college-bound seniors to prepare to meet the revised admission standards.
Formerly, the universities used grade-point averages and standardized test scores as criteria for assured admissions, however these measures have now been eliminated by the revised policy.

The new policy also delegates authority to the universities to admit students eligible for what was formerly designed as "conditional admission." This allows universities to review admissions of student applications on a case-by-case basis where academic deficiencies exist. This designation was changed to Delegated Admission and will require students to have a class rank at least in the second quartile, or at least a 2.5 grade-point average. Applicants with extenuating circumstances who do not meet the minimum qualifications have the opportunity for an appeal.

"It is important that the universities consider many factors for admitting students to our public institutions," said ABOR President-elect Chris Herstam of Phoenix. "I think this action is critical to ensuring a fair and reasonable opportunity for admission for all students."

"I am impressed with the outstanding and visionary leadership of the board," said ASU President Michael Crow. "The new policies are milestones in the continuing process of achieving further excellence and positioning ASU as the New American University."

The board approved several new facilities for ASU to increase its world-class stature, including the Arizona Biodesign Institute (AzBio), the $69-million project centerpiece of ASU's multi-phase biodesign corridor. AzBio will provide an intellectual and physical environment to allow the state to compete in the global bioscience and technology marketplace.

The board gave support to ASU's plans to build new residence halls which supports ASU's mission to become a more fully residential campus. The board also gave approval for ASU to lease space at the Brickyard complex on Mill Avenue in Tempe to house the Institute for Computer and Information Sciences and Engineering (ICISE). The move allows ASU to centralize its key academic and research programs in computer science, and extend partnerships across the campus. The expected outcome is to enhance ASU's computer science department to a top-rank program.
In April, the board adopted a charter for Arizona Biomedical Collaborative for Education, Research and Health Policy (ABC), a new organizational entity to address and facilitate biomedical education and research needs of the state.

Representatives of higher education and the health care industry are currently working on a comprehensive statewide plan to expedite a collaborative nursing training program at all academic levels, and nursing faculty training at the masters and doctoral levels to better address the state's professional healthcare shortages.

ABC will also foster and coordinate research of interdisciplinary teams of scientists from the Arizona University System, community colleges and Arizona Health Sciences Center. The partnership will share expertise for advancing treatments and possible cures for several diseases such as pancreatic cancer and melanoma, diabetes, Alzheimer's and heart disease. A focused relationship with the Translational Genomics Research Institute and International Genomics Consortium will be a key element in fostering translational research.
