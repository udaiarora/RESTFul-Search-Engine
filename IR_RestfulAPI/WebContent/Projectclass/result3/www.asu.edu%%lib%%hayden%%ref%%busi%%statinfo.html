STATISTICAL INFORMATION--GUIDES TO UNDERSTANDING THE NUMBERS

The U.S. government is in the process of reformulating its economic indicators. These works are only guides to what an indicator measures and how they can be used. The actual formula of the components of the indicator may have changed since the book was published. Please consult with our Government Documents Reference Librarians more information if the formula is critical for your work.

  1. By the Numbers. New York: Federal Reserve Bank of New York, .
      http://www.ny.frb.org/pihome/educator/bythe.html

    This web site is maintained by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York to help you understand the economic indicators that the Federal government publishes and how they are used.

  2. Plocek, Joseph E. Economic Indicators: How America Reads Its Financial Health. New York: New York Institute of Finance, 1991.
      HB 3743 .P6 1991 HAYDEN REFERENCE
      HB 3743 .P6 1991 WEST STACKS

    More detailed and technical than Frumkin's work (below), this is a business or economic person's explanation of the importance of 20 leading economic indicators important to investing.

  3. Economic Indicators Handbook: Time Series, Conversions, Documents. 4th ed. Detroit, MI: Gale Research, 1998.
      HC 103 .E26 1998 HAYDEN REFERENCE

    Each indicator has a page or two of explanation followed by actual tables of data for all years available.

  4. Economist Guide to Economic Indicators. New York: Wiley, 1997.
      HA 29 .E28 1997 HAYDEN REFERENCE

    This work starts with chapters on interpretation of economic indicators and the essential mechanics of them. The indicators are divided into eleven broad categories. Each indicator has its own entry with the following information included: what it measures; its significance; how it is presented; the focus of the indicator; the yardstick; the release; an overview; and an explanation.

  5. Frumkin, Norman. Guide to Economic Indicators. 2nd ed. Armonk, NY: M.E. Sharpe, 1994.
      HC 103 .F9 1994 HAYDEN REFERENCE
      HC 103 .F9 1994 WEST STACKS

    A wonderful book for those who don't understand what economic indicators are and do. The introduction has the basic information on indicators as a whole; the rest of the book has a section on each of the economic indicators. Each indicator is identified as to who publishes the information, frequency, content, methodology, accuracy, relevance, and recent trends. Tables of data are used to illustrate the explanations.

  6. Clayton, Gary E. and Martin Gerhard Giesbrecht. Guide to Everyday Economic Statistics. 4th ed. New York: McGraw-Hill, 1997.
      HB 137 .C57 1997 HAYDEN REFERENCE
      HB 137 .C57 1997 EAST STACKS
      HB 137 .C57 1997 WEST REFERENCE

    This slim work is packed with practical examples of how these indicators are calculated and used.

  7. Bernstein, Jacob. The Handbook of Economic Cycles: Jake Bernsteins's Comprehensive Guide to Repetitive Price Patterns in Stocks, Futures, and Financials. Homewood, IL: Business One Irwin, 1991.
      HB 3711 .B46 1991 HAYDEN REFERENCE
      HB 3711 .B46 1991 WEST STACKS

    This book covers the economic cycles of the financial markets. This is not the same as a particular business or product life cycle, but it can be helpful estimating those cycles and the possible effect of the economic cycles on them. The explanation of business and economic cycles generally is very good. The cycle information provided on commodities is helpful to the manufacturers in anticipating some of the pricing fluctuations of needed commodities.

  8. Rogers, R. Mark. Handbook of Key Economic Indicators. Burr Ridge, IL: Irwin, 1994.
      HC 103 .R64 1994 HAYDEN REFERENCE
      HC 103 .R64 1998 EAST STACKS
      HC 103 .R64 1998 WEST STACKS

    This work is written especially for those who have trouble understanding economic indicators. It covers 13 sets of economic indicators, their purpose, how to use them, and how they are calculated. In addition to many charts and graphs, bibliographies for each chapter are included.

  9. Tracy, John A. How to Read a Financial Report: Wringing Vital Signs Out of the Numbers. 5th ed. New York: John Wiley & Sons, 1994.
      HF 5681 .B2 T733 1999 HAYDEN REFERENCE
      HF 5681 .B2 T733 1999 WEST STACKS

    Designed for those who have yet to master Corporate Finance, or can't remember it exactly, this book leads the reader from the explanation to the actual lines in the financial statement that apply. The last half of the book discusses the pitfalls of accounting in financial statements and some of the meanings that can be ascribed to the information.

  10. Crispell, Diane. Insider's Guide to Demographic Know-How: Everything You Need to Find, Analyse, and Use information About Your Customers. Rev. ed. Chicago: Probus, 1993.
      HF 5415.2 .C757x 1993 HAYDEN REFERENCE
      HF 5415.2 .C757x 1993 WEST STACKS

    The first part of this book has articles that help explain what numbers are needed and how to analyze them. Part II provides lists of agencies and companies that provide population related information in more detail than this. A glossary and subject index are included in the back of the book.

  11. The New Field Guide to the U.S. economy: a Compact and Irreverent Guide to Econoimic Life in America. Rev. Ed. New York: W.W. Norton, 1995.
      HC103 .F59x 1995 HAYDEN REFERENCE
      HC103 .F59x 1995 WEST STACKS

    This is very humorously written. It is designed to make economic indicators understandable to those who have trouble understanding economics.

  12. Understanding the Bottom Line: Finance for Nonfinancial Managers and Business Owners. 2nd ed. Hawthorne, NJ: Career Press, 1993.
      HF 5681 .B2 P59 1993 HAYDEN REFERENCE

    This work is intended for managers without finance or accounting backgrounds. It explains in simple terms each line of an accounting statement and how they can be used to understand the financial situation of a company. An excellent source for marketing people trying to analyze their company or the competition.
