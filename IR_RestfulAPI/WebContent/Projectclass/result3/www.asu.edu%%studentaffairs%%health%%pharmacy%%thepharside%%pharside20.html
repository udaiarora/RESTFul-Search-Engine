Gaucher’s Disease

In the fall, a new student with Gaucher’s disease will be attending Arizona State University.  Many of us may not be aware of the symptoms and treatment of the disease.

Gaucher's disease (pronounced go-shay) is a rare inherited enzyme deficiency, which is present in more than 10,000 to 20,000 Americans.  It is a pan ethnic disorder, with highest prevalence in the Ashkenazi Jewish population.  Patients diagnosed with Gaucher’s disease have a genetic defect in the enzyme glucocerebroside, most commonly in the macrophages in the bone marrow.  The disease, if left untreated, can lead to a lethal disorder. 

Gaucher's disease is characterized by a variety of clinical signs and symptoms, ranging from enlarged spleen and liver, lung, kidney, and digestive problems, to growth retardation in children, joint pain, spontaneous fractures, acute bone crisis, nosebleeds, bruising, anemia, and other blood-related problems.  In more severe cases, the central nervous system can also affected.  The presence or absence of the central nervous system classifies the three forms of the disease into type 1, 2 and 3. 

§         In type 1, patients may display a combination of anemia, low blood platelet levels, massively enlarged livers and spleens, and/ or extensive skeletal damage.  However, some type 1 patients show no symptoms and can be identified only by screening or during evaluation for other diseases.  Type 1 disease is characterized by the lack of neurologic involvement.

§         Type 2 Gaucher’s disease, also known as the acute neuropathic form, is characterized by brainstem abnormalities and is usually fatal during the first three years of life.  Type 2 shows no ethnic predilection and has an incidence of 1 in 100,000 live births.

§         Type 3 Gaucher’s disease, called the chronic neuropathic, also shows no ethnic predilection, and is estimated to occur in 1 in 50,000 live births.  The neurological symptoms of type 3 Gaucher’s disease progress slowly and appear later in childhood.  Neurologic symptoms of type 3 Gaucher’s disease include incoordination, mental deterioration, and myoclonic seizures.

Since Gaucher’s disease is a recessive disease, the offspring of two parental carriers has a 25 percent chance of inheriting two abnormal copies of the gene.  Testing is complicated by the fact that genetic expression is extremely variable, and patients testing positive may not show signs or symptoms of the disease.  Therefore, routine testing is not recommended and may have disadvantages, since information may be revealed to insurance companies.

In type 1 disease there is evidence proving that enzyme replacement therapy with Imiglucerase (Cerezymeâ) is beneficial in reducing hepatosplenomegaly, improving hematologic parameters, and, to a lesser extent, alleviating bone disease.  Imiglucerase is an analogue of the human enzyme B-glucocerebrosidase, a lysosomal glycoprotein enzyme that catalyzes the hydrolysis of the glycolipid glucocerebroside to glucose and ceramide.  Imiglucerase is produced by recombinant DNA technology using mammalian cell culture (Chinese hamster ovary).  Enzyme therapy appears to decrease the need for splenectomy in most cases.

As reported in Facts and Comparisons, a clinical trial with Imiglucerase involving 25 patients with Gaucher’s disease noted the following adverse effects: headache (n=3); nausea, abdominal discomfort, dizziness, pruritus, rash, mild decrease in blood pressure, and a decrease in urinary frequency (n=1).  None of the events were judged as serious or warranted medical intervention or interruption of therapy.  All proved transient and did not recur frequently.

In other studies, patients with type 2 disease treated with enzyme replacement therapy showed no substantial improvement in neurological problems, proving that the therapy may not be as successful in treating all symptoms of Gaucher’s disease. The efficacy of enzyme replacement for neurological abnormalities in type 3 remains to be established.

The most contentious issue, and potentially the most difficult for patients and physicians, is enzyme dosage.  The therapy is expensive, ranging from $100,000 to $400,000 annually for each patient. Initial dosage may be as little as 2.5 U/kg three times a week or as much as 60 U/kg administered as frequently as once a week or as infrequently as every four weeks.  60 U/kg every two weeks is the optimal dosage for which most data is available.  After patient response is well established, a reduction in dosage may be attempted for maintenance therapy.

When using Imiglucerase for treatment, it should be diluted with sterile water for injection and mixed in an infusion bag of 100 to 200 ml of normal saline.  The final solution should be administered over one to two hours.

More information on Gaucher’s disease can be found through the National Institutes of Health’s web site at www.nih.gov.
