A.S.U. PHARMACY NEWSLETTER                            ISSUE TWENTY-FOUR

Herbal Update

Echinacea

Reported Uses: Antibacterial, antiviral, immune support

Summary:  Echinacea is an herb used for the treatment and prevention of colds and flu. Trials have produced mixed results in favor and contradictory to its effectiveness.  Echinacea is being promoted as a product for seasonal outbreaks to decrease the duration and symptoms.  Echinacea has also been used externally for wound cleansing, eczema, burns, herpes, canker sores, and abscesses. 

Pharmacology:  Echinacea uses many methods of stimulating the immune system.  These methods include white blood cell function and cell-mediated immunity involving T-lymphocytes, natural killer cells, and macrophages.  Echinacea stimulates white bloods cells to seek out foreign bacteria and cellular debris.  Also, Echinacea inhibits hyaluronidase and stimulates fibroblast production of collagen.

Contraindications/Warnings: General recommendations include a 3 week on and 1 week off cycle.  A caution should be taken with renal impaired patients. Patients with an allergy to the steraceae/Compostae family such as ragweed, daisy, aster, or chrysanthemum or other like pollens should be aware of rare but serious crossover reactions.   

 

Melatonin

Reported Uses: Insomnia and Jet lag

Summary: Melatonin is the primary hormone used to regulate a 24 sleep/wake cycle.  When supplemented,  melatonin has phase-shifting and sleep promoting properties. This supplementation may help patients with a variety of work shifts in which there sleeping schedule is not always constant. Preparations may include a sublingual or oral medication route. Since B-6 is needed to synthesize melatonin in the body, some preparations add it to their formula. 

Contraindications/Warnings: There are no long term studies but excessive dosages may cause increased drowsiness and morning sedation.

Kava Kava

Reported uses: Insomnia, anxiety and

stress relief.

Summary: Kava Kava is a unique plant found in the South Pacific.  It works through a unique mechanism to aid relaxation without impairment to memory and motor functions.

Pharmacology: Kava Kava appears to act on the amygdala complex of the limbic system which moderates many emotional processes.  Unlike benzodiazepines and barbiturates it does not show to significant effect on GABA.

Contraindications/Warnings: Liver toxicity has been reported with high doses. Patients on anxiety and depression medications should not use Kava Kava.

Red Yeast Rice

Reported uses: Hypercholesterolemia

Summary: Red Yeast Rice (monazcus purpureus) is yeast which grows naturally on starch or silage. Later it is cultivated on rice and ground into a fine powder. It has been shown to lower LDL cholesterol and triglycerides while increasing HDL cholesterol.

Pharmacology: Eight compounds with HMG-CoA reductase inhibitory activity have been identified, including lovastatin and in active hydroxyl acid metabolite.  In addition to containing HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors there are other active compounds.  These compounds include unsaturated fatty acids, amino acids, phyosterols, isoflavone, and saponins, and trace elements such as selenium.  Clinical trials show that red yeast rice is a natural and effective way to have significant lowering of total cholesterol, triglycerides, and a decrease in LDL and an increase in HDL. 

Contraindications/Warnings: Since Red Yeast Rice is in some ways identical to commercially available cholesterol medications the same warnings apply.  Patients should not use in combination with other statins. Routine testing should be done to evaluate liver functions and to monitor course of therapy.

Black Cohosh

Reported Uses: Premenstrual and postmenopausal symptoms

Summary: Black Cohosh has been used for centuries to treat painful menses.  The herb contains phytoestrogens which mimic estrogens. Some preparations have these phytoestrogens standardized. These include formononetin and triterpernoid 27-deoxyactein. Salicylic acid is also found in black cohosh which will give it anti-inflammatory and analgesic properties.  Another compound found in black cohosh is cimicifugoside. This compound affects the hypothalamus pituitary function, which is believed to be overall beneficial for the management of symptoms.

Contraindications/Warnings: Contraindicated for patients with estrogen-dependent tumors or endometrial cancer. Use with caution with patient with allergies to salicylates.  Patients on hormone replacement therapy and oral contraceptives should also use caution along with those at risk of thromboembolic disease or stroke. 

Roger Allen

Source- Lexicon Natural Products

Ephedra,

Wherefore Art Thou?

On February 6 the Food And Drug Administration (FDA) declared dietary supplements containing ephedrine alkaloids prohibited for sale. This prohibition includes products that contain ephedra. In recent years, ephedra products have been marketed as dietary supplements to promote weight loss, increase energy, and enhance athletic performance.  Ephedra is a naturally occurring substance that comes from botanicals. The principal active ingredient ephedrine is an amphetamine-like compound that can powerfully stimulate the nervous system and heart. Ephedrine alkaloids are found naturally in a number of plants. The FDA released the following statement concerning their actions: “We have concluded that dietary supplements containing ephedrine alkaloids pose a risk of serious adverse events, including heart attack, stroke, and death, and that these risks are unreasonable in light of any benefits that may result from the use of these products”.  The FDA made this decision by stating that the risks of ephedra outweigh the benefits.  Ephedra is found in numerous over-the-counter products.  Some of the most common products are Hydroxycut, Ma Huang, Ripped Fuel, MetaboLITE, and Xenedrine.  This ruling does not include traditional Chinese herbals or herbal teas.  Additionally, prescription medications that contain ephedrine or ephedra will not be affected. Patients who are currently taking a product that may contain ephedra should consult with a pharmacist or physician to determine whether use of the product should be continued.

Rob Berger
