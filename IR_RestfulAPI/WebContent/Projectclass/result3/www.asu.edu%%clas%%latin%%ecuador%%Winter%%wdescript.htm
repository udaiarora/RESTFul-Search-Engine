ECUADOR: The Amazon and the Andes Field School

Winter Course Descriptions

The 300 & 400 level courses are for undergraduate credit and the 500 level courses are for graduate credit.

Language Classes:

ASB 294/494/598: Amazonian Culture & Sustainable Environment (3 credits). Prerequisites: basic ability to speak and understand Spanish.  Students may sign up for this crosslisted course under either the Anthropology rubric (ASB) or the Religious Studies rubric (REL).  Course requirements: 1) Critical field journal, and 2) written research project 10-20 pages.
SPA 101: Elementary Spanish (4 credits).  Fundamentals of the language.  Emphasis on listening, speaking, reading, and writing.

SPA 102: Elementary Spanish (4 credits).  Fundamentals of the language.  Emphasis on listening, speaking, reading, and writing.  The continuation of SPA 101.

SPA 201: Intermediate Spanish (4 credits).  Continuation of the fundamentals.  Emphasis on the development of the skills of reading, listening comprehension, speaking, writing, and culture.

SPA 202: Intermediate Spanish (4 credits).  Continuation of the fundamentals.  Emphasis on the development of the skills of reading, listening comprehension, speaking, writing, and culture.  The continuation of SPA 201.

FLA 494/590: Beginning Kichwa (4 credits). This course provides students with immersion-based instruction in Kichwa (Runa Shimi) as it is currently spoken in Ecuador (Napo and Otavalo dialects). Fundamentals of the language are covered. Emphasis on listening and speaking.

FLA 494/590: Intermediate Kichwa (4 credits). Emphasis on the development of fluency in conversation. Students engage in more advanced study of Kichwa semantics to deepen their understanding of the Andean/Amazonian symbolic world.

FLA 494/590: Advanced Kichwa (4 credits). Emphasis on the development of fluency in conversation. Students engage in more advanced study of Kichwa semantics to deepen their understanding of the Andean/Amazonian symbolic world.

Religious Studies Classes:

REL 332: South American Religious Traditions (3 credits).* This course is appropriate for undergraduates or for graduate students who do not meet the Spanish skills requirements for "Field Study of Andean/Amazonian Cultures" listed below. All materials will be translated into English. This course fulfills the ASU General Studies requirements in 1) Humanities and Fine Arts, and 2) Global Awareness. Course requirement: Critical field journal analyzing the learning experiences of each day.

REL 494/598: Amazonian Culture & Sustainable Environment (3 credits).* Prerequisites: basic ability to speak and understand Spanish; graduate student status or permission of the instructor. Course requirements: 1) Critical field journal; and 2) written research project 10-20 pages.

Grading policy and deadlines for turning in assignments: Because the faculty will not return from Ecuador until after Winter grades are due, all students will receive "Incompletes" which will be converted to grades by April 1st, 2007. Undergraduate courses have two important components: 1) participation in the various field learning experiences; and 2) the critical field journal. The journal must be typed and turned in by February 15th, 2007. No journals will be accepted after February 15th. Grades in graduate courses are based on three components: 1) participation; 2) the critical field journal; and 3) the research project. The journal must be turned in by February 15th, 2007. The research project must be turned in by March 15th, 2007.

Please note: Course syllabi and grading policies tentative until the first day of class

*Further explanation of field coursework: REL 332 and REL 494/598: Field Study of Amazonian Culture provide different rubrics for a field study of the oral history, literature, art, and religious life of the Quichua people. In all of these classes, our time is structured around a particular learning encounter or series of encounters with the Quichua world. For example, the class observes an Amazonian healing ceremony or listens to elders recount their memories of the Amazonian rubber boom or learns about the meaning of traditional hunting grounds while camped out in the rainforest with a Quichua family. Following the experience, students write about the event in a journal. After the class has had time to write in their journals, the class meets for a discussion of the event.

The key difference is that REL 332 is carried out in English and does not require Spanish. The entire class works together as a group in various field learning experiences. This class is primarily designed for undergraduates and can be used to meet ASU General Studies requirements. Graduate students who do not speak Spanish may also enroll in this courses. REL 494/598: Field Study of Amazonian Culture is primarily designed for graduate students. They require the ability to speak and understand Spanish. Greater flexibility for individual or small group field research is built into these courses.
