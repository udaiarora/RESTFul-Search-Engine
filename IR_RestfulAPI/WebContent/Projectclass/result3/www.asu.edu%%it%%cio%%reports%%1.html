ARIZONA STATE UNIVERSITY — INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY REVIEW

SUMMARY OF FINDINGS AND RECOMMENDATIONS OF THE EXTERNAL REVIEW TEAM

December 2003

James R. Bottum
Vice President for Information Technology
Purdue University

Ken Klingenstein
Chief Technologist
University of Colorado at Boulder

Joel L. Hartman
Vice Provost for Information Technologies & Resources
University of Central Florida

This report provides a summary of recommendations of the external IT review team that visited ASU on December 1 — 2, 2003. The team was charged with assessing the current status of information technology planning, resources, programs and services at the university and providing external guidance and perspective to ASU. The specific focus of the IT review is to assess current issues and future directions, as guided by a set of questions directed to the panel by Provost Glick. Each question is followed by a brief summary of the team's comments and recommendations. It should be remembered that these comments are based upon the information gathered during the two day visit and not the result of an in-depth study.

Are the university's IT resources strategically positioned for current and future needs?

  • There is little historical evidence of comprehensive strategic planning, both at the university level and within the Information Technology unit.
  • The central IT budget and staffing levels appear low for an institution of IT's size and aspirations, and it appears that the unit has had difficulty balancing its resources with its mission. The organization does not exhibit a "can do" attitude and seems to defensively retreat behind a perceived lack of resources.
  • Communication between IT and campus academic and administrative leaders is mostly of a tactical nature, rather than strategic. Consequently, IT's IT vision, directions, and challenges are not widely recognized or understood throughout the institution.
  • Although IT East and IT West have made it a priority to equip classrooms with multimedia presentation capabilities and network connections, this appears to have been a low priority on the main campus. Faculty interviewed by the visiting team lamented the lack of these resources. Faculty also complained about cumbersome scheduling procedures for technology equipped classrooms. Another area that was identified as needing improvement was the student computer labs, where many computers are reportedly as much as 5-6 years old and running old software.
  • Faculty also pointed to a lack of high performance computing capacity as a shortcoming and a noticeable level of frustration was expressed by faculty who reported they do not believe they can obtain access to the recent T-Gen high performance computing cluster. The team heard reports of high performance computing clusters being developed at the departmental or project level. There appears to be no process of information sharing, articulation, or policy setting in this area, leading to suboptimal use of resources. There is clearly a need to foster a community of faculty with current or emerging needs for high performance computing and networking working in conjunction with Information Technology so that existing resources can be coordinated, shared and new resources planned in a more holistic fashion.
  • Another area where current needs are not being met is in disk space for email, and possible other areas such as Blackboard and web hosting.
  • The current administrative computing strategy is flawed in two significant ways. First, the strategy appears to be based more on the avoidance of making the required investment than developing the administrative applications and services required to meet the needs of IT's constituents now and into the future. An effective forward-looking ERP strategy needs to be defined and costs identified so that attention can be drawn to the considerable planning, implementation and funding requirements. The second flaw is the lack of a "visible" application platform architecture. For ERP systems, other enterprise-wide applications (calendaring, card access, facilities, etc) and departmental systems to interact, exchange data and authorizations, and be accountable, the set of shared application services (databases, web servers, application servers, directory services, etc.) must be identified, developed, and supported.
  • Planning and implementing a new ERP system typically takes 5 — 10 years. Given the age of IT's ERP systems and their reliance on COBOL, planning efforts should start immediately for transition to a new ERP system.

Are there current/short-term or potential points of failure that need to be addressed to insure that sufficient, reliable IT resources are available to meet university needs?

  • The university data center appears to be orderly and well maintained. The average age of the primary servers or clusters is low, and extensive use of identical rack servers running Windows or Linux serves to increase reliability and reduce operating costs. However, Information Technology has identified several single points of failure that need to be addressed immediately for critical systems. The team heard several anecdotal reports of reliability issues with regard to Blackboard and electronic mail services. In both cases, the causes appear to be a combination of external factors and internal system management issues.
  • The existing campus network is in serious need of an upgrade to address capacity, security and reliability issues, and there is a need for an extensive new infrastructure to serve the research and educational facilities planned or under construction.
  • In addition to the needed network upgrades, there are significant vulnerabilities above the network level that have not been explored, prioritized, or assigned funding. IT staff across campus stated that there was a high likelihood of a major privacy spill, where sensitive student, financial, or research information could be hacked or exposed to the public. IT lacks a proper response unit and strategies to address denial of service (DOS) and other network attacks. The central Web authentication service has not kept pace with demand and is not equipped for its current load. One of the factors that may hide these vulnerabilities is the lack of IT auditors in the campus and system audit service. Some assessment function is necessary so that the less visible points of failure can be identified and addressed before serious problems occur.
  • There is no centralized security point for coordinating and leveraging distributed security efforts.
  • Funding limitations have led to a lack of capacity planning, and project identification and prioritization. The lack of strategic planning and direction allows dramatic shifts of priorities when any funding becomes available.

Do existing capacities, technical capabilities and plans adequately address future university needs? Are university efforts to explore and evaluate new technologies and applications sufficient to guide planning for the future?

  • Faculty and staff described the process central IT used to select, test, and deploy a university spam filtering solution as "top down and inward looking". The rollout of this service caused a significant fraction of the email instabilities during Fall 2003 that the team heard about. It was not clear to the team how central IT is evaluating emerging technologies, and despite the numerous opportunities that exist IT does not appear to be partnering with research faculty in this area.

What are some possible targets for improving the breadth, quality and the efficiency of university IT resources and services?

  • There are many current and emerging campus IT needs, as described in this document and known to many at IT. What is needed is not so much a list of projects or targets of opportunity, which already exist in great number, but rather fundamental improvements in campus IT planning, collaboration with other campus units and leaders, priority setting, and effective communication. There is a need to move information technology from what Bill Graves calls "random acts of progress" to a more strategic (e.g., linked to institutional goals and objectives) and systemic (e.g., broad, long range vision) way of doing business.
  • The campus has a fragmented approach to enterprise-wide applications, both ERP and others, that warrants a solid underpinning to provide the levels of service needed now and in the future.
  • IT security lacks effective coordination among distributed departmental and central staff and needs a focus point and additional staffing.

How should the university go about addressing the computational needs of its research community? What organizational model should be deployed?

  • It does not appear that a thorough analysis has been done on the nature of research computing needs. Resources need to be leveraged into some form of resource grid.
  • Beyond these high-end computing resources, the campus should take steps to improve the support of research desktops. Contract workstation support services at many campuses provide centralized economies, negotiated local customization, reliable backups, and improved overall campus security. IT could provide seed funding for such a useful research computer support service, with ongoing support from projects and grants.
  • Given the diversity of computational needs and interests, the review team recommends that IT establish a faculty driven "cyberinfrastructure" task force, to include representation from central IT, that will review and document IT's existing computational resource base and its researchers' current computational, communications, data, and visualization needs and obstacles. The task force should then make recommendations on how IT can plug its resource holes and tie its existing resources together into a "technology grid" that will allow optimum accessibility and use of resources for the maximum number of researchers.
  • All future centrally-funded distributed computing investments should be guided by this planning process. A diversity of computing architectures should be pursued in building the grid to match the diversity of application and data needs among faculty. Constructing a computing resource grid will also present faculty with research opportunities in areas such as peer to peer storage. As it learns to manage this infrastructure, central IT can then partner with faculty in a way that it is not doing now, and turn the infrastructure into a "living laboratory" for research and education. New central investment in both infrastructure and staff to operate the environment is strongly encouraged to meet current needs and accommodate the vision for future university research growth.
  • Central IT should be the integrator and/or operator of such an environment but faculty should steer the choices and selection of architecture and research directions. It is likely that IT will not only have to expand staff resources in existing areas to be able to meet this challenge, but also add new core competencies by bringing in staff with new capabilities to insure success.

Online course delivery is becoming more critical to the mission of the university. We would like your input on how we could better serve the university community in this area.

Although the number of online courses is increasing, the university does not have a clear strategy for this activity. Faculty and course development services for online instruction are provided by at least three units: the Center for Learning and Teaching Excellence, Distance Learning & Technology in the College of Extended Education, and the IT Instruction Support group. These units have recognized the duplication of services, and have taken steps to improve inter-unit coordination; however, more needs to be done to more cohesively integrate the services and resources of these units. The university should take steps to: select and plan a strategy for on-line and technology assisted courses; explore new pedagogies and improved instructional models; standardize approaches and develop clear and consistent instructional models; develop and implement scalable faculty and course development processes to insure quality; and implement systematic assessment for all technology assisted courses.

What "next steps" do you recommend to improve the IT environment at IT?

The visiting team provides the following as "next steps" that we believe have potential to improve the overall IT environment at IT.

  1. The review team observed a disconnect between academic and administrative units and the central IT group. The current state of central IT organization and operation is not sufficiently aligned with the university's directions to become a significant partner. It should also develop strategies for partnering with faculty to further the university's research and learning missions. We note that this is not an easy or quick process and will take a significant effort.
  2. The Provost or President should assign responsibility and authority to empower the CIO and the Information Technology unit to set university-wide standards in areas such as: e-mail, calendaring, directory, authentication, networking and online learning. Standards should be set in collaboration with the distributed IT community. The IT Advisory Council could be an appropriate forum for advice and coordination of distributed IT resources if all departments and colleges participate.
  3. The CIO should initiate an IT strategic planning process. The review team heard that there was no planning because there was no money. Continuing this approach seems to assure that there never will be any resources to meet IT's needs, or a plan worthy of financial support. The university administration should clearly articulate planning assumptions and expectations related to IT throughout the institution. IT can then work to create an IT strategic plan aligned with these directions and provide a detailed set of commonly understood technical roadmaps outlining resource requirements that should allow IT to better allocate resources currently in place and also articulate the need for additional resources.
  4. The university's administrative computing systems will soon be at the end of their life cycle and need to be replaced. In the meantime, the current systems will likely not scale to the university's stated enrollment goal of over 90,000 students, and the lack of integration between systems will become increasingly limiting and problematic. Planning needs to begin immediately to assess the true useful life of current administrative applications, and to develop and gain consensus and support of university administration for a plan to have replacement systems in place when the legacy systems inevitably reach the end of their functional lives.
  5. The review team recommends that the university establish a cross-cutting faculty-led task force to include central IT participation under the guidance of the VP for Research to develop a forward looking plan for utilizing and advancing IT's cyberinfrastructure. The task force should be charged to develop a plan and a business model for harnessing the existing computational resources in a manner that insures maximum support for, and leverage by the broad research and learning communities.
  6. WEB resources such as the MyASU portal are organized by departments, not services. Faculty, staff, and students should be included in efforts to create a new model that provides access to university services, information, business transactions, and communications.
  7. Institutional strategies and models for online learning should be developed, and support resources restructured to provide consistent, scalable support for faculty development, course development, and online learner support.
