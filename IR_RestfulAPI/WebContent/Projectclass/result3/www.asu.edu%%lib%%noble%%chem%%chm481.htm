Advanced Strategies
Researching a term paper for a high school or lower undergraduate class is a simple linear process of building a knowledge base via an encyclopedia, plus a small number of books and/or journal articles. Literature searching at the graduate or professional level must be comprehensive and is more of a circular or spiral process that involves research, discovery, research again, more discovery, research again, more discovery, etc. until the searcher has compiled a comprehensive list of resources.

At the graduate or professional level, the searcher must use multiple databases to cover all aspects of the topic, especially, as in this instance, when the subject itself (geochemistry) is trans-disciplinary in scope. Additionally, the searcher must be familiar with the content, indexing, terminology and search engine of each of the databases so that comprehensive results can be achieved.

For this exploration of applicable databases, the topic remediation of heavy metals, especially mercury will be used; if the topic needs to be narrowed further, the remediation will be limited to water, and if necessary to limit further, to the Great Lakes. Each database will contain an explanation of the content, and how the indexing and terminology effect search strategy.

Tips applicable to all databases for comprehensive searching:

  • Graduate students: the goal is to find everything -- anything that may support your research or anything that indicates that what you want to do has already been done before. Don't try narrowing the search too much or you may eliminate critical material.

  • Undergraduate students: If your selected topic retrieves too much, narrow your topic to a specific geographic region (country, state, etc.) and/or a type of environment (water vs soil vs air; arctic vs desert; freshwater vs oceans)

  • Think creatively. Are there other ways to describe your topic? Try looking at the problem from the opposite angle.
    Example: remediation is taking the material out, however, good resources may be found by thinking of terms that put the material in, such as contamination or pollution.

  • Use the databases' thesaurus (may also be called Controlled Vocabulary, Subject Headings or Descriptors) to determine:
    • other words/terminology to use in the search
    • if different terminology is used to describe the topic
    • if terminology is used only during a specified time-period

Content of the Database

  • As the name implies, Chemical Abstracts is geared to the chemist. All areas of chemistry are covered, including applied chemistry.

  • The types of chemistry publications covered are journal articles, books, conference proceedings, U.S. technical reports, patents, and doctoral dissertations.

  • Coverage starts with 1900.

  • Almost all records contain abstracts, so keyword searching usually provides good retrieval on concepts but may not be good for searching compounds because of the indexing policies.

Search Engine

Use the Chemical Substance or Reaction Search for:

  • substance names, especially those that are complex (use either name, structure or molecular formula searches)
  • isotopes (use molecular formula search), and
  • group or class of compounds with a common substructure (use structure drawing)
  • elements (use the name search)

Use the Research Topic Search for:

  • chemical "concepts" such as "heavy metals" or "trace elements"
  • May also be used for:
    • elements (use the name of the element, no need to put in the element symbol)
    • simple compound names of one or two words (use the most generic/common name of the substance)

Tutorials explaining how to use the search engine are available at: http://www.cas.org/SCIFINDER/SCHOLAR/interact/index.html
 The library workstations can play the tutorials without having to download; scroll the page down to "View the tutorial on the web" and select either "general chemistry" or "chemical engineering".

How Indexing and Terminology Affects the Search Strategy

  • Some indexing is missing from very current records (items published within the previous two months) and very old records (1900-1966); to retrieve records from these time frames, be sure to use a variety of keywords to describe the concepts.

  • Geographic locations are not indexed extensively; locations are only retrieved via the author's terminology in the title and abstract. Use the Research Topic search to add geographic terms and do a seach for each of the different ways to describe the location.

    Example: to retrieve articles about the Great Lakes use the following terms:
    • Great Lakes
    • Lake Superior
    • Lake Huron
    • Lake Erie
    • Lake Michigan
    • Lake Ontario
    • Niagra River
    • Ohio
    • etc.
  • The indexing for chemical substances is highly specialized via the use of CAS Registry numbers. Each element, isotope, compound, alloy, and complex has it's own unique registery number. Consequently:

    • Chemical elements or compounds with simple names can be found using either the Chemical Substance - Name search or the Research Topic search and entering the simplest name of the element/compound.

    • Isotopes must be searched using the molecular formula via the Chemical Substance search.

    • Chemical compounds with complex names must be searched via the Chemical Substance search.

  • Indexing is "to the specific" so only those compounds specifically mentioned in the record are indexed. "Class or group headings" such as "mercury halides", "mercury chalcogenides", "trace elements", or "heavy metals" are only used when those groups are a major concept of the paper and if several substances within that category are named in the record. Consequently, to retrieve all mercury halides, each mercury halide must be identified via name, registery number or structure.

  • Although there is a "controlled vocabulary" for Chemical Abstracts, it is not available online. Use the following methods to help determine the terminology to use in your search:

    • Examine search results for the terminology used; find several records that are of particular interest and look in the Indexing portion of the record; repeat the search using this terminology

    • Examine search results to see if retrieval "ends" or "begins" at a certain point; this indicates that different terminology was probably used during the time frames not covered. Examine several records published near these ending or begining points to see if other terminology is present in the title, abstract or indexing fields.

    • Also, use terminology that was found in the thesauri of other databases.

Ei Compendex
http://www.asu.edu/lib/resources/db/eicompen.htm

The Content of the Database

  • Indexes over t000 Engineering Journals and Conference Proceedings
  • Covers all areas of Engineering
  • Contains over 7 million records in the database
  • Covers from 1969 to the present; for prior to 1969 use Engineering Index (T1.E52x Science Reference)

Search Engine

  • Natural Language with "Boolean" capability
  • Characteristics
    • Automatically inserts "and" between terms within the search box
    • Automatically stems:
      • Example: remediate returns remediate or remediation or remediating
    • Use truncation for unusual endings
      • Example: mercur* returns mercury or mercuric
    • Default sort is by relevance; option available to sort by date.
    • Limits available
      • Document type (e.g. journal article, conference article)
      • Treatment type (e.g. experimental, literature review)
      • Language
      • Date

How Indexing and Terminology Affect Your Search Strategy

  • Called "Controlled Vocabulary"
  • Some terms added recently (dates indicate when these terms were added)
    • Example: "Remediation," "Bioremediation"-1998
    • Example: "Removal" (1993)
    • Example: Mercury (metal) -1993
    • Example: Mercury compounds - 1986
  • Some terms used for older literature are no longer used'
    • Example: Groundwater
      • Groundwater (1993)
      • Water, underground (used before 1993)
  • Use Thesaurus to identify broader, narrower, and related terms
    • Example: Remediation:
      • Broader term: Pollution Control (1993)
      • Narrower term: Bioremediation (1998)
      • Related terms: Contamination (1993), Land reclamation (1975)

GeoRef
http://www.asu.edu/lib/resources/db/georef.htm

The Content of the Database

  • As the name implies, GeoRef is geared to the geologist. All areas of geology are covered, including applied geology.

  • The types of geology publications covered are journal articles, books, conference proceedings, publications from the USGS, state geological surveys, and geological societies, maps, technical reports, master theses and doctoral dissertations.

  • International geology is covered from 1933 to the present; North American geology is covered from 1785 to the present.

  • Less than 15% of the records in the database contain abstracts (i.e. summaries).

Search Engine

Use the Advanced search if you prefer to have the boolean operators and field options (where the words will be searched in the records) displayed.

Use the Basic Search if you are comfortable with boolean operators (and, or, not), truncation (*), nesting (), and field codes (de = descriptor, ti = title, etc.) and can compose sophiscated search strategies in one line.

Example: mercury- in de and (remov* or remediat* or contaminat*) and (great lakes in de or ontario in de)



How Indexing and Terminology Affect Your Search Strategy

  • The subject content and the geographic location are listed in the descriptor (de) field. It is very important to use the terminology employed in the descriptor field as more than 85% of the resources only have their subject content listed in there and in the title.

  • Use of the Thesaurus will determine the scope of the term, more specific (narrower terms) that will automatically be picked up by the search, related terms that searcher will need to account for manually if applicable and if the original term is not used, or used sparingly, other terms will be suggested.





    With the information that the term remediation was not used prior to 1993, the search strategy needs to account for that. So the strategy in this database would change from:

    mercury and remediation

    to:

    mercury and (remediation or reclamation or water treatment)

    Also, the thesaurus says that the term bioremediation is a more specific (narrower) term so the above search will retrieve all articles labeled as bioremediation as well as those labeled remediation.

    If only bioremediation techniques are wanted, click on "bioremediation" and view the scope notes, broader, more specific, and related terms for that concept. Adjust the search strategy accordingly.


  • All geographic terms must be searched in the descriptor field to eliminate false drops in which the author works for an employer with the geographic term in the name.
    Example:
    The search heavy metals and kansas in de is better than heavy metals and kansas, as the second search would retrieve this article (and others) about Germany. (Because the author works at Kansas State University):
    TI: Heavy metal trends in floodplain sediments and valley fill, River Lahn, Germany.
    AU: Martin-Charles-W
    AF: Kansas State University, Department of Geography, Manhattan, KS, United States
    SO: Catena (Giessen). 39; 1, Pages 53-68. 2000.
    PB: Catena-Verlag Rohdenburg. Cremlingen-Destedt, Federal Republic of Germany. 2000.
    CP: Federal-Republic-of-Germany
    PY: 2000
    LA: English
    DE: Central-Europe; concentration-; deposition-; Europe-; fluvial-environment; Germany-; heavy-metals; Lahn-River; pollutants-; pollution-; sampling-; sediments-; stream-sediments; suspended-materials


  • Geographic and regional terms are autoposted up through the heirarchy, enabling the searcher to use broader regional terms that will retrieve resources about specific locations within that region. Example:
    A search for mercury and great lakes in de will retrieve all articles about Lake Superior, Lake Huron, Lake Michigan, Lake Erie and Lake Ontario even if the author does not have the term Great Lakes in the title.
    Example:
    TI: Lake sedimentary records as regional archives of mercury; a Lake Erie example.
    AU: Simmers-B-S; Gottgens-J-F
    AF: University of Toledo, Department of Biology, Toledo, OH, United States
    BK: In: Great Lakes, great science, great cities.
    SO: Program and Abstracts of the ... Conference of the International Association for Great Lakes Research. 42; Pages A102. 1999.
    LA: English
    DE: Chordata-; concentration-; cores-; enrichment-; Great-Lakes; lacustrine-environment; Lake-Erie; lake-sediments; mercury-; metals-; North-America; Pisces-; pollutants-; pollution-; sediments-; transport-; Vertebrata-


    NOTE: County information is not consistently applied; for example, searching (mine or mines or mining) and Yavapi County in de may not retrieve all articles about mines surrounding Wickenburg; a better search would be (mine or mines or mining) and (Yavapi County in de or Wickenberg in de or Wickenburg in de). To be sure that absolutely everything would be retrieved the best search is (mine or mines or mining) and arizona in de.
  • Chemical elements are included in the descriptor (de) field with the full name, not the symbol, ex. mercury-

  • Chemical compounds are not consistently handled. In some cases, an article about mercury chloride may only have mercury- in the descriptor (de) field, in other cases, the compound name mercury-chloride appears, in others the compound name only shows in the title and in still others the molecular or line formula may be used in the title or abstract, ex. HgCl2. In articles about multiple compounds or a class of compounds two individual terms may show up in the descriptor field, ex. for an article about mercury halides there will probably be mercury- and halides-. To search compounds, use all forms described here.

    Example:

    mercury chloride or hgcl2 or mercury halide or (mercury- in de and halides- in de)

  • The term "ores" in the descriptor field (i.e. mercury-ores) is used for economic, commodity or geological deposits information. If looking for mercury as a waste product of mining or processing, use the term mercury.
