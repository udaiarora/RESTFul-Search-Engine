July 8, 2004

Arizona Leading Index inches upward in May

The Bank One Arizona Index of Leading Economic Indicators rose in May to 124.5 - 0.2 percent above the previous month's revised 124.3 and 3.5 percent above the May 2003 level of 120.3 (1987 = 100).

Materials inventories from the Business Conditions Survey, employment, hours worked in manufacturing, and the inflation-adjusted value of the M2 money supply were all positive contributions to the Leading Index. Negative contributors were delivery times, new orders, and production from the Business Conditions Survey as well as the inflation-adjusted value of Maricopa County residential building permits. The sensitive materials prices component was neutral.

"In October 2003, the Leading Index showed signs of strength and began to rise at a faster pace," said Dawn McLaren, an economist with the Bank One Economic Outlook Center at ASU's W. P. Carey School of Business. "While the Index recently has slowed its pace somewhat, there is no indication that the state is foregoing its economic recovery. In fact, the economy is now in a growth cycle."

Recovery from the recession was painfully slow, as employment showed only modest gains until the beginning of 2004, according to McLaren. "After a long period of lingering in the neutral area, Arizona now ranks among the top states when it comes to job growth. The goods-producing sectors have managed a 2.5 percent growth in jobs in May compared to May 2003, while the service-providing sectors have fared even better, showing a 4.3 percent increase for the same period."

Sales of existing homes in the Greater Phoenix area have reached record highs, with year-to-date sales as of May showing a 35.9 percent increase over the same period last year. However, mortgage rates have crept upward recently and will likely dampen the market somewhat in the future.

"Although all indications are that the economy is in a period of stable growth, there are reasons for caution," McLaren said. "The Federal Reserve Bank appears to be raising interest rates for the first time in four years - slowly, as it does not want to create shock waves in the national economy. Other factors may shake the local economy, such as the increase in fuel prices and in steel and aluminum prices. Arizona is a copper-producing state, and copper has seen increases in price over the last few months. Tourism is expected to improve, but events in Iraq have discouraged travel, even if only temporarily."
