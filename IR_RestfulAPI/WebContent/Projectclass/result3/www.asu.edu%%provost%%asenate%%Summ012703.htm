3.B  University President?s Report (Michael Crow).

 

Thanks George, and welcome back to everyone.  I will focus today on the inflection point that we have now reached relative to our primary investor.  Our primary investor is the state of Arizona.  The State of Arizona now has two budgets that will soon be on the street.  1) the Governor?s budget and she has recognized the role of the University as a strategic asset, building on the state?s future and is protecting the University from any reductions in their investment that they make in us, in 2003 or 2004.  She has also provided on a less than optimum basis additional resources in her budget plan for enrollment growth funding.  We are in the process of attempting to correct what we believe to be a technical error in the Governor?s budget, as best we can.  As some of you may know, the universities are also going to receive the JLBC (Joint Legislative Budget Committee) proposal, which will be released later this week.  It is highly probable and seems certain that those two budget proposals will be different.  The University will not be held to the exact same status in the legislature in their budget proposals as we were by the Governor, nonetheless, it offers us an opportunity to engage in debate about the role of the universities in the state?s future, which is something that we intend to do.  Just to remind you, our budget request for this year is simplified to two things: 1) enrollment growth dollars to help us here and particularly on the East Campus and on the West Campus.  2) a special funding request to support research infrastructure facilities, beyond those that are already being put in motion, which we need to build on all three of the campuses.  We are advancing those agenda items with a separate strategy for each one.  The 22 to 1 pathway is one strategy and for research infrastructure, a trajectory is being pushed by another strategy.  What I can say to you is we will wait to see how the Governor and the legislature engage over their differences, to then determine our specific elements of our approach to engagement.  We are preparing behind the scenes in every possible way, and even directly with the Governor and the legislative leadership, and in addition to that we have teams of people that are working in both those areas.  This includes special advisors brought in from the outside to help us reach particular audiences.  I just wanted to let you know our basic strategy is to focus on enrollment growth and research infrastructure.  Obviously, we support the Governor?s budget because it is best for the universities.  We will attempt to facilitate solutions as best as we can, if they cannot come to an agreement on the two versions of the budget. 

 

I wanted to talk about the fifty million dollar gift to the Business School, by Mr. Bill Carey, an investment, real estate banker from New York City.  Gifts are always complicated matters that have to be negotiated.  There are objectives that one hopes to attain with a gift and there is the fact that all gifts are not cost-neutral.  As a part of this gift, we have gone to the Business School and have put in process a series of changes that will be an exemplar of what will happen with other gifts that we are in the process of pursuing right now, some of which are of this same scale or larger.  With all gifts and all private contributions like this, our hope is to use these external resources to help us to enhance our competitiveness.  To do that, we have provided the Business School with the construction of a new facility in the Tempe Center property, a dual use facility, that will involve commercial and academic space as well as other types of space.  We have agreed to cover half the cost of that facility from University resources, and we will raise the other half from other assets ($70 million total cost for that building). In addition, we have agreed to additional fund raising, and we have asked that the Business School move in the direction of developing a new type of financial model to help them obtain their goals and objectives.  All those things are a part of the process of accepting this particular gift.  Our goal is to utilize gifts that come into the University to build better units, not just as resources that come in and are used to offset expenses and provide budget relief.  It has to be a lot more significant than that.  That is a part of our process, but it is insufficient by itself.

 

I want to comment on something that came out of the Regents meeting last week.  We have been working to gain independence for the University?independence in terms of setting tuition, independence in terms of determining our own future and independence in terms of building our own identity.  As a part of that, we have asked for independence in terms of admission standards.  Admission standards right now are basically determined by the Regents.  We have asked the Regents to now set a standard, and we are advising them on what that standard should be, an automatic admission, an honorific condition, that any university in the state university system could use?it is likely that it will be class rank or some combination of class rank and grade point.  Then all admission standards below that honorific admission, will be set by the individual universities.  In our case, by the individual universities and the individual schools within the university, working in consultation with the Provost.  Why are we doing this?  We believe that it offers us the best chance of building the student body that we think we need in our various programs to help us to be more successful and help them to be more successful individually.  We believe that we have a better shot at doing that than with a general admission standard, which is what is presently implemented by the Regents and the Board of Regents policy.  We have had a first reading and debate about this policy shift at the last Regents meeting, and we intend to move in this direction over the next couple of weeks.  Going forward you can expect the following from the Regents : 

  • Approval of dramatic undergraduate tuition increases for in-state students, with requirements of expanded financial aid that come along with that.
  • Approval of modest tuition increases for out of state students and modest tuition increases for graduate students.
  • Differentiation between our own schools in the development of fee structures above the tuition base. 
  • In certain schools such as the Law School and graduate programs in the Business School and a few others, tuition will move toward market level, determined by forces beyond the control of the legislature.

My own belief is that these changing direction opportunities provided to us by the Regents and negotiated with the Regents, will give us many degrees of freedom.  We will have more flexibility of going forward in terms of new programs, new revenues, etc.

6.A.1  Plus Minus Grades.  Last time we ran short of time at the Senate meeting in November and didn?t have enough time to formally introduce the report of the Committee on Plus Minus Grades.  Again, that information is on our Web page and I hope you have taken the opportunity to download the report of the committee and are sharing it with your faculty members, because it is time now to address that issue.

 

I chaired the Committee on Plus Minus Grades, so technically speaking I should step down from being presiding officer of the Senate, and report to you as chair of the Plus Minus Committee.  However, without objection, may I just report to the Senate on behalf of the committee?.  The executive summary of that report was distributed to senators electronically.  Appendices A-F of that report are available on the Senate Web site.  In addition, Senate Resolution #2 comes from that committee for a first reading today (was distributed).  You may recall at the November meeting we raised the issue of how we can proceed, because in March of 1992, resolution #23 of the Senate established a plus minus grading system for ASU.  You may also recall from our discussion in November that the system was never implemented. Lattie Coor was determined it would take about 1,000 hours of IT programming time in order to establish such a system, so, it was not in effect implemented.  What we have then is a resolution that has continued to reside on the books but has never been implemented by the University President.  We have some options to deal with this situation; 1) we may have a vote to reconsider this motion 2) it was also suggested that a motion to reconsider is not appropriate with a motion that is ten years old.  I reviewed Robert Rules of Order and have added a technical point to the resolution that is coming to you from the committee?rather than going the reconsideration route, it seemed to me the proper motion would to rescind the previous resolution--and that is now a part of Senate Resolution #2.  In November, I referred to this as Senate Resolution #3, but it is #2 in our order of business.  That is the first action item of the resolution. 

 

What the resolution then goes on to establish or offer is a different plus minus grading system.   The executive summary of the report could not really provide the richness of detail the committee debated.  There are a lot of arguments in support of, and a lot of arguments in opposition to, and the Table 1 that is in the full report, which is something that we showed you earlier but did not hand out this time, showed 8 different systems in place at various universities.  There are even subsystems available at some of those universities.  These are not sloppy universities; faculty at other universities have argued these issues over many years and have come to very different conclusions about what might be the best system.  So, the committee is hoping that we will have a good discussion of this at the February Senate meeting and before that, I want you to take this to your faculty and get their input. This is something that the faculty should decide.  The Executive Committee came forward with a recommendation that we do in fact implement plus minus grades for the transcripts.  The pluses and minuses can be given by faculty in all courses, and will appear on a student?s transcript.  However, when they are converted to grade points, the committee recommended that the A+, A, and A- all be given the same grade point, a 4.0?that a B+, B, and B- all have a 3.0.  In others words, not decrementing and incrementing the .3 that many plus minus grade systems as well as the one in Senate Motion #23 recommend.  The rationale for that is complex; there are multiple reasons that the committee came forward with such a proposal.  The main reason was that the committee simply saw difficulties with implementing the plus minus grades and we will talk more about that in February.  Right now, I am only allowed to present what the report of the committee is.  There were concerns at the C- level?that a C-, a grade point of 1.67 would cause problems for the university and for students, which the committee felt were unnecessary.  At the highest level, there would also be some impact, with A- grades, in terms of students who are in the summa cum laude category.  The committee is not arguing necessarily that we shouldn?t implement the plus minus system, but this certainly gets one on the books.  We at least want to have a resolution on the books.  Introducing that resolution, what you will hear next month is that the committee hopes that ultimately that there will be no plus minus grading system, it simply will remain as it is.  That comes forward at the next meeting.  That is my report as the chair of that committee.
