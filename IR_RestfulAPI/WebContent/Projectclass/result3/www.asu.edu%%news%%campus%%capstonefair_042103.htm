April 21, 2003

Capstone Fair highlights doctoral education

On the next ASU annual employee trip reduction survey, the university may want to add a new category for those who make their own air-friendly fuel. It could be called the "Conz Category," named for Preparing Future Faculty (PFF) fellow David Conz, who produces clean-burning biodiesel fuel for his vehicles.

Biodiesel fuel is a key component of Conz's PFF activities and his dissertation research. He is one of 36 PFF Fellows who will be featured during the annual Capstone Fair, from 2 to 4 p.m., April 21, in the Memorial Union Ventana Room.
The Graduate College administers PFF, a development program for doctoral students pursuing college and university teaching careers.

"The record of placements for Preparing Future Faculty graduates is impressive, and the high satisfaction of fellows has helped build the PFF program as a hallmark of doctoral education at ASU," says Bianca L. Bernstein, dean of the Graduate College.
PFF introduces students to faculty roles at a variety of institutions. Students participate in seminars, reading groups and workshops on diversity in the academy, legal issues in the classroom and laboratory, pedagogical issues regarding technology, cooperative learning, and preparing for the job market. Those who complete a second year in PFF also display portfolios at the Capstone Fair.

"The PFF experience opens doors for students interested in academic careers," Conz said. Preparing Future Faculty enabled Conz to participate as a full member of a senior thesis committee at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University in Prescott and to guest lecture there on the sociology of technical innovations.

Enrolled in the Ph.D. program in sociology at ASU, Conz aims to finish his dissertation - "Policy Effects on Sociotechnical Networks" - next year. Conz and Professor Ed Hackett study the social dynamics of research, and they have a paper in review for publication by the refereed journal "Research Policy."

Last year, Conz built a processor that produces biodiesel fuel. Mixing used vegetable oil with lye and methanol, he makes 30 - 50 gallons of biodiesel every three - four months. Conz runs his 1979 Mercedes and 1983 Chevy van on biodiesel fuel and hasn't patronized a gas station since July 2002. Biodiesel does not improve mileage-per-gallon rates, but it releases far fewer greenhouse gases and particulates into the air than petroleum diesel or gasoline does.
