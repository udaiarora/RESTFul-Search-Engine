Back Matter Requirements and Guidelines

Contents and Order
The back matter may consist of the following pages, in this order:
  1. Notes (if you have elected to group them at the end of the document)

  2. References (AKA “Bibliography” or “Works Cited”)

  3. Appendices

  4. Biographical sketch (optional)
The back matter, including appendices, must meet the same margin requirements (see “Margins”) as the rest of the document.



Pagination of the Back Matter
 Pagination of the back matter continues consecutively from the last page of text; but, as with chapters, the first pages of the notes and/or references and appendices cover pages are counted but not numbered. The biographical sketch, if included, is the last page of the document, and it is not paginated.

Notes
If you have grouped the notes at the end of your document, begin on a new page, counting it but not numbering it. Number the subsequent pages. Follow your style guide or journal for the numbering type and format of notes. If you single-space the notes, it is advised that you double-space between them for improved readability. Indicate notes in the text with corresponding superscript numbers. If you use notes, please choose a word-processing software and a printer that allow for superscripts.



References
Style guides and journals vary widely in the treatment of references. Your comprehensive list of references may be called “Works Cited” or “Bibliography,” but certain requirements apply to the list regardless of its name. Your style guide determines the format for all entries and their overall organization; therefore, you must be familiar with the style of citations and references used by your journal or style guide, ensuring that it addresses all of your source types. Please be aware that some style guides provide more than one option for reference style, depending on the discipline (e.g., humanities or sciences), so you will want to choose the option that is best suited to your discipline.

When you submit your document for format review, the advisor will spot-check the format of your in-text citations and reference list, the order of references, and the completeness of entries in relation to the style guide you have indicated on the Format Approval Sheet. You should complete a similar, more thorough check to ensure the information you provide to your readers is accurate and that you are not plagiarizing. You will also want to verify that your reference list entries match any coinciding in-text citations. You are ultimately responsible for the completeness and accuracy of your references.



Electronic or Online Sources
Most style guides include some information about how to cite electronic media/publications. The APA (2001), MLA (1998), and Turabian (1996) manuals all dedicate at least a few pages to this topic. If your style guide or journal does not cover the treatment of such sources, please refer to The Columbia Guide to Online Style by Janice Walker and Todd Taylor (Columbia University Press, 1998) or its companion website www.columbia.edu/cu/cup/cgos/idx_basic.html. This guide provides models for citing electronically accessed sources in both humanities and scientific styles.

Generally, the following information is included in citations of websites, so you may want to collect it as you conduct your research:
  • The author's name (if known) or screen name/alias

  • The full title of the document, cited as a journal article would be cited by your style guide

  • The title of the complete work (if applicable)

  • Version or file numbers (if applicable or known)

  • The date of the document's publication or last revision (if applicable or known)

  • The full http address (URL); some styles require the address to be put in angle brackets

  • The date the researcher (you) visited the site, in parentheses
Check your specific style guide or The Columbia Guide to Online Style for the inclusion and proper order of such information.



Appendices
If you and your committee agree on the need for appendices, designate each appendix with a letter (“APPENDIX A”) or roman numeral (“APPENDIX I”) and a descriptive title in ALL CAPS, and provide a separate cover page for each (see Figure 16). The cover pages are counted but not numbered. Subsequent pages are numbered with the same font and size as the rest of the text. The appendix titles are listed in the table of contents (see Figure 5). All appendix materials must meet the usual margin requirements, reducing xerographically or by using a smaller size, if necessary, to meet the pagination and margin requirements. If your research includes human subjects or animal subjects, you should include the appropriate approval documents in an appendix. Any copyright permissions should also be included.




Biographical Sketch (Optional)
If it is common in your discipline, recommended by your committee, or important to you, you may include a biographical sketch as the last page of your document. This page is not counted or numbered and should not be listed in your table of contents. It should be presented as one single-spaced paragraph. Present your biographical information in an academic and professional tone. (See Figure 17.)
