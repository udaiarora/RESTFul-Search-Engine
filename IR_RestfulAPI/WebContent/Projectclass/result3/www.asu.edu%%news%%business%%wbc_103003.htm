October 30, 2003

Western states chart highs, lows on rocky road to recovery

As the "jobless recovery" rolls on, employment growth data have been showing few signs of life, but this may be changing. Leading indicators are showing that the job market is improving, according to an analysis in the current issue of the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast.

"At last there is some good news about the economy," said Dawn McLaren, editor of the Western Blue Chip. "The Institute for Supply Management's Report on Business is showing some positive signs, construction spending is on the rise, and the Index of Leading Indicators is moving up. New home sales have been strong."

"However, Americans are in debt - a lot of debt - and a lot of it is in mortgages," McLaren noted. "During lean times, assets become a source of financing and many people refinanced their homes, at very attractive interest rates, in order to continue spending. One of the economic indicators not yet showing recovery is consumer confidence."

The rising number of nonbusiness bankruptcies is evidence of this problem - especially in states with the slowest job growth. "In most states, the number of bankruptcies rose in the second quarter of 2003 compared to the second quarter of 2002," McLaren said. "Hawaii, a state in which job growth has been ranked in the top five in the country for almost a year, nonbusiness bankruptcies fell during that time period by 7.7 percent. However, in Colorado, where job growth ranked close to the worst in the nation during 2002 and improved only marginally in 2003, nonbusiness bankruptcies rose by 30.2 percent."

Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast publishes the consensus forecasts of panels of economists in 10 Western states. Panelists are drawn from leading firms, universities and state agencies. The Western Blue Chip also publishes historical data for each state.

The Western Blue Chip is published 10 times a year by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute at Arizona State University's W. P. Carey School of Business. The cost is $110 for 10 print issues ($99 for e-mail subscription). For subscription information, call (800) 448-0432.
