March 24, 2004

Environmental artist to speak at ASU

How do artists collaborate with architectural/engineering design teams to integrate their work into public spaces? As one of the nation's foremost environmental artists, Buster Simpson reveals, often with tongue in cheek, our relationship to our natural surroundings.

In his free lecture titled "Art in the Public Domain," the Seattle-based artist will discuss his past and current public art commissions for streetscapes, airports, private and commercial buildings, and transit stations for buses, trains and light rail. Simpson was chosen to be the public artist for the light rail bridge, which will feature different colored lights, over Tempe Town Lake. The lecture will be held at 7:30 p.m., April 1, at ASU's Neeb Hall.

When working on a public art project, Simpson takes cues from the site's condition, history, social and political realities, existing phenomena, and ecology. All of these components are being taken into account as he designs the Tempe Town Lake light rail bridge, scheduled for completion in 2006.

"The light rail project provides an opportunity to consider stand alone and/or an integrated approach," says Simpson, whose site-responsive artworks can be found in New York, Washington, Oregon, Virginia, Missouri, Florida, Colorado, Massachusetts, Ohio, California and Canada. "In either case, the work will be about place and legible to the public."

Simpson has received four fellowships and project grants from the National Endowment for the Arts. He was recently profiled in Sculpture magazine, and termed "a liberal humanist in avant-garde clothing, a parodist like Oldenburg, but with a mossy green heart tied to the American urban landscape." His work can be viewed at (www.bustersimpson.net).

"Buster Simpson: Art in the Public Domain" is presented by the Herberger College of Fine Arts Office of Public Art. For information, contact Dianne Cripe at (480) 965-0951.

This lecture is part of the annual Public Art Lecture Series.

This article appeared in the March 12, 2004 issue of ASU Insight.
