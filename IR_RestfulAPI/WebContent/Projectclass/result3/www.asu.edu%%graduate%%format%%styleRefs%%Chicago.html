Chicago Style Guide Quick Reference

This quick reference should not be used as a style guide; it is only a reference tool to be used along with the most recent version of the Chicago as indicated below.

The Chicago Manual of Style. 15th ed. Chicago: The University of Chicago Press, 2003.
www.press.uchicago.edu/Misc/Chicago/cmosfaq/about.html
The Chicago Manual of Style must be followed for headings and subheadings, tables and figures, block quotations, text citations, and references (i.e., notes and bibliography, reference list). However, regarding overall document considerations (i.e., margins, pagination, etc.) the Format Manual requirements supersede those of Chicago. For more information, see Using Style Guides at www.asu.edu/graduate/format/UsingStlGds.html. For definitions of terms used herein, consult the index of the manual.

Headings/Subheadings
Distinguish levels of headings using italics, bold, etc. but no underline. Format each level consistently (2.24, p. 64). For numbered headings, see section 1.76 (p. 26).
  • 1st level: centered, initial caps only
  • 2nd level: flush left, initial caps only
  • 3rd level: indented, initial caps only
  • 4th level: run-in, sentence-style with a period at the end, usually in italics (1.74, p. 25)
Block Quotations
Set off block quotations if they are more than 100 words or at least 8 lines (11.11, p. 447). Set off block quotations from the rest of the text by indenting lines from the left and either (1) using less space between the lines (e.g., single-spacing) (recommended) or (2) using a smaller type or different font (not recommended) (11.12, p. 447).

Figure (Illustration) Captions
Label all types of illustrations (i.e., drawings, photos, charts, graphs, maps, etc.) with either "Figure" or "Fig." below the corresponding figure, followed by an Arabic number (see 12.12–12.14 for numbering advice, p. 479), a period, and a caption. Sentence-style capitalization is recommended for all captions, whether full or incomplete sentences (12.32–12.34, p. 484–485).

Table Titles
All tables should appear as close to the corresponding text as possible. Place the label "Table" followed by an Arabic numeral (see 13.12–13.13 for numbering advice, p. 499) above the table and flush left or centered. A title should appear either (1) on the same line, separated by punctuation or by space or (2) on a separate line below the heading (13.18, p. 500). See 13.16 for capitalization guidelines (p. 500).

About Citing Sources Using Chicago
Choose one of the two basic systems: author-date style or notes-and-bibliography style (16.1–16.4, p. 594–595). Common elements in both systems are discussed in 16.9 (p. 596). To compare the styles, see 16.10 (p. 597). Consult with your chair, if necessary, to determine which system is accepted in your field. If you choose Notes and Bibliography, you will also need to determine whether you will use footnotes, endnotes, or chapter notes (16.19–16.24, p. 599–600).

Author-Date Style
In-text Citation: Parenthetical References
Cite the author's last name, followed by the publication date, without punctuation between them and all in parentheses (16.108, p. 620–621). Where the author's name is mentioned in the sentence, cite the publication date in parentheses following the author's name wherever it appears in the sentence and before a mark of punctuation (16.112, p. 622). To cite a particular part of a source, include a comma and page number(s) after the basic citation; for journals, include the volume number and a colon before the page number(s) (16.109–16.110, p. 621–622). Consult p. 622–624 for formatting specific types of sources.

References: General/Arrangement
Arranged alphabetically by author last name (16.93, p. 617), hanging indent, double space between entries (p. 643–699).

References: Books and Parts of Books
Author's last name, comma, first name (reverse order for co-authors), followed by a period, the publication date, and a period. Insert book title in italics and sentence case followed by a period. Insert city of publication, a colon, name of publisher, and a period (p. 647–687).

References: Journal Articles
Author's last name, comma, first name (reverse order for co-authors), followed by a period, the publication date, and a period. Insert article title in sentence case without quotation marks, a period, the journal title in italics and headline capitalization, followed by the volume (issue number in parentheses, if applicable), a colon, the page number(s), and a period (p. 687–705).

Notes-and-Bibliography Style
In-text Citation: Notes
To cite a source in-text, place a superscript Arabic numeral at the end of a sentence, clause, and/or quotation (16.25–16.26; 16.30–16.31; p. 600–601). If notes are used with a full bibliography, use a concise notes citation version: author's last name, a comma, title of work in italics and headline capitalization (i.e., title case), and page number. If notes are not used with a bibliography, use a full citation the first time a source is cited (16.3, p. 594). Subsequent notes citations may be in shortened forms (16.41–16.50, p. 603–606). See below for full notes citation.

Bibliography: General/Arrangement
Arranged alphabetically by author last name, usually placed at the end of the document, and may be divided into subsections such as books or articles (p. 612–614). Notes and Bibliography: Books and Parts of Books Author's first and last name (same order for co-authors), followed by a comma (in notes, N) or period (in bibliography, B), and the book title in italics and headline capitalization. Insert city of publication, a colon, name of publisher, and year (all enclosed in parentheses if notes). (For notes, may add a comma and page number following parentheses.) End with a period (p. 647–687).

Notes and Bibliography: Journal Articles
Author's first and last name (same order for co-authors), followed by a comma (N) or period (B). Insert article title in quotation marks and use headline capitalization, followed by a comma (N) or period (B). Insert journal title in italics and headline capitalization, followed by the volume (issue number in parentheses, if applicable), a colon, the page number, and a period (p. 687–705).
