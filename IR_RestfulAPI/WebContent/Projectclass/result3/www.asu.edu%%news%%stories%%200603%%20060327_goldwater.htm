March 27, 2006

University lands 4 Goldwater Scholars

For only the second time in ASU history, four young undergraduate students - all but one just turning 21 years old - have scored big in the competitive world of science and math awards, winning national Goldwater Scholarships.

They already are deeply involved in research projects at the Biodesign Institute, TGen and the Center for Solid State Electronics Research, where they are studying cancer cell biology, thermonuclear fusion, enzyme catalysis and proteins.

The winners - all of them juniors at ASU - are:

. James Cronican, a biochemistry major from Omaha , Neb.

. Shannon Fortin, a biochemistry major from Phoenix .

. Lawrence Mickelson, a materials science and engineering major from Mesa.

. Paul Schmit, a physics major from Glendale .

Fortin, Mickelson and Schmit came to ASU as President's Scholars, while Cronican came to the university as a National Merit Scholar.

The $7,500 awards are the nation's premier undergraduate awards for students in the natural sciences, math and engineering. ASU has long been a leader in the awards, with 33 ASU students winning Goldwater Scholarships over the past 13 years. This is the first year the university has had four winners, the maximum number that can be nominated from a four-year university.

"This was the toughest year yet for the competition," says Gerald Smith, president of the Goldwater Foundation in Springfield, Va. "The caliber of these youngsters is amazing. Colleges and universities are doing a much better job in getting students involved in undergraduate research. It's a good omen for the future of research."

Fortin worked at the ASU Nuclear Magnetic Resonance laboratory for two years, with researcher Ron Nieman, and for the past year has been doing brain cancer research at TGen. She plans to become an oncology researcher and is a leader in the ASU Society for Women in Science.

Schmit has been working with atomic force microscopes at the Biodesign Institute under biophysics professor Stuart Lindsay. He hopes to become part of an international team of physicists and engineers developing alternative energy in the form of controlled nuclear fusion power.

Cronican has received an Edson Student Entrepreneur Initiative grant to research the feasibility of constructing a reliable, automated system for plasmid preparation that will save time and money in enzyme research. He has been working with Wilson Francisco, an associate professor of biochemistry, and Frederic Zenhausern of the Biodesign Institute.

Mikelson, the oldest member of the group at almost 24, works with Cody Friesen, an assistant professor of chemical and materials engineering, on making and testing phononic crystals. He spent two years away from college on a church mission in Argentina, and he aspires to become a research professor.

A field of 1,081 students representing the cream of the crop were nominated by the faculties of colleges and universities nationwide, and 323 were selected Goldwater Scholars on the basis of academic merit. The scholarship program honoring Sen. Barry Goldwater was created to encourage outstanding students to pursue careers in math, science and engineering.
