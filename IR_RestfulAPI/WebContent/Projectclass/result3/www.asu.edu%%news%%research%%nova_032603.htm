PRESS RELEASE NO.: STScI-PR03-10

ASU gets second Nature cover in March: new kind of nova discovered

Astronomers using NASA's Hubble Space Telescope are keenly watching a wave of light sweeping though space from a mysterious star that unexpectedly brightened, like a camera flash popping off. The changing shapes of swirls and arcs in the object show that this is not a permanent feature in the sky, but a rare and ghostly event in our effervescent galaxy.

This unique outburst is revealing the netherworld of black interstellar space as light continues to reverberate off black dust clouds, like an echo off canyon walls. The observations of this so-called "light echo" promise to provide astronomers with a unique CAT-scan-like probe of the three dimensional structure of shells of dust surrounding an aging star. Light echoing off circumstellar dust in our Milky Way galaxy was last seen in 1936, long before Hubble's exquisite view could uncover remarkable new details.

The results appear today in the British science journal Nature and images are featured on the magazine's cover. "The light echo gives us a recording of the star's unusual eruption and allows us to follow a detective hunt to figure out how this star is evolving. Hubble's view is so sharp that we can also do 'astronomical tomography' of the space around the star," says the lead observer, astronomer Howard Bond of the Space Telescope Science Institute.

Other authors on the report are the Space Telescope Science Institute's Zoltan G. Levay, Nino Panagia and William B. Sparks; the U.S. Naval Observatory's Arne Henden; Arizona State University's Sumner Starrfield; R. Mark Wagner from the University of Arizona's Large Binocular Telescope Observatory; R.L.M. Corradi from the Issac Newton Group of Telescopes in Canarias, Spain; and U. Munari from the INAF-Osservatorio Astronomico di Padova in Asiago, Italy.

The petulant star that created the light echo, called V838 Monceros, flared up in early January 2002. Bond and his team have use the Hubble images to determine that V838 Mon is at least 20,000 light-years from Earth. In spite of this large distance, it was so bright at the peak of its explosion that it was visible through a small pair of binoculars. In fact, it was temporarily the brightest star in our Milky Way galaxy, shining with the brilliance of 600,000 Suns. This tidal wave of light put out enough energy to illuminate surrounding dust, like taking a camera flash to momentarily illuminates a long, cavernous tunnel.

The light is bouncing off dust shells that were presumably ejected by the star in previous outbursts. Some of the light from the latest outburst travels to the dust and then is reflected to Earth. Because of this indirect path, the light arrives at Earth long after light coming toward Earth directly in a straight line from the star itself.

The outburst of V838 Mon was somewhat similar to that of a nova. A typical nova a normal star dumps hydrogen onto a compact white dwarf companion star. The hydrogen piles up until it spontaneously explodes by nuclear fusion -- like a titanic hydrogen bomb. This exposes a searing stellar core, which has a temperature of hundreds of thousands of degrees Fahrenheit.

By contrast, however, V838 Mon did not expel its outer layers. Instead, it grew enormously in size, with its surface temperature dropping to temperatures not much hotter than a light bulb. This behavior of ballooning to an enormous size, but not losing its outer layers, is very unusual and completely unlike an ordinary nova explosion.

"We don't know of any other star that's ever done this kind of double-peaked outburst," said ASU astrophysicist Sumner Starrfield. "The first light peak was red and blue then a strong red peak followed... it just got very, very cool at the maximum energy emission."

"Previously, some stellar evolution theories would have proven this strange outburst impossible," says Bond. "It may represent a rare and unobserved stage of stellar aging." The star is so unique it may represent a transitory stage in a star's evolution that is rarely seen. The star has some similarities to highly unstable aging stars called eruptive variables, which suddenly and unpredictably increase in brightness.

"We can tell from the picture that this is probably a recurring event," said Starrfield. "The fact that there's material around the star means that it has probably been ejected in previous outbursts.

"This is not a single phenomenon - whatever models we make have got to work for something that recurs. And it has to be energetic and it has to eject a lot of material cold and slowly. At the present time, we don't know how to make a model to explain this. It's a mystery star, and very exciting," he said.

The circular light-echo feature has now expanded to twice the angular size of Jupiter on the sky. Astronomers expect it to continue expanding as reflected light from farther out in the dust envelope finally arrives at the Earth. Bond predicts that the echo will be observable for the rest of this decade.
