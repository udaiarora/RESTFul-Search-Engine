August 23, 2005

Passion for poetry powers professor’s pen

Sean Nevin always loved poetry, but never thought about himself as a poet – until he had an “Aha!” moment in India.

Nevin, who teaches poetry at the West campus, was doing environmental writing for a non-government organization in Madras, India, after he graduated from La Salle University.

He did his research at the American Consulate library in Madras and found himself leaving time each day to browse in the library’s poetry section.

He began to realize that poetry was a distraction for him, and started wondering what would happen if he turned his “distraction” into his life’s work.

There in the library, Nevin had his epiphany.

“I said, out loud, ‘I’m a poet,’ ” Nevin says. “I came back to the United States and applied for graduate programs in creative writing.”

He went on to earn a master’s degree at Queens College in literature and creative writing, and a master of fine arts degree from ASU.

Nevin, who also is assistant director of ASU’s Young Writer’s Program, recently won the 2005 Slapering Hol Press Prize, and his winning chapbook of poetry, “A House That Falls,” will be published in November.

“Slapering Hol,” which is old Dutch for “sleepy hollow,” is the publishing arm for the Hudson Valley Writers’ Center, based in Sleepy Hollow, N.Y. He will give a reading there in the fall or spring.

Nevin said his love of poetry was born on the coast of Maine.

“When I was a child, I’d play in the tide pools and look under the rocks,” he says. “The landscape was so visceral. In my first writing, I remembered all that again. I fell in love with detail.”

He wrote his first poetry as a fourth-grader and won a Martin Luther King Jr. contest at his elementary school in New Jersey.

“My poetry was displayed,” he says. “I brought it home, and I remember the feeling I got when I was praised for it.”

Nevin teaches his students that poetry begins with landscapes and experiences in the “real world.” This knowledge then passes through their imaginations and consciousness, and comes out through a new “portal.”

He also teaches his students, young and old, that everyone can write about his or her own experiences.

Through writing, he says, “what’s happening back here (in the mind) comes out in a more human, subconscious way.”

In the Young Writers Program, which is geared to children in fourth through 12th grades, he tries to give the students “a positive experience that they can come back to.”

Nevin also is co-editor of “22 Across: An Anthology of Young Writers.” His poetry has appeared in numerous journals, including the Gettysburg Review, Poet Lore, 5AM, JAMA and Runes: A Review of Poetry.

He has won an Academy of American Poets Prize and a poetry fellowship from the Arizona Commission on the Arts and was nominated for the Pushcart Prize.
