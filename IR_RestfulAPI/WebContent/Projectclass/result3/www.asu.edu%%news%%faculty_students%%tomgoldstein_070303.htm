July 3, 2003

Goldstein to join Cronkite School

Tom Goldstein, one of America's top journalism scholars and writers is joining ASU as the first Frank E. Russell Chair in Business and Journalism.

Goldstein was dean of the graduate schools of journalism at both Columbia and Berkeley. He is a prominent teacher, administrator and journalist who has gained acclaim not only at esteemed educational institutions, but also at top newspapers like the Wall Street Journal and the New York Times. He will join the faculty of both the Walter Cronkite School of Journalism & Mass Communication and the W. P. Carey School of Business at ASU in August.

Anne Schneider, dean of the College of Public Programs at ASU, said Goldstein's keen journalistic sense and his business and legal acumen make him the perfect choice to hold the Russell Chair.

"The Russell Chair was established to foster interdisciplinary work between journalism and other fields, especially business and public policy," said Schneider.

As both a journalist and a lawyer, Goldstein has a unique capability to develop new interdisciplinary programs that will challenge students to broaden their perspective and think critically about various issues, such as the completely different approaches that lawyers and journalists take in considering evidence and research.

"I am thrilled to be returning to full-time teaching and writing after serving more than 13 years as a dean," said Goldstein. "Arizona State strikes me as a vibrant and highly innovative university, and this is an unparalleled opportunity for me to try things that I have never done before.

"I can develop courses across disciplines - particularly business and journalism as well as law," he added. "I am also working on a lecture course for freshman that would familiarize them with some of the pressing issues of the media. These courses fit in neatly with my research interests, and I also look forward to having a chance to think systematically about the big issues facing the media."

Schneider said, "There always has been a tension within journalism between its role as a pillar of democracy and its role as a business that is expected to turn a profit. Journalism has a central place in educating the public about contemporary social issues and public policy, but newspapers, television news and even news on the Internet must pay their own way.

"Students in journalism, business and other fields need to confront this issue, as well as other issues about the role of journalism in contemporary society," Schneider added. "Professor Goldstein will permit ASU to bring new vitality to our discussion of journalism, business and public policy."

The Russell Chair, created during ASU's Campaign for Leadership though a gift from the Phoenix Newspapers, takes a cross-disciplinary approach to education.

The holder of the chair is expected to communicate a vision of the future role of journalism in society as well as the new economy and to translate the implications of that vision into the every day management of the media as a business. The goal is to prepare students and working journalists for success in the increasingly integrated media industry.

The chair is named in honor of Frank Russell, former chairman of the board and publisher for Central Newspapers, the owner of the Arizona Republic and the former Phoenix Gazette. Goldstein will occupy the Russell Chair for a one-year term.
From 1997 to 2002, Goldstein was dean of the Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism. Goldstein served as dean of the University of California at Berkeley Graduate School of Journalism from 1988 until 1996.

He also has taught at Stanford, New York University, the University of Florida and the Kennedy School at Harvard.

Goldstein is a graduate of Yale College, Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism and Columbia Law School.

He was the founding editor of "Juris Doctor," a magazine for young lawyers.
His professional journalism experience includes working as a reporter at the Buffalo Evening News, the Associated Press, the Wall Street Journal and Newsday. From 1973 to 1979, he covered legal affairs for the metropolitan, business and national desk of The New York Times.

From 1980 to 1982, he served as press secretary for New York Mayor Edward Koch. After leaving the mayor's staff, he began to freelance, a career he still pursues.
He is the author of "The News at Any Cost," the editor of "Killing the Messenger," and co-author of "The Lawyer's Guide to Writing Well."

Goldstein also currently is the West Coast editor of the "Columbia Journalism Review."
