February 21, 2006

Company licenses 'designer molecule' technology

Voltaix Inc., a leading manufacturer of chemicals and gases for the semiconductor and photovoltaic industries, has obtained worldwide exclusive rights to an advanced materials technology developed at ASU.

Voltaix has licensed the technology from Arizona Technology Enterprises (AzTE), ASU's technology venturing company. AzTE brings together university inventors and industry to transform scientific discoveries into commercial products and services.

The innovation, which provides "designer molecules" for the production of devices such as cell phones, computers and other wireless devices, helps reduce power consumption and increase system performance. It also could play an essential role in the next generation of solar cells and photonic devices such as lasers and photodectors.

The approach deploys new, custom-built molecules that are used to create materials to build semiconductor devices. The molecules were designed and produced by John Kouvetakis, an ASU professor of chemistry and biochemistry, and his coworkers at the university.

The ASU scientists also created the process used to make the materials, which are "alloys of silicon and germanium (SiGe) in microchip architectures that are used in many high-frequency products," Kouvetakis says.

Academic and industrial research has shown that the quality of these systems depends on the molecular structure of the "precursors," or the chemical compounds that lead to the production of SiGe. The ASU team has designed and synthesized entire new families of these compounds that display superior performance in the production of device-quality SiGe architectures and nanoscale components, and the formation of new optical semiconductors in the silicon germanium and tin system.

"Chip makers are increasingly looking to the primary materials manufacturers for solutions to their manufacturing challenges," says Matthew Stephens, chief operating officer of Voltaix. "Through this agreement, Voltaix is able to provide new materials and new technologies needed to help our customers improve the performance of their devices."

The discovery is so promising that Voltaix and ASU have been awarded a grant from the National Science Foundation SBIR/STTR (Small Business Innovation Research and Small Business Technology Transfer) Program to accelerate the commercialization of the technology. The funds will support development efforts at Voltaix and ASU. The company also provided a gift of $50,000 for graduate student fellowships.

Kouvetakis says the project will demonstrate "proof of concept" applications through partnerships with Voltaix, and their affiliated toolmakers and major semiconductor companies.

The group's designer molecules, and the corresponding processes leading to the semiconductor materials, are novel and initially were developed through exploratory research to support a joint development project on optical devices with Intel.

"Eventually, it will be light, not electricity, that drives high-speed devices," he says.

Kouvetakis has been supported by Hanscom Air Force Base, the Air Force Office of Scientific Research and the Photonics Technology Lab at Intel Corp. in Santa Clara , Calif. , to develop optical modulators and photodetectors in collaboration with Jose Menéndez, Ignatius Tsong and Andrew Chizmeshya at ASU. The Intel Corp. support consists of a three-year grant. Joint patents already have been issued with the Air Force.

John Tolle and Radek Roucka, recent graduates of the ASU team, have formed an SBIR-funded spinoff company to develop related technologies based on collaboration with the Army Night Vision Labs.
