April 21, 2003

Summer program targets childhood reading

For one ASU researcher, an important key to helping children with their future reading skills rests with a simple language building block - words.

Shelley Gray, an assistant professor in Speech & Hearing Sciences, says that in a developing child, vocabulary size is an important predictor for language growth and reading acquisition. Children with poor vocabularies have difficulty understanding and expressing their thoughts and are less successful predicting what printed words might say.

"Vocabulary forms an important basis for learning," says Gray, who is in the middle of a three-year grant from the National Institutes of Health. "If you don't have the right words, you will have trouble talking to your family, teachers and friends. If you can't get your thoughts across, you will have trouble socially. Reading and writing are based on oral vocabulary and language development."

With the help of a second grant, this one from the ASU/Motorola Great Communities program, Gray is putting her research into action to help children who may be at risk for reading deficiencies. The program, Literary Leaps, is being offered through Tempe Elementary School District's (TSD) summer program from 8 a.m. - noon, Monday - Friday, throughout the month of June. It is open to children from all school districts. The project is a partnership between TSD, ASU and the Arizona Literacy and Learning Center.

While the program would be helpful to any child, it is specifically targeted at children who are at risk for reading problems. Gray says by the time students finish kindergarten, they should know their alphabet and be able break a word into parts phonetically.

"Most children will pick these things up normally," she says. "We are concerned about the children who may need more attention and direct teaching in these areas."
At the beginning of the program, the children's early literacy skills will be screened.

The instructors will then customize the lesson plans for each child, providing an extra emphasis for children that may be deficient in a given area. Gray says the class sizes will be limited, while the number of teachers and speech pathologists will be high to allow one-on-one attention for the students.

While the teachers are helping the students learn, they will have an opportunity to learn themselves. Gray says a second important part of the program will help train teachers and speech-language pathologists to screen early literacy skills and to help children "catch up." She says without trained professionals looking for potential learning problems, a child may be able to slip through without the skills that are necessary for future reading and writing success.

"If you can't read in this society you will be at risk academically, socially and economically," she says. "Hopefully we can give the children a boost in their vocabulary and early literacy skills that will carry them into the future."

Tuition for the program is $212 and the registration deadline is May 16. Gray says the Kiwanis Club and Tempe Impact Fund are providing scholarships for low-income children. For information on the summer program call (480) 730-7287.
