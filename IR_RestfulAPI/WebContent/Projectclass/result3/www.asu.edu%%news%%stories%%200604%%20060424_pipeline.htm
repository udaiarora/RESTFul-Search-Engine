April 24, 2006

Groundwork begins for 'innovation pipeline' in nanotechnology

Work is set to start at ASU on building an "innovation pipeline" for turning discoveries in nanotechnology into business ventures.

Groundwork for the endeavor - teaching students how to construct and use that pipeline - is kicking off with support from a $39,500 grant recently awarded by the National Collegiate Inventors and Innovators Alliance to a group of professors in ASU's Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering and College of Liberal Arts and Sciences. The funding will help launch an undergraduate honors course in the fall on the emerging field of nanotechnology and its commercial potential.

"It's another step in expanding engineering education to include an entrepreneurship component," says Thomas Duening, director of the Entrepreneurial Programs Office at ASU's Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering.

B.L. Ramakrishna, an associate professor in ASU's School of Life Sciences, is developing the course based on the proposal "From Discovery to Commercialization: The Greater Phoenix Nanotechnology Innovation Pathway and Pipeline."

Its aim will be to spark students' enthusiasm for the "enormous societal and economic impact" offered by nanotechnology science and engineering, and guide students in exploring ways of bringing technological advances to the marketplace, Ramakrishna says.

Ramakrishna and Duening will work with Vincent Pizziconi, an associate professor in ASU's Harrington Department of Bioengineering. Pizziconi's extensive experience in teaching biomedical engineering product design and development will bring a valuable perspective to this hybrid nanotechnology and entrepreneurship course, Duening says.

Titled "Nanotechnology: Perspectives and Entrepreneurial Opportunities," the course is designed to provide a model for a "real-word" educational experience by bringing together students, faculty, industry and the regional entrepreneurial community.

Leaders of industry and small businesses will share their knowledge with students on commercialization prospects, global competition, regulatory issues and intellectual property rights.

Students will focus on two of the most promising areas in the field, nanobiotechnology and environmental nanotechnology. They will explore the business potential of these emerging technologies, as well as the broad impacts on society.

The course objective will be aided by the newly funded National Science Foundation Center for Nanotechnology and Society at ASU, which is charged with ensuring that "advances in nanotechnology bring about improvements in the quality of life for all Americans."

Class members will form "E-teams" (entrepreneurial teams) to develop marketing and business plans that will be presented to panels of industry experts, attorneys and venture capitalists.

The course will be offered as a pilot for two consecutive years - in fall 2006 and fall 2007 - and students will be encouraged to continue in following semesters to form E-teams. Some of the teams' activities will be funded through the grant.
