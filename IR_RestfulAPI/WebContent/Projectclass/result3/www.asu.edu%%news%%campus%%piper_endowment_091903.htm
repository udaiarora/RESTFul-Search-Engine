September 19, 2003

ASU Creative Writing Program nets $10 million gift for center

ASU announced this week a $10 million gift from The Virginia G. Piper Charitable Trust to establish the Virginia G. Piper Center for Creative Writing at ASU.

The gift is the largest ever to a humanities program at ASU, and follows three other "largest ever" gifts to the university since January - a $50 million gift from William Polk Carey, on behalf of the W. P. Carey Foundation, to endow the W. P. Carey School of Business at ASU; a $50 million gift from Ira Fulton to endow the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering at ASU; and $5 million from Fulton to the College of Education.

The $10 million for creative writing will help elevate the Creative Writing Program from its impressive top 20 ranking to even greater national and international prominence, and will help solidify metropolitan Phoenix as a hotbed for the art of creative writing.

"The enormity of what this gift means for the future of ASU's creative writing program and the Valley's emergence as a leader in cultivating the arts, culture and humanities is immeasurable," said ASU President Michael Crow. "Our creative writing faculty ranks among the best in the nation, and they will use this gift to transform an already superb program into one that is an exemplar."

Original literary production and publication have been encouraged at ASU since its beginnings. In establishing the Center for Creative Writing, the university will have a vehicle for developing and expanding innovative programs, including an annual writers conference, an annual distinguished visiting writers series, new community outreach programs and an international writers exchange.

As part of the gift, the Virginia G. Piper Chair in Creative Writing is being established and will be held by Professor of Creative Writing and English Jewell Parker Rhodes, who also is named artistic director of the center.

"Like the best poetry and prose, the Virginia G. Piper Center for Creative Writing will foster a deep love and appreciation for language and our common humanity," said Rhodes. "The center will be progressive, innovative, and inspire students and citizens of all ages. With utmost passion and commitment, the center will lead in the world arena. I am honored by my appointments to the Virginia G. Piper Chair and the Virginia G. Piper Center directorship."

The Piper Trust is the largest charitable foundation in Arizona and is committed to improving quality of life through programs that support children, elderly, arts & culture, health care and medical research, education, and religious organizations.

"Virginia Piper was known for her carefully crafted prose and deep love for the written word," said Judy Mohraz, president and CEO of the trust. "She would be proud that the Piper Trust, in making this gift to ASU's Creative Writing Program, is advancing an art form she was passionate about and displayed to friends and colleagues through her own eloquently written letters and personal messages."

David Young, dean of the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, said one of his goals is for the Creative Writing Program to rise from its impressive status as a top 20 ranked program to one of the nation's elite top five programs.

"Our program is young, vibrant and built on excellence," Young said. "It is poised to become a national and international leader, invigorating intellectual and creative activity with innovative new programs that promote the notion of the citizen-artist. The extreme generosity of the Piper Trust will ensure our success in taking this program to the top."

Crow praised the ASU Creative Writing Program as an exceptional team of nationally renowned writers, poets and playwrights.

Notables include Jewell Parker Rhodes, author of two writing texts and three novels, including "Douglass's Women," which has been awarded the PEN Oakland Josephine Miles Award, the Black Caucus of the American Library Award and the Before Columbus Foundation American Book Award; Norman Dubie, author of more than 20 volumes of poetry and recipient of numerous awards, including the 2002 PEN USA Literary Award, honoring "Mercy Seat," his collected poems; Alberto Rios, author of eight books of poetry, three collections of short stories, and a memoir, and a finalist last year for the National Book Award; and Ron Carlson, author of seven books and widely acclaimed by critics as a "master storyteller."

Thanks to the gift, plans to expand ASU's creative writing faculty will move forward with the goal of adding depth and breadth to the program to bring enhanced national exposure and reputation. The faculty additions will allow for increased flexibility in teaching and mentoring assignments to help faculty balance the demands of intensive one-on-one teaching that characterizes the program with their need for productive, creative writing time.

Students will benefit greatly from the gift in many ways as well. Dean's fellowships will be established, offering full tuition scholarships and a three-year, $20,000 per year stipend. These fellowships will enable the university to recruit the very best writing students in the country.

Young said that as the ASU Creative Writing Program develops into a top-ranked program nationally, both the campus and the Phoenix metropolitan community will be enriched.

"This program will go a long way in enlivening the local cultural and literary scene," said Young. "Our hope is that the Piper Center for Creative Writing will be the artistic and intellectual heart of a newly vibrant, multinational and culturally diverse world of writers, built on the many strengths of our program."
