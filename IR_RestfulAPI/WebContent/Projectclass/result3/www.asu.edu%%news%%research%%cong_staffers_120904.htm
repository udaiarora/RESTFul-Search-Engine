December 9, 2004

Congressional staffers see scope of ASU research

A five-hour campus visit Nov. 17 helped give 11 congressional district staffers a taste of ASU’s research priorities and initiatives.

The university’s Office of Federal Relations played host to the staff members, who represented eight of the state’s 10 congressional offices. The group visited several key research facilities and received policy briefings as part of their visit.

ASU President Michael Crow gave the group an overview of the university’s pressing concerns. He also discussed his recent trip to Ireland and the progress that nation is making by developing a knowledge-based economy.

The second stop included a presentation and briefing on NASA-related research and activities from Greg Mehall at the Moeur Building . Vice President Jonathan Fink, James Buizer, who serves as special adviser to the president and director of sustainability issues, and Anshuman Razdan, director of the Partnership for Research in Stereo Modeling at ASU, demonstrated the potential policy-making benefits from ASU’s new Decision Theatre at the Brickyard.

The final research facility toured was the Biodesign Institute at ASU, which is scheduled to open Dec. 14. Mike Mobley and Kimberly Ovitt were the hosts for the tour, giving the congressional staffers an overview of the Biodesign Institute’s mission.

ASU’s Executive Vice President and Provost Milt Glick also addressed the group over lunch at the University Club, followed by a discussion of master planning for all four campuses by Senior Vice President for Planning Richard Stanley and Steve Nielsen, ASU’s director of campus planning.

Phoenix ’s Pat Grady and John Chan also participated by discussing specific downtown campus plans and progress. A bus tour highlighting the light rail route and downtown development plans also was included.

“It’s important that our congressional offices are kept up to speed regarding the university’s priorities and plans,” says Stu Hadley, vice president for policy affairs and executive director of federal relations. “These types of tours and briefings are important. This particular effort was unique, given we had so many key staffers on campus at the same time. As we gear up for the next congressional cycle, it’s important that our delegation offices in Arizona, as well as the offices in Washington, D.C., are aware of our priorities.”

For more information about ASU federal relations efforts, see (www.asu.edu/ia/federal/index.html).
