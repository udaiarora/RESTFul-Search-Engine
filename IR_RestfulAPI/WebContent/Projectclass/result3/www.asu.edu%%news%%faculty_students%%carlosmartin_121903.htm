December 19, 2003

Professor builds research on change in construction material industry

Low-cost, energy-efficient housing may be possible with the use of plastic blocks. But potential homeowners may not jump at the chance to buy a house built with plastic blocks, home developers may not want to build with such material.

Carlos Martín, a new School of Construction professor and recently appointed endowed SRP professor of energy and environment, studies the cultural and industrial barriers to technological change in housing, and believes radical change is difficult.
"Building officials are suspicious of new technologies," he says, "and consumers are skeptical too."

In his new position, Martín will have a joint appointment to the College of Architecture and Environmental Design and the Del E. Webb School of Construction in the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering.

According to Martín, there are many more obstacles to making any changes in housing than just builder unwillingness and consumer skepticism.

"City codes, lending and insurance policies, utility placement, land-use regulations, land costs and much more dictate what can be built in terms of design or technology," Martín says.

Trained as both an architect and engineer, Martín became interested in architecture when he was very young, and went on to study at Stanford University, University of Michigan and M.I.T.

"Throughout my architectural studies, I realized that there were a lot of things missing, things we weren't talking about. I kept looking for that extra piece."

In his doctoral program, Martín began searching for the missing pieces by working with scholars in such disciplines as history, design and sociology to study the cultural and industrial barriers to housing changes.

He continued his quest as a research engineer for the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development for three years, studying how policy issues affect housing and design.

The reality, he says, is that architects' and engineers' influences on the housing market, of which they are a part, has been constrained, and to make any changes they must look at the big picture.

Architects and engineers have had some success in introducing housing innovations, Martín says.

"Energy-efficient windows and water-conserving bathroom and kitchen fixtures have been widely accepted by homebuilders and home consumers.

"One of the primary reasons for their success is that they are not significantly more expensive than their less energy-efficient counterparts. So, their 'payback' is shorter and consumers don't have to make the hard choices between what they can afford and what they would like."

Highly touted straw-bale and rammed-earth homes haven't been as successful for several reasons, Martín says.

"Since the technologies haven't been fully tested in a variety of conditions, building officials don't know how to handle or inspect them. They're still very labor intensive, and there's still some consumer apprehension about them. Appraisers don't have many comparables for them and their subsequent sales values really are ambiguous," he says.
