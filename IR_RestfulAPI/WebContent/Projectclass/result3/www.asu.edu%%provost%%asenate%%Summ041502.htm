3.A  Senate President?s Report (Morton Munk).

           

Welcome to the last meeting of the Academic Senate of the Academic Year.  I want to diverge from our normal agenda because we have with us a distinguished guest, Regent Jack Jewett.  Regent Jewett was appointed to the Arizona Board of Regents in 1998.  He is president-elect of the Board, and he will serve as a board member until 2006.  If you examine his record, much of it has been devoted and still being devoted to, community affairs and public service just as he is doing today.  He served for 10 years as a member of the AZ House of Representatives, and since then has given his time generously to serve on local and state boards.  In his other full-time job, he serves as senior vice president for public policy for TMC Health Care in Tucson.  Yes, Regent Jewett is a graduate of the University of Arizona, but I have seen him in action at Board meetings and I can tell you that he is ecumenical, and he is also a very strong and vocal supporter of higher education in Arizona universities.  Please join me in welcoming Regent Jack Jewett. 

 

3.B  Regent Jack Jewett.

 

Thank you very much, Mort, for that kind introduction.  It is certainly a pleasure to be here with all of you; I feel I am in distinguished company.  I must tell you at the outset I have enjoyed working with two people very much this year, let me mention Mort first.  Mort has been very active, involved and helpful in a committee that I am involved with through the Board of Regents.  That is a committee that is looking at the relationship between the academic mission of our universities and the role of athletics and I am going to talk a little bit about that today but it has just been a real pleasure working with Mort in that capacity.  Mort, I also understand that this meeting is your last one as President of the Academic Senate, so, congratulations on serving a full term. 

 

I would also like to pay tribute to a man that I think we all recognize as someone that has provided an extraordinary service to the State of Arizona, Lattie Coor.  I had the pleasure of meeting Lattie when he first arrived on the ASU campus.  I think that was in 1989, and when I was a member of the AZ Legislature.  My first impression was a lasting one--we have a winner at ASU.  I think that Lattie has just done a tremendous job of bringing this institution forward and we are all in your debt, Lattie. 

 

Because I am from Tucson, I think I have some explaining to do as a graduate of the University of Arizona.  I did graduate with a degree in Sociology from UofA.  I saw that your Sociology Senate member is not here today, but I am very proud of my degree in Sociology from the University of Arizona.  That happened a long time ago, in the 60?s.  I guess the honor of being able to serve as a member of the Arizona Board of Regents, being in this imposing group of scholars here, I guess I do not have to answer your questions correctly!  There is not that much of a penalty, I hope. I look back with a great deal of fondness on my time at the University of Arizona and my degree has served me well.  During my time at the Legislature, I was also in the newspaper business.  My family owned a newspaper company in Tucson and in that capacity I learned a lot; let me put it this way, I learned a little bit about a lot of things.  Certainly that was the defining point in my career where I identified public service as something that I wanted to proceed with.  So, I managed to accept a dare to run for the AZ Legislature in 1983.  I was told there was no way I was going to win but that they needed a candidate to fill the ballot out.  On that basis, I said, so, if I lose there is no penalty to pay!  They said, that?s right, and you are probably going to lose.  Well, I won and won five succeeding elections serving ten years in the Legislature.  I then decided that I wanted to leave on my own terms--so, I left office in 1993.  Having served during an historic period in Arizona?s history, for a Republican that was an event in the mid-80?s--to be a party to and vote for the impeachment of Evan Mecham, an individual that was elected to be our Governor, but quickly demonstrated, at least to a majority opposed in the Legislature, that his service should not continue.  A Republican governor being impeached and convicted by a Republican controlled Legislature was something that does not happen very often.  Yet, I think that was a wise decision by the AZ Legislature.  I played a part in that and was proud of my vote.  Then this event became a deciding factor in my leaving the Legislature.  I always characterize this event as the good, the bad, and the ugly.  It was good to be a member of the Legislature, unfortunate, and perhaps bad, that we had to impeach a sitting governor but it was down right ugly when we came upon the scandal that really eroded what trust was left in State Government--when we had a scandal known as AZScam.  That really was unfortunate, and at that time, I decided that this was not what I was here to do, to be involved in a sting operation, and so that contributed to my decision to move on.  As I left the Legislature, I was very fortunate to begin a career at the Tucson Medical Center, and that is where I am today.  The Governor?s appointment of me as a Regent in 1997 was one of the high marks in my life.  It is certainly an opportunity to serve with so many distinguished and outstanding individuals, both on the Board and among our university presidents, within each of our outstanding universities.  This has been a time that I will cherish for the rest of my life.

 

What I would like to do today, since Mort said that I could pick my topic (which is somewhat dangerous, but thank you for that latitude), is talk about tuition.  It is timely in the context of the budget situation in this state.  I would like to talk a little bit about the successor to Dr. Coor, Dr. Michael Crow, and then I would like to spend just a few minutes on the intercollegiate athletics issue.

 

Tuition is a topic that I will probably continue to represent a minority number of Board members on--and my position is different in some respects from our presidents, and perhaps the majority of the Board, but I would like to share my thoughts with you on tuition as a segue into a comment or two on the terrible budget situation, and what is underneath that terrible budget.   I support the 4% tuition increase, which for the in-state students roughly benchmarks with the inflationary number that was calculated through various higher education folks, who calculate those things.  So, 4% seems to be what inflation is.

     

I came to that conclusion of 4%, as opposed to a larger number, primarily because imbedded in the Constitution of Arizona is a provision, not to recite it directly, but it is a provision that says we want to provide full access to every qualified resident of Arizona to a higher education in our state.  It says that education should be as nearly free as possible.  What it also says is that if you qualify, if you are a resident, we want you to have access to these fine universities.  That is number one.  Number two, given the Legislature, and I speak to that because I was there, this legislature and previous legislatures when faced with the kinds of budget situations that we have, as some would call it, a fear that may or may not be warranted.  Frankly having been a part of that process, I come not from a place of fear but from experience.  In my judgment, if we are to be more aggressively outside the spirit of the Constitution with in-state tuition, this legislature is very apt to sweep those dollars into the general fund.  In the Constitution, as I understand what is behind this provision, these are public institutions, and the bulk of the funding needs to come from the general fund of the AZ State Legislature.  However, this Legislature and the previous ones have come up short.  In fact, there is now a tradition to have a tax reduction every year.  Not to share war stories, but as a Republican, I voted, God forbid, to raise taxes in the late 80?s and I voted to do that because we were in the same boat as we are now.  If we were to fund vital services in the state, not only education, health care and social services that are vital, in this time of an economy that is just about ready to rebound but it is leaving some people in a gap, that the State has this responsibility.  I do not believe those responsibilities should be shifted to parents and students, particularly those that are most in need of a higher education.  We have an opportunity to do a better job of financial aid and I hope to do that.   David Burstein asked me what I wanted to do next year, and that is one of the things I would like to focus on, getting serious about financial aid--both merit and need-based financial aid.  I know that Pell Grants are there, and they are always subject to finite funding.  They are subject to a federal formula that has to do with how poor your family is, how many members there are in the family and how many students you have going to the universities.  So, you are able to get some Pell Grant money--but that only affects (I am not sure how many here at ASU) about 6,000 at the University of Arizona, and frankly there are other students that need financial assistance that cannot get it through Pell Grants. We all recognize that tuition is just one component of the cost of attending the university, but it is an important part.  For those reasons I believe that for in-state tuition, it should be pegged to inflation.  Some say that is a populist position.  It has been found to be very unpopular for me personally, but it is based and imbedded in those principles that I have alluded to in the Constitution.  Out-of-state tuition is a different matter with respect to what I would support--probably more than what the presidents will be recommending.  I believe there should be some flexibility and that each of our universities should be able to utilize the economics of tuition to attract, or not attract students from outside of Arizona.  Certainly, diversity is something that we are all concerned with and all want to protect, but out-of-state tuition is an opportunity where we can be more assertive. 

           

On the State budget, I am disheartened that we cannot get our act together as a state and fund what voters in the state want.  They have spoken with unanimity as recently as two years ago, when they adopted Proposition 301 by a decent majority.  To me that sends a very strong signal to our elected officials that higher education is valued in this state and we need to do a better job with it.  Therefore, we are prepared to have a tax that will benefit higher education and, in fact, all of education in the state.  The universities will benefit to the tune of about $45 million per year and I am sure you as a Senate have had discussion on how that money is being used.  Unprecedented, it is administered by the Arizona Board of Regents but only after very close consultation with our presidents, and all of you to make sure that the money will be spent wisely.  Proposition 301 sends a pretty strong signal to the Legislature?Fund education!  Now we are at a point for a second year where I am sure you/we are all very disheartened that we have to sustain one cut after another to the budget, and I guess I could tell you that I do not know what to do about that, but I do have an idea.  It is not going to satisfy necessarily the short-term.  We need to get our act together and be serious about reforming our tax code.  Our tax code is an abomination.  Again, I can speak from some experience having been at the legislature for ten years; every year it is eroded by certain interests that-- have a little break here, a little waiver of tax here, a little tweaking and a little crediting and what have you.

 

I had an occasion to ride with one of our new Regents, Fred Boice, to Yuma on the way to our Board meeting last month, and I was complaining to him about our tax codes and how we had a great opportunity a little over ten years ago to bring into the room some of the best economists and others that would, in an objective environment take a look at what a good tax code would look like.  It would be broad-based; it would be fair both vertically and horizontally.  It makes me conclude that if we are to get at funding for higher education, we have to get away from this idea that the taxes should be worn like a badge of courage.  They should not.  The tax code that is going to provide for the needs of this state is achievable, and I believe it is achievable only when we get business leaders and others and the experts that can help us with this to work together, to design a program that can be taken to the Legislature and have the backing of those very business leaders that support higher education.  Not to oversimplify the process, but I think this could be done in a matter of months, the intellectual work, and I think that we have an opportunity now as we elect a new governor and elect new legislators to put that on the agenda. 

 

To revisit the tuition matter, I am all for 4% once the voters approve an amendment to the Constitution.  If we are serious about raising tuition, let?s remove the provision in the Constitution, and then I look at it very differently.  I come from a newspaper background where I also have this thing about First Amendment rights, also a constitutional provision, so I do not take those lightly.  Enough on budget and tuition.

 

In hiring Dr. Michael Crow, I think the Regents did a good thing.  I think in his selection we are going to find that he will take this institution--as Dr. Coor has taken this institution from where it was in the late 80? and 90?s to where it is today?and move us forward and he will be a very strong president for ASU.  The Regents and the search committee and the committees of the Academic Senate here, I believe, all said thumbs up for Dr. Crow.  I think we have a strong president and he will need the support of everyone in this room and many others, to be a great president of ASU.  It is also exciting to me to think of what he brings to Arizona--his background in research and his commitment in his statement to the Board of Regents as to how he will focus on this job and dedicate his full resources to be a strong president.  In terms of pay, it is what it is; it is more than we are paying Lattie.  I wish we could pay Lattie more.  It is part of the market place, you could say that we are paying him less than a football coach is?a segue into the subject of intercollegiate athletics.

 

Last year, the Knight Commission issued a report that indicated that nationally there might be some issues on intercollegiate athletics that need to be considered at every institution in this country, particularly Division 1A programs.  In addition, we need to look at whether or not we have the right balance and are maintaining the academic integrity of our institution, as we define our athletic programs to be considered excellent.  That is not just a natural thing.  It brings in a variety of considerations and challenges for all of us, particularly this group, to make sure that the academic integrity, no matter who you are, is maintained.  The environment of athletic programs is expensive to operate and maintain, and the market for coaches is continuing to move into the very high-income categories.  The last time we hired a coach I mentioned that I thought I would declare a victory, because we hired a coach for under a million dollars.  There are many major institutions in this country that are paying football and basketball coaches in excess of one million dollars.  In the committee that Mort and I served on, we have tried to do a couple of things.  We have moved very slowly to avoid becoming headline material.  This process is something that requires thoughtful consideration for everyone.  We have convened our presidents, our athletic directors, members of the faculty athletic representatives and others, to take a careful, close look (as Lattie asked us to do last August) at intercollegiate athletics and see where we are.  I think that in Arizona, we are in good shape, and we are in a position to be in even better shape compared to many programs around the country.  We have taken Lattie?s suggestion and we have taken our time.  We have heard from our athletic departments with respect to the academic programs within the athletic department; we have looked at the role of faculty athletic representative; we have looked at how we might be able to make some improvements in that regard; and, we will be looking at coaches? salaries and the contract provisions for coaches? salaries.  We will be looking at other issues as they come up.  I am most gratified, however, that during the time that we started to focus on athletics and academics, last August, I think the Knight Commission report is now being taken more seriously by the NCAA.  I think there is recognition that unless the NCAA acts and unless all major universities act, we have some major issues going forward.  So, the NCAA and the various subcommittees making up the NCAA in various conferences, including the big six conferences, and I can?t name them all but the PAC10 is involved and five other major conferences around the country.  We have settled on a couple of things that I think are major and that is, there will be new rules that will be up for approval later this year at the NCAA Board, and those new rules will say, to the effect, Let?s not just focus on keeping athletes eligible, let?s focus on keeping our student athletes on a track to graduate, so that grade point averages and persistence to graduation are the important things.  Let us make sure that our student athletes have a program, that they will maintain progress each year and can graduate in five years.  That is a huge difference from the way it is now.  Now, as I read it, it just says--stay eligible until your knee goes out, or stay eligible until you use up your eligibility.  Maybe at the end of that you may get a degree, you may not.  In our major revenue sports, we have many student athletes that say, I am going to make it to the big leagues?and some do, but most do not.  Frankly, I think we are letting down our student athletes if we do not help them to make the right choices and stay in school and make progress toward graduation.  We should do the very best job we can, and to me that is academic integrity.  I think that just since we have been focusing on the subject, there has been a shift in thinking and as we move forward we have an opportunity to do good work, to make progress.  As the NCAA adopts new rules, I would like to be the early adopter.  I would like our institutions to adopt them.  They can then differentiate when they are recruiting athletes.  They can say to students, if you come to Arizona schools, we are going to help you graduate and we take that very seriously. 

 

Thanks very much for listening to me today, and I will try to answer any questions you may have.  As Dr. Glick recently said in a meeting I had attended--He was asked a question, and he said with a very straight face, I don?t know the answer to that question, but I could answer it, and it would be a very good answer.  Then he fielded the question to someone else.  I will try to do as good.

 

Senator (Dan) Landers:  This is just a comment, but I want to get it on the record.  I get the same form from student athletes that need to leave my class to go on a road trip, from the athletic trainers too.  Those athletic trainers are getting out of my class the same way the athlete is, but they have no support services whatsoever. They do not have study hall, the NCAA does not track them and they are there before the athletes get there, and they are there after the athletes leave.  So, they are putting in more time than the athlete and are getting no services from the athletic community.  I think that something has to be done about that.  I do not know who tracks them to graduation.

Past President Johnson:  You raised the issue of tuition setting which is always an interesting ritual, and the next board meeting will no doubt be colored by that to a great extent.  It concerns me that even though we have one of the lowest tuitions in the country, we are not ranked well in terms of affordability, and it seems like we have to address that in the short run as well as in the long run. 

 

Regent Jewett:  Absolutely.  I think that moving towards improvement on the affordability scale, we have to do a better job with financial aid.  That is why I think that as I become president of the Board, and I hinted at this in a recent column that I wrote on tuition, that I would like to focus on financial aid, very specifically during this next year.  Part of that will be to convince the legislature to do what the Task Force on Higher Education asked them to do and put some state money into Pell Grants.  I think we need to do a survey for all other opportunities for financial aid.  The Board is putting new policies in place at our April meeting that will provide greater flexibility for our institutions to use financial aid, but we still have to put more money into it. 

 

Senate President Munk: Thank you for coming today.  I would first like to follow up on what Regent Jewett said and share some thoughts with you about the presidential search that we have just completed.  Would I have preferred a smaller committee?  Yes.  Would I have preferred a greater representation from members of the Academic Assembly?  Yes.  However, I do want to say that with very few exceptions the committee worked well together, and certainly came together at the right time, although initially there was a rather broad spectrum of views as you might expect with a committee with such great diversity.  That did narrow with time.  The committee agreed that someone with strong academic credentials and someone with experience at a major university was required, and overall I think that we did very well.  Not only do we have someone that could take the place of a president that has led us far over the last decade, but also I think we may be making new friends among some of the members of the search committee.  We have a few new ASU boosters and friends, and I think that is a real plus.  Speaking for myself, I am very pleased with the outcome.  I believe we have a good match between the credentials, experience and aspirations of the person that was selected and the challenges and the aspirations of Arizona State University.  I think there is cause for excitement.  I want to remind you of something that Lattie Coor said the other day?perhaps we ought to think about fastening our seat belts, we might be in for quite a ride.

 

I wanted to mention to you, perhaps some of you are aware of the fact, that there was a recent report, a second report by the Arizona Chapter of the National Association of Scholars.  This was a report dealing with the General Studies Program and everyone concluded that the General Studies Program is broken and needs fixing.  They drew that conclusion based on the results of a forty question multiple answer examination that was given to 167 students from the three Arizona universities.  Students were invited to participate and take that examination and they were offered five dollars and fifty cents for each correct answer.  Although I am not a statistician, it seems to me that is not exactly how one goes about making an appropriate sample.  However, if the question is, can we improve upon our General Studies program, I think the answer is yes.  We have a good program, we certainly can improve on it, and we need to work on that as well.

 

Another item: you may recall that in October 1999 the Arizona Supreme Court ruled in the Camille Kimball Case that in faculty grievance hearings the grievant has a right to be represented by counsel and has the right to request reconsideration of the President?s decision, based on certain specific grounds.  That ruling also gave subpoena power to the hearing committees.  These requirements, as you know, have been incorporated into ASU?s policies and procedures, but recently the Board of Regents convened a tri-university committee to revise ABOR policy 6-201 which deals with dismissal and suspension without pay, specifically.  The charge was to apply Kimball Case requirements to these issues and the committee has revised the appropriate policies.  The universities have been asked to review that work and we will.  I will ask the Senate Personnel Committee to review the recommendations of the tri-university committee and then make a report to the President and to the Board of Regents.    

 

We are very fortunate to have our president, Lattie Coor, with us today.  This is an opportunity for me to express our collective appreciation to the administration, for their very deep commitment to the faculty and academic professional governance of this university--we truly do have a partnership with our administration.  My experience is that our situation is the exception rather than the rule at most other universities.  I think that we owe a vote of thanks to our administration for maintaining such a collegial and open administration, where we can feel we are very much a part of what is going on at the university.  With that, I will introduce Dr. Coor.

 

3.C  University President?s Report (Lattie Coor).

 

Thank you, Mort.  I had not realized until last week with disbelief that this is the last Senate meeting of the year.  Somehow, it always sneaks up on me faster than I would have expected.  So, I join you for that significant moment as we near the end of the academic year.  Two pieces of business before I say a word about you and your work?and this very promising moment of transition to my successor--and to the next decade of fascinating and promising development of this university. 

 

First, the terrible news.  I bring it to you from Lake Woebegone--about the appropriations process which

had a completely unpredictable level in terms of a timetable.  It means that we will now have to set tuition on April 25 and 26 at the Board of Regents meeting, before we have a clue as to where the Legislature is going to be on the budget.  Even though I believe that we can protect and manage our position, it is not an ideal one.  The primary pieces on the table that I would urge you to watch, and your leadership does a good job of that for you, is the magnitude of the cuts being proposed.  The Governor has recommended an additional 2.5% for all of the universities across the board.  On the already revised face for the new fiscal year, there are as of now about six million dollars for ASU.  There are no salary proposals on the table and the special session for the current fiscal year, the one that took place in November and December, literally erased any commitments we have in the next fiscal year.  Therefore, it will all have to be designed before this legislature adjourns, and there are as of now no salary proposals on the table.  You know the outcome of the most recent special session, which ended up being an across the board increase of $1,450 for everyone, regardless of position and rank or performance, and the one that will be easiest to lose sight of in a public discussion--is the enrollment growth money.  It has a very interesting formula that you have often heard referred to as 22 to 1, which provides us the closest access to a predictable revenue that we have.  It provides us revenue based on the number of additional full-time equivalent students we get.  Under that formula, ASU Main alone next year qualifies for $8 million, yet in the initial budget on the table from the Governor?s Office, the enrollment growth formula is not on the table.  That is important both for ASU West also, which is now on the enrollment growth formula for the first time having reached its base budget.  That figure would be $3.3 million for ASU West, which by the way would be an additional loss of revenue to us.  Discussion on this will extend into mid-May, maybe even beyond the end of the term, and it obviously has major consequences.

 

I speak with great admiration and affection for Jack Jewett, not only in the service he has provided to date as a Legislator and a Regent but that which he will provide in this coming year, and I can tell you that he is one of the few public officials that will utter the T-word.  Thank goodness for that, Jack, and keep on doing it because it is the only way ultimately we are going to reconcile a set of legitimate demands for this state with a revenue base that is not adequate after nine consecutive years of trimming.  Jack accurately described cutting taxes as ?the badge of courage.? I Cut Taxes, Hooray! ?It could be likened to a family standing in their neighborhood and saying, look at our three children, aren?t they wonderful?  We invest less in them than anybody else in this neighborhood, aren?t you proud of us?  But we recognize that there have to be different revenue sources for the future.  I cannot bring you anything other than news that it is a significant issue which will not be resolved before the term is ended.  It probably will not be resolved until mid to late May.

 

A second thing, tuition is on the table.  There will be hearings tomorrow; they will be from 5:00 to 7:00 p.m. in the Business School Building.  There are multiple sites involved.  Jeremy Helper and the student leadership from ASU and the other campuses will be at the table speaking along with the presidents, but it is mostly an opportunity to hear from the students.  Then on Thursday and Friday of next week, the Board will set tuition.  I have recommended a $300 increase in-state, the largest that I have ever recommended in dollars or proportion, and a $750 out-of-state increase.  Pete Likins at the University of Arizona has recommended a $300 in-state and $1,000 out-of-state increase.  John Haeger at NAU has recommended $290 in-state and $750 out-of-state.  I believe that we should be rolling a larger share of any tuition increase right back into financial aid.  That is how we can accommodate in the modern form, as nearly free as possible education, still within these current budgetary constraints.  I will tell you it is always a quality debate on tuition, and I encourage you to stay a part of that debate as we move from here to the meeting next week.

 

Now, just a word about you, Mort, and your leadership.  It was clear to me when I was in that look and see mode in 1989--although I could not come as full-time president until January of 1990--that there was something very unusual about ASU; not only a sense of destiny, the hunger of the quality of this place to continue to be better, but the spirit with which the university went about it and the quality of the leadership of the Academic Assembly.  It is true that in that year I was elected on that search committee?s recommendation and Arlene Metha was the Senate President at that time and she in turn was succeeded by Senate President Alan Matheson, and so it has gone right up through Doug Johnson and now with Mort Munk and George Watson. It is, in my view, one of the most significant forces of collegial decision making that I have seen anywhere.  For reasons that I have never understood, there had been a number of vetoes by the university president of Senate actions, in the few years just before I arrived and I never did explore that.  I just did not understand it.  I sort of made a pledge to the then leadership of the Senate, that I have not had to repeat since then, that we would find ways to hold fast to our views and disagree, unless, or until, we could persuade one another.  I said that I hoped never once in my presidency would I have to veto an action of this Academic Assembly.  There are two little caveats to what I am about to tell you. Because on two instances I said--I don?t think you are right, but if you believe you are, go ahead.  In both instances the rightness of the Senate?s view was correct.  That does not mean that there has been any less vigorous debate, exploration, expression of opinions, but there has been in my view in the best of worlds, a reaching out and exploring and an understanding of how and where we together have accomplished the greater goals of this university.  So, to all of you and your predecessors of these past years, I express my thanks to your leadership, especially to you, Mort, in this your last meeting of this year, and to the leadership that has preceded you and will follow you, I express my thanks.  I will tell you it has been noted by Michael Crow already as he has begun to understand this institution, and I know it will serve him well also.  To all of you I express my thanks for that, and my strong affirmation of what you have already heard about Michael Crow.  He knows us and he will come to know us better.  He understands that this is one of the most promising settings, university and community, state and region in the world, and I know he will join this endeavor without a hitch and will keep the trajectory moving forward.  So, I encourage you to join him, embrace him and give him the same support that you so kindly gave me right from the very day that I came here.  Thank you.
