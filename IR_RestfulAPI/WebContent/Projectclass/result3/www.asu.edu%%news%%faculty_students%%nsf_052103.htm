ASU students earn NSF graduate fellowships for research

The National Science Foundation (NSF) has recognized 24 present and former ASU students for academic and research excellence. Students winning Graduate Fellowship Awards include: Vladas Griskevicius, Cinnamon Jill Hayes, Sophia Kenrick, Alison Polasik, Justin Pucci, Robin Roth and Nick Schmerr. Seventeen other students received honorable mentions.

These awards represent NSF's most prestigious recognition of students, said Gregory Raupp, associate vice president for research at ASU.

"Our students will work with distinguished research faculty mentors and will engage in cutting-edge research in important topics ranging from human perception to new technologies for water purification," he said.

NSF seeks to support students and their research in science, mathematics and engineering in the United States. The NSF Graduate Fellowships offer recognition and three years of support for advanced study to approximately 900 outstanding graduate students in mathematical, physical, biological, engineering and behavioral and social sciences and to research-based Ph.D. degrees in science education. Awards carry a stipend of $27,500 per year and an annual cost-of-education allowance of $10,500, paid to Fellows' institution in lieu of tuition and fees.

Griskevicius, a Ph.D. student in social psychology, is conducting research on persuasion and compliance. Hayes will begin a Ph.D. program at ASU in biology this fall and will focus her research on interactions between symbiotic fungi living within host plants. Kenrick is an undergraduate student in ASU's chemical engineering program who plans to do research related to bioengineering and biomaterials at the University of California, Santa Barbara.

Polasik earned an undergraduate engineering degree in materials engineering at ASU and now pursues a graduate degree at Ohio State University. Pucci, an undergraduate in aerospace engineering, conducts research in electric propulsion and would like to study hall thruster plasma physics at MIT in graduate school. Graduate student Roth is working with James Beckman, one of the preeminent researchers in water purification and desalination. Schmerr, a Ph.D. student in geological sciences, uses seismology to image and interpret regions of heterogeneity within the Earth's interior.

Seventeen ASU students received honorable mentions in the competition. The list includes: Jasmin Abreu, psychology; Sarah Baran, psychology; Joshua Billar, chemical engineering; Nicholas Brindley, mechanical engineering; Jeffrey Dailey, materials science; Jonathan Davis, physiology; Matthew Desmond, sociology; Susannah French, biology; Kathleen Faccia, anthropology; Matthew Fritz, psychology; Andrea Jani, ecology; Gwynne Johnston, physiology; Amy McAdam, geosciences; Katie McLaughlin, psychology; Brandon Moore, neuroscience; Adam Hartstone-Rose, anthropology; and Mason Thompson, archaeology.

ASU Graduate College Dean Bianca Bernstein will determine the winners of future NSF Graduate Fellowships, among other awards, after recently accepting a two-year appointment as NSF director of graduate education.

"My division at NSF administers this award program," said Dean Bernstein, who will begin her new duties in Arlington, Va., in the near future. "My presence at NSF will be quite beneficial to ASU. I want to encourage ASU students to maintain their high level of excellence in research and in competing for the prestigious NSF Graduate Fellowships."
