February 3, 2003

Regents discuss modification of financial aid, admissions

Tuition, financial aid and admission dominated the discussion for the Arizona Board of Regents (ABOR) meeting Jan. 23 - 24 in Tucson. The three issues are central to the board's Changing Directions Initiative, a move to allow each of the state's three universities to seek its own identity, while increasing accessibility to higher education.

ABOR President Jack Jewett of Tucson, says as the board moves toward increasing each university's distinctive quality, its overriding goal is to make higher education more inclusive, improve the state's student success rates and meet Arizona's work force needs. These goals can be accomplished if universities are allowed greater flexibility to play a more centralized role in determining the institution's policy and outcomes.

The board reviewed two likely admission policy scenarios during its study session in preparation for writing a new admissions policy to support the differentiation initiative. Jewett said the new policy would be ready for review at the March 6 - 7 meeting.

The first scenario is nearly identical to the current admissions policy. It ensures access at one of the state universities for the top 25 percent of all entering resident freshmen based on class rank or a 3.0 grade-point average or scoring exceptionally on a college entrance exam, if the student has no more than one required course deficiency. No required course deficiencies are allowed by the current policy.
Arizona high school graduates ranking in at least the top 50 percent with at least a C-plus grade-point average may also be admitted to a state university on a case-by-case basis under the current ABOR policy. It mirrors the proposed policies in each scenario.

While the second scenario - which was endorsed by the university presidents - closely patterns the first scenario's admissions criteria, it puts the admission decision-making and accountability measures in the hands of the universities.
ASU President Michael Crow said this flexibility allows universities to be more inclusive to Arizona students by considering various individualized factors such as a student's leadership characteristics, special abilities and talents and their drive for success, while controlling for grade inflation. By having more control in the admissions decision-making, the universities also have more control in determining their retention and graduation rates, he said.

"ASU can focus its energy on admitting most students in the top half of the high school graduating class into programs where they will be academically successful as long as there are tuition levels to generate revenues robust enough to maintain quality, continuous and dependable funding by the state legislature for enrollment growth and adequate funding available to expand ASU's academic facilities to accommodate growth," Crow said.

The board also heard new language modifying its current policy to set aside need-based student financial aid received from tuition revenues. The new policy increases need-based financial aid from 8 percent at ASU and UA, and 9 percent at NAU, to 14 percent. The additional need-based financial aid is intended to offset the impact resulting from any significant tuition increase.

"It will take more revenue than is provided by this set- aside to accomplish individual university or board goals for financial aid," said Jewett. "There is a lot we can do, but we can't do it all (at a board level). The state must also step in and do its part."
The board is expected to approve the modified financial aid policy during its March meeting.

In other action, the ABOR:

. Voted to set undergraduate and graduate tuition for the 2003 - 04 academic year during its March 6 - 7 meeting at ASU. During the meeting, residence halls rates and undergraduate fees will also be set. Graduate program fees will be set April 24 - 25 in Tucson.

. Gave its consent for a $90,000 per year, multi-year employment contract for Head Softball Coach Linda Wells through June 2005. Wells' salary was based on her experience and success with the softball program. She has been head coach for 27 years, 13 years of which have been at ASU. Wells' salary will be paid entirely from revenue generated by the intercollegiate athletics department.

. Consented to offer ASU Head Women's Golf Coach Melissa McNamara a $70,000 annual, multi-year employment contract through June 2005. The board agreed to raise the annual salary on July 1 and on each consecutive July 1 thereafter in percentage increments depending on achievement and performance milestones. McNamara's salary will be paid entirely from revenue generated by the intercollegiate athletics department.

. Is considering a policy revision to allow tuition waivers and remissions for graduate teaching and research assistants and associates. The proposed policy change would allow universities to treat tuition waivers and remissions as tax-exempt compensation while permitting the institutions to properly charge tuition benefits to federal grants or contracts.
