February 22, 2006

Researchers look to reduce emergency room waits

On News Year's Day this year, Banner Baywood Medical Center in Mesa closed its emergency room for three hours when it couldn't handle the number of patients showing up at its doors.

Banner Baywood is far from alone in being challenged by a growing demand for emergency care. The American College of Emergency Physicians released a study in mid-January reporting that Arizona's hospitals, along with those in many states, face overcrowding that threatens their ability to provide critical health care - and renders that them ill-prepared to cope with a large-scale crisis.

The search for solutions already is under way at ASU.

Jeffrey Cochran, professor of industrial engineering at the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering, is collaborating with Banner Health on a Door-to-Doc (D2D) program, a "tool kit" for implementing operational changes aimed at getting patients more timely and effective emergency health care.

The work is funded by a two-year, $600,000 grant from the Agency for Healthcare Quality and Research (AHRQ), part of U.S. Health and Human Services. Twila Burdick, vice president for organizational performance, is leading the effort for Banner Health.

The target is to ensure it typically will take no more than 20 minutes for a patient to be seen by an emergency room physician.

Of course, hospitals experience atypical situations. At times such as winter flu season, it has been known to take as long as 12 hours for patients to get treatment, Cochran says.

"There is no process that cannot be overwhelmed," he says. "Nobody can afford to run a hospital with enough capacity for the busiest hour on the busiest day of the year."

But D2D promises methods that will enable physicians and nurses to give prompt attention to patients - while providing quality care in a cost-effective fashion - at almost all times.

The key is a "whole hospital" approach to hospital management, taking into account the "chain-reaction affect" of each area of care on the other, Cochran says.

"All the parts are connected," he says. "The big thing is collaboration."

It's a matter of working smarter, not necessarily harder or faster, he says.

The research is focusing, in part, on aspects of systems flow and "queuing" theory: looking at how and why lines form, how they grow and diminish, and how people perceive and react to them.

That involves creating computer models of emergency departments to study their operations in relation to other hospital departments, and educating managers about how systems-flow principles can be taught to hospital staff.

The most popular of these education methods is a David Letterman-style list of the "Top 10 Ways to Make a Throughput Worse," which Cochran wrote and has presented to more than 1,000 Banner health employees. It helps each of them understand what they can do in their specific emergency care roles to improve patient flow.

Studies show waiting times can be reduced if doctors, nurses and staff can perform their emergency-care assignments concurrently, whenever possible, instead of strictly segmenting each task.

"Processes have to be done in parallel, not just one thing after another," Cochran says.

For that, he says, "you need something like an air-traffic controller" to direct the various things that need to happen in an emergency-room operation to keep the system from getting into the rut of a start-and-stop-again routine.

Basic efficiency, however, is not the overriding objective for D2D. The bottom line is the human factor: patient safety and access.

Because the result of emergency room overcrowding is often poorer public health, "the consequences of what happens in making these kinds of system improvements is so fundamentally important," Cochran says.

"I'm strongly drawn to this," he says, enough so that he foresees the endeavor continuing to be the major focus of his research for the remainder of his engineering career.

Phoenix-based Banner Health is one of the largest not-for-profit health care systems in the country. It has 20 hospitals and a variety of other services in seven Western states.

Banner Health's size and the diversity of its facilities provide a "living laboratory" for testing the effectiveness of D2D, Burdick says.
