MISSION, GOALS AND OBJECTIVES

Arizona State University’s (ASU) goal is to become a world-class university in a multi-campus setting. Its mission is to provide outstanding programs in instruction, research, and creative activity, to promote and support economic development, and to provide service appropriate for the nation, the state of Arizona, and the state’s major metropolitan area. To fulfill its mission, ASU places special emphasis on the core disciplines and offers a full range of degree programs--baccalaureate through doctorate, recognizing that it must offer quality programs at all degree levels in a broad range of fundamental fields of inquiry. ASU will continue to dedicate itself to superior instruction; to excellent student performance; to original research, creative endeavor, and scholarly achievement; and to outstanding public service and economic development activities. As a result of this dedication, ASU was named to Research Extensive (formerly Research I) status in 1994, recognizing ASU as a premier research institution.

SUMMARY OF BUSINESS PROCESSES

ASU is unique in its structure, culture, processes, products, services, and physical facilities. Within our major areas, i.e., Information Technology, Facilities Management, Public Safety, Student Affairs, Research, etc., each organization has documents that address their own unique requirements and key personnel that respond to the kinds of disasters that they anticipate. The Maximum Acceptance Outage also varies by organization and depends upon the business function that is being impacted.

Colleges and departments work hard to reduce, mitigate, and reduce problems from occurring as well as preparing in advance for problems that affect their organizations. Equipment breakdowns, technical glitches or failures, power outages, and human error do occur and are handled in the normal course of doing business so a detailed listing of these impacts would be exhaustive.

In the event of fire, earthquakes, floods, or other natural disasters, major accidents, acts of terrorism, large-scale military operations, chemical or biological attacks that would affect the campuses, the safety and security of our students, and employees (faculty and staff) is our first priority. To accomplish this goal, we have business continuity plans in place to address how ASU responds – recovers – and restores services in event of these emergencies.

We evaluate and revise those plans as necessary to ensure quick communication and action in the event of an emergency. We use every method of communication available to us, including e-mail, voice mail, the ASU website home page http://www.asu.edu/, external media, posters, and flyers. In addition, ASU has established an information phone line (480) 727-9911 that updates the status of the University as needed and provide instruction in the event of an emergency. We are in the process of adding a toll-free version for out-of-town callers.

We pilot tested a BIA survey document within two critical areas – Purchasing and Business Services and DPS. (See Attachment)

Given the size and complexity of ASU, however, we realize that the coordination of the distributed disaster continuity and business recovery plans is a significant challenge. Therefore to improve our current practice, ASU in partnership with the University of Arizona and Northern Arizona University is going to purchase Strohl System software.

The Strohl System is designed for large organizations like ASU that has its main campus located in Tempe and additional campuses in West Phoenix, East Mesa, and the Downtown Center in Phoenix. With the Strohl System, the University will be able to maintain the continuity and recovery plans, documents, and contact and personnel information about employees from different colleges, departments and locations into one central database. We anticipate purchase in FY 2003.
