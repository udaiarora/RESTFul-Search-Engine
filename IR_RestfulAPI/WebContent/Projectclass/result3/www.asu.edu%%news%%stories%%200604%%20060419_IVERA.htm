April 19, 2006

Conference highlights indigenous education efforts

Speakers, performers, education scholars, practitioners and students from across North America will gather on ASU's Tempe campus April 27 - 29 to attend the 2006 Indigenous Issues and Voices in Educational Research and Assessment (IVERA) conference.

The conference will explore issues affecting the educational experiences of families from indigenous cultures. It will feature a wide range of keynote speakers, papers and performances, and the sessions will explore a range of scholarships related to indigenous teacher education, community-based research and decolonizing methodologies, and language and cultural identity issues. One of many highlights of the conference will be a presentation by award-winning Seattle filmmaker Jill Friedberg, who will discuss her new film, "Granito de Arena" (Grain of Sand), focusing on indigenous teachers' grassroots organizing in Mexico.

IVREA, led by the College of Education's Divisions of Curriculum and Instruction and Educational Leadership and Policy Studies, is an extension of the Relevance of Culture in Assessment (RACE) conference. RACE was initiated at ASU in 2000 to provide a national forum for exploring how cultural differences between individuals and communities influence educational assessment within schools and mental health settings.

"There are many shared struggles, themes and movements across indigenous peoples and much to learn from these experiences," says Beth Blue Swadener, a professor of early childhood education and policy studies.

Swadener and her colleague, Caroline Turner, a professor of educational leadership and policy studies, were selected by the College of Education 's dean, Eugene Garcia, to co-chair the conference. Planning committee members include College of Education faculty members Teresa McCarty, Kathryn Manuelito, Maxine Roanhorse-Dineyazhe, Mary Eunice Romero, Timothy Begaye and Alfredo Artiles, and American Indian Studies assistant professor Susan Miller.

Hyun-Jean Yi and Beth Fredricks, College of Education graduate students, also served on the planning committee.

Featured conference presenters include:

. Bryan Brayboy, assistant professor of education and director of the Center for the Study of Race and Diversity in Higher Education at the University of Utah.

. Joseph Suina, an associate professor with the Department of Language, Literacy and Sociocultural Studies and director of the Institute for American Indian Education at the University of New Mexico.

. Maenette Benham, a professor with the Department of Educational Administration at Michigan State University .

. Julie Kaomea, an assistant professor with the College of Education at the University of Hawaii-Manoa.

The conference will open with flute music composed and performed by Randy Kemp, a Chocktaw/Euchee-Creek and a graphic designer at ASU. The program includes ATOA, a Polynesian cultural heritage troupe featuring young performers and traditional food prepared by students in the indigenous teacher education program at ASU.

Suina will deliver the opening keynote address, which Swadener says will focus on his many experiences as a Pueblo community leader and as a researcher in New Mexico. Additionally, Benham's presentation will focus on her current project, titled "In our Mother's Voice II: Indigenous Learning and Leading Models for Contemporary Practice."

For more information, visit the Web site (http://coe.asu.edu/ivera) or call the College of Education at (480) 965-6502.
