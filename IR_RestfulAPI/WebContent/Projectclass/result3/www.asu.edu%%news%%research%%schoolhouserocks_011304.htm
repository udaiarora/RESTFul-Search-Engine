January 13, 2004

ASU expecting to get rocked by schoolkids

At a NASA Press Briefing last Thursday at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory in Pasadena, California, Arizona State Korrick Professor of Geology Philip Christensen made an announcement that was sure to interest kids around the country, and strike terror in the hearts of mail carriers everywhere -- particularly those on the ASU campus.

Christensen, the lead investigator for the Mini Thermal Emission Spectrometer (Mini-TES) instruments on the Mars Exploration Rovers, announced to the thousands of watching school classrooms around the country that he was starting a new outreach program which would allow students to participate in the research that Mini-TES is conducting. Students could participate by helping his lab assemble a sample of Earth rocks for laboratory testing and for comparison to those on Mars. In other words, Christensen asked millions of kids around the country to gather stones for science and to mail them to ASU. The program is aptly named "Schoolhouse Rocks."

Mini-TES is based on innovative technology developed by Christensen that allows the analysis of minerals through reading the infrared emissions of rocks and soils. Though not visible to the human eye, materials have "colors" in the infrared spectrum that, when carefully analyzed, can tell scientists their chemical composition. Christensen has used this technology to develop a range of instruments that are currently being used in the lab, on Earth, and for remote sensing on Mars. Christensen currently has two instruments orbiting Mars on the Mars Global Surveyor (TES) and Mars Odyssey (THEMIS) spacecraft, and Mini-TES instruments on both MER  rovers -- the largest assembly of instruments ever deployed in space at one time.

Christensen is also nationally known as an innovator in science education. Seeing space as a way to get students interested in science careers, Christensen founded ASU's Mars Education Outreach Program while principal investigator for the TES instrument on the Mars Global Surveyor, and expanded the program with the Mars Student Imaging Project, a program that allows grade school, middle school and high school students to use the THEMIS camera on the Mars Odyssey Spacecraft to take new images of the martian surface and to conduct original scientific research.

Details of this heavy mail experiment are available on the NASA website at: http://marsrovers.jpl.nasa.gov/classroom/schoolhouse/ . For more information about the Mars Student Imaging Project, see http://msip.asu.edu .
