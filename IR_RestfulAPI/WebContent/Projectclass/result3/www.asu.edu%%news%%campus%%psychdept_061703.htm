June 18, 2003

Psychology department gains space for teaching, research

The ASU Psychology program has the largest number of undergraduate majors in the College of Liberal Arts & Sciences, and has experienced considerable growth in the development of its research programs.

This growth has created a need for additional space to enable the department to enhance its teaching and research programs.

In an effort to support the department's increasing needs, 7,300 square-feet of space in the Technology Center, located just north of the Psychology Building, has been made available. The Air Force and Army ROTC programs currently occupying the space will move to the University Services Building.

Beginning in August, Psychology researchers and staff members will begin moving into the building, which is being renamed Psychology North. Additional Psychology employees will move into the building over the next 18 to 24 months.

The combination of this building with the Psychology Building to the south creates a two-building complex that reflects the university's commitment to support and enhance the teaching and research programs of the Psychology Department.

ASU Psychology Chairman Darwyn Linder says the expansion is very good news for his department, which has suffered from a chronic shortage of space for the past five years.

Linder says that even while housed in limited working spaces, Psychology has consistently been one of the most productive departments in the university in the competition for external funding, bringing in more than $4.5 million in new funding in each of the past few years.

"The new space will allow us to increase the number of new proposals we submit, because we can now be confident that we will have the space needed to do the research," Linder says.

Eventually the space will be renovated, partitioning large spaces into smaller laboratories, work rooms and offices. Unfortunately, there is neither time nor funding available to complete renovations before the beginning of the Fall semester, so for the time being the space will be utilized as is.

"Nevertheless, the immediate effect will be to mitigate congested conditions in the Psychology Building by moving researchers who are now forced to share space and to work at a restricted pace into space in which they can work much more efficiently and effectively," he says.

Linder says he looks forward to the move, and sees it as the next step in making an already productive department even more successful.

"This new space will greatly benefit our students, as well as our faculty and staff, because virtually all of our research provides learning opportunities for our 1,500-plus undergraduate majors as well as our 110-plus Ph.D students," he says.
