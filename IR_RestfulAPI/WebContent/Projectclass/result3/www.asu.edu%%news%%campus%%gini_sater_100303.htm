October 3, 2003

Sater builds ties to village through housing project

For one ASU employee, global engagement has taken on a personal meaning after spending two weeks in Romania helping to build homes for low-income families.

Gini Sater, assistant to the vice president for University Undergraduate Initiatives, spent two weeks this August in Beius, Romania, leading a team of 11 volunteers in a housing project for Habitat for Humanity. The trip, through the organization's Global Village program, was designed to help the local Habitat affiliate complete a four-plex for several Romanian families.

"People talk about trips like this as life-changing experiences," she says. "I like to think of it more as a life-affirming experience. It is rewarding to know you have contributed in some way to the quality of life for a family, and it also reminds you of all of the good things you have in your own life."

As the team leader, Sater was charged with planning the trip, selecting her team of volunteers and coordinating with the Habitat affiliate while the group was in Romania. The construction was led by Romanian builders and a professional foreman.

It was Sater's second trip to the town of 10,000 people near the Hungarian border. In 2001, Sater worked with a team that renovated an old, communist-era apartment building. It was such a rewarding effort that Sater wanted to lead a return trip.
Sater says the team performed mostly exterior work - installing siding and stuccoing the second floor of the building.

"We also tiled the roof, which meant moving at least 2,000 pieces of tile from the delivery truck, to the house and then onto the roof," says Sater. "We finished the roof on our last day, and everyone cheered loudly when that last piece of tile was put on."

The team, which stayed at a family home, also got to learn about life in a small town in Romania and the challenges that Romanians face as their country develops.

"Habitat's philosophy is that families work together to build their homes and those of their neighbors," says Sater. "We got to work with the future homeowners and learn what their lives have been like before and after the fall of communism. We also worked with orphans who are in a job training program, so we were able to learn more about their life experiences as well."

Sater encourages people to consider volunteering with Habitat, noting that global engagement can mean anything from spending a Saturday volunteering in south Phoenix to spending a week in South America. She says volunteers don't need construction experience, just a desire to help others.

For information about local, national and international Habitat for Humanity projects, visit the Web at (www.habitat.org).
