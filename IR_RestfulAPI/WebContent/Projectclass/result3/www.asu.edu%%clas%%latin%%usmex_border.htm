BORDER STUDIES AT ASU

           

            The U.S.-Mexico border is a diverse and complex region that inspires the research of over twenty faculty members at Arizona State. Because Arizona's growth and development strongly impacts México as well, ASU has developed progressive programs for the study of the U.S.-México border region. Arizona State has responded to the continuous integration of two cultures in the Southwest by forming the Southwest Borderlands Initiative, The North American Center for Transborder Studies, and the Chicana/Chicano Department of Transborder Studies. These programs have created opportunities for valuable researchers in border studies, such as Raul Rodriguez, Raul Izaguirre, Orde Kittrie, Eddie Brown, and Dale Furnish, to join ASU in our efforts to represent both the United States and México.

            Many ASU faculty members hope to provide an insights the impact of global processes on local communities in México and the United States. The division of labor in the export processing zones of México has caught the attention of faculty who study issues of gender, examining the effects of transnational corporations on the lives of women in Tijuana and Ciudad Juárez The fusion of American and Mexican art and culture has influenced historians and film makers to incorporate the architecture and geography of the U.S.-México border into their fields of study. Meanwhile, the Southwest Consortium for Environmental Research and Policy provides a locus for research concerning the environmental impact of border crossing, both with regard to immigration and international trade.

FEATURES

Law professor, Orde Kittrie joined ASU in 2004, and was recently named one of the four top Latino international law professors in the United States by the Hispanic Outlook in Higher Education magazine. He was raised by a Mexican-American family and has always been aware of tensions between the United States and Mexico.  “At the same time,” says Kittrie on ASU Insight, “I was aware that there is clearly common ground between the two countries.”  Orde Kittrie is a key figure in international relations at Arizona State. 

Raul Rodriguez will soon be joining ASU as the chair for the North American Center for Transborder Studies. Mr. Rodriguez has extensive international experience, and has participated as a speaker and lecturer in more than 30 countries on business, financial, managerial and economic development topics.  As the CEO and Managing Director of the North American Development Bank, Raul developed an extensive knowledge of the financial needs of the U.S.-Mexico border region.  Mr. Rodriguez also served as the Executive Director of the Mexican Foreign Trade Bank and was Mexico’s Trade Commissioner in Canada during the NAFTA negotiation.

Raul Yzaguirre is the president and founder of the National Council of La Raza.  He is a professor of practice in the Department of Community Development and Civil Rights at Arizona State.

RESOURCES
  • Southwest Borderlands Initiative

The Southwest Borderlands Initiative has two primary objectives.  They are the strengthening of existing ASU scholarly and instructional resources on the Southwest with emphasis on the region along the United States-Mexico border, and enhancing institutional recruitment and retention efforts toward building a faculty fully reflective of the Southwest Borderlands' diversity.
          

  • SCERP 
The Southwest Consortium for Environmental Research and Policy or SCERP, a collaboration of five U.S. and five Mexican universities located in all ten border states, assists U.S.-Mexican border peoples and their environments by applying research information, insights, and innovations.
  • ASU HAYDEN LIBRARY: Mexico-U.S. Relations (History 498)
Selected list of reference books, online indexes and databases, web sites, and guides to primary sources for research in Mexico-U.S. relations
  • North American Center for Transborder Studies
NACTS is a tri-national initiative that connects local communities, governmental officials and public sector agencies to academic research institutions in an effort to produce holistic data and provide greater understanding of border issues across disciplines.

Earlham College Border Studies Program
This program is situated on the U.S.-Mexico international border and based in the metropolitan area of El Paso, Texas and Ciudad Juarez, Chihuahua, where the complexities of the borderlands are most apparent. This region is the subject of extensive global debate on such hotly contested issues as immigration, free trade, labor, the environment, human rights, and the consequences of globalization.

BorderLinks is a not-for-profit organization that conducts travel seminars focusing on the issues of Mexican border communities
