Good News for Spam Sufferers!

Over the past several months, LIST has been contacted by a number of library staff members concerned about a dramatic increase in spam mail delivered to their Outlook Inbox. To address these concerns, Dean Schmidt has agreed to a one-time bulk purchase of Spam Inspector, a spam-blocking software tested and recommended by the campus IT department.

In order to establish the number of licenses to be purchased, LIST is scheduling an evaluation period for you to download, install, and use the trial version of Spam Inspector during the month of October. (Instructions below.) If you are satisfied with the results, we are asking that you submit your name, location, and workstation ID # to your ERR Rep, who will in turn submit the list to Rob Fidler at rob.fidler@asu.edu. If you decide not to request this software, simply uninstall the application through the Control Panel option “Add/Remove Programs” at or before the end of the trial evaluation period. Please note: If your computer is a LIST Managed machine, please contact Jeremy Kurtz at 5-1902 or via email at jeremy.kurtz@asu.edu if you wish to participate in this trial.

Spam Inspector is one of several spam-filtering products ASU’s Information Technologies (IT) tested in recent months. A number of these programs were found to be equally satisfactory in their performance and ease of use. The decision to use Spam Inspector is based on the numbers of existing campus users and a discounted pricing structure for Arizona State affiliates. To evaluate Spam Inspector, download and install the 15-day trial version available at http://www.giantcompany.com, between now and the end of October.

Make certain you download and install the trial version for Microsoft Outlook 2000 and 2002, not any others. The application is supported on the following operating systems: Windows 95/98/ME/NT4.0/2000/XP; it works with Outlook 2000 and 2002, IMAP and POP mail. The software does not work for web mail, and currently there is no version for Macintosh.


“Overview” Page Added to Multi-Database Search

By popular demand, we have created an overview and tour of the newly-unveiled Multi-Database Search tool. The Multi-Database Search is the name given to the search tool that is emerging from the ARL Scholar's Portal project (more info from ARL at http://www.arl.org/access/scholarsportal/). MDS is designed to search information from multiple data sources and then provide a sortable listing of these resources that can contain direct links to electronic journal entries, PDFs and, in some cases, full text. It is often described as an “academic Google.”

ASU Libraries' MDS is still in its infancy. Though the MDS is operable, it is in active development and is constantly being improved. Currently we are working on adding more resources and more search profiles to the search engine as well as redesigning the current interface, which includes creating more descriptive and help information, as well as additional refinement of the interface’s look and feel.

Check out the new overview/tour page at http://zportal.lib.asu.edu/zportal/MDSOverview.html.

We have high expectations for the Multi-Database Search and we need your input to ensure that these expectations are met. Please send your comments about the MDS to ScholarsPortal@asu.edu.

 

SilverPlatter Databases Successfully Migrated to SilverPlatter Internet Service!

SilverPlatter databases such as BIOSIS Previews, PsycInfo, and Periodical Abstracts Full Text have been locally maintained for many years. Recently we transferred these databases to Ovid's SilverPlatter Internet Service. Ovid now hosts all our WebSpirs databases (over 40 databases). They provide 24/7 technical support at their site in Norwood, MA. This has allowed us to give our users the same database service without the server administration and software maintenance formerly required by the local databases. The transition has been completely seamless to our users, has resulted in better support for the databases, and has allowed us to simplify our database pages by removing the little-used “Windows client” option.

 

New Databases Available

These new databases have been put online: http://www.asu.edu/lib/top-menus/new.html.

SBRnet: Sports Business Research Network
This database has market research reports, consumer surveys, news releases, government data, and articles.

International Million Dollar Database
This database contains directory information for international companies with sales of one million dollars or more. Obtain pertinent business information on companies throughout the world (except the United States and Canada).

Mediamark Reporter: National Study
The database contains surveys, statistics, consumer data, and polls.

Transitions Online
Transitions Online – a leading Internet magazine covering Central and Eastern Europe, the Balkans, and the former Soviet Union – is the online successor to Transitions magazine.

Black Drama
The Black Drama database contains around 1000 plays by more than 170 playwrights, together with detailed, fielded information on related productions, theaters, production companies, and more. The database also includes selected playbills, production photographs and other ephemera related to the plays.

Anthropology Plus
Anthropology Plus brings together into one resource Anthropological Literature from Harvard University, and Anthropological Index from the Royal Anthropological Institute in the UK. The index offers coverage of all core periodicals in the field as well as local and lesser-known journals, from the late 19th century to the present.

Global Market Information Database (GMID)
This database contains information on key business intelligence on countries, companies, markets and consumers. It is an integrated on-line information system covering over 350 markets and 207 countries.

Q.     "I've been hearing about denial of service attacks a lot lately – what exactly is a denial of service attack, and how does it affect me?"

A.     A Denial of Service (DOS) attack is the result of an attacker using one or several computers' network bandwidth to "flood" the network and virtually grind everything to a halt. A good analogy of bandwidth and network traffic is the width of a freeway and cars, respectively. The freeway is only so wide, and once it fills up with cars, everything slows down. Essentially, a Denial of Service attack is an attacker-induced network traffic jam.

This has happened on campus recently in the form of the SoBig and Nachi worms. When a machine gets infected, the worm tries to infect any other machine it can connect to, and this increased network traffic causes the Denial of Service. You may have even noticed the effects of a DOS attack yourself. Perhaps the most obvious recent result of a DOS attack has been the inability to connect to your Outlook account, or one of the remote library databases. And when a connection is made, the response time is very slow.

What this means to you is that it is important not to open any questionable e-mail attachments, and to contact LIST if you suspect any unusual activity on your computer. Fortunately, Library computers had very few instances of these worms, so everyone is doing a good job and being proactive! Every DOS attack thus far has originated from areas outside the Libraries, which is something we can all be proud of.
