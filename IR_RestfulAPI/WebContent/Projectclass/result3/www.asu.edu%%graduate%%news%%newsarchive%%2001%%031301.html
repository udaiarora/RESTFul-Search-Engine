ARIZONA STATE UNIVERSITY GRADUATE STUDENTS WIN WILSON FELLOWSHIPS

Graduate students in the Department of History at Arizona State University have claimed two of the 16 Woodrow Wilson Postdoctoral Fellowships awarded this year. The ASU fellows are Tracy Leavelle and Douglas Seefeldt, who emerged from a pool of 500 applicants nationally. Both students are former Preparing Future Faculty fellows. Preparing Future Faculty is the ASU Graduate College¹s innovative teacher training program for doctoral students who plan to pursue faculty careers.

Leavelle will conduct his fellowship work at the Louise W. and Edmund J. Kahn Liberal Arts Institute at Smith College in Northampton, Mass., during the next two years. In the first year, he will participate in a Kahn Institute collaborative research project called "Religious Tolerance and Intolerance in Modern and Ancient Worlds." The Lubbock, Texas native will also teach five classes over the two years as a member of the American Studies Program.

Seefeldt, a native of Littleton, Colo., will serve a two-year postdoctoral fellowship at the University of Virginia in the Virginia Center for Digital History (VCDH), beginning this fall. The fellowship will include teaching in the Media Studies Program, research at the VCDH, and will allow time and support to continue his research.

"Virginia is beginning a Lewis and Clark Bicentennial Project in the fall of 2001, and I will help create, plan and develop the Center¹s digital project on Lewis and Clark and the environmental history of the American West," Seefeldt said. "I will also participate in a faculty colloquium that will plan undergraduate curriculum in concert with the campus-wide initiative."

Seefeldt, who earned his bachelor¹s degree in cultural studies at Hampshire College in Amherst, Mass., said, "My goal is to be a productive member of a history department faculty at a liberal arts college after completing my postdoctoral fellowship. I have a strong commitment to research and service, but my teaching goals are what motivate me to be a professor. During the next five years, my goals are to continue developing as a teacher by learning and practicing new pedagogical techniques and to contribute my own scholarship to the historical profession."

Leavelle had previously earned a bachelor¹s degree in anthropology and Native American studies at Dartmouth College. He is planning an academic career to pursue his interests in teaching and research.

"Being a Wilson Fellow will provide a tremendous opportunity to accelerate my academic career," Leavelle said. "I will have ample time to develop an ambitious research program and will gain valuable new teaching experience at an outstanding liberal arts institution."

Seefeldt said, "It is a great personal honor to be a Woodrow Wilson Fellow, and the fact that two of the 16 positions available nationwide went to students from the ASU History Department is a significant achievement. But it is also a comment on both the fine mentoring we have received from our dissertation committees and the quality of the entire history faculty."

Leavelle said he came to the Ph.D. program in history at ASU because of its strength in American Indian history and to study with Professor Peter Iverson and others. "My interests are in American Indian and early American history," he said. "My dissertation, ¨Religion, Encounter, and Community in French and Indian North America,¹ is a study of religious culture in the French-Indian mission communities of the seventeenth- and eighteenth-century Great Lakes region."

"I chose to come to ASU after completing the master¹s program at the University of Oregon for the opportunity to study western history with Professor Peter Iverson and to study environmental history with Professor Stephen Pyne," Seefeldt said.

Seefeldt¹s doctoral studies center on the history of the American west and comparative environmental history. "My research and teaching interests are in the social, cultural and intellectual modes of understanding the past," he said. "I am currently exploring place-making, commemoration and identity, and how pasts are created for present purposes. My dissertation is titled, ¨Negotiating Western Pasts: Place and Public Memory in the Twentieth-Century American West.¹ This project is an examination of the construction of western identity through the negotiation of public memory in four contested sites of memory, one in each of the Four Corners states: the Cuerno Verde rest area and the town of Colorado City, Colo., the Mountain Meadows Massacre monuments in Enterprise, Utah, the copper town of Kearny, Ariz., and Blue Lake near Taos Pueblo in New Mexico."
