November 5, 2003

International space research gets younger

Once again, American and Russian space scientists are working side-by-side in a collaborative research project. The international science team is learning to bridge their cultural differences in order to achieve an important scientific goal. While such efforts have been undertaken in the past, the exhilaration of cross-cultural cooperation makes every attempt an adventure.

But then there's the fact that the scientists are all still in high school....

On November 3 through 5, 23 students and four teachers from Saratoga Springs High School (from Saratoga Springs, NY) and two students and two teachers from Chekov, Russia will visit the Mars Space Flight Facility at Arizona State University. Using distance learning technology, the two groups have been studying science together all fall and collaborating on a science project as part of NASA's Mars Student Imaging Project (MSIP). The group is looking for the existence of water on Mars and will do so by examining the morphology and features found in craters.

MSIP is a NASA-funded science education program that allows elementary, middle and high school classes to do real-life planetary exploration and study using the NASA 2001 Mars Odyssey spacecraft's Thermal Emission Imaging System (THEMIS) visible light camera. Based at ASU, the program has been running since spring 2002 and approximately 75 classes participate in the program each year.

Participating student groups submit brief proposals for specific science issues that they would like to study on Mars. Once the proposal has been accepted, the groups complete a multi-week science curriculum under the guidance of a local teacher, supported by science education specialists at ASU's Mars Education Program. At the end of the program, students participate in Mars research by using THEMIS to take a unique image of Mars relevant to the topic they are studying. The groups either travel to ASU for three-day visits to acquire the image, or they do the work remotely via the internet. The image they acquire is the intellectual property of the students.

The Mars Education Program is an outreach activity of ASU, developed initially in connection with research activities involving the Thermal Emission Spectrometer experiment on NASA's Mars Global Surveyor spacecraft. The program operates the Mars Student Imaging Project with funding from NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) in Pasadena, California. JPL manages the 2001 Mars Odyssey mission for NASA's Office of Space Science in Washington. Principal investigators at Arizona State University in Tempe, the University of Arizona in Tucson and the Johnson Space Center, Houston , operate the science instruments. Additional science investigators are located at the Russian Space Research institute and Los Alamos National Laboratories, New Mexico.

Lockheed Martin Astronautics, Denver, is the prime contractor for the project, and developed and built the orbiter. Mission operations are conducted jointly from Lockheed Martin and from JPL, a division of the California Institute of Technology in Pasadena.

Information about MSIP can be found on the internet at <http://msip.asu.edu> . Additional information about the 2001 Mars Odyssey is available on the Internet at: <http://mars.jpl.nasa.gov/odyssey/>
