Students help design Tempe boathouse

When the new boathouse is built at Tempe Town Lakes in a couple of years, some current Arizona State University architecture students may see a piece of themselves in it.

Or, at least, their design ideas.

Sixty ASU architecture students, both graduate and undergraduate, have been putting their creative powers to work to come up with designs for the boathouse, which will accommodate the needs of community and the growing number of rowers on the lake.

The body of work will be like a "library of ideas" that Tempe city officials can browse through before going out to look for professional architectural services, said Max Underwood, professor of architecture. "The student work will inform everyone of what the potential of the project is."

Who would have thought, for example, to design three vertical buildings where the boats can be on full display?

Or to make it a place that would be attractive for weddings? Or to include a workout room for the rower-athletes?

The students' designs are as varied as they are. Proposed building materials range from concrete to aluminum, and suggested design features include a lighthouse-like structure and a building with a dramatic façade like that of the Sydney Opera House.

Each design, however, ties together the boathouse and recreation area with open space, gardens, terraces and places for sitting by the lake and watching boat races.

"It will be like Tempe's 'living room,'" Underwood said.

Several of the students actually took rowing classes to better understand the sport of rowing, its rituals, and the mechanics of housing rowing shells.

They discovered that the north shore site poses some challenges for the long boats. There isn't much room to maneuver while exiting and entering the boathouse, so every inch of the lakefront has to be utilized.

John Meunier, who is teaching an undergraduate class that is participating in the boathouse project, said the boathouse is "something you'd like for people to enjoy, something that gives pleasure. The boats will make it special."

Meunier, former dean of the ASU College of Architecture and Environmental Design, said the idea of having students work on the boathouse is beneficial for everyone.

"When students are asked to address a real problem you have many varieties on the theme - a very rich source of ideas for the community.

"The university should throw out of a lot of ideas so the community can argue with them, or perhaps, adopt them."

Also teaching the students are Scott Murff and Meredith Banasiak.
