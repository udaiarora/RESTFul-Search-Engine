September 18, 2003

HBLI hosts forum on education issues

ASU's Hispanic Border Leadership Institute (HBLI) is challenging people's way of thinking about the educational system and its affect on the Latino community.

HBLI is hosting "Latino Education in the Southwest All One System," on Sept. 19, in the ASU Memorial Union. The one-day event will focus on solutions to educational problems faced by the Latino communities in the Southwest.

HBLI Executive Director Leonard Valverde says the forum, free and open to the public, is an expansion of the "Platica Speakers," series that HBLI introduced seven years ago. He says a large group of education experts will share their viewpoints and provide plenty of beneficial information to students, faculty members and administrators.

"As history shows, the education of students of color in the United States has been inequitable and therefore inferior," says Valverde, who is also a professor of Educational Leadership & Policy Studies at ASU. "Since the passage of Congressional legislation under the Great Society banner of President Lyndon B. Johnson, education of students of color has improved unevenly, and over these 40 years, some regression has taken place. The all-day forum will focus on lessons learned about what works, successful programs, not the usual 'let's discuss the problems.'"

Presenters will include Jorge Chapa, director of the Hispanic Research Institute at Indiana University. Chapa will be speaking on "The Future of Latinos and the Implications for Education Systems as Revealed in the Latest Demographics," from 8 to 9:30 a.m.

"The top three challenges the U.S. educational system faces are educating children who are not fluent in English, educating Latinos whether or not they are fluent in English and finding the money to pay for their vital services," Chapa says. "I hope audience members will become aware of how Latino demographic and educational trends present a challenge that we all must address in order to ensure the social, political and economic future of the United States."

Other featured speakers throughout the one-day event include: Shernaz Garcia, associate professor and graduate adviser with the University of Texas at Austin; Baltazar Acevedo, professor with the University of Texas at Brownsville; Augustine Gallego, chancellor of the San Diego Community College District; Roberto Haro, professor with San Francisco State University; and Eugene Garcia, dean of the College of Education at ASU.

Frank Lopez, executive director of the U.S. Hispanic Chamber of Commerce (USHCC) Foundation will be the keynote speaker during a special reception and dinner at 6 p.m., Sept. 19. This event, by invitation only, will feature Lopez's presentation, "How We Can Support and Improve the Public Education of Latino Youth." The USHCC Foundation is committed to giving Latino youth alternatives for life preparation and life-long learning by developing and implementing initiatives to awaken and nurture the entrepreneurship spirit of Latino youth.

Valverde says the event will serve as an eye-opening experience to people who attend.

"We want to inform people that Latinos have many assets that the educational system is not taking advantage of," Valverde says. "That Latinos are as capable and talented as any other student group, that they are just as eager to learn when they start school as any other student."

For information about the Hispanic Border Leadership Institute, visit the Web at (http://www.asu.edu/educ/hbli/).
