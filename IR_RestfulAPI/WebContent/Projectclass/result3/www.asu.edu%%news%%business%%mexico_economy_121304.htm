December 13, 2004

Mexico economy picks up pace in 2004

The expansion of U.S. industrial production this year has helped boost Mexico’s economy, which is on a path of growth following three years of stagnation, according to the latest issue of México Consenso Pronósticos Económicos.

“Over the last few months there has been a vigorous recovery in investment,” according to Mario Correa, an economist with Grupo Financiero INVERLAT. “This, along with healthy growth rates in private consumption, makes it clear that the internal engines of the economy are back on line. This will produce an impetus that will last at least through the first part of 2005, producing a GDP growth rate above 4 percent.”

Correa predicts that growth will continue through 2005, despite political tensions heightening in advance of the 2006 presidential election. “ Several factors will affect the Mexican economy in the coming year. On the external front, the pace of growth of the U.S. economy will be of primary importance. Although it is likely that the U.S. economy will keep growing, the huge trade and budget deficits represent a serious threat; an eventual adjustment seems unavoidable. Part of this adjustment will take the form of higher interest rates, which will put some pressure on the conditions faced by emerging countries in the international markets.

“On the domestic front, it is expected that economic policy will continue to be used responsibly, thus assuring macroeconomic stability and allowing growth to continue, despite the pressures expected from the political side.”

The México Consenso de Pronósticos Económicos is published quarterly by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute in the W. P. Carey School of Business at Arizona State University, and may be accessed online at www.wpcarey.asu.edu/seidman/eoc/mexico/. Paper copies are available upon request. Call (480) 965-5543 for more information.
