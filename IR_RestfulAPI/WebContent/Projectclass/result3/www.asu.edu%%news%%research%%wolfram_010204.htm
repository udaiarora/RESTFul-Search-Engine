Scientific innovator, Stephen Wolfram will speak at ASU

Stephen Wolfram, a one-time science prodigy, an innovator in computing and software technology and a pioneer in the emerging field of complexity science, will speak at 3:30 p.m., Thursday, Jan. 8, in Murdock Hall room 101 of at Arizona State University.

Wolfram will describe the ideas and discoveries contained his book, "A New Kind of Science," and their implications for various fields of science. An extended question and answer period will be included. The talk is free and open to the public.

Wolfram is a well-known scientist and creator of Mathematica, a widely used, high-level mathematics program that allows computers to work through scientific problems. He is the CEO of Wolfram Research Inc., Champaign, Ill.

Wolfram has garnered a reputation in science circles as a genius and as a sort of counter-culture icon. He published his first scientific paper at the age of 15 and earned a Ph.D. from the California Institute of Technology, Pasadena, when he was 20. In 1981, he was the youngest recipient of a MacArthur Prize Fellowship.

"A New Kind of Science" was published in 2002. It describes a new approach to science that Wolfram developed over a 20-year period. Basic to this approach is the idea of studying not traditional mathematical equations but instead rules of the type embodied in the simplest computer programs. A key discovery is that such rules can lead to behavior that shows immense complexity and mirrors many features seen in nature.

Wolfram built on this to tackle an array of fundamental problems in science, from the origins of apparent randomness in physical systems, to the development of complexity in biology, the ultimate scope and limitations of mathematics, the possibility of a truly fundamental theory of physics, the interplay between free will and determinism and the character of intelligence of the universe.

Wolfram is now developing a series of research and educational initiatives based on his scientific ideas.
