Task Force on Academic Professionals – PROGRESS REPORT


Printer Friendly Version (Microsoft Word)

 

Preamble

The Task Force on Academic Professionals was appointed by the Office of the Provost in September 2003 to address issues surrounding the status and role of Academic Professionals (APs) at Arizona State University (ASU). The AP Task Force was comprised of individuals from various AP categories and faculty from ASU East, Main and West (see Task Force Roster at www.asu.edu/provost/ap/). The Task Force received its charge from the Office of the Provost (see Charge at www.asu.edu/provost/ap/).

Specifically, the committee was to:

1) clarify the distinctions between continuing status and tenure and make recommendations about the appointment options for academic professionals;

2) delineate the range of performance expectations for academic professionals and clarify the criteria for appointments to continuing status and probationary versus year-to-year tracks;

3) recommend specific ways in which ASU could improve its policies and procedures governing review for promotion and continuing status;

4) recommend ways in which ASU could create an all-university Promotion and Continuing Status review process; and,

5) make other recommendations about academic professional status.


Introduction

Academic professionals at Arizona State University typically hold an advanced degree and have acquired a high level of expertise. They actively engage in activities that significantly impact the institution’s tripartite mission: education; the creation and dissemination of knowledge; and external embeddedness.

Academic professionals support the mission of the University through the application of their specialized training, skill, and knowledge. Through professional contributions that expand the knowledge base in their disciplines, APs enhance the reputation of the University. Whereas some academic professionals engage in classroom teaching, generally the position descriptions of academic professionals emphasize other responsibilities. The range of position categories for academic professionals has proliferated over time such that a simple definition is difficult to pinpoint. For example, some of the diverse position titles include: Librarian, Administrative Professional, Research Professional, Fine Arts Specialist, Museum Professional, Archivist, Academic Associate, Research Scientist, and Research Engineer. (See Appendix D for a complete list of AP titles) Additionally, academic professionals increase the university’s functionality by fulfilling myriad service roles within the university, the community, and professional organizations.

Because the academic professional employment category is so diverse, over time its clarity has become convoluted. On one hand, it has provided hiring flexibility that has helped move ASU closer to its goals in some areas. On the other hand, due to the broad range of positions aggregated into the category, the category has lost focus and is not broadly understood. Historically, provisions of the category have been misunderstood. In some cases, management decisions may not have been well informed; and in other cases, benign neglect has proliferated misuse of the employment category. One goal of this Task Force is to provide information that clarifies understanding of the AP category. Another goal is to make strong recommendations regarding appointment and evaluation options so that the mission of the University is better served through its APs.


Guiding Principles for Determining Academic Professional Status and Rank

The Task Force articulated and was guided by the following principles:

  1. That ASU should recruit and retain the best qualified individual for the position.
  2. That ASU should recognize that continuity and stability are important features of conducting institutional research, instructional programs, and institutional mission.
  3. That ASU should strive to stay competitive with peer institutions and the business community and respond appropriately to market forces.
  4. That ASU should anticipate the lifecycle of a project/program, and identify expertise required during the lifecycle.
  5. That ASU’s tripartite mission should guide the recruitment of academic professionals, just as it does for faculty ranks.
  6. That ASU should offer options that the best academic professional candidates view as attractive for their career progression.

Charge #1: Clarify the distinctions between continuing status and tenure, conduct research on employment options for individuals with positions similar to our AP positions at peer institutions, and make recommendations about the appointment options for AP’s at ASU (e.g., do we need to create the option for multiple year appointments; do we have a strong rationale for appointments with continuing status?).

Distinguishing Between Continuing Status and Tenure

The Arizona Board of Regents (“ABOR”) is the governing body for the three state universities in Arizona. ABOR policy defines tenure as an employment status awarded to faculty members by the president on the basis of demonstrated excellence in teaching, research and service. Once received, tenure creates a legitimate claim of entitlement to continued employment, unless the tenured faculty member resigns, retires or is subject to release or dismissal by the university. ABOR policy defines continuing status as an employment status that, once awarded to academic professionals by the president, creates an expectation that the president will renew employment for successive appointment periods, unless the academic professional retires, resigns, or is subject to release or dismissal by the university.
Arizona Board of Regents policy 6-201, Conditions of Faculty Service;
Arizona Board of Regents policy 6-301, Conditions of Professional Service

ABOR’s distinctions between the definitions are:
1) A stated standard of excellence in the criteria of teaching, research and service is required for tenure; a standard of excellence is required for continuing status as well, although the criteria and performance categories are not stated in ABOR policy for continuing status. However, university policies require academic professionals to meet the standard of excellence in the categories of performance, professional development/contribution and service to receive continuing status. The continuing status review must be based on unit-developed criteria and proceed through the same multi-level peer-review process that is akin to the tenure review process.

2) Tenure creates an entitlement or legal, contractual right to continued employment for faculty, absent the stated bases for severance. Continuing status creates an expectation of renewal for successive periods, which is not equivalent to an entitlement and is not synonymous with permanent employment. ABOR policy provides the same rights and requires the same due process procedures for tenured faculty and continuing status academic professionals prior to termination for cause or release for reorganization. Although by definition, tenure creates a stronger right, once tenure or continuing status is awarded, the deprivation of either status requires similar treatment, protections, cause, and process.

Generally, courts have addressed tenure as the means to assure academic freedom, which is the freedom needed by the institution to pursue its educational goals without undue intervention by government and the freedom required by faculty to pursue their research goals and teaching activities without undue interference or censorship by the institution. The American Association of University Professors (“AAUP”) discusses tenure as a means to two ends, to assure the faculty member’s freedom in teaching, research and extracurricular activities and to provide sufficient economic security to make the professions attractive to competent individuals. ABOR’s academic freedom policy applies to faculty and academic professionals who teach or conduct research. ASU’s academic freedom policy applies to faculty and academic professionals.

The Task Force supports continuing status for academic professionals and recognizes the need for consistent standards, criteria and review process with appointment options, including year-to-year, multiple-year and continuing status contracts.


Peer Institution Research on Position Classifications Similar to ASU Academic Professional Positions

A sub-group of the Academic Professional Task Force conducted a series of informal surveys of ASU peer institutions to determine the employment status of individuals with job responsibilities similar to those performed by ASU academic professionals. The surveys were conducted by telephone interview, e-mail, and visits to peer institution web-sites.

The surveyed ASU academic professional job titles fall into one of four general categories: Information & Materials Management, Research, Pedagogy, and Support. Due to a wide variation in position nomenclature and distribution of responsibilities among ASU peer institutions, it was difficult to identify one-to-one job title equivalencies. However, in the majority of cases, our peer institutions provide a category of academic appointment in addition to faculty and classified staff.

Peer Data: Information & Materials Management Positions

Within the Information & Materials Management category, Librarians comprise the largest single group of academic professionals. Librarians hold faculty status (with eligibility for tenure) among ten of ASU’s fifteen peer institutions, while librarians at the remaining five peer institutions hold an academic appointment similar to ASU’s academic professionals. Academic status at these peer institutions often includes many of the same rights and privileges as experienced by ASU academic professionals, including the equivalent of continuing appointment, eligibility for sabbatical leave, academic senate representation, and the protections of intellectual and academic freedom policies. (See Appendix A for Library peer data and Appendix B for peer information on Museum Professionals)

Peer Data: Research Positions

The Research category includes Research Scientist, Research Engineer, Research Administrator, Research Professional and Research Technologist. Most of the Research Scientist/Engineer positions fall under the categories of professional or academic staff and have year-to-year, multi-year or continuing appointments. Many institutions did not have Research Scientist/Engineer equivalent positions. Most of the Research Professional positions fall under the categories of professional or classified staff and have year-to-year, multi-year or continuing appointments. Most of the Research Technologist positions fall under the categories of professional or classified staff and have year-to-year, multi-year or continuing appointments. The data on the Research Administrator position is very weak because most of the people contacted at peer institutions were uncertain about identifying similar positions in their institution.

It is clear from this survey that the functions performed by research based academic professionals can be achieved with many models. However, the difficulty in comparing jobs across peer institutions makes it difficult to obtain a high quality comprehensive data set. Consequently we are not able to determine which model may be the most effective for research based APs. (See Appendix C)

Peer Data: Pedagogy Positions

APs in this category include Administrative Professionals, Clinical Professionals, Fine Arts Specialists, and Instructional Professionals. While the number of positions ranges across university units, most are in the College of Law. To assess the status of instructional and clinical professionals, the existing data on clinical programs in United States law schools was evaluated and an informal inquiry among some of the country’s top legal clinical educators was conducted. Unfortunately, the national data on legal clinical educators is sketchy and inconclusive. However, anecdotal data, including accounts from experienced, nationally-respected clinical educators indicates that schools offering tenure track status and the faculty culture engendered by a unified system have an extraordinary hiring advantage over schools with non-tenure track positions. The survey data strongly suggest that in order to attract and retain excellent candidates for open positions, ASU must preserve the employment stability offered to APs in pedagogical positions. (See Appendix D for a list of position titles at ASU within the Academic Professional category, including Pedagogy)

Peer Data: Academic Support Positions

Seven ASU peer institutions were surveyed. At all seven peer institutions, academic advisors are either faculty or another form of academic appointment. In addition to year-to-year appointments, three of the peer institutions offer an optional "career status" for non-faculty academic/professional appointments. Faculty positions offer tenure-track appointments, academic senate representation, sabbatical leave, and the protections of academic freedom. (See Appendix E)

Task Force Recommendations for AP Employment Options

The Task Force recommends maintaining the options for probationary/continuing status and year-to-year appointments. In addition, we recommend establishing an additional option for academic professional appointments beyond continuing status and year-to-year. We recommend a multiple year appointment option, with the additional option of a rolling appointment period. We think having these three options will allow greater flexibility in hiring the caliber of expertise needed over time. The improved stability of employment offered by a multi-year appointment provides an incentive commensurate with the level of expertise and enhances our ability to compete in the marketplace. The task force developed a matrix of guidelines to be considered when hiring an AP on a continuing status track, a multiple-year track, and a year-to-year track. (See Appendix F)


Charge #2: Delineate the range of performance expectations for Academic Professionals, and clarify the criteria for appointments to continuing status and probationary versus year-to-year tracks.

The Task Force has delineated the criteria for establishing probationary/continuing status, multiple year, and year-to-year academic professional appointments below. In all cases it is critical that the department/unit fully articulate the type of academic work to be done; the criteria for appointment; the written position description and performance expectations; the evaluation and review process; and, the salary range. It is also recognized that updating of the position description is likely to occur over time, reflecting evolution in unit goals and priorities. (See Appendix F)

Criteria for Making Academic Professional Appointments

Probationary/Continuing Status Appointments

Probationary/continuing status appointments are generally made for the following reasons:

  1. the need for high level or unique, critical expertise, along with the need for long-term institutional commitment to ensure continuity; and
  2. prevailing market forces require the kind of sustained stability of employment continuing status offers; and
  3. an expectation exists that the academic professional will participate, long-term, in advancing the mission of the university and/or discipline through his/her academic expertise, with position effectiveness, professional contributions and institutional and professional service.

The department/unit guidelines articulate individual and unit standards for continuing status review, as well as the post-continuing status review process. Excellence in position effectiveness, professional contributions, and service are expectations and are benchmarks by which the quality of contributions for academic professionals who seek or hold continuing status or who hold multi-year contracts can be measured. There is an obligation for a continuing source of funding when hiring an AP into a probationary/continuing status position. There is no term limit for an employee with continuing status.

Multiple Year Appointments with the Possibility of Rolling Appointment Periods

Multiple-year appointments are generally made for one or more of the following reasons:

  1. to provide appropriate expertise and unit support that is more than one year in duration; or
  2. to provide appropriate expertise for a grant/contract funded project; or
  3. to provide department/unit with flexibility within market forces; or
  4. to enhance national recruitment efforts.

Year-to-Year Appointments

Year-to-year appointments generally are made for one or more of the following reasons:

  1. to address a short-term academic need; or
  2. to assume responsibility for a limited activity; or
  3. to provide department/unit flexibility.

Charge #3: Identify, evaluate, and recommend specific ways in which ASU can improve its policies and procedures governing review for promotion and continuing status; include suggestions for better documentation of P&CS cases. Work with CAPS on developing ACD policy language to operationalize recommendations.

At each level of the administrative structure, e.g., unit; college, school, or library; and university, a minimum of two independent reviews are conducted: the first by a peer committee and the second by an administrator. Guidelines at each administrative level articulate the make-up of the peer review committee, with the following stipulations:

  • Peer review committees must consist of at least three members.
  • Below the university level evaluation, all members of peer review committees and all administrative reviewers must be from within ASU.
  • Guidelines and procedures for establishing peer review committees must be developed and approved by appropriate governance processes within each unit.

Ideally, peer review committees consist of academic professionals, on continuing appointment, from within the same administrative structure. In those units with few academic professionals, however, unit guidelines may allow for greater flexibility in the configuration of peer review committees. Such units may allow peer review committees to include individuals from other units, or from other employment categories.

Review Process Standards for APs Seeking to Achieve Continuing Status

The Task Force recommends that all departments/units be required to establish a documented process for AP probationary and continuing appointment reviews. These documents should be reviewed by the appropriate college on a regular basis. We recommend that there be broad dissemination to all university departments/units regarding the documentation required for an AP position. Departments/units should be aware of the documentation requirements prior to hiring an AP. When making multi-year appointments, the criteria for renewing (rolling) must be pre-determined. The criteria for performance and review/continuance must be shared and made clear to any category of AP hired, at the time of the hire. Standards by which an academic professional is evaluated for continuing status include position effectiveness, professional contributions, and service.

Position Effectiveness

Each academic professional will have position expectations dependent upon the unit’s mission, stature, and goals. Excellence is achieved in the context of the department/unit in which the academic professional works. Standards for performance include:

  1. Effective and continuous accomplishments which relate to the missions of the department/unit and university;
  2. Effective accommodation of change in the position or environment in which the individual has worked during the period under review;
  3. Contributions to organizational goals and objectives;
  4. Innovation and creativity in professional practices;
  5. Continuing growth in one’s position and professional expertise;
  6. Application of professional standards and practices; and
  7. Knowledge and application of professional standards, guidelines and protocols.

Each academic professional’s expected contribution to the mission and goals of the department/unit and university is specifically defined in the individual position description. An academic professional is effective in her/his position when s/he sustains and enhances these mission and goals.

Professional Contributions

Professional contributions of an academic professional are those activities associated with the dissemination of the results of his/her creative activity and professional expertise. Such activities include publication in appropriate journals; presentations at scholarly meetings; efforts to transfer techniques and practices resulting from creative activity at ASU to other institutions; organization of conferences or other scholarly meetings; providing consultations and evaluations drawing on the person’s expertise; and research, teaching, leadership, management or administration roles within the individual’s specialty beyond those stated in the position description for the individual.

The individual’s role in these activities is important in evaluating these contributions. The individual’s contributions should be more than simple participation and more than a collection of random efforts. The contributions should reflect an active, directed pursuit of goals that impact and advance the practice and scholarship with the individual’s field.

Institutional and Professional Service

Academic professionals with continuing or probationary appointments are to actively
serve the institution, the profession, and the community. Service of academic professionals is characterized by the application of subject knowledge and professional expertise beyond the scope of assigned responsibilities.

Service includes such activities as unit search committees, roles in shared governance, contributions to campus committees, and/or similar activities within professional organizations. Service may also include community activities that draw upon professional expertise or subject knowledge and/or advance the University by meeting the needs of the greater public. Unit guidelines outline the elements contained within each of these three criteria (in units with only one or a few AP’s, these may be incorporated into the position description). Guidelines include indicators for evaluating performance.

Review Letters

The purpose of letters of review is to contextualize the APs professional contributions, both within the university and within the professional discipline. Letters both internal and external to ASU should be solicited; the percentage of external letters is proportionate to the professional contributions area of the AP’s position description. Unit guidelines determine this proportionality; however, at a minimum, three letters must be external to ASU.

Academic units, in consultation with the college, have primary responsibility to procure letters from external experts to evaluate the candidate’s accomplishments. The unit should procure no fewer than four and no more than ten letters for use in all levels of each promotion and continuing status review. Relevant information about the candidate the unit, the criteria for promotion and continuing status, and the continuing status procedures should be sent to outside experts for use in their reviews. For the internal process, the unit should carefully explain and document each reviewer’s expertise and relevance for the case. Candidates will be asked to suggest reviewers, but the administration of the unit has the ultimate responsibility for identifying a selection of strong reviewers. In consultation with the unit, the dean of the candidate’s college/ approves the choice of outside reviewers. In instances when a candidate believes that there may be conflicts of interest with or unreasonable biases by certain persons, the candidate may provide a written list of people s/he believes should be excluded from consideration for review letters.

The procedures articulated in ASU’s ACD Manual regarding the review process for probationary/continuing status appointed APs seeking promotion should continue to be followed.


Charge #4: Review and recommend ways in which ASU can create an all-university Promotion and Continuing Status review process. These recommendations should include attention to differences between the current ACD manuals (i.e., ACD and ACDW) and how these might be brought into alignment.

We recommend establishing an All-University Promotion and Continuing Status Review Committee. Individuals appointed to this committee will serve as voting members on all cases from all ASU campuses for the duration of their appointment. The Office of the Vice Provost, upon recommendation by deans/university administrators will appoint members for staggered three-year terms of appointment. Committee membership shall consist of at least one representative from each of the ASU campuses. The committee shall consist of an odd number of members. Additionally, for each case being considered, a unit representative, who is a non-voting member, will put forward the case. This representative presents the case, summarizes the candidate’s file and addresses committee members’ questions about the case, articulating unit criteria and standards, as necessary. The unit representative will be selected by consensus of the AP candidate, department/unit chair/head, and dean/unit administrator, as appropriate.


Charge #5: Make other recommendations, as necessary, about Academic Professional status.

  1. The Task Force recommends that a post continuing status review process or its equivalent be established. A committee should be appointed to develop and define an appropriate process.
  2. If the recommendations for a multi-year appointment, as described in this report, are accepted, the AP Task Force recommends that a committee be appointed to develop criteria by which multi-year appointed employees may achieve promotion.
  3. In order to operationalize Task Force recommendations that gain approval, CAPS (with University-wide representation) and other appropriate ad hoc committees should develop ACD policy language.

** Charge #5 remains under development during March 2004
