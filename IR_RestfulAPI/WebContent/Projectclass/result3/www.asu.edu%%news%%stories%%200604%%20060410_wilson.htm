April 10, 2006

Pulitzer Prize-winner outlines 'Future of Life'

Edward O. Wilson, a Harvard University research professor emeritus, will share his particular vision of "The Future of Life," at 7 p.m., April 13, at the Galvin Playhouse in ASU's Nelson Fine Arts Center on the Tempe campus.

Wilson, Dubbed "Darwin's natural heir" by the Guardian newspaper in 2001, also will speak on "Eusociality: Origins and Consequences" at 11 a.m., April 14, in the Memorial Union's Ventana Room. Additionally, he will participate in an open house for the new Center for Social Dynamics and Complexity at 2 p.m., April 14, in the Interdisciplinary Science and Technology Building (ISTB 1).

In addition to receiving two Pulitzer Prizes, one that he shares with ASU life sciences professor Bert Hölldobler for "The Ants," Wilson holds the distinction of being named one of Time magazine's most influential Americans in 1996.

Wilson's ideas and theoretical concepts, not without controversy, have profoundly influenced generations of young scientists and "ignited debate," Hölldobler says.

"Edward O. Wilson began as an entomological systematist and became one of our greatest representatives of the scholarly synthesis in science," he says.

He also credits Wilson's writings with revolutionizing public awareness of "the complex and fragile texture of nature."

"Research during the past several decades has revealed global biodiversity to be far richer than previously imagined," Wilson says. "When small invertebrates and bacteria are included, probably more than 90 percent of the species on Earth remain undiscovered. At the same time, ecosystems - and the species they contain - are disappearing at an accelerating rate due to human activity."

According to Wilson, there are affordable ways of slowing - and even halting - the decline of biodiversity. To do nothing, Wilson believes, would cost as many as half of the earth's life forms in this century; irrecoverable loss through the combined assaults of habitat destruction, invasive species, population, pollution and overharvesting. Doing nothing "will result in an irreparable loss in wealth, environmental security and spirit for the generations to come," he says.

Wilson's road map for "the future of life" provides cogent solutions combining grassroots efforts with economic enrichment of those developing countries that harbor hot spots. It seems like a daunting task to chart. But in a recent "ABC News" interview, Wilson stresses the common denominator: man's deep affiliation with nature.

"It doesn't matter whether you believe Darwin got it right or that the Genesis story is literally true," Wilson says. "We can all agree that, however it got here, the living creation on which we all depend for our existence is something we don't want to see destroyed."

Time magazine notes that Wilson is fond of saying: "The loss of biodiversity is the folly our descendants are least likely to forgive us."

Wilson 's lecture, which is free and open to the public, is presented by the School of Life Sciences in the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences, with support from the School of Human Evolution & Social Change and the Wrigley Lecture Series of ASU's Global Institute of Sustainability.
