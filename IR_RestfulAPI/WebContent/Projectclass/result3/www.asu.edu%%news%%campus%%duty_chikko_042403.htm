April 24, 2003

ASU employee looks back on life in Iraq; grateful to U.S. troops

Like many of her fellow Americans, Madeline Chikko has been watching a lot of news lately. But for her, the scenes from Iraq don't represent some unfamiliar, foreign place.

To Chikko, business manager for the Advanced Public Executive Program at ASU's Downtown Center, Iraq is her birthplace and childhood home.

She and her family fled Kirkuk, Iraq, 27 years ago to escape the brutal reign of Saddam Hussein. Chikko's family members, who are Assyrian Christians, immigrated to Chicago, where she lived until moving to Phoenix three years ago.

She says she will never forget April 9th when coalition troops took over downtown Baghdad. At 4 a.m. that morning she received an enthusiastic phone call from her brother in Chicago.

"'It's over!'" he shouted breathlessly,'" Chikko recalls. "'It's the beginning of the end of Saddam's brutal regime! Madeline, wake up, it's here; the day is finally here!'"

"'I know,' I told him. 'I've been up all night watching CNN.'"

After talking with her brother, Chikko says she turned her attention back to the scene unfolding on television.

"I was awake, but I felt like I was dreaming," she says. "I was watching overjoyed Iraqis' euphorically dancing in the streets of Baghdad. It was amazing!"

As she watched Saddam's statue being pulled down by U.S. Marines, Chikko says she felt conflicting emotions. The scene was spectacular, but Chikko would have preferred to see the Iraqis topple the statue on their own - even if it had taken hours.

"I believe, even if emblematic, that toppling the statue with their own hands would have given them - and all the rest of the Iraqi people - a sense of pride, the feeling of being part of the revolution and the overthrow of Saddam," she says. "But, even in this moment of symbolic victory, they were powerless. They couldn't even bring the statue down by themselves."

Chikko says her mother is thrilled and has already started talking about making a trip back to see family in Kirkuk, the town where Chikko says she and her family suffered countless basic human rights violations.

"Many times in the middle of the night they would raid my home and go through our personal belongings, tearing framed pictures off of walls, slashing mattresses and pillows looking for evidence to prosecute my father and brothers," she says.

Leaving everything and fleeing Kirkuk was an agonizing decision for Chikko's father to make.

"For fear for his life and ours, he decided we should escape the country and leave behind our relatives, friends, possessions and belongings," she says.

Chikko says it is because she knows firsthand how vicious Hussein's regime has been, that she is so grateful to the U.S. troops now liberating the people of Iraq.

"To those with sons or daughters, sisters or brothers serving, I would like to say thank you," she says. "And, to assure them their loved ones are not only protecting our freedom here, but they are laying the foundation for a peaceful, secure and a free Iraq."

Chikko says her hope for the Iraqi people is that they will soon experience what it feels like to live in the same kind of democracy she and her family enjoy living here in America.

"It is only fair that they know this freedom, since they paid in full for it with their blood, tears and honor, and more," she says. "I want the people of Iraq to live in peace, for all the people of Iraq to find a peaceful coexistence that will allow them to rebuild and prosper."
