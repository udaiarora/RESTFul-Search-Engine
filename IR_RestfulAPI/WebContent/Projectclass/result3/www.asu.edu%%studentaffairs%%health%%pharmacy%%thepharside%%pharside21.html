A.S.U. PHARMACY NEWSLETTER                            ISSUE TWENTY-ONE

Legumes Forever!

A recent study reported in the Archives of Internal Medicine examined the role of legumes (e.g. peas, beans, peanuts) in reducing the risk of coronary heart disease (CHD).  With a cohort of 9,632 men and women, studied over an average of 19 years, the study supported what Mom knew all along.  Results showed that patients that ate legumes at least four times per week had a 22% lower risk of CHD and an 11% lower risk of cardiovascular disease (CVD).  So, EAT YOUR PEAS!   CKL

 

New Drug for Genital Herpes?

Herpes Simplex Virus type 2 (HSV-2), which is commonly associated with genital herpes, affects approximately 47 million Americans.  About 5 to 80 percent of American adults have oral herpes, usually caused by HSV type 1 (HSV-1).  The new herpes therapy could benefit patients with acyclovir-resistant herpes strains.  The compound is called BILS 179 BS, can be given orally, and is said to be more potent than acyclovir against skin and vaginal lesions in mice with HSV-1 and HSV-2, with no associated toxicities.  However, the compound has not been tested for human safety, so toxicities have not been determined.   CY


ADHD guidelines and blinded trials


The Academy of Pediatrics has issued its recommendations for treating attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) in school age children.  Many of the guidelines concern stimulant medications such as Ritalin (methylphenidate) or Adderall (dextroamphetamines).  Among other items, the authors point out that stimulant dosages are usually not weight-dependent and it is important to find the best dose for a particular individual based on target outcomes.  Some youngsters may only need symptom relief on school days and need not be medicated on the weekend.  If one medication is ineffective, even at its highest dose, the clinician should recommend another stimulant.  The Academy indicates that at least 80% of children with ADHD will respond to one of the stimulants indicated for this condition.

Interpretation of the outcomes of stimulant treatment can often be tainted by the subjective reports of parents, teachers and other caregivers.  For this reason, many clinicians are ordering the first few weeks of stimulant therapy as a “blinded” prescription.  Working with a collaborating pharmacist, the clinician actually orders three different treatment dosages.  For example, Ritalin may be prescribed as follows: 5mg morning and noon, 10mg morning and noon, placebo morning and noon.  The pharmacy customarily provides six vials of medication, two sets of three, one set for home one for school.  Each vial is labeled either A, B or C and has sufficient doses for one week.  Only the pharmacy knows which pair of vials contains which dosage.  A diary is kept with input from various observers and at the end of three weeks this information is shared with the prescriber.  The pharmacy is then called and the dosages are revealed to the clinician.  This approach is being used more frequently for a variety of stimulants and is being considered for other medications as well.  It’s a clever way to minimize bias in evaluating therapeutic outcomes and an excellent opportunity for a collaboration between prescriber and pharmacist.     CKL

 

POISON CONTROL


The American Association of Poison Control Centers (AAPCC) has established a nationwide toll-free telephone number for the nation’s poison control centers.  To be connected to the center nearest you, simply call 1-800-222-1222.

Local numbers will continue to work for any of the 130 centers in the country.  Poison control centers operate 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

ALTERNATIVES TO GELATIN CAPSULES

Currently, all prescription medications in capsule form contain gelatin. Gelatin is a protein made through the hydrolysis of collagen derived from the skin, connective tissue and bones of animals.  It is most commonly obtained from beef and pork products. This presents a problem for those who do not eat anything containing certain animal products due to cultural, religious or vegetarian diet requirements. Individuals who request preservative free products or may have an allergy to soy, wheat and/or dairy products, are also presented with a problem since gelatin capsules usually contain at least one or more of these components.  The two alternatives available are tablets and vegetable capsules. Tablets may not be an option for those with allergies, requests for preservative free items or if the medication only comes in capsule form.  Vegetable capsules are derived from hydroxypropyl methyl cellulose, a widely used ingredient in pharmaceuticals.  They contain no animal by-products and many are free of preservatives, soy, wheat and dairy products.  Any medication that can be obtained in powder form by a compounding pharmacy, can be put into vegetable capsules.  People with requests concerning these issues can be referred to a compounding pharmacy for assistance.   RS

 

NEW PRESCRIBING RULES FOR ACCUTANE

Effective April 10, 2002, every prescription for Accutane (isotretinoin) must have a yellow qualification sticker in order to be dispensed.  This is part of the new SMART (System to Manage Accutane-Related Teratogenicity) program developed by Roche. Physicians prescribing Accutane must be familiar with Roche’s “Guide to Best Practices” and sign a “letter of understanding”.  This certifies to Roche their understanding of the pregnancy prevention rules which include an initial two negative pregnancy tests (serum or urine) and the use of two forms of contraception for those that are sexually active.  Physicians are then considered registered prescribers and are sent the Accutane Qualification Stickers.  Prescribers must also

administer monthly pregnancy tests during the course of treatment, the prescription can be no more than a 30 day supply at a time, the prescription must be written within the previous 7 days, no telephone or computerized prescriptions can be accepted, and an Accutane medication guide must be given to the patient each time it is dispensed.        RS

 

Grapefruit Juice: To Drink, Or Not to Drink, That Is the Question!

In recent news articles, it has been stressed that patients taking the medications listed below should eliminate grapefruit juice from their diets.  Patients have questioned the validity of this claim, and should be educated on whether to avoid grapefruit juice altogether, or to avoid ingestion within hours of the dose.

  

aslovastatin (Mevacor)

atorvastatin (Lipitor)

simvastatin (Zocor)

cerivistatin (Baycol)*

alprazolam

cisapride

cyclosporine

felodipine

midazolam

nisoldipine

triazolam

saquinavir

Grapefruit juice decreases the metabolism of these drugs.  One glass of grapefruit juice is enough to cause an interaction, and following regular ingestion the effect can last for up to 3 days following the last glass.  Since most patients take medications on a regular basis for diseases, these metabolic alterations could be risky, the safest approach would be to avoid grapefruit juice altogether.  Other alternatives, such as orange juice, may be more appropriate.

*Withdrawn from market          CY

Cows, Friends or Foes?

Pass the gas, please

There are over 96 million cows in the US alone.  These animals produce many wonderful things for us humans to consume. Cows serve mankind in many ways.  What a great domesticated beast!  Milk, steaks, cheese, ice cream, ribs, gelatin, big macs, and so much more.  But not without a price. Besides our obvious food bill price, we pay a huge cost due to cow flatulence.  Yes, I’m talking about methane, one of the greenhouse gases.  The worldwide cow population produces over 80 million tons of methane each year.  All that methane is equivalent to 1.7 billion tons of carbon dioxide.  It’s causing quite a stink.  There is hope, however.  A company from Virginia called Global Livestock Group is mastering the science of cow flatulence.  Using their patented “ruminant flatulence-monitoring technology” they are able to document changes in methane output.  They have found that supplementing feed with molasses and urea greatly reduces ruminant flatulence.  So far they have been successful in third world countries where they can sell the gas reduction to power companies as an offset to tight greenhouse emission laws. 

 

Udderly Grateful


The average dairy cow produces 7 to 10 gallons of milk each day.  One impediment to this production is mastitis, an inflammation of the udder cause by bacterial infection.  Half of our cows get this at some point in their milk producing life.  The US dairy industry claims that this loss of milk production costs over $2 billion per year.  Science comes to the rescue again, however.  The folks at the USDA, specifically the Gene Evaluation and Mapping Laboratory have engineered cows designed to produce a protein in their milk that is unfriendly to bacteria, resulting cows with healthy, happy udders, and more milk for the masses.  The protein is later destroyed when the milk is pasteurized.

 

Foreskinned is forewarned


This amazing development comes from Massachusetts.  A company called Organogenesis produces three inch round skin grafts called Apligraf.  It’s the first product of its type to get FDA approval for treating diabetic and venous leg ulcers.  These grafts have already been used on over 35,000 patients.  So what’s it got to do with cows?  Well it turns out that tendons from calves contain a collagen that can be cultured with human cells from the foreskin of newborns to produce the skin like tissue.  And remember, the valves from bull hearts are sometimes used to replace worn or damaged human ones.  Cow glands also provide us with bovine heparin, bovine thrombin and a variety of other hormones or their precursors.  Don’t ever throw anything away!

 

More Udder Uses


Genetic manipulation has led to many new uses for the amazing udder.  Genetic scientists can turn cow udders into “bioreactors” producing a variety of useful drugs for humans.  Our friends at Genzyme Transgenics in Massachusetts are brewing human albumin in udders and they have a antithrombin 3 (for blood thinning) in clinical trials.  Pharming of the Netherlands is working on udder “pharmed” fribinogen and collagen.  In Scotland, PPL Therapeutics has Holstein clones producing Factor VIII (a clotting protein lacking in hemophiliacs).                       CKL  (thanks and a hat tip to WIRED magazine)

OTC SALES COULD HELP CONFIRM BIOTERRORIST OUTBREAK


A study recently published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences discusses the possible use of monitoring sales increases of OTC cough and cold remedies and its association with individuals treating early symptoms caused by a bio-terrorist attack.  A statistical model was created by researchers that would raise concern of an outbreak when sales of certain OTC medications exceeded a specific level, giving health officials early warning.  The model was tested with data from a 1979 outbreak of anthrax in Russia.  The system detected a 36% increase in sales within the first three days of the outbreak. Because people would try to first self-medicate the early symptoms such as cough and fever from anthrax exposure for example, the model shows that an increase in OTC medication sales above twice the average daily sales is enough to suggest a possible outbreak.  While this model may help public health officials by providing additional information concerning an outbreak, there are many variables to be taken into account that could affect the sales of OTC medications.  Furthermore, this model does not provide any information as to what specific pathogen is causing the outbreak and would only be useful in a large-scale bio-terrorist attack involving at least five hundred people.  The best source of information for investigating outbreaks, bio-terrorist or otherwise, is still going to be the hospitals. However, this statistical model could be used as an additional source of confirmation for public health authorities in identifying an outbreak.     RS

 

No More Lollies to Get Your Jollies


The FDA has declared nicotine lollipops as illegal, saying the type of nicotine added had not been tested for safety.  They determined that lollipops were being promoted as smoking-cessation aids, which under federal law are considered drugs.  Therefore, the drugs must be approved before they can be sold.  The laced suckers were being sold by several Internet pharmacies.

Anti-smoking groups have urged the government to stop lollipop sales, arguing that children could get hold of the lollipops and become hooked on the nicotine, leading them to cigarette smoking later. 

Pharmacies selling the lollipops believe that the lollipops are effective because they give smokers the same hand-to-mouth motion as cigarettes.  Furthermore, the pharmacies would consider making the lollipops with the type of nicotine used in FDA-approved products.

The government has ordered pharmacies to stop sales immediately and urged smokers to use FDA-approved products like gum and patches.     CY
