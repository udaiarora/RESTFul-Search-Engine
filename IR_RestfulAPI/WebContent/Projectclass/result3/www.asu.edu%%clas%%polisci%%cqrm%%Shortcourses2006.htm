SC1: Methodological Innovation at the Intersection of Qualitative and Quantitative Methods
(Morning class, specific times to be determined)


Instructors: David Collier, Henry E. Brady, Jason Seawright, and Thad Dunning, Department of Political Science, UC Berkeley
 

The field of methodology is currently in a period of innovative and fruitful interaction between qualitative and quantitative methods. Yet much remains to be done in terms of bringing together the best thinking from both the qualitative and quantitative traditions, in the effort to push this innovation further. Within that framework, this short course will explore (1) selected topics in conceptualization and measurement, (2) tools for causal inference such as natural experiments, (3) current critiques of causal inference as it is practiced both by qualitative methodologists and through statistical modeling, and (4) the complex question of how to evaluate these critiques and their implications for future directions in substantive research.

 

Approximately two weeks before the APSA meetings, those who register for the course will be sent electronic copies of several articles that provide relevant background reading. Brady and Collier, Rethinking Social Inquiry: Diverse Tools Shared Standards (Rowman & Littlefield, 2004) also serves as background reading for the course. However, presentations will be designed so as to be accessible to participants who have not had an opportunity to read these materials.

SC2: Strategies for Field Research in Comparative and International Politics
(Afternoon class, specific times to be determined)


Instructors: Melani Cammett, Brown University
Lauren Morris MacLean, Indiana University
Benjamin Read, University of Iowa

 

Conducting fieldwork abroad is often an essential component of research in comparative and international politics. However, the task of gathering data can present a host of practical problems and concerns. Even under the best of circumstances, with excellent collegial relations with local scholars, politicians, and administrators, key contacts may be unhelpful or unwilling to be interviewed. Valuable archives and other collections of primary materials may be organized in a way that is not helpful to the researcher. Both time and money may run out before essential data have been collected. Thus, obstacles encountered in the field can derail even a well-thought-out research design.


This short course focuses on identifying, anticipating, and resolving some of the logistical problems frequently associated with overseas field research. Among the challenges we will consider are how to: (a) prepare in advance so as to work as effectively as possible from the beginning of your field research; (b) use new technologies (e-mail, web, cell phones) in the field; (c) organize and manage vast quantities of information; (d) interact constructively with scholars in the host country and gain access to key contacts; (e) manage time; (f) make the transition from data collection to data analysis and writing in a timely manner; and (g) cope with professionally, politically, and personally uncomfortable situations


Participants will be provided with document templates that may be useful for carrying out field research, including sample correspondence. The course will be valuable for first-time field researchers, for scholars who would like to consider how to improve field techniques, and for those who teach classes on research methods.
