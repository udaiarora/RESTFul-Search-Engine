Project Overviews

Arizona Policy Choices 2007: Sustainability for Arizona 

The 2007 edition of APC will present a primer on sustainability for Arizona. The report will provide clear definitions, examples, and data to help policymakers and voters understand how to solve the “puzzle” of sustainability from a state and local policy perspective. It will also feature the contributions of a wide range of state leaders and experts who offer their insights and experiences on achieving sustainability in the competing realms of the economy, environment, and society. The report will conclude with a concise analysis of the state’s policy choices on sustainability.

 

Domestic Violence in Arizona

Domestic Violence in Arizona: The Criminal Justice Response is the next step in understanding the official response to domestic violence cases in the state, following Morrison Institute’s successful study of police views in Layers of Meaning: Domestic Violence and Law Enforcement Attitudes in Arizona. The Governor’s Commission to Prevent Violence Against Women has asked Morrison Institute to identify and analyze the outlooks and attitudes toward domestic violence and service systems among prosecutors, judges, victim advocates, probation officers, and victims. The study will use surveys, individual interviews, analyses of case statistics, and review of programs to create new policy and planning information. The results will provide policymakers a clear idea of what happens statewide to domestic violence cases from start to finish.

 

The Arizona Sun Corridor Megapolitan

Early in 2007, Morrison Institute for Public Policy, an Arizona State University resource for objective public policy research and analysis, will release the first comprehensive report on the Arizona Sun Corridor, one of 10 "megapolitan" areas in the U.S. The idea of a megapolitan region—a massive web of interconnected urban and urbanizing areas—is a bold new understanding of place for Arizona business, government, and residents.

Authored by national and ASU experts Rob Lang (Director, Metropolitan Institute, Virginia Tech) and John Hall (Professor, School of Public Affairs, ASU) with policy analysis and recommendations from Grady Gammage, Jr., and Rob Melnick, Morrison Institute’s report will present data and analysis of housing, the economy, governance, and the environment. In addition, the report will discuss future projections for the Sun Corridor and their implications for current public policy thinking and decisionmaking. The graphic, easy-to-use report is the next step in Morrison Institute’s more than 20 years of analyzing Arizona’s urban growth.

 

Arizonans’ Trust in Their Police: Elements, Variations, and Limits

The Arizona Peace Officers Standards and Training Board (Arizona POST), the state agency that trains and certifies law enforcement officers, has asked Morrison Institute to determine what elements explain the levels of trust Arizonans currently have in their law enforcement officers; describe the variations, if any, in trust levels among Arizonans; and examine what types of police policies, attitudes, or conduct could undermine this trust. Through research, focus groups, and surveys Morrison Institute will enable Arizona POST to better match the official requirements that police applicants must meet and sworn officers must maintain to the values and expectations of the public they serve.


Future Tense: Issues and Ideas for Pinal County

In 2008, leaders and residents of Pinal County will complete a major amendment to the county’s comprehensive plan. With rapid growth a reality and so much land already spoken for, the 2008 plan is vitally important to creating a thoughtful, desirable future. But it will be very difficult to achieve without a common vision of what “desirable” means as the 5,370-square-mile county moves from its small-town heritage to a big-city, big-county future.

Morrison Institute for Public Policy, with support from the Pinal County government, will provide Pinal County’s leaders and residents with new information and ideas that will support the development of an enduring vision and a strong plan.

Research Components
Through research with Pinal County residents and leaders, state and national urban growth and planning experts, and officials in other counties across the country, Future Tense: Issues and Ideas for Pinal County will detail the issues facing Pinal that a comprehensive plan could address and provide ideas for vision, livability, and competitiveness. The research would include such activities as:

• Analysis of demographic, economic, land use, and other data to describe current and future trends in Pinal County
• Interviews with county and municipal officials, experts, and community leaders
• Focus groups with leaders and managers in business, economic development, and community organizations
• A public opinion survey to determine residents’ outlooks on the county’s growth, its future, and current and potential community issues and perceived trade-offs

The findings will be reported in June 2007.
Further information about Future Tense: Issues and Ideas for Pinal County is available from JD Godchaux, (602) 496-2013.





Arizona's Latino Workforce

Latinos comprise an ever-increasing proportion of Arizona's population. The expanding Latino population represents a great asset as do the African-American and American Indian populations, but concerns have been raised about the implications of today's comparatively low levels of academic achievement and high drop out rates among these Arizonans. Many state leaders have called for new education initiatives for school-age Latinos. However, little has been done to see how workforce and adult education programs, which are intended to increase adults' job-related and language skills, are affecting Arizona's Latino workforce and other minority group workers. On behalf of the Governor's Council on Workforce Policy, Morrison Institute is:

? Describing the adult Latino and other minority workforces in Arizona today, their labor force status, and issues specific to them as workers.

? Researching English Speakers of Other Languages programs and whether there is a gap between supply and demand for instruction in ?vocational? English.

? Profiling effective upgrading programs for similar workers in comparable locales across the country.

Ongoing Projects and Research


Arizona Policy Choices (APC)

The Institute annually publishes a policy research report (APC) on a topic of special and timely concern to Arizona's policymakers. It emphasizes policy choices for consideration. Authors include academics, policy practitioners, and public officials. In 1997, APC focused on tax policy; the 1998 edition addresses urban growth; the 1999 edition defines, describes and illustrates the "new economy" and the 2001 edition identifies five major challenges to Arizona's future.


Arizona Policy Choices 2005: How Does Arizona Compare?
Few types of data interest policy makers and the public more than comparisons. Plus, in almost any policy issue, the source and type of comparisons make a big difference in analysis and choices. Morrison Institute is developing a publication that will contain a wide range of reliable, generally accepted comparisons of Arizona and other states as well as metro Phoenix and Tucson with other metro areas in areas that are of concern and debate in the state.

Greater Phoenix Leadership ? Ad Hoc Issues Analysis

Morrison Institute provides research support that expands Greater Phoenix Leadership's (GPL) capacity to engage in public policy issues. GPL members receive timely and objective information and analyses on critical issues through a variety of products including policy briefs, reports and presentations. Issue areas to date include urban growth management, affirmative action, academic standards, corporate giving, and workforce development, growth management and open space protection, and Proposition 301.


Quality of Life Study

Morrison Institute conducts annual research on the issue of quality of life—both factual and public perceptions. The goal is to provide an objective tool that defines and measures quality of life in the Valley and compares public perceptions of it to actual “indicator” data. The report—What Matters in Greater Phoenix: Indicators of Our Quality of Life—aids decisionmaking as public and private sector groups come together to solve problems and improve the Valley for all residents. This project is sponsored by numerous business groups.

What Matters - The Maturing of Greater Phoenix is now available for online viewing. It is fourth in the series of indicators of our quality of life, 2004 edition.

This study will be conducted once every few years. Reports in 1997, 1998 and 1999 suggest that "indicator issues" do not move appreciably in only one year.
