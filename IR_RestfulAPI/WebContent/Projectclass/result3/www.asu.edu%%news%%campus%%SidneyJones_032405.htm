March 24, 2005

ASU’s Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict Presents Lecture by Controversial Authority on Indonesian Strife

When people want to really understand contemporary Indonesia, they ask Sidney Jones, a human rights advocate who the country’s authorities recently deported.

Indonesia, the world’s largest Muslim nation, is one of the most strategically important countries in Asia. It is also extremely complex politically, with a multitude of ethnic groups spread across a vast archipelago of islands and currently troubled by several simmering conflicts.

Jones, currently the South East Asia Project Director of the International Crisis Group, has a long history of involvement with human rights organizations working in South East Asia and, according to the New York Times, “has emerged as a leading analyst of Indonesia, with a particular expertise in the country’s chief terror group, Jemaah Islamiyah.” She currently works out of Singapore, following her recent deportation from Indonesia, an official government reaction to the fact that “her knowledge ran deep – too deep, it seems for the comfort of Indonesia’s top intelligence officer (again, according to the New York Times).

Valley residents will have a rare opportunity to learn from Jones’s unique insights into religion, culture and conflict in South East Asia as Arizona State University’s Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict presents a talk by Jones entitled “Religion, Terrorism & Human Rights” as part of its Alternative Visions lecture series. The event is scheduled for Wednesday, March 30 at 7:30 p.m. in Old Main’s Carson Ballroom, This lecture is free and open to the public, but tickets are required (see details at the end of the release).

Sidney Jones has been an advocate in the field of human rights and conflict resolution for over twenty years. She has also worked for Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International and the Ford Foundation. She has traced the development of terrorist networks in Indonesia and across South East Asia, working on conflict resolution policies to aid the transition to democracy in the region. She is one of the foremost experts on religion, terrorism and human rights in South East Asia, and she continues to advocate for policies and actions to prevent terrorist attacks such as have occurred in Bali and Jakarta.

As South East Asia Project Director for ICG, Jones and other South East Asian ICG analysts based in Jakarta prepare analytical reports on the sources of conflict and violence in the region, with a particular focus on Indonesia. Jones has examined separatist conflicts (Aceh and Papua, Mindanao); communal conflicts (Poso, Moluccas); and ethnic conflict (Kalimantan). Her team has also looked at Islamic radicalism, producing a series of reports on Jemaah Islamiyah and its operations in Indonesia and the Philippines. It also looks at issues of security sector reform and decentralization in Indonesia. A widely published commentator, Jones frequently briefs the media, international organizations, and government representatives on these issues.

Jones’s visit is part of a series of activities the Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict has sponsored focusing on the strategic importance of South East Asia. Last fall, the CSRC sponsored a related conference entitled “Religion and Conflict in Asia: Disrupting Violence,” and the center is also involved in a State Department-funded project on understanding conflict and implementing conflict resolution in Indonesia.

For more information Jones’s lecture, see http://www.asu.edu/clas/csrc/lecture_series_jones.html.   To reserve free tickets for the event, please call 480-727-6736, or email csrc@asu.edu (please include the number of tickets requested and your mailing address).
