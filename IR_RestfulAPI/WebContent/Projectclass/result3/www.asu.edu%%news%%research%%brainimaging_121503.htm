December 15, 2003

Brain imaging aids researchers' understanding of Alzheimer's

A team of ASU researchers is combining non-invasive brain imaging techniques with sophisticated data analysis methods to help uncover new answers in the fight against Alzheimer's disease.

The ASU team, led by Associate Professor of Psychology Gene Alexander and including Psychology Professors Leona Aiken and Steve West, will join with other Arizona researchers in a $4 million research grant funded by the National Institutes of Mental Health. The research grant, headed by Dr. Eric Reiman, the study's principal investigator and a brain imaging researcher and psychiatrist at Banner Good Samaritan Medical Center, is an extension of a previous five-year study.

At the heart of the research is the use of positron emission tomography (PET) and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), non-invasive brain scanning procedures. Using these techniques, researchers are able to identify physical changes in the brain over time, including those individuals who have a gene considered a susceptibility factor for late-onset Alzheimer's. By the end of the grant, researchers will have more than nine years worth of information on each participant.

The gene, apolipoprotein E e4 (APOE e4), is found in about 25 percent of the population and is present in many, but not all Alzheimer's patients. The presence of the specific gene increases a person's risk for developing Alzheimer's dementia.

The researchers will study cognitively normal individuals without the gene, those with one copy of it, and those with two copies of it. By comparing the three different groups, the researchers hope to understand what brain changes occur over time in each group and possibly how or why they happen.

The ASU team will have an important role in analyzing the scans and developing new ways to look at the data. Alexander, director of the Neuroimage Analysis Laboratory in Psychology, has been a part of the project for four years and will be working with Aiken and West to develop and apply new methods to study the brain changes over time.

"The opportunity to follow a group of individuals over an extended time is really unique in brain imaging research," says Alexander. "Studying brain changes that occur in individuals with increased genetic risk for Alzheimer's may aid early detection and help evaluate treatments and prevention therapies."

Aiken and West, experts in statistical analysis, joined the team to study the "trajectories" of decline in the brain scans. By looking at brain imaging data collected over time, they will be able to compare trajectories of decline among subjects and examine what factors account for the differences among the trajectories.

" Instead of having one group of patients, we will be able to look at the differences between people and how those differences relate to one another," West says.
Aiken, who along with West has used this form of trajectory analysis with other health conditions, says the research is novel in its use of brain imaging data.

" This has been done before with measures of cognitive function," says Aiken. "What is entirely new is the application of these analysis methods to direct measures of brain structure and function."

The research grant includes several institutions that participate in the Arizona Alzheimer's Disease Consortium (ADC), including Banner Good Samaritan Medical Center, the University of Arizona, the Mayo Clinic Scottsdale and ASU.
