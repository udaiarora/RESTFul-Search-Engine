June 11, 2003

Record number of ASU students win Fulbrights to study abroad

In a banner year for ASU Fulbright Scholarships, three more students have won funding for a year of graduate study and research abroad, bringing the number of Fulbright awards this year to nine. This is higher than any other year in ASU's history.

"We had some very dedicated faculty members working with talented students on cutting edge research and good, solid project ideas," said Janet Burke, associate dean of the Barrett Honors College. "The Fulbright Scholarship is particularly dependent on faculty mentors who are guiding students in interesting research or who are willing to help students think about possible projects of their own."

Student contestants apply for a particular country and find specialists who are willing to work with them on their chosen course of study. They receive full travel, living and academic expenses for an academic year. ASU's winning record for students who apply is double the national average.

Jamison Gray, who is studying for his master's in German, has been awarded a Fulbright to study at the Museum of the Brothers Grimm in Kassel, Germany. He will compare the English translations of one of the Grimm fairy tales, looking at whether the darker elements of the tale have been so diluted that the richness has been lost.
Deborah Boyer, a doctoral student in art history who is a Navy veteran and has traveled widely, has received a grant to study in the Philippines. She will examine the works and ideas of Filipino artists at the turn of the 20th century, researching their contributions to the country's evolving national consciousness.

Bethanne Altringer will be starting a master's program in the history of urban development and planning at the University of Granada in Spain. A December 2001 graduate in economics and psychology, she became fluent in Spanish during a recent year of study in Chile. She is interested in the development challenges in the world's poorest areas and wants to research ways to increase sustainable future development.

"Our students have been able to win so many awards even as the competition has become infinitely tougher than ever before," said Burke. "This says a great deal about the quality of our students and the strength of their ideas for overseas study projects."
