May 22, 2006

Crouch to join University of Hawaii

Eleven years ago, Peter Crouch became dean what then was ASU's College of Engineering and Applied Sciences - and he admits that "my view of where you could go with engineering was a lot less ambitious than it is now."

But his colleagues say the expansive scope of engineering at ASU today - and the far-reaching ambitions of the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering - owe much to Crouch's steady, yet forward-thinking and creative, leadership.

Crouch, who is also ASU's vice provost for global engagement, was appointed the new dean for the UH Manoa College of Engineering May 19.

As he departs ASU, Crouch "leaves quite a legacy as a positive force for ASU and the (engineering) school, and a strong foundation for the next dean to build on," says Milton Glick, ASU executive vice president and provost, who appointed Crouch dean in 1995.

Since then, Glick says, the school has made extraordinary strides in research and academic programs.

Edward Hall, the school's executive associate dean for research, says Crouch "brought stability and foresight to the school that opened up the way for innovation and entrepreneurship."

Those in the engineering community outside the university agree.

"Peter had a clear vision of the areas that needed improvement . and he has just been a kind of perpetual-motion machine in refocusing the school," says Craig Barrett, chairman of the board of Intel Corp. and chair of the Fulton School Dean's Advisory Committee.

Gary Tooker, retired chairman of the board of Motorola, and Shelly Esque, director of corporate public affairs for Intel, worked with Crouch during much of his time as dean.

"I've seen him elevate the visibility of the (engineering) school and become an effective consensus builder in reaching out to the business community," Esque says.

She and Tooker credit Crouch as a prime mover in solidifying ASU's relationships with industry, government, and other colleges and universities - efforts that helped draw financial and community support. In the time Crouch has been at the helm, the school's funded research has quadrupled, from $10 million to $40 million.

Most important, Tooker says, has been Crouch's work in pushing engineering researchers and students into the global arena. He helped develop industrial, government and academic partnerships in Europe, India, China, Korea and Mexico. In the past year, he has held a dual post as ASU's vice provost of global engagement.

Under Crouch's direction, the school moved into the top 50 nationally ranked engineering programs (47th of 185 graduate programs; 41st of 116 undergraduate programs), with five of 10 graduate-level specialties ranked in the top 30.

He credits the investment by Ira A. Fulton for enabling the engineering school to improve in the areas "that are critical in competing with the highest ranked schools in the country."

The school also has become involved in major cross-disciplinary research that partners engineers with scientists and researchers in a variety of other fields through such ASU endeavors as the Biodesign Institute, the Global Institute for Sustainability and the Arts, Media and Engineering Program.

Crouch has overseen the hiring of about 160 of the Fulton School's faculty of about 200 members. Those hires include six members of the prestigious National Academy of Engineering, and more than 20 winners of the National Science Foundation Career Award.

Says Crouch: "I like to think that, in the 11 years, we have moved the Fulton School from one known for its size to a school distinguished by its qualities."
