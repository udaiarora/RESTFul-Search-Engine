December 22, 2005

University academics, enrollment soar

ASU again was one of the top universities in the nation for the number of freshman National Merit Scholars enrolled. ASU ranks fourth among public universities and 12th overall, with 162 scholars enrolled in the fall 2004 freshman class. The fall 2005 class added 155 new National Merit Scholars, bringing the total number of National Merit Scholars enrolled to 532 – and making ASU one of the leading schools in the country in attracting these top-ranked high school graduates.

Other academic achievements included:

• Continuing an amazing record of accomplishment, two more ASU students were named to USA Today’s All-USA Academic Teams. ASU has had more students named top U.S. undergraduates by the newspaper over the past 11 years than any other public school. Only Harvard and Yale have had more.

Scott MacIntyre, who graduated in spring 2005 in piano performance in the Herberger College of Fine Arts, was named to the first team. Jared Niska, who graduated in December 2004 in bioengineering from the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering, was named to the second team of 20 students.

• ASU’s Barrett Honors College was heralded as one of “America’s 100 Best” in the May 2005 issue of Reader’s Digest. In a cover feature on “America’s 100 Best,” the magazine included it in a “treasure trove” of extraordinary people, places and innovations that demonstrate excellence in America.

• Jason Rugolo of Scottsdale, a junior in physics who is doing research on nanoporous metals in a mechanical engineering lab, received a Goldwater Scholarship, the nation’s highest award for undergraduates who are planning careers in scientific research.

• Taylor Jackson, a junior in biology and society who came to ASU as a National Merit Scholar from Hattiesburg, Miss., was named a Udall Scholar for his service and research in the area of environmental policy.

• Qing Jiang, who received her bachelor’s degree in piano performance from ASU in May, received a full scholarship from the Juilliard School of Music.

• ASU is “academically, a rising star in the world of research,” says Princeton Review in its 2006 edition of “The Best 361 Colleges.” Among ASU’s many assets are the nationally ranked Barrett Honors College, as well as outstanding offerings in engineering, business, design, education and journalism, the publication reports.

• The fall 2005 freshman class at ASU was the largest, most accomplished and diverse in the university’s history. A record 8,467 first-time freshmen from all 50 states and 35 countries are enrolled, almost 10 percent higher than fall 2004 and double the number from 1995.

Nearly three out of every 10 freshmen (28 percent) ranked in the top 10 percent of their high school graduating class. This group of high achievers is larger than the entire freshman class at Ivy League schools Princeton or Yale.

• ASU attracted 72 National Hispanic Scholars, the most ever. More than 30 percent of freshmen from Arizona are minority students, and a record 26 percent of the freshman class comprises ethnic minorities – almost 400 more than last fall.

• ASU had the largest enrollment of minority students in the state, and one of the highest Hispanic student populations in the nation in the fall semester. Minority enrollment was at 13,822, almost 1,000 more than last year. A record 7,284 Hispanics made up almost 12 percent of the student body this fall. The overall student population was the most diverse in ASU history, with increases in all ethnic groups. The highest increase was among Native Americans, which grew 16 percent to 1,437 this fall. ASU also had 2,194 African-American students and 2,907 Asian-Americans.

• Fourteen students from ASU won prestigious Fulbright awards to study abroad in 2005 – 2006, making ASU the fourth-highest producer of Fulbright awards among all public colleges and universities in the United States. ASU Fulbright applicants had a higher acceptance rate than almost any other school, with 36 percent of all those who apply receiving awards. At Harvard, Yale, Columbia and Princeton, only a quarter of applicants were accepted.

• ASU’s overall population of minority faculty members rose to a record 22 percent, the highest in the university’s history. In addition, 41 percent of the new hires in the last two years are female. ASU has a higher number of Hispanic faculty (129) than UCLA, and three times the number of American Indian faculty (26) as either UCLA or the University of Texas at Austin, both peer institutions that serve high minority populations.
