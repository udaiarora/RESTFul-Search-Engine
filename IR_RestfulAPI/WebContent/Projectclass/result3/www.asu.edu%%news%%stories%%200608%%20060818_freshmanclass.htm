Students move in to new residential complex

About 900 students – including Woodrow D’Anjou, pictured above, a freshman education major from Portland, Ore. – have started to move into Hassayampa Academic Village (HAV), a gleaming new ASU living complex at the northeast corner of Apache Boulevard and McAllister Avenue. They’re among 6,300 students who moved in and took part in Sun Devil 101, ASU’s annual Welcome Week Aug. 14 - 18.

As in the other freshman halls, HAV has tutoring, advising, classrooms and academic coaching on site. It’s part of a continuing effort to create living and learning communities that help students succeed at the university.

Five buildings are organized around courtyards at Hassayampa, which is named for the river that winds through western Arizona.

Students live in groups of 40 to 50, in four-bed and two-bed suites, with shared study areas and a bathroom for each suite. The buildings have lounges with a kitchen and access to an exterior courtyard for each community of 120 students. There are wireless zones throughout, LCD monitors in the lounges, and an Ethernet port for every student. Four levels of security exist to enter rooms, using keys and cards.

The second phase of HAV, which will provide about 1,000 more spaces, will open next fall.

 

 

 

Freshman class on course to break records in scholars, diversity, size

ASU’s freshman class this fall is projected to be the largest in university history, exceeding 9,000 students. While official enrollment numbers won’t be available until the 21st day of class, this projected number of first-time freshmen is more than twice as large as the entering class 10 years ago.

Texas resident and former ASU student Christi Barrett, left, helps her friend, Lauren
Stockdale, a sophomore psychology major from San Diego, shop for textbooks. ASU students are returning to campus as classes begin Aug. 21.

A record 186 freshmen National Merit Scholars had registered for classes by mid-August, with 64 of them hailing from Arizona. Last fall, ASU enrolled 155 National Merit Scholars, the third most of any public university in the nation and 13th overall. ASU anticipates this fall’s enrollment will maintain ASU’s prominence with this prestigious program.

“Nowhere else in the nation will you find the type of energy and focus that we are bringing to the student experience, throughout all of our programs and on each of our campuses,” says Martha Byrd, dean of undergraduate admissions. “It is exciting to welcome such an outstanding class of freshmen to ASU.”

Also, 92 National Hispanic Scholars had registered for classes by mid-August, 20 more than last year. This group of scholars has increased 300 percent in five years and will likely be one of the largest numbers of National Hispanic Scholars enrolled at any university in the country.
Almost a third of the Arizona resident freshmen are minority students. ASU also projects that 27.6 percent of the freshman class as a whole will be students of color. That’s an 11.4 percent increase from last year and a 164 percent increase in 10 years.
Reflecting the diversity of the state, ASU has made significant gains in the number of Hispanic freshmen, with a projected 13.3 percent increase this fall.

Sarah Auffret, sauffret@asu.edu
