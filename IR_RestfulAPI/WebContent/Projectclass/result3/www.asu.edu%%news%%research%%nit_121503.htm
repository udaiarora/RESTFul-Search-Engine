Arizona State and University of Michigan team up to develop brain neurovascular material

TEMPE, Ariz. - Arizona State University and the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor, are teaming up to market a new material that promises to become an effective non-surgical way to treat blood vessel defects of the brain.

Neural Intervention Technologies Inc. (NIT), Dexter, Mich., has been working towards commercialization of the material, called ALGEL, for two years. The material will begin clinical testing in 2004 and is expected to be marketed in 2005-2006.

ALGEL was developed by Tim Becker and Daryl Kipke, founders of NIT. They began working on the material when Becker was a doctoral student and Kipke was a professor at Arizona State. Becker is now the chief technology officer of NIT and Kipke is a professor with the University of Michigan.

The licensing agreement is between Arizona Technology Enterprises (AzTE), the technology licensing arm of ASU, NIT and the University of Michigan.

"By pooling our intellectual property, ASU and Michigan have created a more valuable technology portfolio," said AzTE CEO Peter Slate. "In this case, the sum is worth more than the parts."

"The University of Michigan is pleased with the results of this collaborative effort with Arizona State University, and looks forward to continuing the relationship as U-M scientists pursue the development of next generation technologies," added Robin Rasor, director of licensing at the U-M Office of Technology Transfer.

ALGEL is a sugar-based polymer that is derived from alginate, a natural material that is more than 95 per cent water. It is non-adhesive, non-toxic and has tissue like properties. The material was developed to be used with microcatheter technologies and provide a safe and effective treatment for neurovascular lesions.

Currently, patients with neurovascular lesions or brain aneurysms are treated either through surgery or through newer endovascular (non-surgical) treatments. Surgical techniques risk the possibility of damaging other brain structures. Endovascular treatments are less risky but the materials typically used are platinum coils (to fill an aneurysm) or cyanoacrylate derivatives (which act like a superglue).

ALGEL is a marked improvement over these materials because it has properties that are more in tune with the environment in which they are placed.

"ALGEL is injected as a liquid through microcatheters into vascular defects and when gelled, it is strong, soft, non-adhesive and biocompatible," said Kipke, who did basic research on the material while at ASU (from 1992 to 2001).

"ALGEL results in controllable and non-toxic filling for the vascular defect," he added. "These properties provide advantages over the glues, polymers and coils that are currently used as treatments."

"AzTE is excited about NIT's potential. This technology can have far reaching implications for patients with blood vessel defects of the brain," Slate added. "ALGEL is just one example of an invention created within academia that is reaching the marketplace through effective technology venturing."
