A.S.U. PHARMACY NEWSLETTER                            ISSUE TWENTY-THREE

Drug News and Views


"Natural" Supplements

There is new info about the safety of Kava (also known as "intoxicating pepper").  Recent cases have demonstrated a strong link to liver toxicity, one patient requiring a liver transplant after two months of high dose Kava for anxiety.  Kava use may also lead to a pellagra-like dermopathy which resolves when the drug is discontinued.  In California there have been cases of people being cited for DUI after ingesting Kava tea.  Tea prepared at "kava bars" may be four times more potent than store bought tea bags.  So far, judges have dismissed the cases for lack of evidence of Kava's intoxicating effects.  Advise patients to use caution!

There's new evidence that garlic supplements can interfere with HIV protease inhibitors, most importantly saquinavir, and perhaps Crixivan, Norvir, Rescriptor, and Viramune, which are metabolized via similar pathways.  Many AIDS patients attempt to augment there therapies with alternative products such as garlic pills so this interaction may occur frequently.  Another good reason to ask about supplement usage.

Ok, now some good supplement news…

A study recently published in The Lancet showed that glucosamine can be a safe and effective alternative to NSAIDS for osteoarthritis.  This study and others have demonstrated pain relief on par with ibuprofen therapy and may delay joint damage (joint space loss).  Patients need to know that it may take 1 or 2 months to reach maximum pain relief, so continue NSAID therapy for 4 to 8 weeks.  Combo products that also contain chondroitin are more expensive and don't seem to work any better than glucosamine alone.  Glucosamine may be worth taking if only for the benefit of delaying joint space loss. Pain may be managed in more traditional ways.

 

Vaccines

A recent survey conducted by the CDC showed that 25% of parents think that we give kids too many vaccinations.  Do we?  Experts don't think so.  Examples cited include the pertussis vaccine.  In 1974 Great Britain curtailed pertussis vaccination due to consumer concerns that the vaccine led to Sudden Infant Death Syndrome (SIDS).  In 1978 Great Britain reported over 100,000 cases of pertussis and 36 deaths due to the disease.  Many myths have been circulating about the safety of various vaccines.  For a quick run down on these misconceptions visit: Six common misconceptions about vaccination and how to respond to them, on the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention Web site at: http://www.cdc.gov/nip/publications/6mishome.htm.

Appropriate prescribing of antibiotics in respiratory tract infections

How many times a day do you see patients for acute respiratory tract infections?  And how often do you struggle whether or not to prescribe an antibiotic, especially when a large majority of these infections are caused by viruses against which antibiotics are ineffective?  A large percentage of these infections will eventually resolve spontaneously, without any medical intervention.  And the press has been replete with studies suggesting that unnecessary antibiotic prescribing increases antibiotic resistance.

So, what is a provider to do? 

A recent article published on March 20, 2001 in Annals of Internal Medicine provide updated guidelines to streamline antibiotic use, thereby saving patients money and decreasing antibiotic resistance.  In summary, the article states:


1)      The diagnosis of nonspecific upper respiratory tract infections or acute


rhinopharyngitis should be used to denote an acute infection that is typically viral in origin and in which sinus, pharyngeal, and lower airway symptoms, although frequently present, are not prominent.

2) Antibiotic treatment of adults with nonspecific upper respiratory tract infection does not enhance illness reduction and is not recommended.  Studies specifically testing the impact of antibiotic treatment on complications of nonspecific upper respiratory tract infections have not been performed in adults.  Life-threatening complications of upper respiratory tract infections are rare.

3) Purulent secretions from the nares or throat (commonly observed in patients with uncomplicated upper respiratory tract infections) predict neither bacterial infection nor benefit from antibiotic treatment. [1]

Andrew Draper R. Ph., PharmD candidate

“It’s That Diet Time of Season Again”


                                                HERIBERTO DUARTE

Spring has officially set in and for many it already feels like the start of a long summer. Dieting and exercising are on the minds of many, as we get ready to fit into those bathing suits and hit the pools. The “F” word is what we dread to hear: F---, that’s right FATS!  As we begin to eat healthier, many are misled or are not clear about how much fat is actually in the foods we eat.

 

Fat Replacers

Low-fat, reduced-fat and fat-free foods have become a staple in the diets of many seeking healthier lifestyles. Reducing fat intake as well as calories is aided and abetted by eating food with lower fat content. Fat Reduction ingredients are necessary for reducing the fat in foods while maintaining taste and texture. This poses a challenge to anyone who loves the smooth texture of a salad dressing, the delicious sweet-creamy feel of frozen desserts,  the moist, tender taste of cake or the rich flavor of cheese or chocolate.  Texture and taste often influence food choices more than dietary recommendations - Fat is where is at!

Maintenance of healthy skin and regulation of cholesterol require fat. Dietary fat provides fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E and K and aids in their absorption from the intestine.  Dietary fat is an important source of energy.  Carbohydrates, proteins and fats are the building blocks from which fat reduction-ingredients are manufactured.

What are the different kinds of Fats?

Dietary fats are the fats found in foods and are essentially part of a healthy diet.  Fatty acids are the building blocks of dietary fats, which contain a mixture of saturated and unsaturated fatty acids. Unsaturated fats are further subdivided into monosaturated and polyunsaturated fats.

Saturated fats: saturated fatty acids are mostly found in animal products such as meat and whole milk. Butter and lard are high in saturated fatty acids. Levels of LDL-cholesterol or “bad cholesterol” in the blood rise as more and more saturated fatty acid are consumed.

Monosaturated: monosaturated fatty acids are found mostly in plants and seafoods. Olive oil and canola oil are high in monosaturated fatty acids. These types of fats tend to lower LDL- cholesterol blood levels.

Polyunsaturated: polyunsaturated fatty acids are also found in plants and seafoods. Safflower and corn oil are high in polyunsaturated fatty acids. These types of fats tend to lower levels of both LDL- and HDL- cholesterol in the blood.

Note: a high blood level of LDL-cholesterol increases the risk of fatty deposits that tend to form in the arteries, which increases the chance of a heart attack. On the other hand, higher blood levels of HDL-cholesterol or the “good cholesterol” have a protective effect and help against heart disease.

People may shriek when they hear how much cholesterol certain products contain, but cholesterol is actually a vital component in your body’s metabolism.  The liver synthesizes cholesterol that the body needs, helping the body produce steroid hormones for endocrine function, processing food, and bile acids for digestion. Only foods of animal origin contain cholesterol, which may increase the risk of coronary heart disease.

How much fat can I consume daily?

The American Heart Association recommends that calories from fats be between 15% and 30% of total calories. There are also guidelines set out by the National Cholesterol Education Program (NCEP) that classifies risk for heart disease based on blood cholesterol levels;

<200mg/dl     Desirable total cholesterol

200-239        Borderline-high total cholesterol

                                                           > or = 240     High total cholesterol

The NCEP also saturated fat be 8-10% of total calories and cholesterol be less than 300mg per day. If this fails to reduce blood cholesterol to desired levels, then saturated fat would be reduced to 7% and cholesterol to no more than 200mg per day.

MYTHS & FACTS:

Myth: reduced-fat foods are always low in fat!

Fact: These products are not necessarily “low fat”, to know the facts, you would need to read the label for contents and nutritional information.

Myth: low-fat means low calorie.

Fact: While a product might be lower in fat, the calorie content may be equal to or greater than that found in the regular product. Note: products that were always high in carbohydrates/sugars are now claiming to be “low fat” and “low cholesterol”, but consumption of large amounts of these products aren’t really healthy.

Myth: fat-free means taste-free.

Fact: Today’s products are significantly improved compared to early attempts at fat-free concoctions. Adding a bit of fat or a substitute can make improvements to taste and texture.

Myth: not all calories are created equal.

Fact: If your caloric intake exceeds your calories burned, excess calories may be converted to body fat.
