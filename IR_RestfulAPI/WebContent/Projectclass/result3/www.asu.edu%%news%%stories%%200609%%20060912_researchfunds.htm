September 13, 2006

ASU research expenditures top $200 million per year

ASU passed a significant milestone in research expenditures by reaching $203.5 million in the fiscal year that ended June 30.

This marks the first time research expenditures ? the amount of money spent to perform research and discovery ? has surpassed the $200 million level at ASU. In the last six years, ASU has doubled its research expenditures.

ASU is making a name for itself in research cirlces through large interdisciplinary efforts such as the Flexible Display Center, funded by a $43.6 million Army grant.

?ASU's research success comes because faculty members and the talented students across the university are making significant discoveries in a wide range of fields,? says ASU President Michael Crow. ?The scholarship being performed in all disciplines and at all four ASU campuses is what is building our reputation as a place where innovative, advanced scientific work is performed.?

Several factors have come together leading to the significant growth of ASU research expenditures, according to Jonathan Fink, ASU vice president of research and economic affairs.

These factors include:

? The quality of ASU faculty and staff researchers.

? Investments in new research buildings and infrastructure.

? A growing emphasis on new scientific disciplines and technologies by ASU researchers and faculty.

?ASU is one of the youngest major research universities in the country, and as such we have to work very hard to compete with more established schools,? Fink says. ?But the $200 million in research expenditures now makes us one of the leading innovation engines in the state.?

The $203.5 million total research dollars for fiscal year 2006 comes from a variety of sources. ASU had spent $174.6 million in funds received from the federal government and industry, $23.6 million in Technology & Research Initiative Funds from state sales tax revenue, and $5.3 million in funds received by the ASU Foundation specifically for research projects.

Key facilities

Fink says the increase in research funding is the result of major new ASU research facilities, such as the two buildings of the Biodesign Institute; three completed Interdisciplinary Science and Technology Buildings (two on Tempe campus and one at ASU Polytechnic); and MacroTechnology Works at ASU's Research Park.

These new facilities have helped ASU secure major projects, including:

? A $14 million Gates Foundation grant for Roy Curtiss of the Biodesign Institute.

? A $43.6 million Army grant that led to the formation of the Flexible Display Center.

? A recently announced $18 million grant for the incoming dean of the Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering, Deirdre Meldrum, for the Microscale Life Sciences Center.

Faculty focus

But it's not the buildings alone that attract major grants to ASU.

?The growth is fueled by the creativity and energy of our faculty members,? Fink says. ?ASU has received a disproportionate number of grants to support interdisciplinary research at the interfaces of different fields. That is where much of the excitement is, in terms of societal need and scientific challenges, and our university's culture has successfully embraced this highly competitive realm with great enthusiasm.?

Research infrastructure, such as the Biodesign Institute Buidling B funded by the state's voters, have played a key role in ASU's research growth.

Examples cited by Fink are a Department of Defense award for improving brain function; a National Science Foundation-supported project on anthropological studies of the factors controlling the rise and fall of societies in different parts of the world; a suite of grants to explore how cities function; and groundbreaking motion-capture studies that culminated in the premier of a modern dance performance at New York's Lincoln Center.

?Funding agencies like the National Science Foundation have acknowledged ASU as one of the pioneering institutions that are blazing new interdisciplinary research paths,? Fink says.

He expects the rapid growth of ASU's research to continue for several years, as the university gears up for many new initiatives and cutting-edge research.

?We do not believe that the current research expenditures fully reflect the major research investments that have taken place at ASU and in the state of Arizona since 2002,? Fink says. ?These include the new research buildings, newly recruited research-active faculty members, expansion of our technology transfer capabilities in Arizona Technology Enterprises and new state initiatives such as Science Foundation Arizona.

?Reaching and sustaining a level of $400 million per year in the next several years will put us in a category that, while not at the top of the academic pecking order, would provide us with considerable recognition and respect and provide even more of a return on our community's investment in ASU.?
