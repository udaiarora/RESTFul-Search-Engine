Plate tectonics authority details Earth’s mysterious interior

While humanity builds giant telescopes and spacecraft to probe the marvels of outer space, right beneath our feet is an environment that is every bit as exotic and nearly as unknown. Now, thanks to recently developed technology, the mysteries of the deep earth are beginning to be revealed.

ASU’s Department of Geological Sciences is sponsoring a talk by David James, one of the scientists whose discoveries have made this exploration possible. James will deliver a free public lecture, "Revealing the Mysteries of the Earth's Deep Interior: Plates, Plumes, and the Birth of Modern Seismology," at 7 p.m. , Jan. 27 in the Physical Sciences F-wing Room 166.

Jameshas been a leader in the development of plate tectonic theory and has more than three decades of experience in leading research expeditions to many regions, including South America and southern Africa . His lecture will focus on the development of seismic imaging tools that have led to revolutionary advances in our understanding of the structure and dynamics of Earth's interior. He will describe how seismic images are obtained and the vital clues they yield for solving mysteries as diverse as the causes of earthquakes and mountain building to the origin of diamonds formed deep in the ancient roots of continents.

James has been one of the foremost scientists to advocate harnessing revolutionary digital and microelectronics technology to design and build a powerful new class of portable broadband seismometer systems. He was a leader in promoting the development of portable broadband array instrumentation and methodology that is now used worldwide to make 3-D images of the Earth’s crust and deep interior. He produced the first three-dimensional seismic image of crustal structure beneath the central Andes and, in the early 1970s, published the first comprehensive plate tectonic model for the evolution of the area that synthesized seismic, gravitational, geochemical, and geological data.

James is a Senior Staff Scientist in the Department of Terrestrial Magnetism at the Carnegie Institution in Washington , D.C. He is a fellow of both the American Geophysical Union and Royal Astronomical Society, and edited The Encyclopedia of Solid Earth Geophysics (1990). He received his Ph.D from Stanford University in California in 1967.

The lecture is sponsored by Incorporated Research Institutions for Seismology (IRIS), Seismological Society of America (SSA), and ASU’s Department of Geological Sciences.
