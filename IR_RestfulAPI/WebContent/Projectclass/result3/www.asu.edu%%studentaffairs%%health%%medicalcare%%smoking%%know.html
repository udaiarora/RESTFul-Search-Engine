What's the best way to quit smoking?
Quitting smoking is the single best thing that you can do to improve your current and future health. The best way to quit smoking is the one that works for you. If you have been thinking about quitting, it may be time to weigh the benefits of quitting against the perceived benefits of smoking. Perhaps it’s clear to you that quitting smoking would be a wonderful thing to do, but you’re afraid. You’re afraid that you’ll gain weight, be bored, nervous, no longer be “cool,” or that you’ll fail again. Part of successful quitting is gaining the confidence to quit.

Why is quitting so difficult?
In order to quit, smokers really have to deal with three main aspects of the cigarette smoking habit: (1) the nicotine in cigarettes is physically addictive, (2) smokers tend to form patterns of behavior that incorporate cigarette smoking, and (3) we tend to give the act of smoking great psychological and social power. To quit, one must deal with all three: the addiction, the habit, and the psychosocial aspects. Smoking as few as five cigarettes a day for several days is often enough to cause nicotine addiction. Withdrawal symptoms such as irritability, insomnia, and difficulty concentrating can occur when nicotine is no longer available. We all have patterns of daily living: morning routines, driving routines, telephone routines, meal routines, and many more. Smoking a cigarette is often “triggered” by some of the normal occurrences during a typical day. They are familiar to most smokers. The cigarette with the morning coffee, or when on the phone, after a meal, while driving, when drinking alcohol, on “break,” and many other routines are quite common among smokers. Misconceptions about cigarette smoking are that it reduces stress, controls appetite, enhances image, and diminishes boredom.

What is the first step to successful quitting?
Formulating a plan that not only helps you stop smoking but transforms you into a nonsmoker is an important part of the quitting process. When thinking about quitting smoking, it’s a good idea to take a close look at your smoking behavior. Examine when you smoke, as well as where and why. You might imagine that you are going into a battle and that cigarettes are the enemy. If you are a good “general,” you will make sure that you know the enemy and that you have a solid battle plan as well as a backup plan. Don’t be afraid to confront your smoking habits. Spend some time reviewing each cigarette smoked in a typical day and devising a strategy for not smoking each one. Some will be easier to omit than others. Have two or three strategies ready for those critical times when you are most likely to desire a cigarette. It is a good idea to start by setting your quit date, the first day that you stop smoking forever. Mark it on your calendar and plan a busy day.

What are the key parts of a good smoking cessation plan?
Remember, you should be prepared to deal with the three main aspects of smoking. There are many types of nicotine replacement products available to help prevent the symptoms of nicotine withdrawal. Nicotine products (ie, patches, nasal inhalers, sprays, and gum) are available both over-the-counter and by prescription. Your pharmacist can help you to select the one that is best suited to your smoking patterns. These products work well when used to avoid withdrawal symptoms as you work on creating a behavior modification program.
Behavior modification is very important if you want to quit forever. Changing the little patterns of behavior throughout the day can help to eliminate or diminish urges to smoke. Minor changes such as where you sit for that morning coffee or what cup you use can have a major impact on your ability to avoid smoking. When an urge to smoke occurs, try to distract yourself. Smoking urges tend to be short-lived, so if you can distract or delay, the urge may pass. In some social situations, especially when alcohol is involved, it may be best to simply avoid or escape the setting. As often as possible, think of yourself as a nonsmoker.

What will lead to continued success?
If you can come to think of yourself as a nonsmoker, and you’re comfortable with not smoking in any situation, you should never smoke again. You’ll feel healthier, more self-confident, and be able to handle anything that life can put in your path, without reaching for a cigarette. Quitting forever can be challenging but may be facilitated by others such as family, friends, or a trained smoking cessation counselor. Don’t hesitate to ask for help.

What are some other resources for smoking cessation information?
There are many organizations that are prepared to help you quit smoking. Check with your state affiliate of the American Lung Association for Freedom from Smoking classes; many states have resources and personnel funded by tobacco excise taxes or tobacco settlement money. Also, the Internet has thousands of sites offering information on smoking cessation (searching for “smoking cessation” on one search engine yielded over a quarter of a million “links”). Ultimately, you are your own best resource. Get motivated, apply willpower, and use the tools available to you. Remember, quitting smoking is the single best thing that you can do for your health

(Carl Labbe R.P.H. originally appeared in Pharmacy times 12/2000)

Things to Do in Preparation For Quitting
  • Come to terms with your reasons for smoking.
  • Start reducing the number of cigarettes that you smoke each day.
  • Delay your first cigarette of the day by 1 hour or more each day.
  • Toss out all that paraphernalia used in smoking (ashtrays, lighters, etc).
  • Clean! (the car, home, work area, clothes).
  • Make the produce section of your market your best friend (think healthy snacks).
  • List your reasons for quitting and post them around.
  • Set a quit date that is meaningful to you.
  • Have some support people that you can call when you are tempted to smoke.
  • Have some rewards established for specific milestones in your quit (1 week equals a new CD, a month equals a day at the spa.)
