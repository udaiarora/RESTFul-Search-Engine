ASU targets retention, graduation rates

Student retention and graduation rates are two areas ASU is focusing on to measure excellence and achievement.

At the Arizona Board of Regents (ABOR) meeting Nov. 20-21, university presidents gave individual reports that focused on the criteria used to rank academic programs by various national organizations and publications, including U.S. News and World Report.

ASU President Michael Crow, Executive Vice President and Provost Milton Glick and Executive Director of Institutional Analysis Joseph Matt presented the national contexts related to rankings and used the U.S. News and World Report ranking system as a vehicle for discussion of goals for excellence and achievement.

Glick presented ASU's efforts that aim toward a "win-win" strategy for students and ASU. These efforts include: continued emphasis on student retention programs; focus on the development and visibility of academic programs; and focus on quality of faculty and reduction of class sizes.

"We are 'people's' universities," Crow said. "We need greater resources to fulfill our mission and move forward to help hardworking kids from hardworking families who wish to gain a high quality experience in higher education."

Crow said ASU is focusing its efforts on the reduction of class sizes, specifically for gateway courses such as English and mathematics.

These gateway courses are usually required for graduation, have a large amount of students enrolled and are difficult for students who may need more individualized attention.

ASU's ongoing partnership with community colleges allows students to attend community college, where some courses like mathematics, are smaller. This provides students an opportunity to successfully complete gateway courses before they transfer to ASU.

Glick is hopeful this ongoing partnership and the new ASU-Maricopa Alliance will help students complete their college education at ASU and the university obtain better national rankings.

"We hope the new ASU-Maricopa Alliance will give us a better opportunity to help those students who are at risk of dropping," he said. "This will also assist us in tracking students. It's really a matter of taking care of your people first."

Enrollment funding formulas will be discussed during a study session at the Jan. 22 - 23 ABOR meeting at ASU.

Arizona's universities presented their fall 2003 Enrollment Report, including headcount (total number of part-time and full-time students) and the number of full-time equivalent students. Overall, Arizona university system headcount enrollments increased 1.4 percent, from 113,869 in fall 2002, to 115,464 in fall 2003. ASU Main Campus' total headcount for fall 2003 was 48,901; ASU West, 7,105; and ASU East, 3,551.

Also at the meeting, ASU President Michael Crow presented the ASU Arts and Business District Gateway Plan. The proposed gateway is one component of the university's Comprehensive Development Plan. The site consists of approximately 13 acres of ASU-owned land at the old Tempe Center site on the southeast corner of Mill Avenue and University Drive in Tempe. The redevelopment of this site will include the new location of the W. P. Carey School of Business, the expansion of the Fine Arts Center as well as the College of Architecture & Environmental Design Building, to create the western gateway to ASU.

"The Gateway Development will enhance the goal of embedding university activity into the life of Downtown Tempe," Executive Vice President of Administration and Finance Mernoy Harrison said.

Discussion forums have not been planned, but Harrison said there will be ample opportunities for community input on the plan.

To learn more about the Nov. 20 - 21 Regents meeting, visit the Web at (www.abor.asu.edu).
