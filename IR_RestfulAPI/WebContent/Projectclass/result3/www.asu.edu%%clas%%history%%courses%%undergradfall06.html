Fall 2006 

HST 300: The Birth of the Modern: Europe and Its Others
Dr. Andrew Barnes
SLN: 16127, Internet

This course looks at how over the course of the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries Europeans gave up one set of ?others,? that is, social outsiders (witches, Jews and the poor) and during the Enlightenment replaced these with a new set of ?others? (women, Africans and Asians). History 300 is a historical methods course, so this subject matter will be discussed with a view toward also giving students some basic introduction to how historians develop and debate historical theses.

HST 300: Urban Rebellion
Dr. Matthew Whitaker
SLN: 73401, TTh, 1:40-2:55, EDB 212

The many urban rebellions that rocked U.S. cities and forced Americans to confront inequity and injustice embedded in our nation can be looked upon as causative components of myriad dynamic and influential movements.  These movements include the black freedom struggle, the Chicano and American Indian Movements, protests against U.S. military action in Vietnam, the Feminists Movement, contemporary conflicts ignited by racial tensions and allegations of police brutality, and more clandestine acts of individual and collective resistance.  This course will examine that which has inspired, and continues to inspire, the cultural and tactical responses to various forms of marginalization, exploitation, and discrimination in twentieth century America on the one hand, and systemic and societal constructs that create and perpetuate racial, economic, gender, and political disunity and inequality in urban America on the other.  By analyzing this history, and referencing core ?American? values, such as freedom, the desire for self-determination, and resistance to oppression, this course ultimately seeks to help students develop a more holistic intellectual base from which to evaluate the importance and limitations of urban rebellion as a device to secure and maintain justice and freedom.

HST 300 : THE AMERICAN STRUGGLE FOR WOMEN'S SUFFRAGE
Dr. Gayle Gullett
SLN: 22706, TTh, 3:15-4:30, Cowden 215

In this class history majors move from reading history to creating history. To do this students will learn all the necessary skills?how to read documents critically, to build arguments that explain past events, and to communicate their understanding of the past. Students will engage in this active, participatory learning process by examining how American women?and some men?struggled to gain the vote, a campaign that stretched from 1848 to 1920. Students will seek to understand why this struggle took so long. Students will also ask if winning the vote was significant and, if so, why was it important? To answer these questions, students will learn about individuals, the women's suffrage movement, and all kinds of politics. Students will teach themselves how to write history while they are learning about women's struggle for citizenship. Students will read the opinions of other historians?and challenge them. Students will read documents written by suffragists and anti-suffragists and use these documents to write their own explanations of the struggle for the vote.

HST 300: American Memory: The Civil War and Reconstruction
Dr. Brooks Simpson
TTh, 10:40-11:55, EDB 212
SLN 05864

How do Americans remember the Civil War and Reconstruction? How have those memories and impressions changed over time, and why? Why do different people remember different things about the war and its aftermath? This course is not about the Civil War and Reconstruction itself, but about how various groups of Americans have understood, remembered, and celebrated the war and its aftermath. Students will discuss readings, conduct short research projects, and interact with the community of Civil War fans as they gain an understanding of how historical memories are formed and how they function.

HST 300: The Overland Trails, 1840-1860
Dr. Susan Gray
TTh, 9:15-10:30, EDB 212
SLN: 28124

HST 300 is a writing-intensive course in historical methodology intended to prepare students for their senior seminars (HST 498). HST 300 is required for the major in history, and it is taught topically by various members of the department. This particular version of the course examines in some detail an epic event in United States history: the uprooting of tens of thousands of people from their homes and their endurance of a grueling trek of many months to the far reaches of the continent. Most, but by no means all, of the overlanders were white, native-born, and impelled west by the promise of something better. Some sought more and better land, others gold, and still others sanctuary from religious persecution. Some, however, sought nothing at all, for the decision to emigrate was made for them by their fathers, husbands, and sons, or by their masters. Whatever the overlanders' reasons for travel, the journey proved the trip of a lifetime. In this course, students will explore the rich and varied historical record of the trail?diaries, letters, memoirs, and guidebooks?as well as modern writings and films by historians and other commentators. The course also explores the perspectives of the Indigenous peoples, for whom the consequences of a migration corridor through their homelands were disastrous.

HST 300: Creating Communities. The 19th and 20th Century Immigrant Experience
Dr. Christiane Harzig
MWF 9:40-10:30, EDB 212
SLN 48640

(Im)Migrants have always shaped and altered societal trajectories and demanded recognition. The course will look at immigrants' encounters with receiving societies and their social, political and cultural negotiations. Ethnic communities provided the framework for institution building and identity construction, they also function as a stepping stone to move beyond. Because community formation is a complex process and a historically contested issue, it is also a useful concept to historical "tools." It will introduce a broad range of sources as well as the various historical theories and methods (cultural vs. social historical analysis, quantitative methods, oral history, "classical" hermeneutics, construction and deconstruction). The course will focus on U.S., Canadian and European experiences, questioning the old vs. new immigration country paradigm and U.S. exceptionalism.

Harzig, Christiane and Danielle Juteau, eds. The Social Construction of Diversity. Recasting the Master Narrative of Industrial Nations. New York, Oxford, UK: Berghahn Books, 2003

Hoerder, Dirk, ed. The Historical Practice of Diversity. Transcultural interactions from the Early Modern Mediterranean to the Postcolonial World. New York, Oxford, UK: Berghahn Books, 2003

Iacovetta, Franca, ed. A Nation of Immigrants. Women, Workers, and Communities in Canadian History, 1840-1960. Toronto: University of Toronto Press, 1998.

Daniels, Roger. Coming to America. A History of Immigration and Ethnicity in American Life. New York: Harper, 1991.

Gabaccia, Donna. Immigration and American Diversity. A Social and Cultural History. Malden, Mass.: Blackwell, 2002.

HST 300: Fascism: Politics, Culture, and Everyday Life in Mussolini's Italy and Hitler's Germany
Dr. Anna Holian
MW, 1:40-2:55, EDB 238
SLN: 93929

During the 1920s and 30s, fascist and authoritarian movements rose to prominence and power throughout Europe. This course examines the rise and fall of the European fascist movements, focusing in particular on Italy and Germany.  It emphasizes politics, culture, and everyday life under fascism. What distinguishes fascist regimes from traditional authoritarian regimes in both the style and the content of their politics? What made fascist movements so popular?  How did fascist movements appeal to the public? What was it like to live in a fascist country? How were women's experiences of fascism different from men? How was Italian fascism different from that in Germany? These are some of the key questions we will consider in the course. Class meetings will generally consist of a brief introductory lecture followed by discussion of the reading. Sources will include scholarly monographs, fiction, film, art, and architecture.

HST 302: Navajo History
Dr. Peter Iverson
SLN 37096, MW, 1:40-2:55, Coor 186

This course will provide an overview of the Navajo Nation, from the origins of the people to our own time. We will examine how and why the Dine emerged as a major indigenous community in the Southwest. The class will be centered primarily in the period from the mid-19th century to the present. Thus we will consider such topics as the Long Walk, imprisonment at Fort Sumner, the Treaty of 1868, the importance of livestock, the evolution of weaving, the addition of additional lands, schooling, traders and trading posts, the attempted imposition of federal authority, the creation of chapters and the council, livestock reduction, World War II, Navajo leadership, modern economic development, and urbanization. We will not ignore the presence of racism and discrimination, but we will emphasize the ability of the Navajos to forge their own future. Along the way we will discover what constitutes the heart of history--not the memorization of random dates and facts but rather the power of memory, the creation of tradition, the significance of place, the richness of language, and the employment of imagination. The instructor taught at Navajo Community College (now Dine College) during its first years and has taught American Indian history for more than 30 years. This semester will mark the third time he has offered this class at ASU.

HST 302: Jazz and Culture in America
Dr. Andrew Barnes
SLN: 46620, Internet

This course provides a survey of the cultural reaction to jazz from its emergence as a distinct form of music in the first decades of the twentieth century to its inclusion in the intellectual academy as an accepted form of music in the last decades of the twentieth century. Through the study of texts and music, the course hopes to provide students with a sense of how both jazz and its audiences evolved over the century. While some consideration will be given to jazz as it was experienced in Europe and the rest of the world, the main focus will be on how jazz developed in the United States. Because the concern is to connect jazz styles with other contemporary cultural phenomena, coverage will decline after 1970, by which time most of the styles presently maintained had come into existence.

HST 306: Race and the Law in US History
Dr. T. J. Davis
SLN 70970, M, 6:40-9:30, SS 229

Relations between races have determined much of the history of the United States of America. Indeed, the United States ? as elsewhere in what came to be called the Americas ?  developed as a massive encounter between and among groups identified racially. Race and the Law in U.S. History surveys the historical development of public policy and attitudes toward groups identified in U.S. history as races, that is as groups perceived as significantly different ? culturally, physically, or socially. The course treats race as a constructed reality, not as a matter of natural science.  It pursues the contours of legal doctrines and social practice from the framing of the U.S. Constitution in 1787 to the 21st Century. It examines the roles of law in creating, sustaining, or negating racial identity; it also examines the roles of law in promoting progressive or repressive treatment of racial groups. In that regard, the course pays particular attention to "legal restrictions which curtail the civil rights of a single racial group," as U.S. Supreme Court Justice Hugo L. Black put it in Korematsu v. United States, 323 U.S. 214, 216 (1944), which treated the United States' internment of Japanese-Americans in concentration camps during World War II (1941-1945). Thomas J. Davis, Race Relations in America (Westport CT: Greenwood Press, 2006) serves as the course text.

HST 306: American Environmental History
Dr. Paul Hirt
TTh, 10:40-11:55, PEWB 144
SLN 58863

This course examines environment and society in America?past and present.  Through historical and contemporary readings, class discussions, and various writing assignments we will consider 300 years of human occupation of North America and its environmental consequences, paying special attention this semester to environmental problem-solving, the evolution of environmental values in the U.S., environmental politics since 1945, and, as a bonus, the history of the Grand Canyon.

HST 306: War and American Society
Dr. Brooks Simpson
TTh, 9:15-10:30, LL 60
SLN 65984

War has been fundamental to shaping the American nation-state and its social order: in turn, how a society wages war reflects the values and priorities of that society. This course will offer both a chronological and thematic overview of Americans at war, both on the battlefront and the home front, from the colonial origins of British North America through the present day. Students will prepare a research paper on some aspect of the American military experience: lectures and readings will highlight the major themes of the course.

HST 307: Slavery in Africa
Dr. Chouki El Hamel
SLN: 79765, TTh, 1:40-2:55, ED 204

Although one of the major topics of current historical research has been the African diaspora in the Americas, a less researched, although equally important, aspect of the global African diaspora is that internal to Africa. Over time, blacks migrated --voluntarily and for the most part involuntarily-- across the Sahara to North Africa. The islamization of northern Africa in the seventh century led to a huge increase in trade, especially in the trans-Saharan slave trade. This course will focus on the institution of slavery in Africa and its different forms in different historical periods and geographical locations.  We will examine theories that have developed in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries among scholars of different origins in their attempt to reconstruct the history of slavery in Africa.  This course is designed to provide students with an overview of significant issues focusing on the historical, economic, political, and cultural relationships among and between black sub-Saharan Africans and North Africans.  We will examine how race/ethnicity and gender; trade and religion affected African slavery; the impact of the decline of the trans-Saharan trade on slave trading and slavery; the impact of notions of gender on the trading of slaves; the historical relationship between black Africans and their descendants in North Africa; the self perception (identity) of the early blacks in North Africa; the emancipation of slaves; the social role and the economic status of freed slaves, and finally we will analyze the current ideas and existing knowledge in the study of the Africa Diaspora.

HST 498: The Russian Revolutionary Movement
Dr. Laurie Manchester
SLN: 48283, MW 5:15-6:30, ED 228

This seminar focuses on the intellectuals, workers and peasants who contributed to the Russian revolutionary movement which culminated in the communist revolution of 1917. By reading together different types of translated primary documents written by revolutionaries and Russian citizens, utilizing theoretical works to analyze such documents, and studying recent and classic historiographical debates on the theological, ideological, cultural and social origins and outcome of the revolution, students will acquire the skills to research and write their senior research seminar paper on any topic of their choice connected with the revolutionary movement. Research topics can span from an aspect of the revolution = s nineteenth century origins to a facet of its legacy in the post-revolutionary Soviet period. In addition, students will be required to give several extended oral presentations over the course of the semester, write book reviews on a scholarly monograph they are employing for their research papers, write a short paper on a historiographical debate, hand in a rough draft, preliminary bibliography and prospectus of their final paper, and participate actively during each class meeting.
 

HST 498: Europe and Africa
Dr. Chouki El Hamel
SLN: 74042, TTh 10:40-11:55, ED 232

This course is about Europe's relationship with Africa since the first significant encounter of the Portuguese with Black West Africa in the 15 th century until the modern era.  It was a complex relationship that was at times characterized as cooperative and at other times as antagonistic.  The evolution of this encounter is tied to the tumultuous dynamics of modern history.  First we will talk about the role of black Africans in the making of Mediterranean civilizations and the Impact of Africa on Europe.  Then we will examine the European attitudes toward African civilizations, the Age of European Discovery, early contacts, and colonial relationships.  We will discuss the ideas and notions Africans and Europeans held about each other and how the language used to designate the ?other' changed over time.  Colonized peoples for instance had a foreign language and culture imposed on them, but they have also appropriated them as tools to resist the colonial regime and sometimes to solve postcolonial problems. Topics to be examined include concepts of slavery in and out of Africa, the Diaspora, Orientalism, European imperialism, colonialism, the impact of colonial rule, religious and cultural movements, the rise of nationalism, the creation of racial theories and issues of ethnicity and identities in modern Africa.

HST 498: History of Racism
Dr. Chouki El Hamel
SLN: 68997, W, 6:40-9:30, ART 240

This course is designed to provide students with an overview of significant issues focusing on relationship between ethnicity, ?race', class and gender.  This course will focus on the different approaches to racism and racialized identities to analyze structures of power relations in different historical periods and geographical locations.  We will examine theories on human classification as a process of exclusion and generating inequality form antiquity to pseudo-scientific modern racism.  We will read important texts to help us understand the origins, formation and development of ideas of ?race' and racism in the context of religious crusades, slavery, colonialism and imperialism.  As a case study we will discuss the ideas and notions Africans and Westerners held about each other and how the language used to designate the ?other' changed over time.  Colonized peoples for instance had a foreign language and culture imposed on them, but they have also appropriated them as tools to resist the colonial discourse and regime and sometimes to solve postcolonial problems.

History 498: Racial Barriers in the United States and Canada in the 20th Century
Dr. Wendy Plotkin
TTh, 10:40-11:55, ED 230
SLN: 79744

This course will examine the history of formal and informal barriers to equal residential opportunities for racial and religious minorities in the U.S. in the twentieth century. Formal barriers include government-initiated or -supported measures such as laws barring the naturalization of foreign non-whites as U.S. citizens until the early 1950s; racial zoning ordinances that legally restricted members of particular races to certain areas of cities and towns through the 1940s; racial and religious deed restrictions and restrictive covenants (i.e. private mutual agreements to not sell or lease property to specified groups) drawn up by real estate developers and property owners and enforced by U.S. courts until 1953; and federal, state, and local programs that included explicit racialist provisions. We will also investigate government-initiated or -supported measures (such as exclusionary/?expulsive? zoning and urban renewal) that were ?race neutral? on the surface but, intentionally or unintentionally, reduced residential opportunities for racial and religious minorities. Informal barriers include the use of violence, harassment, threats, persuasion and other means of preventing racial and religious minorities from residing in certain parts of the city or suburbs. Among the theoretical issues we will examine are the role of law and the state in creating and sustaining racism, the significance of residence in modern societies, and nature of racism in the post-Civil Rights U.S.

HST 498: U.S. HISTORY IN 20 TH CENTURY
Dr. Donald Fixico
T Th 12:15-1:30, EDB 208
SLN 14890

This is an undergraduate research seminar for history major on U.S. History in the 20 th Century with an emphasis regarding American Indians.  Each person will research primary and secondary sources to complete a 25 page research paper, plus bibliography and title page with an abstract on an approved subject by the instructor.  The objective of this course is to familiarize students with primary and secondary sources while researching, analyzing and constructing an argument or thesis to be proven from the sources used.  Keep in mind that a quality research paper of primary and secondary sources could be used for sample writing for applying to graduate or law school.  We will also read and analyze the scholarship of 3 articles published in the Journal of American History.  (Go to the web site of the Organization of American Historians under publications, click Journal of American History, click JAH cover, and click to the correct issue.  You need to do this from the library.)

HST498: British Empire Seminar
Dr. Kathryn Lynass
MW, 1:40-2:55, ED 232
SLN 49628

This course is designed for graduating seniors in history and coalesces what students have learned over the course of their studies in history.  The course will provide a critical understanding of the issues, figures, and events that shaped the development of the British Empire from its beginnings in the sixteenth centuries, through its heyday in the nineteenth century, its decolonization in the twentieth century, and its changed role in the present.  Students will write a finished paper on their own research topic based on information learned in the course, thus gaining a firm foundation for understanding the political, economic, social, and cultural developments in the British Empire and developing their abilities to examine important questions within the history of the British Empire.

HST 498: Memoir, Revolution, and War: Europe and the Russian Revolution
Dr. Pamela Stewart
MW 5:15-6:30
SLN: 48283

This seminar examines the historical contexts of first-person narratives of revolutions and wars in Europe, focusing especially on the events and timeframe of the Russian Revolution.  Students will read memoirs, other translated primary documents, select secondary sources about the subjects at hand, and study historiographical issues relevant to events and the use of autobiographical accounts in the production of history.  By doing so, students will acquire the skills to research and write their senior research seminar paper on any topic of their choice associated with the course.  Topics can include a particular component of the Russian Revolution, its origins, or the development of the Soviet state through World War II; they may also address aspects of European revolutions and wars between approximately 1870 through 1945.  All research must engage at least one first-person account as a significant, though not singular, component of the final paper.  Students will be required to participate in seminar discussions and at times, guide them when discussing assigned readings.  In addition to short library assignments, students will write a book review on a scholarly monograph they are using for their research and write a brief essay on a historiographical issue relevant to their research.  They will submit for review a preliminary bibliography (primary and secondary sources) and prospectus of their paper, and participate in peer reviews of their paper's introductory paragraphs.  First drafts of papers will also be collected.  Finally, students will also give an oral presentation of their finished project, utilizing PowerPoint, not more than 10 minutes in length.
