How do I know if an E-Mail is Legitimate?

Determining if an e-mail arriving in your account is legitimate is a sometimes tricky endevor. Here are some tips for how to recognize a fake e-mail and what to do if you receive one.

Recognizing phishing mails

It is easy to uncover a crude phishing scam. For example, if you get an e-mail from a bank you’ve never opened an account at, then don’t follow the link and enter your personal information. Now, if you actually have an account at the institution it gets more interesting.

You’ll want to look at the message carefully to see if it is a phishing scam. Are words misspelled? Sometimes scammers operate in a second language and they give themselves away by using poor grammar.

You should also examine the link provided. Does it really go where it appears to go? For example, I could tell you that I’m giving you access to the government’s Top Secret Database at https://www.TopSecretDatabase.gov but if you click the link you’ll find that you’ve been directed to a different site. The best way to prevent this is to copy and paste the link (don’t click it) to your address bar. However, you can still get tricked by URL’s that look legitimate but have one or two letters switched.

The best way to avoid becoming a phishing scam victim is to use your best judgment. No financial institution with any sense will e-mail you and ask you to input all of your sensitive information. In fact, most institutions are informing customers that “We will never ask you for your personal information via phone or e-mail”.

Remember! If you are unsure whether or not an e-mail from a bank or other company is valid, you can always give the company a call to see if it is real or not. It is better to be sure than to give away your account information to a strange.

Reporting phishing mails

Before reporting these types of e-mails to email-q@asu.edu or to the Postmaster of the originating node, you should ALWAYS include the header information of the e-mail message in question. Since e-mail addresses in spam e-mails are usually forged, the header information is necessary in order to attempt to track the source of the note. Without the header information, WMA (or the Postmaster of the originating domain) cannot take any action to deter future abuse.

Where Do I Find Header Information In My E-Mail?

  • In Pine, while viewing the message, press "H".

  • In Outlook for PC, while viewing the message, from the View menu, select Options.

  • In Outlook for Mac, while viewing the message, from the File menu, select Properties.

For unsupported e-mail packages that are not listed here, refer to the help features of that package.
