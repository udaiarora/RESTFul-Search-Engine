"Student Health and Wellness Center Formulary Design and Management"

Based on a presentation by Carl Labbe, R. Ph. & Alan Andresen, M.D.@American College Health AssociationAnnual MeetingSan Diego, CAJune 3, 1998


Student Health and Wellness Center pharmacies are, for the most part, small in size and serve specific, generally healthy patient populations. Most operate under some type of formulary. Many patients and even providers have negative feelings towards formularies resulting from bad experiences with restrictive managed care formularies. This usually is a result of having perceived that our access to care was being limited. Many of these managed care organizations, such as a hospital or HMO, base their formulary decisions on pharmacoeconomic research. Student Health formularies will not and should not mirror those of nearby managed care organizations. This does not mean that Student Health formularies should not be built upon sound pharmacoeconomic principles. Formularies, used properly, are useful tools to manage costs and quality in any setting. Good formulary design and management can increase clinician efficiency, reduce costs, maximize therapeutic benefits, reduce workloads and most importantly, improve patient health. Both patients and providers will accept a well-managed formulary, enmeshed in responsible and caring policies. Credibility is very important. If your committee makes well reasoned and well-justified choices, then your formulary will be immune to all but positive criticism.

The P&T Committee
A formulary is normally designed and maintained by a Pharmacy and Therapeutics (P&T) Committee. A representative P&T committee is essential for good formulary management and acceptance. Choosing committee members that are interested and that will aggressively support their personal point of view makes for a healthy P&T committee. Physicians, pharmacists and nurses should be well represented. The idea of randomly assigning personnel from each Student Health department does not result in a representative committee. The result is too many disinterested, unproductive members and a few interested members who can control the direction of the committee.

The committee should be selected with input from the chairperson. Various prescriber types should have a representative on the committee. For example, an acute care physician, a GYN nurse practitioner, a general medicine clinician, and a psychiatrist might be your prescribers appointed to the P&T committee. In any case, an effort should be made to involve clinicians with different perspectives on prescribing. Be sure to represent nursing on a P&T committee. Their input has often proven to be highly relevant and representative of a unique clinical perspective. Pharmacist involvement is essential. For our Student Health center we also include a health educator. If possible, a dedicated, knowledgeable secretary is highly desirable.

The Pharmacy & Therapeutics Committee is responsible for three essential areas of Student Health operation: Formulary Management, Drug Utilization Review (DUR) and Policy and Procedure (P&P) maintenance as pertains to pharmaceuticals. The previous sentence is a mission statement for a P&T committee. Your mission statement does not need to be complex. Methodology, membership, motivation and procedures can be outlined elsewhere. Be sure your mission statement reflects what the committee has been chartered to accomplish. In some situations the P&T committee may only influence P&P through an advisory capacity.

Formulary Design
A formulary is a tool, which should be designed to serve the needs of its users. Physicians and other prescribers, patients, pharmacists, nurses and, of course, budget administrators are all concerned that the formulary serve their needs. A well designed formulary will satisfy all these needs, and be readily accepted, if it has been created with the involvement of the interested parties and implemented in a forthright manner.

There are several things to consider when designing or modifying a formulary:

1. Patient population. In college health, the patient population tends towards young and healthy individuals. This group may, however, have tremendous demands for services and medications for contraception, STD prevention and treatment, mental health and various other areas. Also, many campuses have a large international population with needs that may differ from indigenous students.

2. Physician preference/expertise. Physicians and other prescribers are individuals that tend to make therapeutic selections based on prior experience and acquired knowledge. Many times this familiarity is desired in a clinical setting and should be supported by P&T whenever reasonable. However, they should be willing to listen and learn when new, superior products become available. Communication with the medical staff and the Continuing Medical Education (CME) committee is important.

3. Allied health care provider preferences. Nurses, pharmacists and other health care providers often have pertinent information based on their own perspectives. Nurses often point out differences in dosage forms or delivery systems that impact ease of use and therefore man-hours. Pharmacists provide a good understanding of therapeutic equivalencies and can provide usage data for retrospective drug studies.

4. Drug Utilization Evaluation. Whenever possible, drugs being considered for formulary status can be compared to other treatment choices available. A brief comparative study, based on package inserts, drug monographs and current pricing data, should be prepared and distributed to committee members before the next scheduled meeting. This will allow all participants to prepare for the issues to be discussed. Samples of this type of "handout" can be seen in Exhibits 1 and 2. These particular studies were authored by Russell Newman a Pharm. D. candidate from the University of Arizona College of Pharmacy on a site rotation. Drug monographs, package inserts and other informational materials are also appropriate.

5. Drug Utilization Review. Retrospective studies of drug use can be very helpful when reviewing the status of drugs currently on the formulary. Sometimes a simple utilization report from the pharmacy is all that is needed to show that a drug no longer warrants a place on the formulary. If a drug shows little or no movement over a significant amount of time it lacks the velocity that is desired for most formulary products. Several drugs with similar indications may also be reviewed for cost effectiveness.

6. Pricing. The cost of the drug being considered or reviewed can be an important factor. Again, the pharmacist can be an important source of information, providing current costs and notification of price changes to the committee. The cost of a drug is significant when considering cost-effectiveness, especially when drug supply costs have been rising at an annual rate ranging from 9% to 20%. Many Student Health centers are eligible for various preferred pricing contracts either as state institutions and/or teaching facilities. Oral contraceptives are one category where institutional pricing can have a profound impact on product selection.

Drug Utilization Review (DUR)
In addition to the DURs conducted for formulary modification, these studies can be helpful to document prescribing habits. This information can be used to assist other departments that wish to determine if their programs are effective in changing behaviors. For example, reports can be created that show last months nursing in-service on self-care led to an increase in triage nurse recommended non-prescription medication. Pharmacy software that supports this function is helpful and is available with most prescription processing applications.

Cost Effectiveness Studies
When performing DUE or DUR you will often encounter or create cost effectiveness studies. These studies attempt to measure the relative cost effectiveness of a particular medication. Be sure to pay close attention to the type of study that you are looking at, as there are degrees of pertinence depending on the type of study. The attached exhibits are rudimentary cost effectiveness studies that look merely at cost per dose or container. Some studies will examine cost per treatment. The preferred study is one that determines cost per outcome. We want to know the best drug based on the cost for a successful outcome with minimal adverse effects and/or subsequent clinician visits. We find looking at cost per treatment to be inadequate.

Policy and Procedure
The P&T committee is responsible for drafting and modifying the Student Health service Policy and Procedure manual section regarding pharmaceuticals and pharmacy services. Once again the pharmacist can alert the committee to changes in procedure necessitated by external forces that require changes in the P&P manual. Physicians and nurses will require P&P changes periodically due to staffing changes, procedural modifications and alterations in services provided. The committee should periodically review the pharmacy section of the manual to make necessary changes or additions. This routine review will save much time when an accreditation survey is approaching.

Two policies that have a dramatic effect on pharmacy services are related by result. One policy provides the pharmacy with the ability to fill prescriptions from providers not affiliated with the Student Health service. These providers, of course, have no knowledge of the formulary. The second policy, then, allows the pharmacy to order products not on the formulary. It is best to order only stock sizes that can be dispensed as a unit for a specific prescription. In other words, you don’t want any "leftovers" in the pharmacy inventory. This policy also gives the Student Health staff more flexibility, and allows the pharmacy to order infrequently prescribed or expensive medications on an "as needed" basis. For most pharmacies, this will only delay initiation of treatment by one day. These two policies can dramatically increase the level of service of a Student Health pharmacy.

All policies are subject to approval of the Management Team, the governing body, administrative committee or director depending on your organization.

Departmental Indicators
Continual monitoring and periodic reporting of selected information can be very helpful to the P&T committee. These indicators can be established within departments to monitor various clinical or therapeutic activities. In our pharmacy, for example, we have monitored the prescribing of the "morning after pill (MAP)," the number of pharmacy interventions and, in cooperation with one of our asthma team clinicians, established an asthma indicator. The MAP indicator not only monitors the count of these prescriptions but also identifies repeat patients who are then offered additional counseling on their next visit. Pharmacy interventions are tracked to document pharmacist involvement in preventing drug interactions, patient allergies or inappropriate dosages or therapies. This indicator has resulted in specific CME programs being offered to prescribers as well as changes to the formulary. Our asthma indicator is a measure obtained by dividing the number of bronchodilator inhalers dispensed by the number of anti-inflammatory inhalers dispensed. This dividend, when viewed over time, has given us some indication of the impact of asthma treatment and adherence to established treatment guidelines at our Student Health center. Other indicators can be created to fit your particular needs. These indicators are reported to P&T and will often trigger involvement with other committees or departments.

Interactivity
The P&T committee should be a highly interactive element of a Student Health Center. P&T provides information to administration, clinicians, nurses, other staff and patients. P&T often interacts with other committees, such as Quality Assurance (QA), Continuing Medical Education (CME) and occasionally Plant Safety/Risk Management. Input from the Student Health Advisory Committee (SHAC) should also be encouraged and considered. This group is well suited to present the needs of your patient population from their own perspective. P&T minutes should be distributed to all department heads, administrators and other interested parties. It is important to make every effort to notify all prescribers, including contracted consultants, of any and all formulary changes. We make use of a pharmacy newsletter to alert clinicians to formulary changes, usage velocities by categories and general prescribing information pertinent to our student health center. Remember too, that P&T activities often have implications for Quality Assurance, Risk Management, Continuing Medical Education, Practice Guidelines or other committees or groups with similar functions. Our formulary is available at our web site for added convenience.

Certain committee members should serve as liaisons with their respective sections or departments. Every effort should be made to advise other committees and departments of the activities of the P&T committee. Many changes may appear to affect only a limited area, but will actually have a ripple effect throughout the facility. Communication will prevent misunderstandings.

Chairperson Considerations
The Director of Student Health and Wellness Center usually designates the chairperson of the P&T committee. Often, a departing chairperson will make a recommendation for a successor to the Director. Being a committee chairperson is sometimes a somewhat thankless task and often requires dedication above and beyond their job classification. There is a lot of work to do. In any case, the P&T chairperson should be committed, not to an asylum, but to managing an effective, reasonable and credible committee.

The chairperson should be a person unafraid to delegate. He or she shouldn’t hesitate to assign research projects to appropriate committee members. Be sure to allow sufficient time to accomplish assignments. It is best to get the agenda and any related readings to members a least one week before the scheduled meeting. Don’t forget to appoint liaisons to disseminate information to other groups. Encourage input from all members and make sure that the entire staff knows that they can make a formulary modification request. There are times when it is appropriate for a requester to be invited to the P&T meeting to support his or her request.

Drug Displays
Another way to get the clinical staff involved in drug selection is to schedule "drug displays." Pharmaceutical representatives can arrange a room in your facility to display their products. This avoids the problem of drug reps waiting in the hallways with patients, hoping to impose on the clinician’s time "between patients." We schedule a drug display for at least two hours, usually 8:00 A.M. to 10:30 A.M. or 11:00 A.M. to 1:30 P.M. These time slots allow the prescribers time to learn about new products at their convenience. It is always made clear that these drug displays are for all staff and we have been very successful with this program.

The New Formulary
A new formulary can be designed using these principles and methods. Many student health centers initiating a formulary can obtain copies of similarly sized schools’ formularies for review and revision. If a formulary is being created for an existing health center, the P&T committee should perform a comprehensive chart review to ascertain the prescribing preferences of its medical staff. A new formulary will often be much like a lump of clay, unappealing to most observers, but ready to be molded and shaped to fit the needs of the facility it serves. It is the challenge of the P&T committee to create a work of art from that lump of clay.

Conclusion
Your formulary can be a helpful tool for the entire staff if designed thoughtfully by credible means. Your Student Health center’s P&T committee is the entity responsible for designing and maintaining the formulary. Make sure it is representative of the clinical staff. Each student health center is different and may need to adapt this model to suit their needs and/or practice guidelines. Make every effort to get those most affected by the formulary involved in its creation and maintenance. Remember to consider the concerns of your patient population and desired therapeutic outcomes. Many drugs can be kept off the formulary if they lack the velocity for inclusion but can be ordered (usually with only a one-day wait) on an as needed basis. Try to develop a regular review schedule that covers all formulary areas so the committee will be proactive rather than reactive. With a little work and plenty of diligence a formulary can be friendly. More importantly, the formulary and the process of maintaining it can result in improved health intervention outcomes.
