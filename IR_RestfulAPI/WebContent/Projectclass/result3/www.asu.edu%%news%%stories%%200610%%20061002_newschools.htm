October 3, 2006

Interdisciplinary efforts bring new thinking to old problems

New schools at ASU ? Putting the pieces in place

Over the last few years, ASU has made major strides putting the pieces in place to realize the goal of becoming a New American University. Take a closer look at the newest schools at ASU. Read more

Jon Fink, a trained geologist, became interested in certain problems in volcanology based on observations he made hiking around volcanoes. Was it possible to predict how fast a lava flow would advance toward a town in its path? Could he model in the laboratory the relationship among how fast the lava flow moved, its underlying slope and how long it had been traveling?

?I initially learned what I could on my own from some engineering courses, but the big breakthroughs came from working closely with a physicist who had a lot of experience in modeling how fluids flow,? says Fink, ASU's vice president of research and economic affairs. ?If I were unwilling to go beyond my discipline, I would have understood less about how the flows developed ? and I would have been able to make fewer predictions about how such flows would behave.?

Interdisciplinary research commonly requires an expert in one field to collaborate with experts in others. But building these collaborations requires considerable effort ? from identifying the right expert in another discipline to writing a successful grant proposal and finding a suitable place to publish the results.

ASU is making it easier for these interactions to develop by putting people from different disciplines together in the same departments, schools, centers and institutes. Fink says ASU is one of the world's most aggressive universities in promoting this kind of experimentation.

The most challenging and critical problems facing society lie at the intersection of fields, and this is why one of ASU President Michael Crow's imperatives for the New American University is ?Intellectual Fusion? ? to create new knowledge by transcending academic disciplines.

Interdisciplinarity involves attacking a subject from various methods and cutting across disciplines to better understand the subject or problem, while a transdisciplinary approach dissolves boundaries, and becomes necessary when something cannot be understood from within a single discipline. An example is sustainability, which originally developed in environmental studies but is now more fully understood with insights from psychology, economics, sociology and other disciplines.

People have long valued an interdisciplinary approach to problem-solving. What is new, says Fred Corey, director of the School of Interdisciplinary Studies in University College, ?is the intentional, focused approach to interdisciplinary thinking as a valuable mode of inquiry? in higher education.

The College of Liberal Arts and Sciences is undergoing a fundamental redesign that breaks down traditional organizational structure in favor of a transdisciplinary configuration.

New schools in the college that reflect this include the School of Earth and Space Exploration, which fuses astronomy and geology with system engineering to produce a new generation of explorers.

The School of Social and Family Dynamics also merges faculty and programs from two previously separate departments ? family and human development with sociology ? to study and advance the most important and valued aspects of human life.

?The ultimate goal is to provide students with the ideal liberal arts education, where they learn in a setting that emphasizes the integration of ideas,? says David Young, dean of the College of Liberal Arts and Sciences.

Jane Maienschein, School of Life Sciences Regent's Professor and Parents Association Professor, says research increasingly requires drawing from multiple disciplines and graduate students, who are traditionally expected to work in one field, benefit from taking courses and studying in other areas.

?We are beginning to develop modular approaches to teaching, so that students can take one unit of, say, statistics or ethics or history to add to their lab research in the sciences,? she says. ?The idea is to make researchers multiple-disciplined so that, while they focus on problem-solving and discovery, they have multiple tools, skills and perspectives.?

Fink points out that faculty members also are winners in ASU's new approach. They benefit by learning more, being able to solve interesting and socially relevant problems, being more competitive for research grant dollars, and being able to attract the brightest students, many of whom are drawn to these new ?emerging problem spaces.?

Jason Robert, a new assistant professor in the School of Life Sciences who is trained as a philosopher, says it was ASU's commitment to transcending academic disciplines that brought him to the university.

?My wife and I left our families 3,500 miles away and came to Tempe so that I could be part of this remarkable experiment in intellectual fusion, as Michael Crow describes it,? he says. ?Being part of the human dimensions faculty group, and being associated with the Center for Biology and Society ? and also the Consortium for Science, Policy, and Outcomes ? gives me a pretty unique opportunity to be embedded deep within an integrative scientific research enterprise, and I just love it.?
