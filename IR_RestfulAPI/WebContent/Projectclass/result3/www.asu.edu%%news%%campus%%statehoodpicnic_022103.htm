February 24, 2003

Statehood Picnic welcomes new legislative members

The ASU Alumni Association sponsored its 20th annual Statehood Picnic, Feb. 17. The informal gathering at Wesley Bolin Plaza, located near the state capitol, has been an annual event since 1983 when the picnic was first held to honor the legislature for its ASU support.

This year, the gathering of ASU supporters, students, parents, faculty and staff welcomed more than 30 new state legislative members and their staff to the tradition. More than 600 people attended the picnic, a record number for the event.
Blake Anderson, ASU state relations director, said the impressive attendance - 45 of the 60 members of the House of Representatives and 15 of the 30 Senate members - demonstrated legislators' great support for the university.

"We are very grateful to legislators for working so diligently to balance the state budget while reaffirming the enormous public contribution the state universities make not only in economic terms but also to the quality of life for Arizona citizens," he said.

Gail Howard, economic policy adviser for Arizona Governor Janet Napolitano, said although the governor was not able to attend the event in person, she supports the universities and their teaching and research missions.

"The governor knows the state universities are a critical engine for the Arizona economy and now is not the time to be making Draconian cuts to their budgets," Howard said. ASU President Michael Crow told the state legislature that providing the necessary financial resources to fund research infrastructure is a key priority for the university. An investment in ASU today will not only help establish Arizona as an important industry force, but it will also enable the university to become a more independent, self-funded entity.

The Arizona Biodesign Institute (AzBio) Mobile Bone Density Lab, a 30-foot long motorized research center, made its debut at the event as one of many quality exhibits presented by ASU students and faculty. Kathy Matt, AzBio assistant director, said the newly completed lab-on-wheels is an integral component to fulfilling the institute's mission of research, teaching and service.

Matt, who researches the effects of stress on the human body, said the motorized lab will reach community populations who may not be familiar with the university's research or other community outreach programs.

Two former students of Matt's, Shauna Traub and Christy Dueck, donated the mobile lab to ASU in December. In making the donation, Matt's former students made their mentor's longtime dream - to bring her research into communities - a reality. Matt has applied for grants that would allow her to also bring the state-of-the-art equipment into economically disadvantaged neighborhoods for much needed health screenings.

Besides offering community bone density assessments, the mobile lab also measures body fat and muscle mass. The test data is then mapped to determine participants' individualized risk for certain disease. Once identified, at-risk participants will also receive counseling on possible intervention measures to slow or eliminate the risk factors.

Matt says most diseases are contracted through a combination of genetic and environmental factors.

"Identifying a potential health problem before it occurs is an important first step toward creating a society with less disease and other serious health problems," she said.

Marla Giarratano, director of ASU Alumni Association Constituent Relations, says her organization and its Phoenix chapter appreciate the generosity of dozens of volunteers who have helped to make the Statehood Picnic successful over the last two decades.

"This is a great opportunity to showcase the wonderful research being done at ASU and to let our legislators see how the university community supports the greater vision of Arizona's future," Giarratano said. "We are so pleased to be able to continue this tradition."
