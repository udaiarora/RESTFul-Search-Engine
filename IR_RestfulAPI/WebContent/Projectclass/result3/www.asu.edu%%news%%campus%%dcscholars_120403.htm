December 4, 2003

Live and learn in Washington DC; scholars internship program

TEMPE, AZ, December 3, 2003 - If you're an undergraduate, it's not too early to start thinking about your 2004 summer plans. If you want to develop leadership skills, check out the prestigious Capitol Scholars Program, sponsored by the ASU College Liberal Arts and Sciences, May 25-July 25, 2004 in Washington, DC.

The Capitol Scholars program gives undergraduates the opportunity to live and work in the nation's capitol. Students not only gain valuable work experience on Capitol Hill and in various public policy governmental and nongovernmental organizations, but also have the opportunity to discuss policy issues with elected representatives and White House appointees.

"The ASU Capitol Scholars Program trains future leaders by providing them with an educational experience rich in both academic and applied knowledge," said Dr. Michelle Chin, program director. "The program is unique in its approach to integrating theory and applied learning, and connecting students with the community, policymakers and alumni. ASU is the only state-supported university to institutionalize a program of this type."

In 2003, Stephen Szostak worked for US Senator Jon Kyl; Lynette Summerill developed a media strategy for the NABRE project at the Joint Center for Political and Economic Studies. Jessica Snorgrass traveled to San Diego, CA to do press advance work for the director of the White House Office of National Drug Control Policy. Mike Ritchie worked on state-level and federal-level trucking issues at the American Trucking Association, then continued his research of highway/transportation issues in Ukraine during his internship at the US Embassy in Kiev, Ukraine. Winter Dowland coordinated an event for the Arthritis Foundation; they paid for her to return in September to attend the event. Scot Mussi worked with George Ramones at The Advocacy Group and took the initiative to arrange a meeting for the Capitol Scholars with Senate Majority Leader Bill Frist. Jessie Sandoval prepared briefing reports for a prospective House Energy and Commerce subcommittee trip to Europe. Bethany Lay wrote a report on alternative fuels for the Federal Highway Administration Office of Transportation Policy. Chad Kennedy's supervisors in the government relations office at Amtrak were so impressed with his work that they ordered him to return to DC next year to continue working with Amtrak.

In one of her weekly class assignments, Winter Dowland wrote: "This experience has been eye opening, and I would gladly do it again. This trip has taught me more than I could have imagined, most of which required me to just be here to experience things first hand. I have met so many fantastic people, who will serve as valuable contacts and mentors as I move forward."

At the end of the summer, Chad Kennedy reflected, "In just seven short weeks I have amassed unprecedented knowledge through personal exposure to the intricacies of our country's political process, established various gratifying personal and professional relationships that will benefit my future, toured major cities throughout the east coast, and visited all of the significant historical sites that embody the heart and soul of America. I have grown so fond and accustomed to a city I had never before visited, and a job that exceeded every one of my expectations, so much so that I do not want to leave just yet. I have been officially infected with a syndrome known around the city as 'Potomac fever.' The only cure for the syndrome is a return in the near future."

ASU Capitol Scholars also earn college credit toward graduation by participating in twice weekly classes and educational field trips, including one that took them to New York's Ground Zero. In DC, Capitol Scholars reside in apartment-style housing at George Washington University, in the heart of DC and just a few blocks from the White House, State Department, Kennedy Center and Watergate.

The ASU Capitol Scholars program is open to all undergraduates and acceptance in the program is determined on academic excellence and participation.

The cost of $3,532 includes housing in DC and New York, roundtrip transportation from DC to New York, some meals, class materials and excursions. Not included are tuition ($742 for 4 credits POS 498), airfare to DC, most meals, local transportation in DC, and incidental expenses. Scholarships are available. All interested applicants are encouraged to apply regardless of their ability to pay for the program.

Applications are available online (www.asu.edu/clas/polisci/capitolscholars) or at the Capitol Scholars office (203 G Matthews Center, before January 9, 2004; 6694 Coor Building, after January 11, 2004). Deadline for general admission and scholarship eligibility is Friday, January 23, 2004. Decisions will be announced February 2, 2004.
