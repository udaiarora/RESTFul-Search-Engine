Unearthing history
Construction site yields relics of vanished town

Shards from a pot in the photo above show the presence of manure (black), used to temper the clay to help it better withstand fire when used for cooking. This archaeological dig occurred in late May under what had been a visitor parking lot at the corner of College Avenue and University Drive in downtown Tempe.

Tempe, Ariz., the home of ASU, is a city built on top of layers of earlier towns. For centuries, the Hohokam had a village on the site, using water from the Salt River to irrigate their farms. And in the late 19th and early 20th centuries the town of San Pablo, a small Hispanic community, occupied the site.

Now a modern college town, no trace of either of these remains, until you start to dig, that is.
When ASU constructed new residence halls north of University Drive in the summer of 2000, excavation of a parking lot revealed Hohokam canals and remnants of a San Pablo boarding house. Now, as the university is preparing the construction site for a new building to house the ASU Foundation, more history has been found lying mere inches beneath another parking lot.

According to ASU Cultural Resource Manager Glen Rice, the site, located at the corner of College Avenue and University Drive, was once the center of downtown San Pablo. Dewey Street, the village’s main drag, ran east-west down the middle of the current parking lot, running from the current Catholic church through Palo Verde West Hall. Most of the buildings of the town disappeared and houses and businesses of old Tempe replaced them. With the growth of the university, these houses in turn were razed.

Surveying the site, Rice expected to find remnants of the older Tempe buildings, but was surprised to also discover the foundation of an older, San Pablo era house.

Roughly dated at 1900 A.D., the ruin includes a floor built with handmade, low-fire bricks, surrounded by a two-foot border of river rocks – the foundation of a thick adobe wall. Adjoining this structure is a newer room with a modern brick wall and cement and wood flooring.

“Clearly someone added on to the building and tried to make it look more up-to-date,” Rice said. “The brick wall represents new technology, though, ironically, it would not have been as comfortable in this climate as the thicker adobe walls, which provide more insulation and can be cooled by sprinkling water around.”

Rice says the adobe design, which includes a wooden post in the middle of the floor to support the roof, was a long-standing building model in Arizona.

“This was an ancient design, made with local materials, well adapted to the area, and probably not too different from houses the Hohokam and other Indians had been building for centuries,” he said.
Also found on the site were a number of artifacts, including a large hand-made Indian pot containing the skeleton of a cat (apparently the burial of a beloved pet). A trash pit yielded numerous glass bottles, which mainly contained veterinary medicines for horses (Rice thinks one of the buildings on the site was a stable), but a Coca Cola bottle (dated 1905 to 1910) is a reminder that the culture here was on the verge of becoming modern America.

However, when the archaeologists dug deeper, they found remnants of an older culture as well. A few feet from University Drive, the team found the remains of ancient Hohokam fire pits at about five feet below the current surface. Rice says the fireplaces, now just rings of soot and ash, are probably signs of field encampments used by the Hohokam while tending their nearby fields.

The dig is being directed by Denise To, a doctoral student in the anthropology department. The team will be excavating the site through May 30.
