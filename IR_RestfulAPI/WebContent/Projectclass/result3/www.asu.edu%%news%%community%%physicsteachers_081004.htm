August 10, 2004

Program aids high school physics teachers

High school science and math teachers from all over the country went back to the classroom this summer as students through the Master of Natural Science (MNS) program at ASU.

Around 150, K - 12 teachers met to attend hands-on workshops in June and July designed to help them become better science instructors. The workshops taught by ASU physics faculty and veteran high school teachers, primarily targets physics teachers, but is also appropriate for chemistry, physical science and math teachers.

According to Larry Dukerich, a modeling workshop leader, only one-third of physics teachers in the country are physics majors. The program provides teachers an opportunity to become more familiar with the content they teach and explores how to use that knowledge in the classroom.

Started in 1995, with a series of workshops, the program expanded to  a full a graduate program in 2001, due to the influence of Physics & Astronomy faculty members, professor emeritus David Hestenes and academic associate Jane Jackson. Instructors can still just attend workshops for professional development or to renew their teaching certificate.

The workshops are unique because they use a modeling method that puts the teacher in the position of a student.

The major emphasis of the program is to let the "students do a lot of the talking with the coaxing and guiding of the instructor," Dukerich says. "The teachers learn to question students to bring out better understanding."

Rich McNamara, Dobson High physics teacher says, "The big problem that students have with science is it's perceived as a bunch of facts, vocabulary and equations to memorize. [The modeling] approach to teaching is much more consistent with the way real science is done."

Jonathan Walters, a student teacher in the Valley, was first taught by a teacher who attended the program and enjoyed coming to the class because it was fun. He is now attending the workshops to improve his own teaching skills.

"I felt like I was learning so much. I wanted to pass that interest on to other students. I want them to be able to understand that science isn't just a set of formulas, but a process of discovery."
