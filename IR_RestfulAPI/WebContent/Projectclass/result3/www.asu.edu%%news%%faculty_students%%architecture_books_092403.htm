September 24, 2003

Professors tackle border environmental issues in books

Two faculty members in the School of Planning and Landscape Architecture have taken a close look at important regional environmental and development issues in recently published books.

The first book, "The U.S. - Mexican Border Region: Overcoming Vulnerability," published by San Diego State University Press, was edited by Planning and Environmental Professor David Pijawka.

The volume chronicles the work of the Southwest Center for Environmental Research and Policy (SCERP), a congressionally established consortium of five U.S. and five Mexican universities, including ASU. Since its establishment in 1990, the group has built a comprehensive analysis of possible solutions to air, water quality and hazardous waste problems that plague the border region.

"The book not only addresses what we have done, but also what we need for the future," says Pijawka, director of the Ph.D. program in Environmental Design and Planning. "It's a retrospective on SCERP, but there are recommendations regarding what we must do for the next 10 years in the region."

Among the issues discussed are the impacts of rapid urbanization and lack of infrastructure, industrial and vehicle based air pollution, surface and ground water contamination, transportation and disposal of hazardous waste and destruction of natural resources. ASU's SCERP program is housed in the Center for Environmental Studies.

Pijawka says the book and the research it is based upon, show the successes of this binational effort.

"We have found that binational collaboration is extremely helpful and useful," he says.

The second book, "Integrated Land Use and Environmental Models," written by Associate Professor Subhrajit Guhathakurta and published by Springer, looks at how urban and environmental models in planning can be designed to complement each other.

It specifically explores the primary modeling techniques of urban and environmental modeling and their differences and similarities. Environmental modeling focuses primarily on non-human natural systems such as plant and animal species, and land, air and water resources. Urban modeling on the other hand, takes into account the human impact and includes political, social and economic concerns.

"The book is really about bringing together two main areas of spatial modeling, urban and environmental," he says. "I'm trying to integrate the two, so that it broadens the horizon of the typical urban model.

"Each area has to engage the other. You can't really think of the environment without the human impact. You also can't ignore the natural environment when building land use change models," Guhathakurta says.

Pijawka says both works tie closely into to ASU initiatives including the Pan American Initiative, Greater Phoenix 2100 and the Consortium for the Study of Rapidly Urbanizing Regions.

"Each of these publications represents significant interdisciplinary efforts and demonstrates the successes of such efforts in real world problem solving," he says.
