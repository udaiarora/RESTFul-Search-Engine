August 14, 2003

Religious studies department helps Arizona teachers, increases outreach

With interest rising in the role of religion in public life around the globe, the ASU Religious Studies Department is helping Arizonans understand the roles different religions play in their own state.

Current Arizona social studies standards require schools to give academic and objective instruction in major world religions. But most public school teachers have not been adequately trained to give such instruction, says David Damrel, religious studies professor.

Damrel and the department hosted 45 middle-school teachers from around the state on July 25, for an outreach conference on "World Religions in Arizona Schools." The teachers were eager to find out how to teach their students about Buddhism, Christianity, Hinduism, Islam, Judaism and Sikhism.

The conference also was sponsored by the ASU Center for the Study of Religion and Conflict and the Arizona Humanities Council.

"There's been so much interest in this from around the state," says Damrel. "We weren't sure what to expect, but we had to put a ceiling on attendance as the conference filled up. Religion touches on the ways different cultures deal with big questions. We gave teachers classroom-ready lesson plans for teaching about religion in a school setting."

The lesson plans were designed by the Arizona Geographic Alliance. Teachers of geography, English, history, art and social studies came from as far away as Chino Valley, Sierra Vista and Tucson.

It's possible to teach about a religion, linking it to cultural traditions, music, art and literature, without advocating for the religion, Damrel says. Teachers can compare and contrast religions by categories: sacred texts, sources of authority and rules of behavior.

"Our challenge is to think about ways of discussing and comparing religions without front-loading the discussion," he says. "Rumor, misinformation and the media shape our ideas about world religions, but this is too important to be left up to the media. It's a complicated subject, one that we need to approach in a responsible way."
Damrel, whose expertise is in Islam, made about 60 presentations in Valley classrooms in the weeks after the terrorist attacks of Sept. 11, 2001, and has continued to work with high schools.

Last year he received a grant from the Harvard "Pluralism Project" to develop a course called "World Religions in Arizona," documenting a broad range of Valley religious communities.

His students in a pilot class last spring went out to churches, synagogues and mosques, conducted interviews and helped write histories on the communities. Owen Anderson, a community college teacher and an ASU religious studies graduate, also helped develop course materials. The results are online at (www.pluralism.org/affiliates/damrel/index.php).

The department hopes to make it part of the regular class offerings.
