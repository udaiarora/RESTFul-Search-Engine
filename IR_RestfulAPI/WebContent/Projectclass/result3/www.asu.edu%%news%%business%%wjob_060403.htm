June 4, 2003

Western states remain in lead amid sluggish U.S. job growth

While most Western states are in the top ranks of job growth, the problem of lagging employment gains nationwide remains of great concern to economists, according to an analysis in the current issue of the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast. The central question in the minds of the experts is whether consumers can be relied upon to continue spending even though the economy appears weak.

Consumer debt as a percentage of disposable income has reached a height not seen for more than 15 years - recording a level of 14 percent in the fourth quarter of 2002, according to Dawn McLaren, editor of the Western Blue Chip. Mortgage debt as a percentage of disposable personal income also has risen sharply - an indication that consumers likely are financing their purchases with equity in their homes. Meanwhile, the growth rate in bankruptcies has been in the double digits in most Western states.

"While business spending, both in human and physical capital, must return to growth in order to get the economy running at full steam, it is consumers who have taken a place of prominence," McLaren writes. "Businesses have been watching consumers' reaction to the threat of terrorism, corporate misconduct and the fall of stock market values, which reduced consumer wealth in investment and retirement funds." It remains to be seen whether businesses will take their cue from consumers and resume spending, even amid uncertain economic times.

Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast publishes the consensus forecasts of panels of economists in 10 Western states. Panelists are drawn from leading firms, universities and state agencies. The Western Blue Chip also publishes historical data for each state.

The Western Blue Chip is published 10 times a year by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute at Arizona State University's W. P. Carey School of Business. The cost is $110 for 10 print issues ($99 for e-mail subscription). For subscription information, call (800) 448-0432.
