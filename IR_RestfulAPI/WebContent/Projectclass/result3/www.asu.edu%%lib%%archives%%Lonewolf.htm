Rosemary Apple Blossom Lonewolf


The exhibit in the Labriola Center features multi-talented Native artist, Rosemary Apple Blossom Lonewolf, 43, who welcomes the “opportunity to show other indigenous women that a dream can take years to see it through development and into fruition.” Exposed to various indigenous crafts in childhood, Lonewolf first began a career as a potter under the direction of her father, Joseph Lonewolf and his sister, Grace Medicine Flower, both leading innovators in contemporary Indian pottery. It was not long before Rosemary Lonewolf began receiving awards for her tiny, innovative, incised seedpots. In time, she also became known for her mural-sized works in clay, and her series of tiles depicting prehistoric American Indians participating in modern day sports are an integral part of the chandler YMCA. Her ceramic mural, Three Sisters, a metaphor for corn, beans, and squash, adorns the inner wall of the Indian garden at the Heard Museum, and was created during a tenure as artist in residence. Recently, the city of Tucson selected her design for a massive bicycle and pedestrian overpass, its concept inspired from the beautiful basketry of the Tohono O’odham people whose prehistoric ancestors inhabited the area. In addition to her work as an artist, she has lectured nationally, and internationally at the first Sino-American Conference on Women’s Issues in Beijing, Peoples Republic of China. Lonewolf believes it is important to draw on the traditions of her culture but at the same time, continue to be receptive to change and adaptation.
