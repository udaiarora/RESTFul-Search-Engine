GENERAL INFORMATION

• In the past, only 45-50 percent of the Indian students returned for their second year, compared to over 70 percent for other ethnic groups. In 1995 the Native staff at ASU set out to change this terrible situation and bring Native students from the lowest achieving group to a better effort in retention and persistency rate.

• Recommended to the tribe that a contract be negotiated between ASU and Navajo Nation and to delegate to the university the operation and monitoring of the scholarship funds provided to Navajo students.

• Involving the tribal government by signing an Intergovernmental Agreement and providing the necessary funds with the university was an important “first step” in the process of student achievement and success.

• It was an important part in the process of getting the parents and tribal officials involved in the education of their children at a major university.

• The next challenge was to recruit more Native American students to come to ASU. "We had to beat the bushes, go to ceremonial events, social gatherings, sporting events, etc. to recruit students".

• The increased success of Indian students at ASU has stimulated more students to want to attend the university. In 1994 the campus enrollment was a little more than 600 Indian students. Today we have over 1,400 Native students.

• Arizona is home to 22 tribes. Now that NAAP has proved its effectiveness with Navajo students, ASU is being asked to expand it to other tribes in the state.

AMERICAN INDIAN INSTITUTE ACADEMIC HONORS LIST

• In 1996, ASU decided to officially honor all Native American students who progressed and who achieved academic success; a total of 56 students whose GPA averaged 3.00 and above were honored.

• In the 2003 Fall semester, ASU produced a record number of 166 students who made the Honor List. The induction ceremony was held February 24, 2004, and students were honored by university faculty and staff. This number represents 15% of the total American Indian Student population.
