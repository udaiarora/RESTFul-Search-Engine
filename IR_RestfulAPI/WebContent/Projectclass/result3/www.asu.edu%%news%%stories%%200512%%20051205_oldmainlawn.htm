Renovation aims to restore Old Main lawn's beauty

Thanks to a $1 million in-kind gift from Phoenix-based Core Construction, ASU’s Old Main soon will look as if it has just stepped out of the history books.

A comprehensive plan
The renovation of the Alumni Lawn represents the culmination of the careful restoration of Old Main to its original majesty for the enjoyment of future Sun Devil generations.

Take a closer look at the renovation plan

The building itself was renovated in the late 1990s – and now its front lawn will be restored to match.

The project, which will be completed in time for spring commencement, will include opening the view from University Drive, creating an area for tented parties and bringing back the historic driveway, says the university’s architect, Ron McCoy.

"The goal is to enhance the beauty of Old Main lawn as a focal point for the university, particularly to the public," McCoy says.

The renovations will include transplanting some of the mature trees, upgrading sidewalks and lighting, and adding landscaping.

One of the major projects is recreating the driveway that once led from University to Old Main.

"Old Main originally had a dramatic drive," McCoy says. "We are going to restore that loop. It will be paved with stabilized, decomposed granite, in keeping with the original drive, and we’ll be able to offer valet parking for special events."

The plans, which have been approved by the Arizona State Historic Preservation Office, call for upgrading the sidewalk and planting more street trees along University to harmonize with the current pattern of trees, in addition to adding subtle uplighting on Old Main in keeping with Arizona’s Dark Sky Ordinance, McCoy says.

The Philomathian Bench, which was given to ASU in 1929 by the literary Philomathian Society, will be moved to a spot near the Piper Writers House, and the lawn will be edged with bougainvilleas and lantana, bringing a touch of ASU’s maroon and gold to the historic area.

Remaining on Old Main lawn will be the historic sundial, which was presented to ASU by the class of 1915.

When the trees are relocated, the lawn will be able to accommodate tent seating up to 4,300 for sit-down dinners, McCoy says.

Core Construction, which is building the McAllister Village, decided to donate the project after a nudge from Diane McCarthy, senior vice president for university and community partnerships at the ASU Foundation, who had worked with the owners of Core on the Challenger Space Center project in Peoria.

Seeds for the renovation were planted when McCarthy joined the Alumni Association as president in November 2003. She realized, standing on Old Main’s third-floor balcony, that she could not see University Drive.

"And you can’t see Old Main from University," she says.

The new driveway resulted from discussions that McCarthy had with Shaun Maurer, president of Core.

"We decided that any change to Old Main lawn should include functionality as well as aesthetics," McCarthy says.

Soon, the construction fence will go up, and earthmovers will arrive to begin carving out that new drive.

There will be dust and disruption on campus, but it will be worth it in the end, McCarthy says.

"The new Old Main lawn will be a gorgeous north end to the campus," he says.
