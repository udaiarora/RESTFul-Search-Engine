ASU to Host Discussion on the Politicization of Science

Stem cell research…. climate change…. genetically-modified organisms…. nanotechnology…. Lately, science is more at home in the political section of the newspaper than on the science pages.

It is a rare thing indeed for science policy to be high on the national political agenda, but the ongoing controversy over the politicization of science in the Bush Administration has received significant coverage in the national media.  Do politics have a larger impact on science under the current administration than under previous ones? Is scientific purity possible - or desirable - when the political stakes are high?

To explore the origins and meaning of this controversy, the Arizona State University’s Consortium for Science, Policy and Outcomes and the Center for the Study of Law, Science, and Technology will host a panel discussion on Wednesday, October 13 in conjunction with the third and final presidential debate, which is being held at ASU’s Tempe Campus.

“Science Defiled - Or Politics as Usual?” will highlight a panel of experts and policy makers from inside and outside the Beltway on the role of science in the political arena. The free public event is scheduled for 3:30 p.m. in Armstrong Hall Room 116.

The panel of science policy experts and policy makers includes:

Charles Arntzen, Director of the Center for Infectious Diseases and Vaccinology in the Biodesign Institute at Arizona State University, and Florence Ely Nelson  Presidential Chair, Arizona State University; Member of the President's Council of Advisors on Science & Technology

Shelley Fidler, Principal, Van Ness Feldman; Former Chief of Staff to the President’s Council on Environmental Quality and Principal Deputy at the White House Climate Change Task Force

Henry Miller, Research Fellow, Hoover Institution, Stanford University, Former Director of the Food and Drug Administration’s Office of Biotechnology

Roger Pielke, Director, Center for Science and Technology Policy Research, University of Colorado

This interactive discussion will be moderated by Robert Melnick, the Director of ASU’s Morrison Institute for Public Policy.

The event is free and open to the public. To RSVP, email cspo@asu.edu or call 480-727-8787. Press interested in attending the conference should call 480-727-8787 in advance to arrange for interviews. For more information visit www.cspo.org or contact the Consortium at 480-727-8787 or cspo@asu.edu.

The Sponsors:
The mission of the Consortium for Science, Policy and Outcomes at Arizona State University is to ensure that science and technology move forward in harmony with broader public goals and aspirations. CSPO aims to enhance the contribution of science and technology to society's pursuit of equality, justice, freedom, and overall quality of life. The Consortium creates knowledge and methods, cultivates public discourse, and fosters policies to help decision makers and institutions grapple with the immense power and importance of science and technology as society charts a course for the future. www.cspo.org

The Center for the Study of Law, Science, and Technology seeks to contribute to the legal system's response to the pervasive and increasing challenges posed by new scientific discoveries and their technological applications. The Center seeks to improve the quality of law and public policy affecting science and technology and supports work in an important reciprocal vein: the scientific study and understanding of law, legal institutions and legal process. The Center encourages interdisciplinary research; promotes development and review of the College of Law curriculum; and provides service for law students, attorneys and others.  http://www.law.asu.edu/?folderid=2720

The Center for Biology and Society promotes research on the conceptual foundations of the biosciences and their interactions with society, through the exploration of Bioethics, Biopolicy, Biology and Law, History and Philosophy of Science, and Sociology of Science. We bring together dispersed research and outreach activities relating to the interactions of the life sciences and society, providing a dynamic locus that expands opportunities for intellectual ferment and increased visibility and funding by creating synergistic collaborations and communication. <http://lifesciences.asu.edu/centerbiosoc/>

Panelists:

Charles Arntzen, Director of the Center for Infectious Diseases and Vaccinology, Arizona Biodesign Institute, and Florence Ely Nelson Presidential Chair, Arizona State University. Dr. Arntzen served as the Founding Director of the Arizona Biodesign Institute until May 2003. He is currently serves on President Bush's Council of Advisors on Science & Technology, is a member of the National Academy of Sciences, and a fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science. Dr. Arntzen is a Regents Professor at ASU and has been recognized as a pioneer in the development of plant-based vaccines for human disease prevention and for disease prevention in animal agriculture.

Shelley Fidler, Principal, Van Ness Feldman. Ms. Fidler assists clients by finding solutions to complex natural resource and energy problems that require in-depth knowledge of the political and institutional makeup of Congress and the Executive Branch. Ms. Fidler served as Principal Deputy at the White House Climate Change Task Force, an interagency group charged with coordinating President Clinton’s climate change policy. From 1995 to 1998, Ms. Fidler served as the Chief of Staff to the President’s Council on Environmental Quality (CEQ).

Rob Melnick, Director of the Morrison Institute for Public Policy, Arizona State University. Dr. Melnick conducts research on urban growth management, education reform and economic development. He is the co-author of three books on these subjects and frequently contributes to the Institute’s policy research studies. Most recently, he was a principal author of Seeds of Prosperity: Public Investment in Science and Technology Research. Prior to his work for Morrison Institute, Dr. Melnick was a senior fellow and vice president of the Hudson Institute where he was in charge of policy studies on employment and education.

Henry Miller, Research Fellow, Hoover Institution, Stanford University. Dr. Miller’s research focuses on public policy toward science and technology, especially pharmaceutical development and the new biotechnology. His work often emphasizes the excessive costs of government regulation and models for regulatory reform. He joined the Food and Drug Administration in 1979, where he served in a number of posts involved with the new biotechnology including as the founding Director of the FDA's Office of Biotechnology. Dr. Miller has authored a number of monographs and journal articles and recently co-authored a book, The Frankenfood Myth: How Protest and Politics Threaten the Biotech Revolution.

Roger Pielke, Director, Center for Science and Technology Policy Research, University of Colorado. Dr. Pielke is a Professor in the Environmental Studies Program and a Fellow of the Cooperative Institute for Research in the Environmental Sciences (CIRES). At CIRES Dr. Pielke serves as the Director of the Center for Science and Technology Policy Research. Dr. Pielke’s current areas of interest include understanding the relations of science and politics, technology policy in the atmospheric and related sciences, use and value of prediction in decision making, and policy education for scientists. He is author of numerous articles and essays and is also co-author or co-editor of three books.

Past Comments by Panel Participants:

Politicians increasingly claim that  policies and decisions need to be based on “sound science,” but panelist Roger Pielke notes that “putting the onus of problem resolution onto science brings all the messy realities of politics into the practice of science. Rather than making politics more scientific, this approach, in fact, makes science more political.”

Henry Miller, Hoover Institution, Stanford University says: “There appears little likelihood that in the foreseeable future science policy will become less politicized or more rational and progressive.  There is no important constituency for sound science policy.  On the contrary, politicization often represents merely pandering to the fears, which sometimes verge on superstition, of a scientifically illiterate and statistics-phobic public.”

The politicization of science has been much criticized lately; however, Miller notes that “these critics seem to have experienced overnight epiphanies.  Not one of them was publicly censorious of the Clinton administration’s blatant and heavy-handed perversion of science.”

Charles Arntzen  notes: “In academic science, we pursue absolute truth and don't like to draw hard conclusions until we get very close to that goal.  In contrast, science-based business decisions often need to be made on the basis of incomplete data in order to get a product to the marketplace.  Political science policy making is even farther away from the academic model; legislators have to accept large uncertainties on risks and benefits of technology and still make decisions on regulation, taxation or funding of science.   One result is a lot of ‘second guessing’ that is rarely of much constructive value.”

Roger Pielke has said: “Assessing the significance of science for policy requires a clear distinction of policy analysis from political advocacy.”
