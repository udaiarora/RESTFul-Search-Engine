April 20, 2005

Numbers reflect Arizona’s dwindling ‘cluster’ industries

Revisions and updates of Gross State Product figures for Arizona are examined in the current issue of the Bank One Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast.

The GSP figures, released by the U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis through 2002, reveal the sources of Arizona output and most important categories of economic activity.

Arizona’s GSP in 2002 was $171.8 billion, equal to 1.7 percent of the total of the states, according to Lee McPheters, topics editor of the Arizona Blue Chip.

“Since Arizona ’s population in 2002 accounted for 1.9 percent of the national population, output per capita in Arizona was below the national average,” McPheters says.

Arizona ’s distribution of output between private industries and government essentially is similar to that of the nation, but there are some notable variations by specific industries.

“The GSP figures show those industries that most strongly differentiate Arizona from the nation as a whole, or the average state,” McPheters says. “Aerospace, electronics and ore mining are still dominant clusters (drivers of economic competitiveness) in Arizona , each more than twice as important as a source of output here than nationally. These three industries all have wages that are higher than the Arizona average of $36,000 (as of midyear 2004). The aerospace average wage is over $63,000, for example. The bad news is that in recent years, none of these high-wage sectors has been a source of new jobs.”

It can be argued that the industries shown in the table are the drivers of the Arizona economy, McPheters says, since they are the industries that are more important here than in the average state.

“With no new jobs in Arizona ’s most important clusters, and employment gains concentrated in industries with below-average wages, it is little wonder that per capita personal income in Arizona continues to lag the nation,” McPheters says.

The Bank One Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast includes the consensus forecast of economists from 19 groups and institutions. It is published by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute in the W. P. Carey School of Business at ASU.
