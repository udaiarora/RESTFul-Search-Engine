January 27, 2003

Professor, alumnus conduct experiments on space shuttle

When NASA's Space Shuttle Columbia launched Jan. 16, it took a little bit of ASU with it.

Space shuttle mission STS 107, currently orbiting the Earth, features a pair of scientific experiments with ASU ties - one by a current faculty member and another by an ASU graduate.

Professor Petra Fromme, from the chemistry & biochemistry department, whose research specialty centers on photosynthesis, is growing on the shuttle a series of crystals from photosystem I, which is the protein catalyzing first step of photosynthesis. The crystals will grow to a much higher grade of perfection under microgravity than under the constraints of the Earth's gravity.

Fromme will expose the newly grown crystals to an ultra-high level x-ray which will provide, after data evaluation, a clear high resolution picture of the protein's structure down to the atomic level. The structure will help researchers gain a greater understanding of how the photosynthesis process - nature's process for storing light energy - works and will help them to eventually biomimic the process, which could be useful in nanotechnology and other applications.

"Nature is much more efficient in energy conversion than anything man has developed," says Fromme, who is in her first year at ASU. "The first step in duplicating it is knowing exactly what the process looks like in nature."

The research is aboard the shuttle under the sponsorship of the European Space Agency. It is the third time Fromme has sent a research project to space on a shuttle mission.

Cindy Hutchens, a 1986 graduate from ASU in mechanical engineering, oversees the Vapor Compression Distillation Flight Experiment aboard the Columbia. It is a full-scale demonstration of technology to turn crew members urine and wastewater aboard the Space Station into clean water for drinking, cooking and hygiene. The experiment could lead to an operational unit being placed aboard the International Space Station in the future.

"The early pioneers in this country could find water along their way, but that's a luxury space explorers don't have. It's a technology we will need to explore the space frontier," said Hutchens, who joined NASA as an aerospace engineer in May 1987.

The shuttle is scheduled to land at Kennedy Space Center in Florida Feb. 1. For information visit the Web at (http://spaceflight.nasa.gov/shuttle/).
