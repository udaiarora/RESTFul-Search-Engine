More education may not mean more vaccination

Children of women with less than a high school education are more likely to have received timely childhood vaccinations than the children of college graduates, according to a new study. “The way the health care system is organized for immunization services may account for the variations,” says Jennie Kronenfeld, sociology professor. The New York Times, Jan. 16.

Time will tell, but it has every opportunity of being very successful," says Ray Artigue, executive director of the WP Carey Sports Business MBA Program. The Washington Times, Dec. 12.

Voting on 'strong mayor' begins today

Larger cities often are run by “strong-mayors,” who are allotted more power.  “I think the evidence shows that large cities can be very well governed and very well managed with either kind of government,” says James Svara, director of the Center for Urban Innovation. Major changes on the land

Major fire worries for the west are “condition class 3” forests — those that have suffered significant changes because naturally occurring fires have been suppressed for decades. Some 80 million such acres exist across the West, much of them mixed pine forests that might have faced low-intensity fires every 10 years had man not intervened. “There’s wide agreement that the elimination of fire is a contributing factor for all this, not just that we stopped fires that naturally had started but that we stopped lighting fires,” says Regents’ professor Stephen Pyne, MSNBC, Nov. 2.

Prehistoric-sized dragonflies? It's in the air

Dragonflies with wingspans of two and a half feet once existed, and scientists have speculated that the reason they grew so large was a higher level of oxygen in the air. To find out if oxygen is the key to the size of insects, environmental physiologist Jon Harrison studied 10 generations of fruit flies, varying the level of oxygen over multiple generations. “They got smaller in low oxygen and bigger in high oxygen,” he says, noting that it may not be a smoking gun, but at least it’s a “smoking fly.” ABC News, Oct. 18.

Enigmatic Eruption: The strange case of V838 Monocerotis

An erupting star near the outskirts of the Milky Way has become one of the most puzzling objects in the galaxy. Its outburst in 2002 set aglow a never-before-seen array of spirals and eddies, swelled to a gargantuan diameter, then faded. Twice more since then it bloomed in size before it cooled and shrank. “It does not resemble any nova that has been studied,” says astronomer Sumner Starrfield. He captured several stunning images on the Hubble Space Telescope camera. Science News, Oct. 14.
