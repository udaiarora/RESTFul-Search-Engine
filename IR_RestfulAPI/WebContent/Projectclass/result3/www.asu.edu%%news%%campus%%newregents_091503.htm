September 15, 2003

Governor appoints Frank, Calderon as Regents

Gov. Janet Napolitano appointed two new members to the Arizona Board of Regents on Sept. 2. Phoenix residents Lorraine Frank and Ernest Calderon will begin their eight-year terms in January 2004.

"The Regents have some challenging work ahead of them as they complete the Changing Directions Initiative and help the universities refocus their efforts," said Governor Napolitano in a Sept. 2 statement. "I know Lorraine and Ernie will be valuable in that process, and I'm confident they'll make sure Arizona's university students continue to receive a world-class education."

The Arizona Board of Regents (ABOR), composed of eight members and two student regents, is mandated by the state constitution to oversee Arizona's public university system of ASU, Northern Arizona University (NAU) and the University of Arizona (UA).

Frank has a long history of service to Arizona, particularly in the areas of education and humanities. She is a graduate of Vassar College and was appointed president of the Council on Humanities 1993 - 97. Frank later became the executive director of the Arizona Humanities Council in 1995. She served as the director of the Regents' Commission on the Status of Women from 1989 to 1991 and was also awarded honorary doctorate degrees from ASU and UA.

"I am really, really excited, because I'm extremely interested in higher education," Frank says. "I certainly hope to make a great contribution, and I look forward to working with them (ABOR). There are a lot of good people working on the board."

Calderon earned his bachelor's degree from NAU and his law degree at the UA. He serves on the NAU Foundation and just completed his term as president of the State Bar of Arizona. Calderon practices law in Arizona with the law firm Jennings, Strouss & Salmon, and has argued cases before Arizona state and federal courts and the U.S. Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals. Gov. Napolitano also appointed Calderon to her School Readiness Task Force earlier this year.

"I still believe in seeking out what's best for the students. I hope I can help find solutions to some of the problems facing education," Calderon says. "But for the first few months I plan to keep my mouth shut and listen, because I've learned that nobody learns anything when they're talking."

Calderon says education affordability is a concern he plans to examine as an ABOR member.

"This (public education) is the only way disadvantaged kids, regardless of their race, get the opportunity to experience higher education. Growing up, my mother was a cook and my father was a copper miner, and I have to wonder, 'Could they afford to send me to college today,'" says Calderon, who was born and raised in Morenci. "I doubt it."

Frank agrees affordability is a problem facing many Arizona residents who seek a higher education.

"The major problem is how students get the best possible education at a figure they can afford to pay," Frank says. "We know that our fees are among the lowest in the United States, but the pressure comes with how to keep the cost down and quality of education high."

Frank and Calderon replace Regents Kay McKay and Don Ulrich, whose terms expire in January 2004. Both appointed positions are subject to state Senate confirmation.

The next Arizona Board of Regents meeting is Sept. 25 - 26 at ASU. For information about the Arizona Board of Regents, visit the Web at (http://www.abor.asu.edu/).
