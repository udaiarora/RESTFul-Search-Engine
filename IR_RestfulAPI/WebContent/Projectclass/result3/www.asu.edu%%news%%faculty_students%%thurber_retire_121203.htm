December 12, 2003

Thurber takes early offer

Professor Frances Thurber is one of 78 ASU faculty members who have opted to take advantage of the Voluntary Separation Retirement Incentive Program implemented in August for eligible employees.

The deadline for making the decision was close of business on Dec. 8, but Thurber didn't need that long to decide she would retire earlier than expected.

At 61, Thurber still has the drive, enthusiasm and commitment that have made her a top academic, teacher and nursing practitioner for 40 years. Unfortunately, health problems have zapped her stamina and she's not willing to compromise the level of activity needed to be on the front lines of nursing when she's teaching.

"I saw the early retirement offer as an opportunity for me to make a break before my production level went down as a result of my health," Thurber says. "When I'm teaching, I want the kind of energy level that allows me to run around the clinical area and see what I need to see."

Thurber joined ASU from the University of Pennsylvania School of Nursing in 1991. She has been a division chair for 12 years, and also runs the Westside Community Center Youth Clinic in Tempe. The clinic provides primary and acute episodic care to students from three elementary schools and one middle school.

Thurber is leaving the ASU School of Nursing at a time when nurses are in high demand and the school is taking measures to respond to that demand.

"I'm really pleased with the emergence of nursing over the past several decades toward a higher level of recognition of the value nursing brings to the health care scene," says Thurber. "Nursing has transformed from a vocation to a profession, with a well-established scientific base and ongoing research generated to support the educational environment."

Thurber's retirement isn't effective until May 15, 2004, and she's not slowing down in the meantime. Thurber is relocating to the ASU West Campus for the spring semester to be the site manager for ASU's hosted Nursing program there.

Thurber said she loves teaching and working with students, but also is looking forward to spending more quality time with her husband of 36 years and delving into some hobbies, such as cooking, reading and gardening. In addition, her church choir will be traveling to Italy in mid-June for a two-week tour that will include stops in Rome, Florence and Venice, as well as a performance at St. Peters Basilica.

Thurber was among about 550 employees who were eligible for the Voluntary Separation Retirement Incentive Program - tenured faculty and academic professionals with continuing status who, by Dec. 1, 2003, had attained the age of 55 and above with 10 years of completed service to ASU. In all, 91 faculty are retiring this year, compared to 45 last year.

The early retirement program gives the university much needed flexibility to expand or alter academic areas of emphasis in a time when current funding levels don't accommodate the addition of new faculty lines, while maintaining existing faculty lines.
