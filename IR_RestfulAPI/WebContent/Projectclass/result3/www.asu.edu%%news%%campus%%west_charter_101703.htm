October 17, 2003

Federal grant expands ASU West charter school program

ASU West's masters degree program for charter school administrators is expanding, thanks to a $2.9 million grant from U.S. Department of Education Secretary Rod Paige's Fund for the Improvement of Education (FIE).

The three-year grant allows for tuition waivers for a second cohort of students, a mentorship component and the potential creation of a center for school choice.

The program, called Leadership for Educational Entrepreneurs (LEE), bridges the ASU West College of Education's master's degree in Educational Administration and the School of Management's MBA degree. LEE Fellows from around the country learn online as well as face-to-face at ASU West and at regional sites.

"This innovative program is representative of Arizona State University's commitment to applying faculty research to critical public policy issues," says ASU President Michael Crow. "With this new funding, the program can expand and have an even greater positive impact nationally on K-12 education."

Eleanor Perry, associate professor in the College of Education, founded the LEE program - the first such program in the nation serving the growing charter school industry - after conducting six years of action research focused on charter school leadership.

"LEE gives participants both the education skills and business skills needed to successfully operate a charter school," Perry says.

Forty states have passed legislation establishing charter schools. Nationwide, 175,000 students attend 2,300 charter schools. Arizona has nearly 500 charter school sites - the most of any state - serving more than 75,000 children.

"As a public university campus, we have a responsibility to work for the success of all children and to help develop talented K-12 leaders," College of Education Dean Michael Awender says. "The LEE program addresses this responsibility and is making a positive impact on the thousands of students attending charter schools across Arizona and the United States."

The grant allows for the expansion of the program to include mentorship for LEE students who are administrators at charter schools in high-need areas, along with leadership development training for their mentors. Individuals selected to serve as mentors will be charter school leaders from high-need areas who have a documented track record of increased student achievement.

"Our approach is innovative in that the mentors will receive professional development training before the mentor/protégé relationship begins, to enhance their mentoring and research skills," Perry says.

"Extending the program to a second cohort and providing for the addition of an administrative mentorship will truly be an opportunity to build highly qualified educational leaders," says current LEE student Anita Mendoza, assistant superintendent of instruction at AmeriSchools in Tucson.

The current LEE cohort, representing 11 states stretching from Rhode Island to Hawaii, began the program in the summer of 2002. The students are scheduled to complete their master's degrees next spring.

The second cohort of 24 LEE students will begin their studies in summer 2004. In the meantime, mentors at high-need charter school sites around the country will be chosen and will begin receiving professional development training.

"I am grateful to Sen. Jon Kyl and Representatives Trent Franks and J.D. Hayworth for their support as we pursued this grant," Perry says.

ASU West is one of only seven recipients in the current round of FIE funding, and the only university among the recipients. The $2.9 million grant is the largest grant ever received by ASU West and the largest of the seven grants funded.

"These grants help charter schools continue to grow, providing more options for students and families, especially those students who attend schools in need of improvement," says Secretary Paige.

More information about the LEE program is available at (www.west.asu.edu/LEEP).
