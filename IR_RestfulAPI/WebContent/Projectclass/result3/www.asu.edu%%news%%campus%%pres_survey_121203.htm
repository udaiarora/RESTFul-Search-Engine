December 12, 2003

Survey shows strong support among employees for Crow's vision

Arizona State University faculty and staff are very familiar with the vision outlined by President Michael Crow, are confident in his ability to achieve the vision, and want more open discussions with him about the direction of the university.

This and other pertinent information was obtained from an employee perception survey conducted by Bruce Merrill, director of The Media Research Program in the Walter Cronkite School of Mass Communication, for the ASU Office of Public Affairs.
According to the survey, some 83 percent of faculty and staff have a very or generally favorable opinion of Crow's vision. Only four percent of faculty, and zero staff, indicated a very unfavorable opinion of the vision. Nearly 92 percent of faculty and staff say they have heard or read something about Crow's vision, with 84 percent feeling very or somewhat familiar with the vision.

"Rather than speculate about how President Crow's vision for ASU is being perceived among faculty and staff, we wanted sound data that could point out gaps in our communication efforts," says Nancy Neff, assistant vice president for Public Affairs. "No one expected the results to be so overwhelmingly positive. In fact, Dr. Merrill cautioned us from the outset that we would have to release the results of the survey even if they showed little support for President Crow and the vision."

Merrill says, "There is always a risk that the results won't be what you want, but the value of this kind of research is that it allows you to determine what the entire faculty/staff family at ASU believes, instead of guessing or reacting to individuals."
Merrill, who has been doing surveys for 35 years, said when he describes polls he often uses the analogy of going to the doctor. "If you're ill, you need to get a good diagnosis and treat it," he said. "With a survey, the results might be bad, but if we have problems, we need to know what they are so they can be fixed."

In this case, Merrill says he is surprised by the magnitude of the support Crow is receiving.

"What makes these results even more impressive," says Merrill, "is that universities, by their very nature, are known for their slow adaptation to change and, in fact, haven't changed much over the past 1,000 years.

"These results indicate ASU faculty and staff have embraced the need to change and feel confident in the direction the university is heading."

Merrill says he is impressed that survey participants were able to cite key points of Crow's vision without being aided or prompted. The question was open-ended, asking those respondents who said they remembered key points or ideas of Crow's vision for ASU's future what points they remembered.

Although the responses varied in order, six messages were recalled by faculty and staff:
. ASU needs to become more embedded in the community;
. Build a New American University, future oriented, global;
. Expand emphasis on research, bring in more research funding;
. Make ASU more financially self sufficient;
. Emphasize high-tech, especially biotechnology; and
. One university with distinctive satellite campuses.

"The strength of asking open-ended questions is that people tell the interviewers in their own words what they remember or what is important to them," says Merrill.
In addition to gauging perception, the survey also provided information on where employees get most of their information about ASU. The number one information source is ASU Insight. Rounding out the top five are word of mouth; several forms of electronic communication, including the ASU Web site; professional meetings; and The Arizona Republic.

The survey methodology was a random telephone sample of 300 full-time staff (150) and faculty (150) at ASU. The sampling frame for the survey was the 2003 - 04 Faculty/Staff Directory. Individuals were given the opportunity to answer the questions either in the privacy of their homes or in their offices at ASU.

The survey and results are available online at (www.asu.edu/asunews/survey).

This article appeared in the December 12, 2003 issue of ASU Insight.
