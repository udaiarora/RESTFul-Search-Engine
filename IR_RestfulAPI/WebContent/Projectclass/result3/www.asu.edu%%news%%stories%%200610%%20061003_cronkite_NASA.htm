October 3, 2006

Cronkite students earn NASA 3-peat

Students of ASU's Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication claimed victory at the NASA Means Business competition for the third year in a row.

NASA Means Business is a national, yearlong competition facilitated by the Texas Space Grant Consortium. College undergraduate and graduate students develop an innovative program that better promotes science and mathematics education to middle and high school students. Entries include traditional ?space disciplines? and others not typically related to NASA, such as journalism, public relations and marketing. NASA chooses the best program for implementation.

ASU Cronkite faculty and students involved in the NASA Means Business competition are part of the Arizona Students Present Interesting Research to Everyone (ASPIRE) group, which also includes faculty and students from the Art Institute of Phoenix. The ASPIRE team developed a strategic communications plan, a Web site and public service announcements (PSAs) that resulted in this year's winning program.

?The strategic communication plan is very helpful in establishing NASA's goals and objectives,? says Gary Kitmacher, NASA's mission manager of communications for the Internal Space Station Program. ?The students frequently bring forward innovative ideas and identify aspects of the program that are important to the public, especially to the younger generation.?

ASPIRE presented its communications plan to top leadership at NASA headquarters in Washington, D.C.

In previous years, the ASPIRE team has had great success in the NASA Means Business program. In 2005, ASPIRE's winning PSAs aired during the American Astronautical Society Conference, on NASA TV and in the visitor's center at the Kennedy Space Center in Cape Canaveral, Fla.

In 2004 and 2005, ASPIRE's PSAs won top awards from WorldFest Houston International Film Festival, and they also won an Accolade competition earlier this year. The PSAs are in English, Spanish and Russian.

A behind-the-scenes documentary created by the 2004 ASPIRE group is under review and could air this fall on NASA TV.

Team leader Ben Higgins presented the group's work at the 2005 American Astronautical Society Conference and was also selected to serve as a NASA representative on a 10-person delegation who lobbied members of Congress.

?This competition is unique in that it brings students together across disciplines for a common objective,? says Higgins, who led the 2005 and 2006 teams and graduated from ASU's Ira A. Fulton School of Engineering in August with a degree in aerospace engineering. ?It's an activity not normally done in academic and professional arenas.?

Past ASPIRE members pursued employment inspired by their experience. Lisa Tidwell works on the Phoenix Mars Mission based at University of Arizona in Tucson, Ariz. Andrew DeSouza is a press relations coordinator for Congressman Mark Kennedy (R-Minn.) in Washington D.C.

?The competition challenges students to transform every bit of classroom knowledge they have acquired into real-world solutions,? says Frances Matera, an ASU Cronkite associate professor and an adviser to the ASPIRE team. ?It links all forms of communication with creativity and action.?

Besides Higgins and Matera, the 2006 ASPIRE team includes the following students and faculty:

? ASU Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication: Andra Corrin Lewis, Kacie Sumner, Kathryn Burnett, Nicole Phelan, Holly Campbell and Andrew Moe.

? Art Institute of Phoenix: Mark Von Bowers, faculty supervisor, Frederick (Rick) Vincent Stefani, Audra Hughes, John Christian Schermerhorn and Alicia Martinez.

For more information on ASPIRE's 2006 NASA Means Business project, visit the Web site (www.foreverforward.us).
