July 2, 2003

Study seeks emerging opportunities at state, local levels

Not all of Arizona's communities are in a position to compete for a high-tech manufacturing presence in their quest for a vibrant economy, but many will find more modest success if they develop an understanding of their own unique strengths and weaknesses, according to the current issue of the Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast.

"It is vital that each community within Arizona, whether rural or urban, understand these opportunities and constraints and make the most of what they have," said Elliott Pollack, contributing editor, in his article previewing a statewide study commissioned by the Arizona Department of Commerce.

The study is targeted at identifying the state's current economic structure, emerging opportunities, and economic constraints. According to Pollack, the purpose is to develop an economic strategy to assist with local, regional, and statewide planning and policy development over the next decade. In order to capture opinions at the local level, meetings were conducted in all of Arizona's 15 counties, including four regional meetings that specifically dealt with tribal issues on Indian reservations. A report covering the public outreach process and the development of strategic findings is scheduled for release this summer.

"The cumulative research allows for some general conclusions to be reached about the state's economy," Pollack said. "Arizona is not an economic monolith, and there is no single policy approach or goal that will maximize the opportunities for all parts of Arizona. However, both state and local economic development policies can be designed to allow communities to make the most of their current economic characteristics - in other words, to be the best they can be."

Elsewhere in the Arizona Blue Chip, Editor Tracy Clark surveyed the consensus panel on its perception of the risks to continued consumer spending in 2003 and 2004. The majority (67 percent) believes that consumer spending will not pose a major problem for the economy this year, and an even bigger majority (73 percent) hold this view when looking ahead to 2004. While Clark noted that the economy has been bolstered by government spending - either directly through defense expenditures or indirectly with various tax cuts - business spending remains at a low point. "While this federal infusion appears to have helped avert a double-dip recession, the economic recovery remains weak, and probably will continue as such until sometime in 2004," Clark said.

The Bank One Arizona Blue Chip Economic Forecast includes the consensus forecast of economists from 19 groups and institutions. It is published by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute in the W. P. Carey School of Business at Arizona State University. For information, call (480) 965-7858.
