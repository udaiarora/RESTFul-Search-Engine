ASU East agribusiness program encourages food trade for peace

Trade between Arizona and one of the world's most war-torn regions is bearing fruit because of the work of researchers in ASU East's Morrison School of Agribusiness and Resource Management.

Clifford J. Shultz, the Marley Foundation Professor of Agribusiness, is leading an ASU team trying to increase trade and decrease international tensions in Croatia, Bosnia-Herzegovina and other Balkan nations. Shultz is working with an international collaboration championing mutually beneficial trade between Arizona, the Balkans and the Black Sea Region, particularly trade in grain and processed food.

"Peace and prosperity through food marketing," he said. "It makes sense for us to promote economic development and constructive engagement in the Balkans rather than military intervention. They don't have to like each other in order to co-exist and co-prosper."

The effort isn't just altruistic, either. Shultz points out that the Balkans and Black Sea Region represent emerging economies with a potential market of more than 100 million consumers.

"These countries have a generally western orientation toward trade and commerce," Shultz said. "We are working to enhance understanding of disparate cultures and systems at the same time we encourage regional cooperation and export development."

The current focus of the ASU East/Morrison School of Agribusiness and Resource Management project is on establishing new links and strengthening existing ones between educational institutions, trade associations and business interests in the Balkans and the United States, particularly Arizona.

Shultz and his team are mapping Balkan region channels for food marketing and export development, as well as evaluating the market potential for food products and trade there. First phase work was done in collaboration with Croatia, a principal market hub for the region.

The researchers are currently in the process of expanding their study to include Bosnia-Herzegovina, Greece and Slovenia and soon will begin work in Macedonia, Serbia, Montenegro and Albania. They are spreading the results of their research through publications, international business and academic exchanges between the Balkans and the United States, university curriculum development and cooperative learning programs.

"Most academics and businesspersons simply are not well aware of the region," Shultz said. "They do not yet understand the business potential and the win-win possibilities in regional understanding, peaceful coexistence and future cost savings resulting from economic development and constructive engagement rather than military intervention."

Over the past couple of years, teams of ASU East researchers and local agribusiness leaders have frequently visited Croatia to strengthen relationships and conduct market and industry assessments.

Currently, Shultz and his research team are administering a survey to measure Croatian and other European consumers' attitudes and intentions to buy American, particularly Arizona-grown, wheat-based products.

Support for the ASU East work comes from the U.S. Department of Education, the Croatian Ministry of Science and Technology, the U.S. State Department, the Desert Wheat Growers Cooperative, the Arizona Grain Research and Promotions Council, the University of Zagreb, the University of Rijeka, the University of Sarajevo, the University of Ljubljana, the University of Tuzla, the American Farm School - Thessaloniki and the University of Split.

Further information and photographs are on the Web at (www.east.asu.edu/balkans).
