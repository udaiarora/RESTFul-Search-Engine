November 21, 2003

Law professor gives her time, skills to students

Striving for top grades and awards is a constant in the competitive atmosphere of law school. But many ASU law students joke that their real goal in life is to make Law Professor Ann Woodley proud.

She coaches their mediation advocacy team, helping them win regionals and place in the top four nationally each year. She also spends much of her free time assisting students with interviewing techniques, helping them become more professional and emphasizing pro bono service.

For her caring and professionalism, both inside and outside of class, Woodley has been chosen as the ASU Featured Faculty for November. The award program began this fall by Co-Curricular Programs to recognize the contributions of faculty in enhancing student learning outside of class.

"Her professionalism, generosity and legal skills are incomparable, and give us an example to strive for," says K Royal, the law student who nominated Woodley. "This past year, she has formed a pro bono mediation project, working with the community, ASU, the courts and local schools to teach alternative means of dispute resolution.

"I have been working on training sessions for all students to increase their confidence and competence in performing pro bono service. As soon as she heard I was developing a program, she volunteered her time. I could not have accomplished such a wonderful and targeted program without her assistance."

Woodley shares her time and wisdom with students outside the class willingly, making them feel that she cares about them as people, Royal says. She also improves the public perception of attorneys, with her work in advocating mediation.

Woodley operates the Lodestar Mediation Clinic at the ASU College of Law, and works with professionals in many fields around the country to develop consistent standards and operating procedures.

"I love interacting with students both inside and outside of the classroom, and I enjoy helping law students learn about alternative ways of resolving disputes," says Woodley.
