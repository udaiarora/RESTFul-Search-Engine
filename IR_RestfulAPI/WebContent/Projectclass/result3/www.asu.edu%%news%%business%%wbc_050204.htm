May 3, 2004

Western analysts predict better job growth for 2004

The latest forecasts for the Western states are more positive than they have been in quite a while - with Arizona and Nevada leading the way, according to an analysis in the current issue of the Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast.

Unlike the spotty performance of 2003, every Western state is slated to add jobs this year, according to Lee McPheters, contributing editor. "The analysts compiled their forecasts for the West after they took a look at the stronger monthly national employment growth figures. If the tea leaves are accurate, Arizona and Nevada will set the pace with a projected gain of 3.6 percent in nonfarm payroll employment for 2004."

It is no coincidence that Arizona and Nevada are projected to have strong population gains along with employment increases for 2004, McPheters noted. The two states consistently add new residents at a faster pace than most other states, year after year. More people contribute to demand for workers in housing, retailing, and services such as health care. Government employment also goes up as population grows.

Elsewhere in the Western Blue Chip, McPheters reports that California supplied more than 700,000 residents to other states between 1995-2000. Outflow from California was mostly to nearby Western states, notably Nevada, which received a net flow of 138,637 from its Golden State neighbor. Another 186,000 Californians found new homes in Arizona.

The Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast publishes the consensus forecasts of panels of economists in 10 Western states. Panelists are drawn from leading firms, universities and state agencies. The Western Blue Chip also publishes historical data for each state.

The Western Blue Chip Economic Forecast is published 10 times a year by the Bank One Economic Outlook Center, an affiliate of the L. William Seidman Research Institute at Arizona State University's W. P. Carey School of Business. The cost is $110 for 10 print issues ($99 for e-mail subscription).
