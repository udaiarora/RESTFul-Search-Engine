February 28, 2006

Kittrie among top Latino international legal experts

ASU law professor Orde Kittrie recently was named by Hispanic Outlook in Higher Education magazine as one of four top Latino international law professors in the United States.

Kittrie, a Mexican-American, joined the ASU College of Law in 2004 following 11 years with the U.S. Department of State. He also serves as an adviser to the Chicano Latino Law Students Association at the College of Law.

"It is a great honor to be named one of the top Latinos in international law," Kittrie says. "I am particularly pleased because my Mexican-American heritage has helped inspire my interest in international relations."

Kittrie's mother, who is Mexican, often took him to Mexico to visit relatives. From a young age, he was exposed to the tensions between the United States and Mexico - and their two very different versions of the history of U.S.-Mexican relations.

"At the same time, I was aware that there is clearly common ground between the two countries," Kittrie says. "Indeed, we Mexican-Americans are clearly living, breathing examples of that common ground. Helping countries to focus on their common ground and ways in which they can work to their mutual benefit continues to drive my interest in international law and international relations."

Kittrie was elected in November to the board of directors of Los Abogados, the Latino attorneys' association of Arizona. Last summer, he helped lead the Los Abogados response to Maricopa County attorney Andrew Thomas' decision not to prosecute a private citizen for holding seven undocumented immigrants at gunpoint. This initiative culminated in Los Abogados and a coalition of 12 other organizations submitting to Thomas and the public a memorandum of law analyzing Thomas' decision and calling on him to reverse it. Kittrie represented Los Abogados in newspaper, television and radio interviews on the issue.

Kittrie also recently wrote a law review article on immigration law reform and the rights of undocumented immigrants. The article, titled "Federalism, Deportation and Crime Victims Afraid to Call the Police," is scheduled to be published in the Iowa Law Review.

"For a Mexican-American professor of international law, Arizona is a particularly exciting place to be, as it is at the center of the very important and extremely heated debate over immigration, border security and U.S.-Mexico relations," Kittrie says. "I hope I can contribute to finding a common ground in this debate."
