3.B  University President?s Report (Lattie Coor).

 

Since the economic downturn, the legislature has been revisiting the budget.  It is now the third time that the legislature has visited ??02.?  It adopted an initial budget in the regular session a year ago.  In that budget there were some rather significant appropriations for a number of functions, most importantly a five percent salary increase pool.  When the economic downturn occurred, they had a special session in November and December and cut the budget in the current year quite substantially.   For ASU and our three campuses, it was 16 million dollars worth of reduction, or 4.56% of our budget, a lot to take under any circumstances but especially now in the middle of the year.  Now even though they are in regular session, they have a practice of being able to suspend the regular session to go into special session.  They are in fact revisiting the current year once again, because the amount of reduction they made in the last special session is still by their estimates 200 million dollars short.  Arizona has a constitutional provision that it cannot run a deficit. 

 

The salary increase pool that was originally voted in at 5% to begin at the first of April, remembering that it had been put ¾ quarters of the way into the year, is now very much in the gunsight of this special session.  The House voted out a bill just last week that eliminates the 5% salary increase.  The Senate is deadlocked at this moment; as you know they are 15 to15 (Democrats to Republicans) for the full 5%, effective April 1.  The leadership of both the House and Senate are very concerned and so much so that they have suspended all regular business, and are requiring the leadership of both the House and the Senate to caucus every day this week until they reach a settlement.  My reason to speak to you about this today is because I believe this issue will be decided in the next week or so.  There are some who are exploring having the start-date slip forward but not beyond the end of June, because if it is the end of June then this fiscal year is over.  We are in a new fiscal year and in the last special session, the whole budget for the next fiscal year has been eliminated, so we will start from scratch.  One of the instruments being considered is moving it forward, and inevitably the argument is should it be reduced somewhat as compared to having it totally eliminated?--I can?t tell you yet what the outcome of that will be.  I visited today with the chairman of the House appropriations committee and with the leadership on the Senate side, and the Senate is still deadlocked with the Democratic caucus holding firm for a salary increase.  I would observe to all of you then on behalf of the network, it is the critical week for that issue.  It could go into next week, but it will be in a sense a decisive week.  The governor has weighed in saying there should be no salary increase, and in fact instructed the universities last Thursday to submit a revised budget for the current year, which would have our salary increases take effect without our getting the money for them.

           

Doug Johnson: A virtual raise?  (Much laughter here.)

 

President Coor: In my view this would be the worst of all worlds, because then we are caught in a terribly difficult financial squeeze with some mandated features.  The Governor has also asked us to show by next Wednesday how we would cut another 4% from the current year?s budget, and we have  responded.  The 2003 budget has not yet begun to take shape, but it is expected to have equally serious difficulties in reaching a balance, because the 800 million originally expected to be the case in the current budget is believed to be a billion dollars short in the next round.  We are urging that they re-look at their revenue figures, because no one seems to be willing to do that, for loopholes or ways in which existing revenues could be generated.  Or, God forbid, other revenue sources, tax revenues, that could be considered to keep the damage from being so substantial.  There will be time to join that discussion, because there has been some talk of holding the 2003 budget until May--something they could do.  I just wanted to alert you that this conversation was going on, and at the next Senate meeting we will continue our report. 

 

Today was our Statehood Picnic.  It is the best day of the year to entertain legislators because the legislature is in session.  All the legislators and their staff are here today but the state is on holiday (President?s Day), so every cafeteria in the capital is closed.  They all come to our picnic, not necessarily for the virtue of coming to it, but we do have the only food in town!  There were some interesting things in the conversation today at the picnic, and there were some sober reflections expressed (on the argument in the Arizona Republic today).  The opening line of the article, you will recall, was ?Arizona is slowly committing suicide by not investing appropriately in its universities.?  There too,  I would urge you to make that known in your conversations with your colleagues, and reaffirm that argument, because I think it is a very credible report, and it has our warts there plus it has the deeper message that is clearly understood, which is that we must try to sustain that level of investment.  My report is complete, but again, I urge you to pay attention to what is happening, especially as this special session comes to an end. 

 

Past President Johnson: Is there any possibility that the Senate Democrats will hold out until after April 1st and this raise will go into effect automatically?

 

President Coor: It is one of the strategies that has been discussed.  When I met with the Senate majority leader of the Democratic caucus today, he did not speak to that directly, but he did say that they were holding absolutely firm for the salary increase.  Suspending the regular session and forcing these caucuses to meet every day will make it difficult to hold out six more weeks.  My guess is that they will have some resolution before that.

5.A  Personnel Committee (Doug Johnson).

 

5.A.1  The Report on the recommendations of the Task Force on Promotion and Tenure.

 

Senate Resolution #3  (2001-2002) is in support of the recommendations of the Task Force on Promotion and Tenure.  This group worked for over a year evaluating our ACD Manual and our procedures for promotion and tenure and came forward with a very elaborate set of recommendations that had been on the web site of the Center for Learning and Teaching Excellence for over a year now.  Our Personnel Committee has evaluated that report very carefully and has met 12 times over the fall semester and recommend the work of the Task Force.  They did their job well.  We have some minor recommendations for improving it, but believe that the Senate should support these recommendations.  I believe that this is a major improvement for the university.  It does provide an emphasis on excellence for both teaching and research for promotion.  It does primarily (and this is one of the best things it does) ask the university to go back to the ACD Manual, which has been written in little pieces over the years and make it a coherent document, a useful document that clearly reflects the expectations and requirements of the university.  I think a couple of other recommendations that are part of this package are also outstanding, such as adding to the emphasis on documenting the scholarship of teaching; a recommendation to create a task force that will deal with peer evaluation and to provide other measures of outstanding teaching that will supplement student evaluation of teaching.  We need to broaden that area, and package it so that it is not a huge burden for faculty to create that document and finish it.  The other recommendation is that of getting our support for the creation of the Provost Personnel Advisory Committee advisory group, which will provide an opportunity to have consistent implementation of promotion and tenure criteria across the colleges of  the university.   We ask each department to revisit their documents for promotion and tenure, update them and communicate their expectations to their faculty.  That communication of expectation is something that sometimes we need to think about, and we want to make sure that it actually happens.  So, the Personnel Committee?s recommendation to you is to support the Task Force recommendations, and I ask you to do so today. 

Senate President Morton Munk: Is there discussion?

 

Senator Cobas: I am troubled--by the way.  I think that this is a very good document.  But I am troubled by section 2.2, which re-authors the criterion of contributing toward a collegial work environment.  That is something to be considered for promotion and tenure.  I think that the way that is phrased could be dangerous.  Somebody could be accused of not contributing to the collegial work environment by simply not being popular, or for holding dissenting views, for being a maverick, and I would like to pose the following question.  Why not simply change the language to the effect of being a disruptive element or member of a department or interfering with the department, then that is something to be considered in your tenure and promotion review.  That is more specific than something like ?not contributing to a collegial work environment.?  If you are a disruptive individual, then that should be something that would keep you from achieving tenure and promotion.

 

Past President Johnson: The Personnel Committee certainly dealt with that issue at some length.  Our concern was similar in nature and our revised wording is recommended to say that faculty should contribute to a productive professional environment.  Collegiality is an emotionally charged word and we are cautious about using it.  But positive contributions to a productive environment are a very reasonable thing to evaluate in a promotion and tenure context.

Past President Johnson:  Serving effectively on committees is certainly a contribution. 

 

Senator Cobas: So, if you refuse to serve on committees, you are being a disruptive member of your department. 

 

Past President Johnson: If one does not show up for committee assignments, I am not sure you are being disruptive, but you are certainly not being productive.

 

Senator Cobas: If you don?t show up for class, if you don?t show up for committee assignments, you are simply not doing what you are paid to do as an individual.
