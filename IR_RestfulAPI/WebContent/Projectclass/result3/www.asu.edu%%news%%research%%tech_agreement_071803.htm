Technology transfer agreement gives ASU researchers tools for developing new weapons to fight antibiotic-resistant bacteria

Developing new weapons against antibiotic-resistant bacteria and fungi is the goal of Arizona State University researchers working with technology donated from Hercules, Inc., Wilmington, Del., a global manufacturer of chemical specialties used to make a variety of products for home, office and industrial markets.

Peter Slate, CEO of Arizona Technology Enterprises, the technology commercialization company for ASU, today announced that Hercules has signed an agreement to transfer to ASU patents and know-how related to new microbiocides for developing potential new biotechnology products.

"Acceptance of this donation is a reflection of ASU's commitment to advancing cutting-edge research, both through university efforts as well as in partnership with industry," said Slate.

"This technology package from Hercules is exciting because it is a new approach to microbiocides that gives broad-spectrum control of many species of bacteria and possible other microorganisms," said Charles Arntzen, world-renowned plant biologist and holder of the Florence Ely Nelson Presidential Chair at ASU. "The products that could be developed may provide alternatives to antibiotics in several markets," he said.

Stopping the growth of microbes, such as bacteria or fungi, has wide application in health care, agriculture, and environmental decontamination. Antibiotics have been successful products for the pharmaceutical industry, but their enormous benefits must also be balanced against emerging problems of microbial resistance. Overuse of antibiotics in agriculture and aquaculture have led to calls for newer, better alternatives.

Current sales of antibiotics and other biocides in the global animal health industry are estimated to be $12-$15 billion annually, and biocide use in aquaculture is valued at more than $1 billion per year and growing rapidly, according to the World Health Organization.

Ed Birge, professor of microbiology at ASU, said ASU's active research in related areas of microbiology will help broaden the opportunities for using the donated technology to develop other marketable products. "There are potential opportunities to utilize new microbiocides in the consumer product markets, such as in the areas of antibacterial soaps and creams," he said.

Jonathan Fink, vice president for Research and Economic Affairs at ASU, said, "It's gratifying that this leading U.S. corporation recognizes ASU's research capability and has entrusted us with a technology portfolio that will have very substantial value when the products are validated by further research."


Richard Sujdak, director of Central Research Services at Hercules, said, "We are proud of our biotechnology research team that originally discovered the novel antimicrobials that are the subject of the patents. However, since the microbiocide uses for these chemicals fall outside our corporate business interests, we are pleased to donate them to an outstanding research university for further development. Given ASU's research expertise in this area, the university is an obvious choice for the donation."

The new technology area will be incorporated into programs of the Arizona Biodesign Institute at ASU. The Arizona Biodesign Institute, under the leadership of world-renowned researcher, scholar and science policymaker George Poste, is focused on improving human health and quality of life through use-inspired biosystems research and effective multidisciplinary partnerships.
