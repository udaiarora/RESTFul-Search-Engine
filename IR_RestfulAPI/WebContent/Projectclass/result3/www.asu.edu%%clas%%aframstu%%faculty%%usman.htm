Aribidesi (Desi) Usman (Ph.D., Arizona State University, 1998, Anthropology) is an Assistant Professor of African and African American Studies since 2001. Desi’s research is based in Africa, especially Nigeria. He is especially interested in issues concerning settlement evolution, sociopolitical and economic organization, the nature of power relations that existed among societies prior to 1900, and how local communities responded to, affected, and became part of the regional and global historical development (e.g., state expansion, the Atlantic economy).

His publications include:

State-Periphery Relations and Sociopolitical Development in Igbominaland, North-Central Yoruba, Nigeria. British Archaeological Report, Series 993, John and Erica Hedges, Oxford, 2001.

"Ceramic Seriation, Sites Chronology, and Old Oyo Factor in Northcentral Yorubaland, Nigeria". African Archaeological Review 20(3): 149-169, 2003.

"Early Urbanism in Northern Yorubaland. In Toyin Falola and Steve Salm", ed. Nigerian Cities, pp. 47-78. Trenton (NJ), Africa World Press, 2003.

"On the frontier of Empire: Understanding rampart walls in northern Yoruba, Nigeria".

Journal of Anthropological Archaeology, 23(1): 119-132, 2004.
