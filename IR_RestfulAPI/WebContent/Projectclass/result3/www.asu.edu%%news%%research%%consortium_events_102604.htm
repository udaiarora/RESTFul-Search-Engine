October 26, 2004

Consortium for Medicine, Society and Values Inaugurates Public Events in Bioscience with Lecture by Pulitzer Prize Winner

Stem cells, cloning, genomics, bioethics, bioengineering, new pharmaceuticals, emerging diseases, a shrinking vaccine supply…  more and more it seems as though one needs to be a biologist or a doctor to understand the important news and issues of the day.

Recognizing that most of the public is out-of-the-loop on understanding many of the bioscience discoveries and clinical innovations that are important to society’s health, education, policies and even economic well-being, a consortium of  Arizona institutions involved in medicine and education have banded together to offer information through public programming.

The Arizona Consortium for Medicine, Society and Values is a partnership between Arizona State University (the Center for Biology and Society and the Barrett Honors College), the Flinn Foundation, Mayo Clinic Scottsdale, Translational Genomics Research Center (TGen) and the University of Arizona Health Sciences/Phoenix Campus. The Consortium plans to offer three to four events a year across the Valley, including large public lectures, panel discussions, policy roundtables, conferences, symposia and workshops.

“We represent many institutions and communities, but we have come together because we recognize the need for a common space where different voices can be heard on important topics  and engage in unbiased public discussion,” said Jane Maienschein, Regents Professor of Life Sciences at ASU, and director of the university’s Center for Biology and Society. “We all recognize that we need to interact more with each other and we need to do more outreach with the public. We also believe that we can do more working together than we each can do by ourselves.”

The inaugural event for the Consortium will be a public lecture by Jonathan Weiner on October 30 at 7 p.m. in the Flinn Auditorium at the Arizona Science Center. Weiner, a winner of the Pulitzer Prize for non-fiction and one of the nation’s most respected writers on the topic on science, will deliver a lecture entitled “Edges of Medicine.”

Weiner has written books on the adventures of bioscience in the field and lab, and on the frontiers of medicine. His talk will be based on his most recent book, His Brother’s Keeper, which chronicles a family’s exploration of cutting edge medical therapies in searching for treatment for ALS.

Weiner is the author of four other widely acclaimed books on science and scientists, Planet Earth (Bantam, 1986, companion volume to the PBS television series), The Next One Hundred Years (Bantam, 1990), The Beak of the Finch (winner of the 1994 Pulitzer), and Time, Love, Memory (Knopf, 1999). He has also been senior editor of The Sciences, the magazine of the New York Academy of Sciences.

Weiner has been a frequent visitor to Arizona and in particular to ASU, where he was the Flinn Foundation Centennial Lecturer and in 2001 a writer-in-residence as the John J. Rhodes Chair in Public Policy and American Institutions at the Barrett Honors College.  That spring, he taught an undergraduate honors writing seminar, Science and Literature, in which he and his students explored the intersections of science and story and the narrative inherent in all discovery.

In addition to the public lecture, in this visit Weiner will participate in a number of other Consortium events, including a public policy seminar with local scholars, clinicians and educators; a reception; and an informal student seminar on writing with his wife, author Deborah Heiligman.  The smaller events are by invitation only, but the lecture is free and open to the public. Reservations are suggested, and can be made by email at cmsv@asu.edu <mailto:cmsv@asu.edu>.

The Consortium has several additional events planned for 2005, including a regional undergraduate bioethics conference at ASU in February, and the visit of National Book Award winner, physician-writer Sherwin Nuland, who will be in residence in March in conjunction with the Barrett Honors College’s Staging of Illness course.
