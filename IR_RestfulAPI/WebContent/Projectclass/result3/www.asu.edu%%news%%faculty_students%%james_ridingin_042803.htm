April 28, 2003

Prestigious American Indian journal moves to ASU

James Riding In, associate professor of justice studies and American Indian studies, has been named editor of one of the most reputable journals for Native American studies, Wicazo Sa Review. The journal has moved its editorial offices to ASU from Eastern Washington University.

Founded in 1984 by longtime editor Elizabeth Cook-Lynn, Wicazo Sa Review examines the Indian past and its relationship to the present, pulling together scholarship about Indian issues ranging from the struggle for land and sovereignty to cultural preservation.

Cook-Lynn has been visiting professor at ASU for several semesters. Susan Miller, assistant professor of American Indian studies, will serve as the new associate editor of the journal.

A reception honoring the editors and those who have been instrumental in bringing the journal here will be 2 - 4:30 p.m., April 30 in the Agriculture Building, room 123.

"The journal has been a voice for Indian scholarship for 20 years," says Riding In. "Elizabeth Cook-Lynn has made a significant contribution to scholarship by working to develop the journal into what it is today. She's been a visionary whose voice has been very important to the field of American Indian Studies."

ASU has developed a disciplinary model of American Indian studies, which appealed to Cook-Lynn. The ASU program offers a bachelor's degree and is expanding its curriculum. The journal offers an international audience for refereed articles by Indian and non-Indian scholars from all over the country.

"Indians have been written about for many years, often by non-natives, some of whom have biases and assumptions," says Riding In. "It's important that American Indian intellectuals take the lead in the field, because familiarity with the topic is important and is one way to challenge the stereotypes, flawed interpretations and misconceptions.

"Not many people who study Indians understand what sovereignty is. Some believe that U.S. policy has been beneficial to Indians. What's missing is a critical analysis of what federal policy has done to Indians, its primary purpose having been acquisition of land and resources."

Riding In plans to turn the journal, now published twice a year, into a quarterly. A special edition edited by Cook-Lynn is coming out in June marking the 200-year anniversary of the Lewis and Clark expedition, an event that is not a cause for celebration among American Indians.

Riding In says the journal's aim is to become a disciplinary instrument to assist American Indians in taking possession of their own intellectual and creative pursuits.

"Bringing the journal to ASU signifies the support and the commitment of the university to help develop American Indian studies," he says. "Indian scholars had a hard time finding places to be published, and this journal has changed that. We've had wonderful support for it from the program director, Carol Lujan, and from justice studies director Marie Provine and Dean Anne Schneider."
