November 20, 2006

New report highlights Arizona?s public policy ideas

The Morrison Institute for Public Policy’s latest publication, “Arizona Ideas: Policies from A to Z for a Livable and Competitive State,” describes 26 public policy ideas that have made a difference in Arizona.

The publication, sponsored in part by The Arizona Republic, analyzes the state in a unique way by highlighting ideas large and small, current and historical and both home-grown and borrowed. While many Arizonans may be familiar with some of the ideas included in the publication, others may be surprised by the diversity of the policies and programs. “Arizona Ideas” is expected to be an invaluable tool to encourage innovation in public policies while informing the public on lesser-known programs, such as Arizona’s “Dark Skies” initiatives and 800-mile Arizona Trail.

“ ‘Arizona Ideas’ does us all a great service,” wrote Gov. Janet Napolitano in her foreword to the publication. “It makes us stop our whirlwind of daily activity to appreciate once again the power of ideas as the basis for public policy. ‘Arizona Ideas’ tells us a lot about our past, explains some of our present and pushes us to continue to plan for the future.”

The “ideas” discussed in the report were selected based on two key criteria: First, did the public policy or set of policies affect a significant population in Arizona or the entire state? And secondly, was the idea original to Arizona or specifically adapted to Arizona’s circumstances?

“ ‘Arizona Ideas’ presents instances when innovation, or sometimes, downright stubbornness, provided the ‘kick’ to address complex public problems,” says Nancy Welch, associate director of the Morrison Institute for Public Policy. “Looking at Arizona in terms of its ‘ideas’ versus its issues, problems and comparisons is both novel and risky. Our goal is that it will generate dialogue and spark more ideas for Arizona’s future.”

Good ideas are easier to talk about than to implement, and many of the ideas covered in this publication have not yet reached their full potential. Taken together, however, they reflect how the public policy process, so criticized at times, can and has produced outcomes that have improved Arizona’s livability and competitiveness.

Karen Leland, karen.leland@asu.edu
