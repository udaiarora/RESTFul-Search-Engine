Birth Control Special!

When Good Pills Fail: Antibiotics and Contraceptives

This topic has brought division among clinicians including: specialists, physicians, nurse practitioners and pharmacists. Some say it?s a ?myth? and others say it?s a ?fact?. What is the true answer? Well, it?s a little more complicated than a ?yes? or ?no? answer.
There are several possible mechanisms that can occur for two drugs to have an interaction. They are:

  • Reduction of absorption.
  • Reduction of 1st pass metabolism.
  • Induction of liver enzymes.
  • Alteration in clearance of other drugs.
If any one of the above mechanisms occurs between two drugs, then the potential for a drug-drug interaction can exist. One or both drugs may be altered.
According to the package insert of oral contraceptives (OCs), the co-administration of antibiotics with OCs may decrease the pharmacologic effects of the OCs, possibly because of altered steroid gut metabolism secondary to changes in the intestinal flora. Antibiotic use can cause diarrhea making the absorption of oral contraceptive less effective. Diarrhea may be the effect of the alteration in normal GI flora. Intestinal bacterial flora secrete a number of enzymes capable of metabolizing drugs, any factor that modifies the intestinal flora may also modify drug activity. Normal gastrointestinal flora also increases the enterohepatic cycling and bioavailability of estrogens; therefore antimicrobial drugs that interfere with these organisms may reduce the efficacy of oral contraceptives. Penicillin, amoxicillin, ampicillin, metronidazole, tetracycline, minocycline, cotrimoxazole, and nitrofurantoin have been reported to have significant interactions with OCs. In addition, rifampin and griseofulvin are known enzyme inducers that can interact with OCs. However, in theory all antibiotics have the potential to interact with OCs by altering GI flora.
      Archer conducted a clinical review study to review the efficacy of oral contraceptives when used concurrently with antibiotic therapy. Archer states that pharmacokinetic evidence from this search demonstrates that plasma levels of oral contraceptive hormones are unchanged with concurrent use of antibiotics, including ampicillin, ciprofloxin, clarithromycin, doxycycline, metronidazole, ofloxacin, roxithromycin, temafloxacin, and tetracycline. There were decreases in plasma levels in women taking rifampin. Archer also states that clinical reports of contraceptive failure with antibiotic use are retrospective, have multiple potential biases, and are not supported by pharmacokinetic data. Archer concludes that available scientific and pharmacokinetic data, with the exception of rifampin, does not support the hypothesis that oral antibiotics decrease the effectiveness of oral contraceptives.
      What should our response be to our patients when they ask us, ?Is there a drug-drug interaction between OCs and antibiotics?? It is the patient?s right to know that there is a potential risk (perhaps very small) of becoming pregnant with concurrent use of antibiotics and OCs. The prudent client would be wise to use a second contraceptive method for the remainder of the affected cycle.
- Qui Vo, student pharmacist

How do you make a hormone?

No, it?s not the old joke. Maybe the question is ?where do birth control pills come from??
I?ll start the tale from the campus where my offspring is busy learning (I hope), Penn State University, but back in the early 1940?s. A botany professor, Russell Marker, was intrigued by the variety of plants that contained hormones similar to human ones. After a brief stint here in the Southwest testing cacti with disappointing results, he read a journal article that told of Mexican yams that yielded high levels of hormones. Before you could say ?hola amigo? he was off to Mexico to harvest yams. He was able to extract a progestin from that first batch of yams and attempted to sell it to drug companies back in the States. Notably, Searle and Merck were already in the hormone business, but used animal starting products. They were not interested, so Dr. Marker started Syntex (merging ?synthetic? and Mexico). According to ?The Pill on Trial? by Paul Vaughan, Marker never saw profits from this venture and returned to Penn State.
      Syntex scientists continued to refine the extraction process and eventually synthesized the progestin norethindrone, which is still found in many oral contraceptives. Searle also had a progestin, northynodrel. These two agents were investigated at the Worcester Foundation in Shrewsbury, Massachusetts by Drs. Charles and Kistner and were found to shut down ovulation in rabbits. Much of this research was funded by Margaret Sanger, noted women?s rights activist and founder of the American Birth Control League, which eventually became Planned Parenthood. Other money came from Karl McCormick heir to the fortune left by Cynthia, inventor of McCormick?s Reaper. After the animal studies were completed the Worcester Foundation made a pill that combined estrogen with northynodrel and began human subjects testing.
      The Planned Parenthood Federation of America commissioned Dr. Gregory Pincus and Dr. John Rock to develop a simple and reliable form of contraception in 1950. These early human trials of Enovid (Searle?s brand name containing 10mg of estrogen) are difficult to examine without meeting with allegations and controversy. With much less regulation than today, the drug was tested on about 6,000 women in Puerto Rico. The high dose did result in effective contraception, but also caused much nausea and vomiting. Psychiatric disturbances, debilitating strokes and as many as 5 deaths have been alleged as occurring but unreported. Remember, those pills had 10mg of estrogen, today?s pills have 20mcg to 30mcg, so those early subjects were taking about 400 times the modern dose.
      Searle?s Enovid-10 was introduced to the U.S. market in 1960. About a year later, Syntex licensed their product to Johnson and Johnson which sold it as Ortho Novum. The formulations were constantly tinkered with and the amount of estrogen per pill plummeted.
But, how do you make a hormone? Today, manufacturers synthesize various progestins and the only link to a natural product that I could find in that process is that the alcohol used as a solvent comes from sugar beets. The hormonal precursor no longer comes from yams. The ethinyl estriadol found in todays pills is synthesized from a soy bean extract. Chemistry! That?s how you make a hormone!

- Carl Labbe R. Ph.

      Recently, the FDA approved 2 new oral contraceptives that utilize 24 active pills with 4 inactive pills- a deviation from the conventional 21/7 dosing regimen. The two new 24/4 oral contraceptives are:

  • Loestrin 24 Fe (1 mg norethindrone acetate and 22 mcg ethinyl estradiol tablets, USP and 75 mg ferrous fumarate tablets). Approved in February 2006.
  • Yaz (3 mg drospirenone / 0.02 mg ethinyl estradiol). Approved in March 2006.
      Oral contraceptives (OCs) have evolved since its 1st introduction in the 1960?s. Today?s 21/7 OCs have lower estrogen levels that results in better side effect profiles. However there are still drawbacks in the 21/7 dosing regimen. The standard 7 day pill free interval has a potential reduction of ovarian inhibition, with increasing monthly hormone-withdrawal symptoms- pelvic pain, headaches, bloating, and breast tenderness during the pill free period.
      By shortening the pill-free interval, the manufactures are hoping to provide contraceptive efficacy while significantly reducing the incidence of hormone withdrawal symptoms. Yaz and Loestrin 24 Fe will both have similar side effects with other OCs.
These two products will provide women an alternative dosing schedule to better fit their needs.
- Qui Vo, student pharmacist


Extending the cycle

      Two student pharmacists recently did a survey to determine patient satisfaction with extended cycle oral contraceptives (ECOC) and to assess why conventional oral contraceptive (COC) users might be reluctant to use extended-use oral contraceptives. Here is an excerpt of their report:
      Patients at Arizona State University and University of Arizona Campus Health pharmacies were administered questionnaires when picking up prescriptions for oral contraceptives. Questionnaires collected ratings of satisfaction with side effects and quality of life with patients? current oral contraceptive and assessed familiarity with, and reasons for and against using, extended-cycle oral contraceptives.
      Oral contraceptives are one of the most regularly used and effective methods of contraception. Oral contraceptives contain either a combination of estrogen and progestin or progestin only. Most women using oral contraceptives are utilizing the combination pill. Dosing schedules are either extended cycle oral contraceptives (ECOC; taking one active pill orally daily for 84 days followed by 7 days of daily placebo pills) or conventional oral contraceptives (COC; taking one active pill orally daily for 21 days followed by 7 days of daily placebo pills).
      A satisfaction and quality of life survey of patients using low dose oral contraceptives conventionally indicated a significant improvement in quality of life. The questionnaire used in this study has also been used in other clinical trials and has been found to be reliable and valid.
      We collected a total of 421 surveys (ASU=200, UofA=221), of which 24 were ECOC and 397 COC.
      There was a significant difference between the overall satisfaction levels of the two groups (?2 = 3.84, d.f. = 1, p = 0.04) with a greater proportion of COC users rating themselves as greatly satisfied with their current oral contraceptive regimen than ECOC users. Moreover, a lower proportion of COC users reported dissatisfaction with their current oral contraceptive regimen than ECOC users.
      Fear of adverse effects was the most commonly reported reason why women would not want to use an extended cycle oral contraceptive regimen (>40% of respondents). The belief that amenorrhea is unnatural, fear of pregnancy, fear of infertility, and cost were also reported by 23-45% of COC users and 12-25% of ECOC users as reasons against ECOC use. Women taking conventional oral contraceptives selected amenorrhea being unnatural as a reason against taking ECOCs significantly more than ECOC users (?2 = 7.15, d.f. = 1, p=0.007; Figure 4). Less than 1% of respondents reported either religion or cultural biases as reasons for not using ECOC.
Not surprisingly, the two groups differed significantly regarding familiarity with and willingness to use an extended cycle oral contraceptive regimen with ECOC users being more familiar and willing than COC users (?2 = 5.54, d.f. = 1, p = 0.01 and ?2 = 6.49, d.f. = 1, p = 0.01). Frequency of spotting experienced by both groups differed significantly as well (?2 = 9.48, d.f. = 1, p = 0.002). Women taking an extended-cycle oral contraceptive regimen reported experiencing spotting more frequently than women using a conventional oral contraceptive regimen. The extended cycle oral contraceptive users also had a lower proportion of women reporting that they never experienced spotting in comparison to conventional oral contraceptive users.

- Hilary Davis, Denise LumLung,
Student pharmacists
