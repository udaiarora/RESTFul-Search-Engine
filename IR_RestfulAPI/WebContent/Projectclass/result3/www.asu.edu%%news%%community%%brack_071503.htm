July 15, 2003

Brack receives Hon Kachina Award

Gay Brack, associate executive director of the Division of Undergraduate Academic Services at ASU, has been named a 2003 Hon Kachina award recipient.

The award is given by the Hon Kachina Council, a non-profit organization affiliated with St. Luke's Health Initiatives. It is Arizona's most prestigious volunteer award, given to outstanding volunteers annually since 1977.

The founder of ASU's Service Learning program, now known as the Academic Community Engagement Services (ACES), Brack has implemented and oversees a variety of tutoring programs, including the ASU Service Learning Program, America Reads, America Counts, JumpStart and VISTA. These programs are currently conducted at a total of 16 sites. In the past 10 years, Brack has supervised more than 6,000 ASU students volunteering 610,000 hours of tutoring and mentoring to more than 11,000 children, youth and parents.

Brack became involved with mentor learning in 1992, when she and her son organized a tutoring program at the Salvation Army South Mountain Youth and Family Center.

The next year, she expanded the program by creating the Supplemental Education Partnership, a collaboration between ASU, the Salvation Army and the Roosevelt School District. After it began in spring 1994, the concept proved so successful, it has grown into an experiential learning program in 16 inner-city locations, and at a charter school on the Salt River Pima-Maricopa Indian community. This program involves 300 - 375 students per semester, who earn college credit while acting as tutors and mentors to children, many with non-English speaking parents.

The Roosevelt School District has given full credit to this tutoring program for doubling the Stanford 9 scores of participating sixth graders, and for helping first through fourth graders to achieve 75 percent mastery on district-wide tests.
Brack says ASU students also benefit from the program in many ways.

"Many of our tutors claim that they learned more than they taught," she says. "In this multicultural environment, they learn a great deal about other ethnicities and about themselves as well. Working closely with two children for a semester is a real growth experience for them."

The tutors teach materials from their studies, currently 34 different subjects ranging from the sciences - plant biology, microbiology - to architecture and African children's literature. Brack says many of the ASU students decide to become educators after their tutor/mentor experience.

The Hon Kachina Council is a group of business and professional people, affiliated with St. Luke's Health Initiatives, who promote and support community volunteerism. The Hon Kachina Council and KNXV-TV ABC 15 will present the Hon Kachina Awards on Sept. 20 at Marriott's Camelback Inn.
