March 19, 2003

SEE ASU puts university research, science activities on display

Every Monday from 3 to 5 p.m., anywhere from two to eight ASU students gather for some "group therapy."

Low voices and a steady stream of thumps emanate from the upstairs room where they are gathered.

It's not a new kind of counseling. It's just the weekly gathering of the group that studies "lithic technology" by banging rocks together with other rocks or antler to make stone tools.

Most of the group's members are archaeology majors or graduate students who have taken on a special interest in tool making.

"We want to learn a little about the behavior and material that went into the stone tools that were crafted throughout prehistory," said Josh Watts, the group's unofficial leader. "And, we all emphasized stone tools in our research."

The ASU toolmakers will be demonstrating their craft at the sixth annual SEE ASU March 28 - 29 at Wells Fargo Arena.

SEE ASU is a free, yearly open house where ASU scientists take the stage to entertain, enlighten and spark interest in science, research and technology in young students' minds.

Friday is reserved for school tours, and Saturday is open to the public, from 9 a.m. to 3 p.m.

Among the more than 40 activities will be "Bugs, Bugs, Bugs," a display of insects from around the world; "You Call This Math?" a display of cryptographic cards, patterns in juggling and puzzling dice; and Ask A Biologist, ASU's Web program for biology students, their teachers and parents, which will have a display of bones.

Most of the exhibits will include demonstrations and hands-on activities, such as the ASU toolmakers, who pick up volcanic rock near Flagstaff and sedimentary/metamorphic rocks from the Payson area to make the tools. They use antler and bone to "knap" the stones, once the first shaping is done.

The ASU toolmakers, or "flintnappers," prize antler, as did their predecessors in the Southwest, because it is a "very hard, tough material," Watts said. "For more control over your work, it helps to have a softer hammer than stone, such as antler. Antler is very hard but it is soft enough that it can be shaped so that it is comfortable to use as a hammer."

"It's the Swiss Army knife of prehistoric times," said ASU Anthropology Professor Michael Barton.

Watts said some flintknappers make replicas that are sold to museums or private collections, while others make stone tools for scientists that experiment with them carving up cadavers or even doing surgery on living patients - a great way to help others and learn about archaeology.
