December 7, 2005

Storad's book plays integral role in governor's literacy project

A room full of rowdy, noisy first-graders can be an intimidating situation for most people, but not for Conrad Storad. With a nearly 15-year history of writing and reading children’s books, he calmly strides into a first-grade classroom with his book in one hand and a colorful puppet on the other.

The room becomes silent – and a sea of young, curious faces peer at Storad admirably. As a local “celebrity” among Arizona’s children, Storad immediately grabs the crowd’s attention and begins a story – not just a typical fantasy story, but a real story.

Storad has a knack for explaining the facts. For nearly 20 years, Storad has been the director of research publications at ASU, serving as the editor of nationally award-winning ASU Research Magazine and the founding editor of Chain Reaction, a science magazine for younger readers.

In addition to his responsibilities at ASU, Storad has written 17 children’s books, some of them coloring books, nonfiction books and what he likes to call “fun nonfiction” or picture books. With a lifelong fascination for science and nature, Storad says it can be a complex subject to learn, so he tries to teach youths about science with visually stimulating books.

“I’ve always wanted to be a scientist – an oceanographer, to be exact,” Storad says. “But writing opened doors for me that I never expected, and I blended the two disciplines.”

After receiving a bachelor’s degree in mass media communication from University of Akron in 1979, Storad served as a science writer for the National Cancer Institute at Kent State University. He relocated to Arizona in 1982, obtained a master’s degree from the Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication and became editor of ASU Research Magazine in 1986. Soon thereafter, a close friend approached Storad to write a children’s book.

“Initially I thought, ‘No way! I can’t write a children’s book.’ But as I thought about it more, it sounded like a new and different experience, so I tried it,” Storad recalls. “Writing for children really challenges you as a writer. You need to polish the story and make every sentence sing.”

Storad says he contemplated all possible topics for his first book, including exotic creatures and unusual plants, but he finally decided on a familiar desert plant: the Saguaro cactus. In 1992, after several story revisions, Storad published his first nonfiction children’s book, titled “Saguaro Cactus,” which teaches students about Arizona’s unique, native plant.

Since it can be difficult to engage children long enough to read a book cover to cover, Storad’s books have a few important elements to keep children engaged in the story: vivid illustrations, simple explanations and rhyme.

“Books have to compete with many things for a child’s attention,” Storad says. “Every word and sentence must be interesting, or the book is short-lived in the child’s mind. Kids want the real story, not so much fantasy. But, they want the real story in a fun way.”

After rave reviews on the first book, the requests began to roll in, one after another. Storad continued writing nonfiction books and was soon approached to write a picture book. He spent a year writing the most popular of his books, “Don’t Call Me Pig! (A Javelina Story),” which depicts a javelina’s life in the desert.

“Don’t Call Me Pig,” published in 1999, took Storad to the next level as a children’s author. In 2001, Storad was named the Arizona Children’s Author of the Year by the Arizona Library Association, and the book itself was awarded the Arizona Children’s Book of the Year by the Arizona Book Publishing Association.

Perhaps one of the most notable achievements for Storad occurred in November, when “Don’t Call Me Pig” was chosen by Gov. Janet Napolitano as the book to read. As part of the governor’s commitment to improve literacy in Arizona, she provides a free book to every first-grader in the state. In her third year of this promise, Napolitano selected Storad’s javelina tale as this year’s book to distribute to Arizona’s 90,000 first-grade students.

At a book reading at Galveston Elementary School in Chandler on Nov. 17, Storad observed as Napolitano read his book to 150 first-graders, who all knew the book and happily screamed aloud, “Don’t Call Me Pig!”

“At the book reading, the governor actually introduced me to the crowd – which was, in a simple term, awesome,” Storad says. “How many times does that happen in a lifetime? I was absolutely honored to the core.”

Although Storad spent his time writing children’s books, he says the “hobby” has helped him excel at ASU. Through his relationships with teachers and librarians, Storad recognized a need for a quality, informative science magazine for young readers. Thus, Storad received the financial backing to create Chain Reaction in 1999, which covers science, learning and creative activity taking place at ASU.

“Chain Reaction is a great example of ASU being an integral part of the community,” Storad says. “Since the late ’90s, ASU’s research publications have appeared in Arizona’s classrooms from kindergarten to high school.”

Over the past 10 years, Storad has visited more than 500 schools, reading his books to more than half a million children. When asked what he is trying to accomplish with his books, Storad smiles and says, “I just want to tell fun stories about real things. I want to create awareness of what’s around us and teach kids to respect it.”

Storad is working on a few upcoming books that will cover such topics as the earth’s crust, fossil fuels, meerkats and pack rats. His newest book, “Life In The Slow Lane (A Desert Tortoise Tale),” hits bookstores and libraries in December.
