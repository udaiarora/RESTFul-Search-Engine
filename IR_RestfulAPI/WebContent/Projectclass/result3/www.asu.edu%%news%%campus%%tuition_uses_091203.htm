September 12, 2003

Tuition increase improves access, ASU student experience

Students are the major beneficiaries of this year's tuition increase, though the increased revenues will lift the entire university. The largest portion provides increased financial assistance for students.

Other improvements being funded with tuition revenues include expanded library hours, improved classroom facilities, better access to advising and increased availability of classes, as well as improved compensation for teaching assistants.

A more accurate estimate of tuition revenues will not be available until the 21st day of the semester, when enrollment numbers are official. Based on current information, however, total revenues from the tuition increase are estimated to be between $34 million and $39 million for Main Campus, and between $44 million and $50 million for the entire university.

Last spring ASU President Michael Crow met with students to learn what their academic priorities were for the use of the funds. The Associated Students of Arizona also surveyed students electronically, and identified a list of their requests.

"We indeed kept the students' priorities at top of mind and attempted to address each of them to an appropriate extent within the resource availability," says Milton Glick, executive vice president and provost of the university. "I believe our budget allocation keeps full faith with the students."

Funds already have been set aside for the highest priority, increasing student financial aid by about 140 percent. Main Campus is expected to receive between $11 million and $11.7 million, and the entire university between $13 million and $14 million. ASU has made a systematic effort to reach out and tell families how to apply for financial aid, communicating in both English and Spanish.

Hours at Hayden Library have been expanded to 114 hours a week, another high priority expressed by students, who were surveyed on what hours they are most likely to use the library. This places ASU third among peer institutions for library access and fifth among the 27 largest academic libraries. Cost is expected to be $170,000.

Students also asked for new desks in the Language & Literature Building, to replace the existing small, 40-year-old desks. New seating will be in place throughout the building this fall - 961 new seats at a cost of $500,000.

Students requested improved technology in classrooms and more computer sites and computing stations. Thirty-three new mediated classrooms with seats for 2,058 students will come on line by the end of the fall semester, as well as a new student computing facility with 150 stations. Tuition revenues of $8 million have been earmarked for their operation and maintenance as well as a share of debt service.

Technology also will be upgraded in 30 additional Main Campus classrooms during the academic year, at a cost of $350,000. ASU West will receive $500,000 in state-of-the-art equipment for a new classroom building, and ASU East will receive $173,000 to improve instructional facilities.

Students showed great concern about the compensation for teaching assistants (TA), and ASU faculty and staff were not opposed to directing tuition dollars toward solving the problem. A partial waiver of in-state tuition was increased from .25 to .55 for a half-time TA and from .125 to .275 for a quarter-time TA, at a cost of $2 million.

To ensure that enough classes are available to serve students, dollars were distributed to units with the greatest enrollment growth and those having difficulty meeting the demand. Though it is a temporary measure that cannot completely address the problem, Main Campus received $2.7 million, ASU West $320,000 and ASU East $550,000.

To improve the student-faculty ratio, ASU West will initiate searches for 40 new faculty, at a cost of $680,000. ASU East will invest $325,000 in tenured/tenure track faculty. Officials say, however, it is a temporary solution to a university-wide problem.

Improved student services and advising were a high priority for students, so enhanced Web access to advising is now available and a task force is looking at other improvements. Other services will be enhanced at ASU West at a cost of $400,000, and at ASU East for $250,000. A feasibility study also is underway on moving forward with plans to create a wireless campus.

"Our tuition, even with the increase, is still among the 10 lowest states in the nation," says Glick. "An ASU education is a very good value, indeed a bargain."

ASU also will cover unfunded staff health insurance costs and increases in contributions to the State Retirement System, at a cost of $8 million. Further efforts are under way to identify funds for performance-based salary increases and strategic investments.
