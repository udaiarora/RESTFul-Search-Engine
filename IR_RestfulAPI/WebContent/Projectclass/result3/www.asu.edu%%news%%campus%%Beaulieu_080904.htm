Beaulieu to lead ASU's Center for Indian Education

David Beaulieu, former director of the U.S. Office of Indian Education, has been named director of ASU's Center for Indian Education. He comes to ASU from the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee, where he is the Electa Quinney Endowed Professor of American Indian Education.

A national expert on policy development that will bolster educational opportunities for American Indians, Beaulieu began his new position this month, replacing Denis Viri, who has served as the center's interim director since January 2002. 

The center is a major research and resource hub in the field of Indian Education and related fields at the local, state and national levels. Founded in 1959, it provides a wide variety of services to American Indian communities and to ASU students, staff and faculty.

 "I am very excited to be assuming leadership of the Center for Indian Education at ASU," Beaulieu says. "The center is the oldest research entity focused on the education of Native peoples, and has significant potential to position the college and university as the leader in the study and advancement of new knowledge related to the education of indigenous peoples worldwide.

"David Beaulieu's work in policy development geared to increase and enhance educational opportunities for American Indians in federal, tribal and state government arenas is significant and wide-reaching," says Eugene Garcia, College of Education dean and vice president for university-school partnerships. "His vision and experiences are firmly aligned with the overarching mission of the Center of Indian Education."

A graduate of the University of Minnesota, with master's and doctoral degrees in education administration, Beaulieu was director of the U.S. Office of Indian Education from 1997 to 2001, and was a member of the Senior Executive Service of the U.S. Department of Education. Beaulieu also has held faculty positions at the University of Minnesota, where he was associate professor and chairman of the Department of American Indian Studies. He was also vice president of Sintè Gleska College, Rosebud, S.D. where he focused on institutional and academic development with the goal of attaining accreditation. In addition to his work as a professor and administrator, Beaulieu served as a member of the Indian Nations at Risk Task Force created to advise and make recommendations to the U.S. Secretary of Education and the nation on the condition of the education of American Indians in the United States.
