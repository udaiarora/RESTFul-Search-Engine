February 20, 2004

Urban ecology symposium uncovers life in the big city

Politicians, government officials, local reporters and even sophisticated urbanites may be able to claim that they have a handle on "what makes Phoenix tick," but if you want to really find out how the city lives and breathes, you would be well advised to come to Arizona State University on Monday and listen to what the urban researchers have to say.

That's because on February 23 from 1 p.m. to 4:30 p.m. the Central Arizona - Phoenix Long-Term Ecological Research project is presenting new and cumulative results from its six-year-old research program at the Annual Poster Symposium, held this year in the Carson Ballroom of ASU's Old Main.

The CAP LTER project is a National Science Foundation-funded, ongoing, multidisciplinary research project aimed at understanding the intersection of environmental, ecological, economic and social forces in urban areas, and represents one of the most established and comprehensive studies in urban ecology in the world.

CAP LTER researchers and community partners will present 53 posters at the symposium, representing a range of  current research topics, including the effects of large-scale environmental changes, human-environment interaction, urban biology, urban biophysics and ecosystem function, methods of defining and analyzing urban spaces/environments, and urban ecology education and outreach activities.

The event begins  at 1 p.m. with a keynote address by renowned ecologist O.J. Reichman, director of the National Center for Ecological Analysis and Synthesis at the University of California at Santa Barbara. He will speak on "The Nature of Synthesis and Collaboration: Research Tools for Urban Ecology."

Following the opening address, the symposium will present CAP LTER research in two separate poster sessions, where printed summaries of research projects will be on display with the researchers present to answer questions. Among research topics that might be of interest are presentations on the effect of human activities on climate, including the urban heat-island effect and factors that mitigate it; presentations on the relationship of economic factors to neighborhood ecology; presentations on air quality and water use research; and presentations on how aerial photography and other forms of remote sensing are being used to analyze Phoenix's vast urban area.

One particular set of presentations also takes a detailed look at some of the distinctive, often largely ignored, nature that surrounds city-dwellers - the urban jungle. Symposium presentations look at urban populations of reptiles, birds, plants, insects and even specialized soil fungi, for example. One poster, entitled "Differences in bird foraging behavior between Sonoran Desert and urban habitats in central Arizona," explains how birds in Phoenix have been "urbanized" and behave differently in the city than they do in the surrounding desert. Other presentations examine how landscaping choices made by homeowners and communities literally determine the character of the urban "forest" and the habitat it creates. Still other presentations examine how human irrigation and pruning practices affect the growth patterns of an urban area's plant cover and involve complex interactions.

The CAP LTER Annual Poster Symposium is free and open to the public. For more information, contact ASU's Center for Environmental Studies at 480-965-2975.
