Dennis Durband

June 5, 2003


MILLENNIUM FELLOWS ADD NEW KNOWLEDGE TO MULTIPLE DISCIPLINES

 
Gram Jones
  
Eric Haas

Recipients of the Millennium Interdisciplinary Dissertation Fellowship (MIDF) personify research that can best be approached through multiple disciplines. ASU doctoral students Gram Jones and Eric Haas, winners of the 2003 fellowship, will add new knowledge to multiple fields with the interdisciplinary dissertations they plan to produce in the coming year. The Graduate College presents MIDF awards annually to promote interdisciplinary dissertation research at ASU. Jones majors in physical anthropology and will combine that discipline with biomechanics in her dissertation work. Haas is majoring in educational leadership and policy studies and will bridge education, policy studies and English in his dissertation project.

"The Millennium Interdisciplinary Dissertation Fellowship supports groundbreaking, innovative interdisciplinary research by ASU doctoral students," said Marjorie Zatz, associate dean of the Graduate College. "These students have proven ability to master the concepts and skills of multiple disciplines and to effectively bridge these fields. They are conducting highly original research and potentially quite significant research."

Millennium Fellows are engaged in research with colleagues from different disciplines and from at least two different academic units on campus. They have already defended their dissertation proposals and will devote the coming year to the writing of their dissertations.

In recent years, conservative think tanks have emerged as significant reference sources for the news media. Haas will examine the presentation of think tank information in news reports on education in a study that should aid education policy makers and practitioners, the public, and researchers in a variety of disciplines. He plans to focus his dissertation on the question, "How do the news media portray discourse about education that originates in 'think tanks,' especially conservative think tanks?"

Haas will work with Mary Lee Smith, professor of educational leadership and policy studies; Karen Adams, co-director of the English Ph.D. concentration in rhetoric/composition/linguistics; and David Altheide, Regents' Professor in Justice Studies. By using a Nexis data base, Haas has identified for examination 1,800 samples from newspaper and magazine articles and radio and television news transcripts. He will review story samples from the time period of 2001–2002, which is significant because it marked the beginning of President George W. Bush's administration, and the president made education reform a key component of his agenda. Haas will apply a linguistics approach, specifically critical discourse analysis, to determine the power relations and ideologies present in news reports on education. This kind of research has never been done in a study focused on education.

"The study should likely critique—supporting or contradicting—the anecdotal and well-marketed publications that contend that the news media has a liberal bias," Haas said. "This study will aid political scientists in continuing to understand the effects of think tanks and the media on the political process in a capitalistic democracy. There are few studies examining how the news media report on education. Understanding the texts of the news reports and the production and interpretation of the news reports should shed light on the construction of public opinion and policy-making, generally, and in education specifically." Jones' experience in two interdisciplinary research enterprises will prove beneficial in her work. She has been a research assistant in the interdisciplinary Partnership for Research in Stereo Modeling (PRISM) project and is currently a fellow in the Integrative Graduate Education and Research Traineeship (IGERT): Neural and Musculoskeletal Adaptations in Form and Function program, both at ASU.

Along with Professor Mary Marzke, Jones has applied to the National Science Foundation for a grant to fund a comparative study of the hand and wrist of two primate species: the tufted capuchin and the common squirrel monkey. The question addressed by her dissertation work is whether there are unique morphological features of the capuchin hand that reflect the distinctive manipulative behaviors of these monkeys. Capuchins frequently utilize repetitive, forceful, manipulative behaviors such as the pounding of food objects in the wild and tool-use in captivity, unlike other monkeys. Capuchins are most closely related to squirrel monkeys based on molecular evidence. Because capuchins and squirrel monkeys are both primarily arboreal quadrupeds, it is likely that differences in their hand and wrist morphology are related to differences in foraging behaviors rather than locomotion. Jones will collect quantitative musculoskeletal data on the hand and wrist of the capuchin and squirrel monkey by using biomechanical techniques. Although capuchins are noted for their superior manipulative abilities that rival those of the chimpanzee, the primate that is most commonly used to model tool-use in early human species and closely related species, studies of capuchin hand and wrist morphology are few and primarily descriptive. Jones' dissertation work will provide new comparative primate data for identifying features of the human hand that may reflect ancestral feeding behaviors and help to tease out unique skeletal features that may have evolved in humans in response to stresses associated with stone tool-making.

John Chance, chair in the Department of Anthropology, said, "Gram Jones' dissertation project concerns the musculoskeletal correlates of the foraging behavior of capuchin monkeys. This research is of considerable significance to the study of human behavior, for capuchin foraging activities stress the hand in ways that are thought to have been common among our early human ancestors. This is cutting-edge research on a significant scientific problem that will interest researchers in several fields."
