August 29, 2003

Design Team helps chart course for ASUís evolution

Rapid growth is a known factor for ASUís future. What isnít known is how the university will evolve to accommodate that growth, which is expected to result in 90,000 students enrolled by the end of the next decade.

The entire university, including faculty, staff, students and administrators, has an opportunity to participate in the planning and design of the universityís evolution.

To get the process rolling, ASU Executive Vice President and Provost Milton Glick appointed a University Design Team to sketch out some suggestions and recommendations for the future of ASU. A starting point for the team was coming to terms with the fact that the Main Campus in Tempe is almost at capacity, meaning much of the expected growth will have to be absorbed by the East and West campuses.

In its initial report, the University Design Team envisions ASU as a single research university with multiple campuses sharing the ASU identity. But each campus will also have its own character and spires of excellence.

This is not an easy task when they must take into consideration factors like enrollment impacts, program constituencies served, program interdependencies for faculty and students, financial resources, organizational alternatives, space, research and accreditation issues.

Members of the team were asked to remove their unit hats and develop a set of possibilities for a five-to 10-year road map for ASU that would enhance the campus identities; accommodate the anticipated growth in student demand by building attractive programs on the East, West and Downtown campuses; and maximize opportunities to enhance the quality and performance of all university programs.

The first phase of the project for the team was to determine the character and identity of each of ASUís campus locations (Main, East, West & Downtown). The team also looked at how to establish differentiation among the four locations as well as examining distinctive missions, thrusts and clusters of programs. The team also examined whether or not each location has the ASU brand.

In its preliminary report, the team determined there should be some commonalities among each campus location. The report recommends that each campus:

• Offer degree programs at the baccalaureate, masterís and doctoral levels;
• Conduct programs of research and creative activities;
• Provide continuing and extended education;
• Offer programs leading to professional degrees at the baccalaureate and post-baccalaureate levels;
• Seek racial and ethnic diversification of the student body and the faculty and staff;
• Support activities designed to promote economic, social and cultural development of Arizona;
• Commit to student success in all aspects of their academic and personal development;
• Serve the needs of women, minority, disadvantaged, disabled and non-traditional students; and
• Encourage faculty and staff to be engaged with the greater metropolitan region.
Glick says no decisions have been made based on the design team report.

“The University Design Team has done an excellent job preparing this preliminary report and providing us with a starting point for the numerous discussions that will ensue,” he says. “As we move forward with this process, itís important that our focus be on what is best for the future of the university and how we can realize our vision of becoming a New American University that is high quality, open and accessible and research intensive.”

The major focus of the teamsí report was to identify programs that are needed at each campus to support or define the identities of each location. The question became, should existing programs be relocated or should new programs be started that help build the image of a particular campus?

“Weíre really in need of input from the university community,” says Glick. “The new design for the university will affect everyone and this is one of the opportunities for everyone to have a say on possible changes. We know that the wisdom of the entire community will help shape the future of ASU in a positive fashion. So, itís very important for people to look at the report and share their thoughts.”

The entire report will be available beginning Sept. 2, at (www.asu.edu/provost/designteam/report/outline.html). The online version provides a link for submitting input, including suggestions, comments or concerns.
