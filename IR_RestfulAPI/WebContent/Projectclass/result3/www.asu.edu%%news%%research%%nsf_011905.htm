January 19, 2005

Conference connects inventors, investors

ASU played host to more than 500 National Science Foundation (NSF) grantees earlier this month at “Manufacturing for the New Millennium: New Products, Technologies, Services and Economic Development,” this year’s NSF conference for the Division of Design, Manufacturing and Industrial Innovation.

During the four-day event, NSF grantees presented their award-winning projects to potential investors and companies interested in technical and economic innovations for manufacturing.

Among the NSF grantees were eight members of the Ira A Fulton School of Engineering faculty, including professors Jami J. Shah and J. Rene Villalobos, the conference’s co-hosts.

More than 800 attended the event, including industry partners and representatives from companies interested in acquiring intellectual property, and investing in or collaborating with grantees on breakthrough products, technologies and processes.

Seminars and sessions focusing on entrepreneurship, connecting with investors, and collaborations and partnerships with industry provided grantees with practical advice from NSF Program staff and industry leaders.

In a session Partnering with Industry, grantees heard from several companies participating in MatchMaker, an NSF program that matches small high-tech businesses with potential investors and strategic partners. Among the speakers were representatives from Proctor & Gamble , Texas Instruments, Lockheed Martin and General Motors, who presented information on how grantees can convert their technology into sustainable and profitable businesses.

Matchmaker companies are looking for innovations in manufacturing technology, and grant recipients were encouraged to think like business owners as well as inventors.

Stressing the nuts and bolts of creating a new business from which to launch an invention, Keith Gutierrez of Texas Instruments’ Semiconductor Group told grantees: “Often we see a great idea from an inventor, but there’s no business plan. It’s important to wear both the inventor and business owner hats.”

For Small Business Innovation Research and Small Business Technology Transfer grantees, collaborating and partnering with industry is a means of bringing their inventions to market more quickly – and there is plenty of opportunity to connect.

According to Edward E. Getty of Proctor & Gamble, “P&G will acquire 50 percent of new products and technology from outside the company.”
