July 18, 2003

University group sponsors restoration of 'Southwest Pieta'

After years of intense sun exposure, the faded color and dull fiberglass surface of "Southwest Pieta" have been restored to their original brilliance.

Los Angeles conservator John Griswold stripped away blistered outer layers, deftly repaired damaged sections with original color, and then reapplied several clear layers of protective coating to one of ASU's most valued public artworks.

Following contemporary conservation theory, Griswold thoroughly photographed the sculpture, located on the Nelson Fine Arts Plaza, before beginning work and used reversible measures to make sure any work could be "undone" if the sculpture needed to be brought back to its original condition. Griswold also consulted with the sculpture's artist Luis Jimenez to match color and texture.

"A large part of conservation is documentation," said Griswold. "In preserving the object for the future, it's important to document what has been done."

The final step in the restoration process is a coat of wax. Noting the fiberglass exterior and comparing it to a Corvette body, Jimenez recommends regular applications of car wax.

Completed in 1987, "Southwest Pieta" was purchased as part of the ASU Percent-for-Art Program and installed on the plaza. The six-foot-tall work depicts a well-known icon of the Mexican culture, the legend of Mexica warrior Popocatepetl and Ixtaccihuatl, the Aztec emperor's daughter. The pair, from incompatible social classes, fell in love against the will of their parents. Though separated in life, the gods turn them into volcanoes - one active, one dormant - for eternity.

Behind the pair, Jimenez includes the eagle, the rattlesnake and the cactus, which all play an important part in the religion and ritual of Native Americans.

"What I like about 'Southwest Pieta' is a kind of commonality of symbols and images," he says. "Those same images and symbols that are so important in Mexico are equally important to us in the United States - certainly the eagle - it's the national symbol for both countries."

The New Mexico artist, who has exhibited his work at the Museum of Modern Art and Whitney Museum of American Art in New York, the Smithsonian Institution in Washington, D.C., and many others, considers this people's art, meaning the images are coming from the popular culture, as is the material. "I am using the same material used for making boat bodies, car bodies and Mack Trucks."

This restoration project was sponsored by the ASU Public Art Fund and administered by the Herberger College of Fine Arts Office of Public Art.

Cripe, with the Herberger College Office of Public Art, can be reached at (480) 965-0951 or (dianne.cripe@asu.edu).
